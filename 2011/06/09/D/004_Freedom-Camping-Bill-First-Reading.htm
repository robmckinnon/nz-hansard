Freedom Camping Bill — First Reading

[Volume:673;Page:19262]

Freedom Camping Bill

First Reading

Hon KATE WILKINSON (Minister of Conservation) : I move, That the Freedom Camping Bill be now read a first time. At the appropriate time I intend to move that the Local Government and Environment Committee consider the Freedom Camping Bill, that the committee report finally to the House on or before 1 August 2011, and that the committee have authority to meet at any time while the House is sitting except during oral questions, and during any evening on a day on which there has been a sitting of the House, and on a Friday in a week in which there has been a sitting of the House, and outside the Wellington region despite Standing Orders 187, 189(a), and 190(1)(b) and (c).

The purpose of this bill is to address the negative effects of freedom camping. Many visitors stopping along our roadsides at rest areas and look-outs face more than a spectacular view. Too often they also face a pile of human waste and toilet paper. This is often a “gift” from a visitor who hired a cheap campervan with no toilet, and then used the rest area or look-out to camp overnight in order to avoid paying camping ground fees. We are an outdoors destination. We want tourists to continue to come here to explore and enjoy our countryside. New Zealanders do just that on their own holidays, and we want to protect our reputation. Inappropriate camping and toileting does this country a disservice, and we do not want this behaviour to be lodged in anyone’s memories when they get home. Freedom camping is an important part of our tourism industry and great Kiwi lifestyle, but we cannot tolerate irresponsible campers spoiling our most iconic areas with human waste and litter.

This is particularly important as we gear up for the Rugby World Cup. Roughly 85,000 visitors are expected to arrive on our shores. For many of those this will be the holiday of a lifetime, so we are acting rapidly to ensure that this legislation is in place by then.

The number of freedom campers has doubled over the past decade to 110,000 international visitors and more than 40,000 New Zealanders. The existing system, in which each of our 67 districts has its own bylaws, is not working for the responsible freedom camper wanting to do the right thing, or for councils wanting to protect their local environment. This bill will enable councils to determine where camping is allowed, where it is restricted to campervans with self-containment, and where it is prohibited. The Department of Conservation will be able to make similar rules on the reserves it manages. There will be nationally consistent signage and practical enforcement provisions. A website will be provided, outlining where people can and cannot camp nationally. Camping at specific sensitive sites and depositing human waste on roadsides is already illegal, but councils and the Department of Conservation have very limited options for dealing with offenders. This bill will increase the tools available to them by creating an infringement regime. This will avoid the need for councils and the Department of Conservation to take expensive court action, and therefore provide a more effective deterrent to inappropriate behaviour.

The bill will provide the same approach as that used for other relatively minor offences, such as parking and traffic offences. People can be ticketed when they camp in an area in which camping is prohibited, or camp in an area where camping is restricted and they breach those restrictions—for example, by using a campervan with no toilet in an area restricted to self-contained campervans, or by depositing waste whilst camping. The bill sets the infringement fee at $200, and I believe that this will provide a strong deterrent for potential offenders. Enforcement officers will still have the option of taking a matter to the courts if they consider that the offence warrants that action—for example, a campervan emptying its sewage tank in a rest area. In that case, a far higher penalty, to a maximum of $10,000, might be imposed. The ability to impose high fines already exists for breaches of council bylaws, including camping bylaws, but this bill provides a uniform regime across council land and public conservation land. It will enable local government agencies to effectively enforce their existing and future bylaws relating to camping.

I must emphasise that this bill does not change the locations where anyone can camp. The presumption is still that freedom camping is not only allowed but encouraged as a core part of the back-country experience. The infringement system will apply only in vehicle-accessible areas near formed roads, near the coast, and near Great Walks. Councils and the Department of Conservation already restrict camping where it would be a problem in specific sites—for example, in city car-parks—where camping could damage natural values, or where there is a more suitable camping site nearby. This bill is designed to allow such decisions to be effectively enforced. In the case of public conservation land, the bill will also provide a clear and transparent method for making such decisions in future, taking into account the purposes for which land is held and any management plans.

In summary, this bill will provide my department and councils with another tool to protect our open spaces, our tourism industry, and the interests of back-country users, and it will reinforce the back-country code. I commend this bill to the House.

Hon RUTH DYSON (Labour—Port Hills) : Conservation is one of those areas that arouse a huge amount of passion and pride from all New Zealanders. What is most disappointing about the debate that has commenced is the total lack of passion, vision, support, or understanding that the Minister of Conservation demonstrated in her speech. That will be a big disappointment, as has been this Government’s contribution to conservation over the last 2½ years.

The Freedom Camping Bill has been warmly welcomed by many groups around this country, and Labour will certainly support its progress to the Local Government and Environment Committee. I am disappointed that we will have such little time at the select committee, as indicated by the Minister, to consider the concerns that have already been expressed publicly. As with many legislative provisions, the devil is in the detail. If we do not get it right at the select committee and report it back to the House, we are likely to cause problems. The rush, I understand, is to make sure the provisions are enabled before the Rugby World Cup. I personally do not think that is a good enough reason to rush legislation and possibly get it wrong.

As the Minister outlined, this provision is really an empowering one so that local councils can determine areas where they say freedom camping is not allowed. That is quite a big step for New Zealand to take. We have often prided ourselves on our welcoming attitude to people who want to get out and about and see the best of our country. We do not welcome people who pollute it. We do not welcome people who abuse the beauty we are so proud of and destroy it for other people. It is important to have that protection, but whether this bill is the right mechanism for doing it is certainly part of the debate we will have.

I am not sure I agree with the Minister that the instant fine regime is the most appropriate, and I do think that there will be more difficulty with enforcement than the Minister has outlined. For example, if a freedom camper gets a fine and then gets in his or her car and drives to the airport to leave the country as previously planned, what happens? Do we just say that that is bad luck? I am not sure we do. The legislation reads to me as if the vehicle owner has the responsibility, but it could be a rental car company—more likely than not it will be a rental car company. Then what happens? Does the rental car company have the onus of passing on the fine that has been incurred—unfairly, in my view? Does Hertz, for example, have the responsibility of chasing that person around the other side of the world in order to get that $200? That detail would be quite important, particularly to rental car companies.

Another concern I have is that there could be some relationship between motor camps and a council. The council will determine the zones where freedom camping is or is not allowed. If there is some relationship between a camping ground and a council, the council might say that it is to their benefit to exclude freedom camping in a large part of that area, for the benefit of the motor camp. That would not be fair on freedom campers. They would be unfairly punished because of that relationship. That is something we also might need to protect against.

Another area of concern that has been drawn to my attention, through quite considerable publicity around this bill, is the issue of trampers. It is very common in New Zealand for trampers to sleep the night at the start of the track or at the end of the track and then start their journey the next day, either on foot or by driving home. If that area is not allowed to be used as a freedom camping area, then those trampers might have to stay some distance away and ruin what is a very important part of tramping tradition of being able to get to the start of the track the night before and then start walking very, very early the next morning. Those are areas that I think deserve the rigour of attention.

I am sure Chris Auchinvole will guide the bill very well through the Local Government and Environment Committee, which in my view has developed a reputation—and a fair reputation—of paying considerable attention to detail in legislation. Our deliberations today were significantly enhanced by the presence of Aaron Gilmore, who contributed very well. It was fortunate he was there, because otherwise we may well have floundered on some of the detail, particularly in the local government provisions. It was extraordinarily helpful, and I hope he is able to sub in for some of the members as we consider this bill.

This bill has arisen because of the Government’s desire, which I certainly support, to ensure that precious areas of our country—areas where people want to visit, see, and enjoy—are protected against anything unpleasant, are damaged, or are destroyed by people who are not as respectful as we want them to be. We do not know whether that happens a lot or is quite rare, but we know that some councils have been particularly upset about it. I know that other colleagues will add to that.

Rural Women New Zealand is not a group that complains lightly. It has put on public record its concerns about the damage to specific environments that is being done by freedom campers. Mount Maunganui locals have complained of behaviour in their area. I am sure they were not referring to Simon Bridges or to any other local members of Parliament; they were referring to freedom campers. That area is a magnificent part of the country, and I am sure Simon Bridges wants to make sure it is protected, as well.

The same has happened in Queenstown and in Picton. Those are areas where we want to make sure that as we parade around the world, inviting people to come here and saying to people that our country is the best in the world and that it is 100% Pure New Zealand, we want to be able to back that up. When those people come to visit, we want to show them the pristine areas we so enjoy.

I find it frustrating, though, that the Minister supports this sort of legislation, as she did in her introductory speech, but at the same time has significantly undermined the ability of her own department—the Department of Conservation—to progress the very issues that this Freedom Camping Bill is designed to protect. Basically, instead of charging the Department of Conservation with protecting our waterways, protecting our land, and enhancing it for the betterment of all New Zealanders and visitors to enjoy, she has handed it over to Minister Brownlee and said he should have a look at the national park areas in schedule 4 to see whether there is anything there worth mining. In the most precious parts of New Zealand, she has said we should have a little surgical mining, as the Prime Minister described it.

I was stunned that the Minister of Conservation was prepared to hand over that part of her portfolio, which should be the area she defends most, to something as outrageous as mining in those protected areas. Members of the public were outraged, as well, and I was very proud that we had thousands and thousands of New Zealanders marching on the street and saying they want to protect the most precious parts of our country. These are the parts of the country that we also want this legislation to protect from freedom campers who are damaging it.

I will return to the actions the Minister has taken in terms of the Department of Conservation. The department is now required to be a profit-making organisation. That is not what the Department of Conservation was set up to do. It was designed to be the advocacy group, the lobby group, and the brains behind the passion that New Zealanders feel about their most important areas.

The Minister has led a huge and negative step backwards in her own portfolio. It is a big disappointment to many New Zealanders who care about our environment more than they care about anything else. I was surprised the Minister did not take the lead when this bill was originally publicised. In fact, it was the Minister for the Environment who took the lead in all of the media. I was quite surprised that the Minister of Conservation introduced the bill today. I thought it had perhaps been transferred to the Minister for the Environment, for some reason, because all of the publicity had Nick Smith’s name attached to it rather than that of the Hon Kate Wilkinson. That is a puzzle, but I am sure it is something I will spend more time discussing at the select committee, and I will see whether we can get to the bottom of it.

As I said, the principle of the legislation to protect our areas that are currently being damaged by freedom campers is supported by Labour. We will support this bill going to the select committee. I want the concerns I have raised—and others I am sure submitters will raise—to be addressed in the select committee process. I am sorry that it will be so truncated because of the Rugby World Cup. I think our rights to enjoy our country are worth protecting, and I will be ensuring that that happens.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH (Minister for the Environment) : I wish to take only a brief call in respect of the Freedom Camping Bill, and to acknowledge and support my colleague the Minister of Conservation, Kate Wilkinson. I acknowledge that Kate Wilkinson is the most practical Minister of Conservation that the department has had in more than a decade. The practical gains she is making for conservation are far better than some of the highbrow dreamer ideas we saw in the years of Labour. We on this side of the House are deeply proud of that work. Yes, this bill is a joint piece of work. The Minister of Local Government, the Minister for the Environment—me—and Kate Wilkinson have worked hard on bringing this package together, as we should. That is how we operate on this side of the House. I know that that side of the House is a bit incapable of that. I know that Chris Carter and Ruth Dyson do not talk these days. We on this side of the House work very closely together.

This bill is a very pragmatic response to the real problem of freedom campers defecating in some of our most iconic spots, leaving a right mess. This bill provides a practical response to that. It is no good leaving every one of our councils to make their own rules when, frankly, the 120,000-odd international tourists who come here actually would not know which district they are in from one day to the next. That is not a criticism; it is just the nature of the international traveller. That is why having a standardised approach between the Department of Conservation and the councils makes such good sense.

I commend the provision in this bill for a $200 instant fine for those who breach the freedom camping requirements. Labour has indicated some opposition to that. Let me just say it is totally impractical to say to the Department of Conservation or to a council that it has to spend 5,000 bucks of ratepayers’ or taxpayers’ money on trying to prosecute some tourist from the other side of the world for defecating at a precious campsite. It is not practical. A $200 fine is a really sensible way of dealing with it.

I was surprised that Ruth Dyson took issue with the provision that the rental car company should be responsible for the fine in the event of a visitor doing a runner. It is the same for speeding fines and parking fines. Do we not care for our environment as much as we care about those things? We all know what happens. If someone goes to Australia and gets a car from a rental car company, the company takes a copy of their credit card. If the person picks up a fine while they are there, it is charged back against them. The most important provision deals with the fact that some rental car companies—particularly those with rental vans without containers—have not been responsible. This bill will put some responsibility back on those companies to play their part in protecting the environment, and that is a good thing.

This bill does protect and retain the right for New Zealand families like mine to be able to go out and enjoy the great outdoors—and so it should. To reassure Ruth Dyson opposite, I tell her that nothing in this bill will constrain the right of her and her family or me and my family to go to the end of the Heaphy Track and camp there for a night before we proceed to tramp. That is quite plain in the provisions of the bill.

The last point I will make is that the underlying presumption of this bill is that people can camp anywhere unless a council specifically prescribes it as a non-camping area. Maybe the difference between this side of the House and Labour is that we trust the councils. We think they are pretty common sense.

Hon Ruth Dyson: Yeah, right! You abolished ours.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: Ruth Dyson wants to stand up for the failure of water management. Only 10 minutes ago she was giving a speech saying she is concerned about water quality. How can she defend the track record of Environment Canterbury, which after 18 years did not have a plan in place for dealing with water in the precious area of Canterbury?

The last thing I want to do is acknowledge Jacqui Dean, the member of Parliament for Otago. This cause is one she has championed both in Opposition and in Government. This issue is not new. It has been around for quite a while. Jacqui, both in caucus and in working with local authorities, has been pivotal in this bill getting to this point. I know that it concerns a particular issue in the Otago area she represents. That district should be very proud of the contribution she has made to this practical bill being brought to Parliament to have this environmental problem dealt with. It is a good bill, and I say good on Jacqui Dean and Kate Wilkinson for taking this practical step to deal with this environmental issue.

Hon MARYAN STREET (Labour) : I take great pleasure in rising to speak on the Freedom Camping Bill. As my colleague Ruth Dyson has said, Labour will be supporting this bill to go to the Local Government and Environment Committee.

This issue is a Nelson issue. It is a big issue in Nelson. The whole business of responsibility for the environment, and the responsibility of visitors to the loveliest parts of our country, is one of the presumptions of this legislation, and I support that. Over the summer in Nelson this issue kept coming up. I will give a couple of particular examples from my own experience. I have the privilege of living just a block away from a park, quite a large park, in Nelson. Parked down the end of a dead-end street that crosses the street I live in have been a number of motor homes and campervans, which began to attract a lot of public attention for two reasons. One was that up to about a dozen of them were parked there. I myself saw them there; it was not hard to see them on my way home. But those campervans were parked there in a place that was not a camping area. That is one issue.

The second thing is that the fouling of that area really upset local residents, including me. It is not good to go to bits of the road that abut parks, where people have stopped and parked their motor homes or campervans, and where, if the vans are not self-contained, they have then proceeded to use the public area as their own personal toilet. It is not responsible, and it is not what we want, either from tourists or from New Zealanders who are camping. It goes for everybody. This bill is about tourists who come to New Zealand to enjoy our picturesque areas. They must know that it is not acceptable for them to foul our beautiful environment any more than it is for any of us to do that. So I support this legislation.

The issue ran hot in Nelson because there was a real concern about the bill preventing people from enjoying our environment. I hope the Minister of Conservation has this approach right in the legislation, and we will find out shortly in the select committee. I think it is about right. The issue is not about preventing people from pulling up, parking, stopping over, and enjoying our lovely environment; nobody wants to stop that. With the people being called freedom campers, though, there was a bit of a backlash in Nelson, and it was said the bill would outlaw a way of life they enjoyed. But that is clearly not the purpose of the legislation, and I would not support it if it were. However, we on both sides of the House require people who want to enjoy Neale Park, or anywhere else in Nelson, to treat those areas with respect, and those areas that give people access to lovely views, water, and recreation in our environment. We do not ask for any more or any less than that.

I want to give another example that is also related to my own experience. Members of my family have a place in the Marlborough Sounds. That is not where I live; I live in Nelson, but it is common for Nelson people to holiday in the Marlborough Sounds and to go there for recreation. I am one of those people, and I thoroughly enjoy it. I will not tell people exactly where the place is, because they might go there and enjoy it too much! It happens that in this little bay where my family has a house there is a legitimate camping site that the Marlborough District Council has said can be a camping site for 2 nights only. But the locals have had to put up signs saying “Please don’t treat this like your personal toilet.”, because the abuse that has occurred in that lovely part of the Marlborough Sounds has been obscene. It has been offensive in anybody’s language. If those offenders can be fined, then that is good. Let us slap an instant fine on them, but, more than that, let us demonstrate to the people who come to our country as tourists that we are equipping the local authorities to do something about this issue.

Where my family is in the Marlborough Sounds, as I said, the Marlborough District Council has allocated space for freedom campers. That is great; it is a beautiful spot. It is nice, and I do not begrudge anybody spending a couple of nights there. But if their vehicles are not self-contained, and are not geared for the disposing of their own waste, then I am sorry but they cannot use our backyard for that purpose. It does not matter whether it concerns a park in Nelson or a dear little bay in the Marlborough Sounds where I am still trying to catch snapper, the protection of the environment that this bill provides is worth supporting.

I have a bit of a problem, which I hope we can sort out in the select committee, with the mechanisms around the instant fine. I think it will be quite hard to recover that from campers sometimes, but if it can be done through credit card bills then that is fine. I remember a very long time ago getting a fine through Hertz, because I travelled in a bus lane without knowing it while driving a rental car in Rome.

Jacqui Dean: How did you know the difference?

Hon MARYAN STREET: Well, everybody was going at a zillion miles an hour, so it was really hard to know. I was just trying to stay alive. I did not know it was a bus lane, and I got a ticket later on through Hertz for travelling in that lane. But I copped that, and saw clearly that it is possible a fine can be extracted later on. Billing someone’s credit card is a very easy way of doing that, so let us explore the practicalities of it.

In essence, this is a constructive bill. I like the fact that it underscores people’s responsibility. We have a responsibility as citizens and keepers of this land to protect it, but the people who come to visit it also have a responsibility to treat our land and this place with respect, as well. I spent a large part of my youth tramping up and down Mount Taranaki. I was born and brought up in New Plymouth, and there we go to the beach and to the mountain. We learnt that if we took something in, we took it out; we did not leave it behind. We should not foul the environment with waste of any sort, and the particularly offensive examples we have had recently in the Nelson area have made me absolutely committed to making sure that this provision goes through and works, so that local councils and the Department of Conservation have the ability to strike fines and to punish people where they are caught. It goes to our reputation as a country and as a tourist destination, and to our own responsibility, to look after and be the kaitiaki, the guardians, of our environment. To that end, I support this bill and so will most of the people in Nelson. Thank you.

KEVIN HAGUE (Green) : I am a freedom camper. I spend my holidays cycle touring this great country of ours, or at least I used to spend my holidays doing that, when I was able to take my holidays at times when I was not competing with peak motorised traffic on the roads. Most nights when I am bike touring I sleep in a bed, especially as I have become older, as it seems more comfortable. However, when I go bike touring I always take a tent, and I always end up camping out some nights—freedom camping. Oftentimes camping is the best or most attractive option, or the only option. I guess I pride myself on leaving my campsite as I found it, except for perhaps a little bit of flattened grass. To me, that is a very valuable part of New Zealand tradition.

Many New Zealanders and, I think we will hear today, many people in this House value it. It pains me to have seen in recent decades more and more places that I have personally camped in and that make really good campsites becoming off limits. Thus, more and more people are channelled into commercial campgrounds and away from being self-reliant.

I also say that it irritates me to see in the Freedom Camping Bill the term “freedom camping”, which I think perfectly describes the New Zealand tradition that I have referred to, as it is used to mean something quite different. Although the definition of that term in this bill will catch the genuine freedom camping I have described, it is really targeted at one particular group of people: those who travel around in campervans that do not have toilet facilities and that are, therefore, cheaper. That is what the bill is actually about.

Let me be clear. I know there is a genuine problem with that group of people. When I was a member of the West Coast Tai Poutini Conservation Board, we received a number of delegations from councils coming to us to complain that almost too many tourists were being attracted to our region by the fantastic West Coast conservation estate. If any members of the House are not familiar with the features of that estate, I certainly recommend it to them. Councils were saying that a proportion of those people would park their campervans in rest areas and at other points next to the road. There was both a problem of litter and a larger problem that many of those campervans did not have toilets. Council staff needed to take time and spend money on a very unpleasant cleaning up process.

There is a genuine problem, but which solution to that problem is most consistent with maintaining rights and freedoms, and with continuing to encourage our vital tourism industry? Unsurprisingly, the Tourism Industry Association is aware of this problem. It had been working on it for some years and had developed a solution. Its solution had an operating assumption that, in general, people come to this country because of our clean, green reputation, and they do not set out to undermine or despoil it. The people who rent these particular kinds of campervans—the cheaper ones—are, in general, some of the most enthusiastic about our environment. They rent these vans because they do not have much money, and the fact they have chosen to spend their limited resources to come here speaks volumes about the high priority that they place on our environment.

Although, of course, there are some ratbags who simply do not care, for the most part the people who rent these campervans would look after our environment if they could, so the smart approach is to make it easier for them to do precisely that. Put simply, creating more places where campervans can spend the night that are sensibly spaced and that have toilet facilities and rubbish facilities is the smart way to go. The campervan companies would be charged with the responsibility of disseminating information about the responsibility to use these places, and information about exactly where they are. That solution was being developed and had not yet been given the chance to work before this approach from the Government and, indeed, from Local Government New Zealand was imposed.

Some critics will say that some local authorities have large, attractive areas and a small ratepayer base, and that is a fair point. I live in one such area, and other members of this House do also. It is a common problem, and I know it is a real one, too. That problem is at the heart of the difficulty of providing good quality health services in many of our rural areas, for example. It is the reason cited by the West Coast Regional Council for its being unable to adequately monitor compliance with resource consents as closely as it would like to.

But let us also remember that these tourists are contributing to local economies by spending on everything except accommodation. They buy food, fuel, visitor experiences, and so on from local businesses. Many businesses benefit directly from having them in the region, and many others benefit indirectly. It seems reasonable to ask that the local economy contribute to some of the facilities needed by tourists, particularly when those facilities alleviate other costs that would otherwise be borne locally, as they would be in this case.

In cases where a local community still cannot afford the cost of these facilities, the view of the Green Party is that the Sanitary Works Subsidy Scheme needs to step up. The Government needs to ensure that the scheme is sufficiently resourced and flexible to be able to assist those local communities to provide campervan facilities. After all, our whole New Zealand economy benefits from the tourism industry, and it is reasonable that the whole economy, therefore, helps to provide the tourism infrastructure required. This is the right approach and should be allowed time to gain traction to deal with the problem.

Instead, the approach being adopted is punitive. It might work—it is possible that it will—but it seems a less likely method to work than the one I have outlined. The problem is that people want to do the right thing but cannot do it. More broadly, it replaces the welcome mat that we extend to tourists with warnings of harsh penalties. This method runs a great risk of being counter-productive.

In all of the debate, which has been going on for some years now, one of the facts I find most curious is that there is almost no hard information about the size of the problem. Perhaps the select committee might challenge officials and submitters to try to provide this information. I suspect that it is an extensive problem, and undoubtedly a very unpleasant one, but it is actually relatively small. There is some irony, then, that this bill is being advanced just now.

I think we established in the House that one dairy cow produces about the same volume of excrement as 14 human beings. The Government is planning for a million extra dairy cows. Despite the Land and Water Forum having provided a historical consensus for both those who use water, such as the dairy industry, and conservationists to put in place a national policy statement on fresh water—

Hon Rodney Hide: They hate farmers.

KEVIN HAGUE: —including Federated Farmers, I say to Mr Hide—the Minister has elected to not include the provision that would have required resource consent for dairy intensification. It seems a little ironic in this debate today. The extra pollution that will be caused by that one decision will dwarf the pollution that could be caused by hordes of campervan tourists. This bill sets out in the wrong direction, casts the net too widely, and uses the proverbial sledgehammer to crack a walnut. The Green Party will oppose it.

Hon RODNEY HIDE (Minister of Local Government) :I must say I was a bit shocked that the Green Party has now joined—

Chris Hipkins: He’s still here.

Hon RODNEY HIDE: —yes, unlike Darren Hughes—the attack on farmers, along with the Labour Party. It is very, very clear that the Green Party hates farmers and the Labour Party hates farmers. Farmers are the very people who provide the backbone of the country, and the fact that those members would turn the issue of freedom camping into an attack on farmers is extraordinary to me indeed.

Let me start by thanking the Minister of Conservation, Kate Wilkinson, the Minister for the Environment, Nick Smith, and their officials for their work on this bill, because, surprisingly, it was a very tough policy issue to work on and to resolve. I also acknowledge the work of Local Government New Zealand in this regard.

Freedom camping has obviously been increasing in recent years, particularly in popular tourist destinations such as Fiordland, Queenstown, Nelson-Marlborough, the Coromandel, and the Bay of Plenty. That increase in popularity has also resulted in an increase in complaints. The Greens asked for some data, so here is some. The number of complaints to the Marlborough District Council about freedom camping have increased from 132 in 2007 to 432 in 2009. The main problem is people who camp on inappropriate sites such as lay-bys, beaches, and car parks, and leave behind human poo and other litter. This behaviour is offensive and it creates a human health risk. Many of the sites used by freedom campers are on local authority or conservation land, and both councils and the Department of Conservation spend a lot of money monitoring and cleaning up these sites. Queenstown Lakes District Council spends $30,000 a year on monitoring patrols, just looking out for the poo.

Although we want to encourage visitors to come to New Zealand and enjoy our country and all it has to offer, we also want them to respect our environment and be responsible campers. At present, we do not have an effective way to deter irresponsible freedom camping or an enforcement regime to address the sometimes offensive behaviour of some freedom campers—just some. Expensive and time-consuming court action is not working; therefore, the Freedom Camping Bill provides a low-cost, simple way of addressing the problems created by some freedom campers. There was a whole mix and suite of solutions to what looks to be a very overt problem that causes environmental degradation. I hate this bill being called the Freedom Camping Bill. To me it is more like the “Freedom Camping is Dead in New Zealand Bill” or the “We’re Going to Tell You Where to Go Bill”. As Damien O’Connor said, we could have come up with an environment protection bill and looked at this whole problem of degradation and contamination of public sites in a much more comprehensive manner that fitted our clean, green environment.

JACQUI DEAN (National—Waitaki) : The real problem with freedom camping, as members have noted, is that although the Government can bring in any number of measures—it might be fines, good signage, or describing places where people can and cannot camp, and whether they have self-containment—the real problem we need to overcome is that we need people who visit New Zealand from overseas, and New Zealanders themselves, to understand that they have environmental responsibilities. We cannot stop people going to the loo in our beautiful places in New Zealand just by the suite of measures we are proposing in the Freedom Camping Bill, but they are a good step along the way. They are an excellent step along the way of reminding people who come to New Zealand that we love them—we love people coming to our country; we love the way they spend money in our country and make a contribution to our society—but please, they must not foul our environment before they go home.

The other point is that unless we get on top of this problem we are in danger of people coming to New Zealand from, say, Sweden, parking up against the shores of Lake Wānaka, seeing the mess left there, and then going back to Sweden and saying that they loved New Zealand but it is actually not as good as New Zealanders crack it up to be. We need to be very careful that we maintain New Zealand’s good image.

The reason this bill is so good is that it is making a stand. Labour members can lament the fact that the bill will not fix the problem, but it will. It will help to fix the problem, because we will remind people of their environmental responsibilities. As I say, no single measure will fix this problem. Once again, it is up to individuals to understand their environmental responsibilities.

In closing, I commend the Minister of Conservation, Kate Wilkinson, for her preparedness to act on this issue in advance of the Rugby World Cup. I congratulate Geoff Ensor from the Freedom Camping Forum, who has worked so hard across many sectors on this bill. I commend this bill to the House.

A party vote was called for on the question, That the Freedom Camping Bill be now read a first time.

Noes 10 Green Party 9; Independent: Carter C.
