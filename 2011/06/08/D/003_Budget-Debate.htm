Budget Debate

  • Debate resumed from 7 June on the Appropriation (2011/12 Estimates) Bill.

CLARE CURRAN (Labour—Dunedin South) : I was talking last night about the way that this Government makes up stuff in the Budget and its policies, and about the elaborate charade that this country has been put through for the last 2½ years while the “Minister for Miscommunications and Misinformation”, Steven Joyce, works out how to give almost $1.5 billion to Telecom to roll out our newest and most important network of ultra-fast broadband. Telecom, which in 2009 helped pay for a report that argued why ultra-fast broadband was not necessary, has spent much time in the last 2 years trying to delay a decision on broadband, because it believes that its existing copper network is sufficient for the country’s broadband needs. It has no incentive to get a wriggle on and deliver fibre to the doors of the people of New Zealand. It will continue to do what it has always done, which is to look after its own interests, not the interests of New Zealand.

Steven Joyce, who could not get away with handing Telecom a 10-year regulatory holiday or an exemption from the commission on oversight on price, will still shortly force through a law in this Parliament that will, amongst other things, allow the prices of our existing broadband on copper to go up while the new network is installed, and there are no guarantees that the vast majority of people will actually be connected for years to come. Take Dunedin, Invercargill, Napier, and Whanganui just for starters. When will they get broadband? “Don’t hold your breath.” is all I can say. There are gaping holes in this programme. I ask where in the Budget is the allocation of the new money that will be needed.

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON (Attorney-General) : It has been very disappointing to listen to members opposite recite what can only be described as a farrago of incoherent bullet points prepared for them by the Labour research unit. I have listened to Clare Curran, Carol Beaumont, Brendon Burns, and Darien Fenton. They have all said the same thing, all with the same lack of enthusiasm and all with the same lack of conviction. It is a pity that the Labour Party research unit minions have to waste their time writing drivel for Labour backbenchers. They would be much more constructively engaged explaining to reporters Mr Goff’s research and development policy, because he certainly cannot explain it.

This Budget continues to chart the course from recession to recovery. It is based on a number of key principles, and it is worthwhile reviewing these principles. The first one is faster economic growth. Forecast economic growth is 4 percent in 2012, with 170,000 new jobs being created by 2015. The second principle is higher national savings. Changes to KiwiSaver will increase private savings, reduce Government borrowing for subsidies, and lift national savings. The next principle is getting back to surplus. We will return the Budget to surplus in 2014-15—a year sooner than was forecast in December. The next principle is repaying debt. Average net weekly borrowing will fall by over two-thirds to about $100 million in 2011-12, and the Government will start repaying debt in 2014-15.

Another principle is rebuilding Christchurch. We have created the $5.5 billion Canterbury Earthquake Recovery Fund for infrastructure in schools, temporary housing, trades training, welfare, business support, and central business district demolition costs—a huge job. All I can say is thank goodness that at this critical time Canterbury has Gerry Brownlee at the helm.

Strengthening education is another principle. Schools and early childhood education will receive an extra $1.4 billion in operating and capital funding out to 2014-15, with total education spending rising to a record—listen to it—$12.2 billion in 2011-12. Constantly vilified by the New Zealand Educational Institute branch of the Labour Party, Anne Tolley is the best Minister of Education in living memory.

Then we have the principle of strengthening health. This Budget delivers an extra $2.2 billion to public health services over the next 4 years—

Chris Auchinvole: How much?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: —including an additional—for Mr Auchinvole’s sake—$585 million in initiatives in 2011-12. Most people run a mile from the health portfolio—Mr Grant Robertson should surely be running from the shadow health portfolio, because he makes one wistful for Ruth—but Tony Ryall revels in the job. He is admired by everyone.

Focusing on front-line services is another principle of the Budget. We are reining in our unaffordable expenditure while protecting our most vulnerable citizens and keeping a keen focus on improving the performance of front-line services, particularly in the justice area. In this regard, I express my admiration for my colleague Simon Power, who has done 9 years’ work in 3 years. He is a great Minister and a fine parliamentarian, whose contribution to public life will never be forgotten. The final principle is building infrastructure. The Government infrastructure programme receives $1.6 billion more for ultra-fast broadband, fibre connections, and KiwiRail, thanks to my very clever and capable colleague Steven Joyce.

We are safe managers of the economy in tough times. Tough times mean tough choices, and this Government has not been afraid to make them. Labour has a problem; in fact, it has a few problems. It criticises every Government move to reduce spending, but refuses to say that it will reverse those reductions. We all remember the “Axe the Tax” bus, and the crowds of two or three people who would turn up to greet it. Now it turns out that those members would not axe the tax after all, though they apparently want to spend about $250 million—

Chris Auchinvole: How much?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: —$250 million, I say to Mr Auchinvole—taking GST off food to save the average New Zealand family $1.50 a week. Middle New Zealand will be pleased to know that under Labour it would no longer have to pay GST on Alban truffles, currently selling at $6,000 a kilogram; Californian pomegranates, currently selling at $30 a kilogram; or Philippine figs, currently selling at $2 each. Judging from what I heard about Mr Auchinvole yesterday, perhaps he had too many of those. So much for Michael Joseph Savage’s party of the people!

Not content with this, Labour is also promising billions more for expensive programmes, like Annette King’s “Ministries for Everything”. Apparently, Labour’s policy to fix New Zealand is to create new Ministers, new ministries, and new departments for everything. It is called the “Hail Mary” pass approach: throw something up in the air and hope that somebody will catch it. New Zealanders deserve better than that. They do not want more waffle and they do not want more ministries; they want their Government to focus on what works. That is what this Government is doing.

What is the Hon Phil Goff doing? Well, he used to be the figurehead. He used to be the social democratic store mannequin on which Labour projected its latest ideas of what middle New Zealand voters might like. The Leader of the Opposition did more costume changes during his first year in Opposition than Lady Gaga. Mr Goff was photographed everywhere: chopping wood in the Sunday Star-Times, riding his motorbike in the Listener—

Grant Robertson: That Minister doesn’t know who Lady Gaga is.

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: The member for Wellington Central is so 1950s. I tell him to get with it. This year, I say to Mr Robertson, we have had nothing but silence. Clare Curran was so frustrated that Mr Goff was making no headway as a great Southern Man that she decided yesterday that it was for her to take up the challenge. The only surprise about her performance at question time yesterday is that she did not turn up with a sheep slung over her shoulders.

With Labour, it is all about style over substance. Nowhere is that more evident than in its attitude to the arts. Let us look at the film industry. National has always championed the film industry. We formed the Film Commission in 1978, although Helen Clark liked telling people she formed it—another example of her zealous regard for the truth! Only last week the member for Wellington Central was talking about film as a contributor to Wellington’s economy, but look at his party’s behaviour over The Hobbit last year. When it came down to a choice between trade union Australian thugs and the New Zealand film industry, the Hobbit-haters in the Opposition sold out the film industry faster than one could say “unobtanium”, that precious metal so sought after by earthlings in the film Avatar, which of course was created in Weta Workshop.

Grant Robertson: These popular culture references demean the Minister.

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: Mr Robertson has never seen Avatar, because he is so uncool.

The same thing can be said about New Zealand music. Labour gets all excited about New Zealand Music Month, but for this Government every month is New Zealand Music Month. Turning up to a few functions and eating canapés does not amount to supporting the arts, in my book. We have a wonderful story to tell about the arts. We have a wonderful story to tell about the Budget. These are the areas of core focus for this John Key - led Government. We will always walk what we talk. This is a great Budget, and I say to the member for Clutha-Southland: “Well done, faithful servant.”

GRANT ROBERTSON (Labour—Wellington Central) : Chris Finlayson once asked my colleague Charles Chauvel why he had taken an instant dislike to him, and Charles said: “It just saves time.” That was in reference to the member who has just resumed his seat, whom I shared a podium with on Sunday night, when even the national secondary schools’ Shakespeare competition entrants booed that Minister because he was so nasty. He simply cannot deliver a speech without being nasty. [Interruption] Mr Finlayson is correct; I presented the award that is in his name at the University of Otago Sheilah Winn Shakespeare Festival. It is the “Christopher Finlayson Award for Nastiness”. That is what the award is; that is what was read out. That is correct, is it not, I ask Mr Finlayson.

We come here to debate the Budget, and I am sure, Mr Speaker, you are glad that I will do that. This Budget was christened by John Armstrong as “a fairytale of high hopes”. That is certainly what it is: a fairytale of high hopes that has projections, jobs in the never-never, but no plan for growth, no plan to lift New Zealanders out of poverty, no plan to reduce the income inequality in this country, and no proper plan to grow the economy in this country. I prefer another, musical title for this Budget: “Bill English’s Fiddler on the Hoof”. That is what it was: a tinkering, a fiddling, but no real plan to drive this economy forward. There is no real plan to reduce social inequality in this country, and no real plan to protect and enhance our environment. In short, it is a failure of a Budget.

What we saw today in the House on the health budget was, I thought, instructive. The Minister of Health, Tony Ryall, stood up in front of the House today and did not even want to own his own press release from Budget day. He did not even want to admit that his press release acknowledges that the health budget falls at least $170 million short of standing still. Mr Ryall stood up and claimed that more money has gone into health. He likes to claim that a record amount of money is now being spent on health. Well, when I went to the supermarket the other night I spent a record amount of money on my groceries, but that does not mean there were more items in the shopping basket, and the same thing applies in the health system.

The health system is falling behind. Two successive Budgets from this Government have got nowhere near meeting what the health system needs just to keep services at the level they are at now, because we have an ageing and growing population, health costs and health inflation rise faster than normal inflation, and the cost of medical equipment and treatments is growing. Unfortunately this Government has failed yet again in this Budget to give district health boards the amount of money they need to provide services as they are now. That is the reality. There are now only two options available to district health boards: to cut services or to increase charges. Those are the two options that this Minister is leaving the health system.

We all know that we live in tight financial times, but what we could have seen from the Minister today was some honesty about that. He could have stood up in the House and said that he acknowledged that only $420 million extra was put in and that that is not enough to make ends meet. But we did not see that. We had obfuscation from the Minister on that score.

It is difficult for people to look at the health budget and understand exactly where the cuts are, but it is quite clear that in areas such as public health service purchasing we have seen cuts of about $38 million. National contracted services, which sound benign but include Healthline and hospital chaplaincy services, have been cut as well. We have also seen cuts in national mental health services, in the Primary Health Care Strategy, and in health workforce training and development. Those are the core elements of a Budget for health and they have been cut back, and the Minister was less than up front in the House today about those cuts. That is $165 million worth of cuts that the Minister was not prepared to acknowledge today, but the truth is that that is the gap in this health budget and he has not been able to fill it.

What we saw in the Budget was no real commitment to primary care, which is an area that members on this side of the House believe in. We believe that prevention is better than cure. We believe the bias of our investment should go towards primary health, public health, and our children’s health, and we did not see that in this Budget. In fact, we see that primary health care is still at 1.3 percent of the health budget. As a country we have to get it clear in our heads that health is not something that starts in a hospital. Health is something that starts in our homes, our communities, and our schools. That is where we need to be investing, in the early interventions that will help Kiwis stay healthy, rather than parking ever-flasher ambulances at the bottom of the cliff. We need an investment in primary health care and public health that is greater than the current 1.3 percent.

We need to see health in a wider context. There was a very good project in this Budget; it was the rheumatic fever initiative. I congratulate the Minister on putting that money into the rheumatic fever initiative, but in the end we will not address rheumatic fever—a disease that should not be present in New Zealand—without addressing overcrowded homes and cold, damp housing.

Health needs to be seen in a wider context than just initiatives, hospitals, and hospital services. We need to see the wider context of housing, employment, reducing inequality, the taxation system, public transport, and eradicating poverty. Those things will make the fundamental difference to our health status and the health status of New Zealanders. This is recognised right around the world. In July Sir Michael Marmot will visit New Zealand from Britain, and I encourage all members of the House to listen to what he has to say. In the UK he has put forward a comprehensive plan for addressing what we call the social determinants of health—that is, the things that people experience in their lives that affect their health outcomes.

The New Zealand Medical Association has picked up that plan in an excellent document, its Health Equity Position Statement. In that document the New Zealand Medical Association, which is not known to be the most left-wing of organisations, acknowledges the importance of addressing the background causes of health status. I quote from one element of its statement: “The NZMA: … Believes that in order to eliminate inequities in health a whole of government approach will be required. In particular, policies addressing education, employment, poverty, housing, taxation and social security should be assessed for their health impact. [The New Zealand Medical Association] Believes that economic growth should not be viewed as the sole measure of a country’s success and that the fair distribution of health, well-being, and environmental and social sustainability are equally important goals.”

The message coming from the doctors of this country is that we must see health in a wider context. Economic growth is important to health, reducing poverty is important to health, and creating jobs is important to health, and this Budget failed on almost every one of those indicators.

When we look at the report from the Prime Minister’s Chief Science Advisor, Sir Peter Gluckman, he backs up the advice of the New Zealand Medical Association to invest in our children. That is why I am incredibly proud of the work that Annette King has been doing on the importance of children and on the agenda for children that will ensure we invest in children. We must make sure that children get the antenatal, birthing, and post-natal care they need. We must ensure that children are enrolled with well child providers and that B4 School checks are being undertaken. We must ensure that school health services are kept going, unlike what is in the review that is being undertaken by the Minister at the moment. We need to make sure that we invest in the things that will allow our children to learn. School health services are brilliant, not just because they make sure that people get the primary care that they need but because they allow children to learn. We know that if children do not learn, that is the beginning of the slippery slope. That is what Sir Peter Gluckman is telling this Government. That is what the New Zealand Medical Association is telling this Government.

From Labour’s point of view, we know there is not a bottomless pit of funding for health expenditure. We will, when we get into Government, be looking to ensure we find the efficiencies and innovations in the health sector that will allow us to invest more money in keeping New Zealanders healthy. That should be the goal of our policies.

I have one last point on that matter. The regional public health unit of the Hutt Valley District Health Board has estimated that it will cost a family living on the minimum wage 50 percent of their income to put a healthy meal on the table. That is not good enough. It will cost a beneficiary family 71 percent of their income to put a healthy meal on the table. We have to support families so that they can put healthy food on the table. Labour has policies to do that: we will take the GST off fresh fruit and vegetables and we will make the first $5,000 of income tax-free.

Labour will continue to advocate policies that are about keeping New Zealanders healthy in our schools, our communities, and our families. We will continue to invest in hospital services, but Labour also believes that primary health care and public health are essential to the future well-being of our country.

Hon MAURICE WILLIAMSON (Minister for Building and Construction) : I start by putting a little bit of context around the Budget debate. This is the 24th Budget debate I have sat through in this Parliament. I have to say that of the 24 Budgets I have known—probably only three short of your count, Mr Speaker—this Budget was drawn up in the toughest circumstances that any Minister of Finance has faced.

I always like to think of little analogies, or parables like this. If a father had told his family: “When we get to Christmas, I’m going to be buying you a 65-inch plasma TV”, and everyone in the family was excited, and if, as the year rolled on, the father lost his job and the business they had went bankrupt, or whatever, when it came Christmas the father would have to say to the family: “You know, we’re in circumstances where I can’t buy you that plasma TV.” What worries me intensely about Labour members, in view of their catcalls all the time, is that they seem to have ignored the fact that when the last election campaign got under way at the beginning of 2008 and we campaigned on a number of things, the world was a very different place—an incredibly different place.

As the global economic crisis started to really bite and take out huge chunks of the world’s economy, the sharemarket in the United States took one of the biggest dives, the property market in the United States fell through the floor, and we experienced the shockwaves right across the world. Portugal, Iceland, Greece, and Spain—“the PIGS”—took a real smack-around, and here in New Zealand the finance company meltdowns and the Government guarantees that had been made in respect of those companies started to change the entire landscape.

If it was not bad enough that the global economic crisis meant that the whole world was a different world and a different paradigm, along came a number of mother Nature smack-arounds, just to make us really realise where we were. So I give absolute unbelievable credit to the Hon Bill English for what he did in this Budget. He did not get into a slash-and-burn mentality. He did not get into a “Let’s tear the place to shreds” mentality. He laid down what I think is a very solid foundation, a bedrock Budget, which does a number of key things.

Before I go into those key things—because I think it is worth rehearing them for this House—I want to say that in respect of my own portfolio I want to show some gratitude to the Minister of Finance. At a time when most Ministers were being told: “There is no new money; what part of ‘no’ do you not understand?”, Bill English saw the reason in a number of cases we put to him. I thank him for that. In one case we got some new money for the electronic SmartGate at customs.

I am enormously proud of that project. In recent weeks over 60 percent of all eligible arrivals at the Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch airports have been using SmartGate on arrival as an alternative to using the manual desks. The huge efficiency gains and saving of time has allowed customs officers to direct their work at the secondary queue. That is where the bad eggs are, where the drug smugglers and all the other evil people are. It has been a fantastic success. Bill English gave us some new money in this Budget to extend the SmartGate project, both for arrivals and departures, and I thank him for that.

We also inherited 2½ years ago a Statistics New Zealand that was in a state of meltdown with regard to its technology platforms. It had fully depreciated schemes that were outdated, and there were many different variable platforms. At a time when things are tough, when there is plenty going on, it is hard to convince a Minister of Finance that the statistics portfolio is what one would call high on the radar for New Zealand. But at a time when things were tough, he saw the benefit. He saw the merits of what good economic statistics like GDP and the household labour force survey can mean for our planning, and we got a Budget allocation of $58 million over the next 4 years for putting in place some new systems.

Having said that, I return to the key things about the Budget. One of the headline-grabbing facts and figures that is used by the media—I hear the Opposition using it all the time, and it is a really sobering number—is that while we have our books where they are, the Minister of Finance has to borrow about $300 million a week. In fact, it has been $380 million, because the economic circumstances around the world in terms of the money markets has meant that it was worthwhile taking advantage of some of the deals going—and, yes, some of that was in excess of the requirement.

We can argue around the margins—whether $300 million, $310 million, $320 million or, indeed, $380 million was necessary—but it was about $300 million a week. I think most New Zealanders, if they multiply that figure by 50 weeks, soon work out how many billions that borrowing is adding to our debt. What did Mr English do in this Budget? He set about putting us on a path to reducing that level of borrowing, and doing so very rapidly. I think this headline is worth repeating: we are currently borrowing $300 million, yet average borrowings will fall by over two-thirds to about $100 million in the next financial year—that is, 2011-12. And the Government will start repaying debt 2 years later—that is, 2014-15.

I know that most New Zealanders who have had tough times at home and have struggled to make their family budget work know what we are talking about. These are not times of plenty. There were times when Labour was in power when the world was booming. The world economy was in a jubilant mood. Commodity prices were going through the ceiling, and there was no end to the morrow. In the United States money was flowing through the sharemarket, and the Dow Jones knew no top. In some years Labour turned in an $11.5 billion surplus. Well, New Zealanders know that the world is not like that at present—and, yes, the economic cycle will come back and one day there will be better economic times for the entire world—and Mr English recognised that and did something about it.

Mr English also recognised that there is only one way to get oneself out of trouble long term, and that is economic growth. Every family knows that it is no good sitting around whingeing and complaining that they cannot afford this or that. The best thing they can do is try to increase their income, increase their wealth, and increase their financial position. Treasury’s numbers, based on the books that Bill English delivered to this House, show that we are looking at 4 percent growth in the GDP of this country in 2012, and, as a result, 170,000 new jobs by 2015.

This is where the rubber hits the road in terms of the difference between Labour and National. We hear it in questions from Jacinda Ardern and others about how many jobs the Government is creating. Other than when Labour was in power and created a huge number of bureaucrats within the concrete towers around here, the Government actually does not create many jobs, at all. We employ a few people in our offices and a few people in the ministries, in the bureaucracy, but the vast, vast bulk of jobs out there are in the private sector.

Jobs are created by risk takers who put their own money at risk, who put their own skin in the game, and who actually invest to grow the economy overall and make some money. It is a really simple model. They will take on staff if they have some confidence that the economy is on the up, that their products will have customers, that their books will be in good shape and in black ink, and that they will be able to retire some of the debt to the bank. This Budget will have restored a very big chunk of business confidence and restored a very good view of the future to New Zealanders. That is why Treasury’s advice to the Government is that, with the confidence we will get from this Budget, we can be looking at about 170,000 new jobs by 2015—jobs created by the private sector, not by employing a much fatter and greater bureaucracy.

Mr English had to trim some elements of various programmes because they were just not sustainable. What was done to KiwiSaver? The $1,000 was left; a whole range of the elements in KiwiSaver were left. There was an increase in the amount of private savings required and a reduction in the Government contribution, and overall savings will increase because of it. New Zealand has to focus on that. I will say in my last 20 or so seconds that over 24 years I have sat in this House and tried to assess Budgets against their times, and this was the toughest time ever to have to put together a Budget. Mr English delivered what I think is a superlative Budget. The Opposition members are struggling to find things to say against it. I give him 9.9 out of 10, simply because one should never give 10 out of 10 in case there is room for improvement. I am delighted to be a member of this Government and support this Budget.

IAIN LEES-GALLOWAY (Labour—Palmerston North) : I will start by doing something that is probably a little unusual—that is, congratulating Maurice Williamson on actually referring to the Budget in his speech. It is the first time I have heard anybody from that side of the House talk about this Budget and try to promote it from National’s point of view. We heard Chris Finlayson talk about 1978 and what previous Labour Governments had done, and about what Labour policy might be, but Maurice Williamson actually tried to talk about the Budget. Having said that, of course I have to take exception to some of the points he made.

Hon Maurice Williamson: Oh, no!

IAIN LEES-GALLOWAY: I know—I know that Mr Williamson is tremendously surprised at the position I am taking.

Mr Williamson talked about the plan for growth, and the income side of the ledger. It was interesting to hear him talk about the income side of the ledger, because that is precisely what is missing from this Budget. There is no plan for growth, there is no plan for jobs, and there is no plan for creating a good, stable business environment. Mr Williamson talked about businesses appreciating the fact that they will have customers who will have money in their pockets and who will buy those businesses’ products. Well, there is absolutely nothing in this Budget to put money into the pockets of working families, of working New Zealanders. People will be no better off as a result of this Budget.

In fact, the only thing this Budget focuses on is making cuts—cuts to the public sector. That is the only focus in this Budget. It is entirely focused on expenditure, so there are cuts to KiwiSaver and cuts to Working for Families. Those are the sorts of things that make working New Zealanders more nervous about their financial situation, and less likely to go out and spend money, which is bad news for businesses in New Zealand. They will not take on more workers and will not create more jobs in this country. So the effect is in fact the exact opposite to what Mr Williamson claimed this Budget is all about. Nevertheless, I appreciate the fact that Mr Williamson attempted to talk about the Budget itself, because I understand why National members would refrain from doing that. It is a lacklustre Budget. It lacks a credible plan: it lacks any sort of plan for growth, it lacks any sort of plan for jobs, and it lacks any sort of plan for getting on top of the $16.7 billion deficit that this Government has created in just the 3 years it has been in charge. Of course members would not want to put their names to this Budget.

I particularly want to talk about the very lacklustre approach to health care that this Budget takes; $165 million is the gap between what the Government is prepared to spend on health and what is needed for health care just to stand still. It is not good enough for members to get up and say that the Government is spending more on health care than it ever has before. Of course it is spending more on health care than it ever has before—

Hon Dr Jonathan Coleman: Thank you—thank you.

IAIN LEES-GALLOWAY: —because every Government, I tell Mr Coleman—

Hon Dr Jonathan Coleman: Dr Coleman to you, sonny.

IAIN LEES-GALLOWAY: —I will take that: Dr Coleman—spends more on health care each year than it did the year before. The question is, what is the quantum? Is it enough to keep up with inflation?

We know that inflation in the health system is far, far greater than it is in everyday life, and that people are struggling with 4.5 percent inflation in everyday life. Grant Robertson said that people are going to the supermarket and spending more on their groceries than they ever have before, but that does not mean that they are getting more for that. Although we are spending more on health care, we are not getting more for our health dollars. So why are Government members getting up and crowing about something that every single Government has done every year before? That is the question.

This health Minister and this Government have their health priorities absolutely up the spout, completely in the wrong place. As important as it is to focus on elective surgeries and waiting lists, which are noble things that we have to get our heads around and tackle, the fact of the matter is that the Government is sucking money out of what will actually improve our health system over time, and what will improve the health of New Zealanders over time—that is, public health care. We need to keep people well in this country so that we can reduce the demand on our health services, which are already stretched. But there is no plan—there is absolutely no plan from this Government to improve the well-being of New Zealanders. That means that for year after year the demand on our health system will simply remain the same—if not get worse—as our population ages, and as we see more and more problems in our population. We all know that the greater the disparity between the haves and the have-nots, the more health issues we will see in our nation.

This Government is creating a much greater gap between those who have and those who do not have. That growing inequality will cause more costs in the health system over time than any other thing this Government could possibly do. This Budget is the second in a row that has failed to meet the cost of delivering current health services, and if this track continues, then our health system will be in a huge amount of trouble in a very, very short period of time.

The cost of living in this country is an issue that all New Zealanders are having to grapple with, as costs go way beyond what people can afford while their wages stay static. One of the things—and this is important for the health system—that helps to keep the cost of living under control in this country is affordable medicine, both through Pharmac and through the fact that the Government subsidises prescriptions so that people can get medicines at a reasonable rate. But there is a huge threat looming to the affordability of medicines in this country, and it is the fact that as we negotiate the Trans-Pacific Partnership, this Government is not prepared to take Pharmac off the bargaining table. In our first year we turned the corner: a little bit of afforestation. Last year there were an extra 4,500 hectares. This year we are expecting 5,700 hectares and that is real environmental progress. We have seen in this Budget the huge commitment to the Global Research Alliance on Agricultural Greenhouse Gases on reducing agricultural emissions and a commitment of $50 million for the Agricultural Greenhouse Gas Research Centre. That research is absolutely crucial for New Zealand but crucial also for the globe, in the sense of how we produce food to feed another 3 billion people expected by 2050 while containing emissions. The important balance this Government has struck with the emissions trading scheme is ensuring that New Zealand meets its Kyoto obligations at least cost, that we start to make the transition to a low-carbon economy, and that we pace ourselves realistically, relative to the progress being made in other countries. It is interesting that at a conference I attended recently New Zealand was openly described as making more progress than any other country in the Asia-Pacific region, with the balanced approach we are taking to the huge challenge of climate change.

Another key area of work has been strengthening the national direction under the Resource Management Act. Last November we completed the new national coastal policy statement. In February we gazetted a national policy statement on renewable electricity, and in May added that on freshwater management. We are currently consulting on a national policy statement on biodiversity. I note that in the 9 years of the previous Labour Government there was one national policy statement in 9 years. In the complete 9 years of the previous Labour Government there was one national policy statement in 9 years. We have done three in 1 year—three in 1 year and that is very real progress, and equally so in respect of national environment standards. Last November we put in place national regulations requiring water metering. We cannot manage fresh water unless we measure it, and we put those regulations in place last year. That will move us from 33 percent of water takes being measured to 98 percent by volume being measured, within 6 years. In January I announced the new air quality standards, including the introduction of offsets. Last week, as part of our work with the Green Party, I announced a new national environment standard on contaminated soils. These steps reinforce the stronger national direction that this Government is giving to resource management.

The Budget makes provision for $15.2 million for the long overdue clean-up of the Tūī mine. This is the largest clean-up project that has been undertaken since Māpua a decade ago. That project was not well managed by Labour, and there is a real challenge for us to make sure that we do a good job of that clean-up. This Budget also contains a new contestable fund for clean-ups. It is interesting to make this comparison. Between 2009 and 2014 this Government is budgeting to spend $94 million on the clean-up of our rivers and lakes; $94 million. How much did Labour spend on clean-ups in its last 5 years in office? It was $16 million.

Hon Steve Chadwick: $17 million.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: No, check the Budget tables. We are spending five times as much on clean-ups of our lakes and rivers as was spent by the previous Government. I say to members opposite that words come cheap; financial commitment is what counts.

If we look in terms of the commitment to the new Environmental Protection Authority that will take effect on 1 July, the new legislation that we have brought together in respect of the exclusive economic zone, and the new legislation dealing with a very practical issue of freedom camping, we see that this is the sort of practical, environmental, blue-green progress that this Government is making in this space.

I also note the appointments that we made to the Environmental Protection Authority and the board. I was astonished by the claim by the Green Party, and I quote what it said: “None of the eight board members of the EPA love this country and want to look after it.” What absolute arrogant nonsense! How dare it say that a man like Anake Goodall, the chief executive of Ngāi Tahu, has no love for this country? How dare it say that about the likes of Gillian Wratt, a very experienced botanist, who is the chief executive of the Cawthron Institute? That is the sort of arrogance we get from the Green Party. One does not have to be a card-carrying, sandal-wearing Green Party member to love this country. That is evidenced in this Budget by a very balanced and strong commitment to our environment. This is a sound, effective Budget that is appropriate for the times, and the House should back it.
