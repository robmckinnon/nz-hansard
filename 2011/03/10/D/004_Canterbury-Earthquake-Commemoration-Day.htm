Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill

Procedure

Hon SIMON POWER (Acting Leader of the House) : I seek leave for the introduction and passing of the Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill prior to 6 p.m. today.

Mr SPEAKER: Leave is sought. Is there any objection to that course of action being followed? There is none.

First Reading

Hon KATE WILKINSON (Minister of Labour) : I move, That the Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill be now read a first time. I thank the House for supporting the passage of this bill through all its stages today.

The bill provides for 18 March 2011 to be a day of commemoration in those parts of Canterbury—that is, in northern and central Canterbury—that observe Christchurch Show Day as Canterbury Anniversary Day, in order to commemorate the devastating consequences of the Canterbury earthquake on 22 February 2011 at 12.51 p.m. Just over 2 weeks have passed since Christchurch was shattered by its second major earthquake. This bill has been introduced to allow for the people of Canterbury to take a day to come together as a community, and as a region, to grieve together and mark an event that has so deeply affected each and every one of us. We are still in the response stage of the earthquake. There is an enormous amount of work left to do, and that will be ongoing for years, but next Friday we want to take the time to provide an opportunity for all of Canterbury to mourn. It will also be a chance to celebrate the lives of those who have been lost. There is no denying that the wounds are still open for everyone to see. Next Friday’s memorial service will not bring an end to the grieving process, but I hope it will help Canterbury at the beginning of this long road ahead.

This bill provides for next Friday to be observed as a public holiday known as Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day. Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day is to be treated, in those part of Canterbury that observe Christchurch Show Day as Canterbury Anniversary Day, as a one-off public holiday for the purposes of the Holidays Act 2003, and as a day to which section 65C(1) of the Education Act 1989 applies, which requires schools to be closed.

The provisions of the Holidays Act 2003 governing observance and payment for public holidays will apply to Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day as follows: employees are entitled to be paid if Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day, 18 March 2011, would have been a normal working day for them. If employees do not work on Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day, they are entitled to be paid what they would have earned had they worked on the day; if employees work on the day, they should be paid at least time and a half for the time they actually work on that day. They are also entitled to an alternative holiday if the day would have been a normal working day for them.

I am aware that this will place some unexpected costs on some businesses in the region, and of course this was taken into consideration when the Government discussed creating a one-off public holiday for the region. However, there was no getting away from the fact that this earthquake is the greatest tragedy ever to strike the Canterbury region. Most people in Canterbury will want to attend the memorial service, but without a public holiday it would be much more difficult for many of them to do so. It is important that all of Canterbury is able to take a day to mark this tragedy, and we hope that businesses in the region will support this public holiday. Many businesses are not able to operate right now, and the Government is providing support to them and to thousands of employees.

The welfare of the people of Canterbury is what matters most at this time. I expect that tens of thousands of people will want to attend the memorial service, and I encourage everyone who can get to Hagley Park to do so. The Government considers that to mark a very significant event, it is appropriate to have a public holiday for the people of northern and central Canterbury. I commend this bill to the House.

Hon ANNETTE KING (Deputy Leader—Labour) : The Labour Opposition will be supporting the Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill. We are determined to work alongside the Government and the community to make sure that Christchurch is a better place for everybody, including our children. One way we can do that is to ensure that this day of commemoration is able to take place.

I am one of those who have been to Christchurch over the last 2 weeks, on two occasions, and I have seen firsthand the devastation and the impact on the lives of so many people; not only people who live in Canterbury but also family and friends right around New Zealand. I do not think I have ever seen such devastation in one of our cities or towns in New Zealand. We have had many disasters over many years, but I do not think I have seen, in my lifetime, such devastation.

Yesterday I went to Lyttelton, and unless one has been there one will not believe that almost that entire town has fallen down. It will take a lot of hard work to rebuild it, as it will in many parts of the city of Christchurch. As we have said on a number of occasions over the last 2 weeks, we feel for the people of Canterbury and we want to be part of the rebuild of that area as fast as we can. We want to ensure that they have the opportunity to work, live, and play in a most wonderful part of New Zealand. As a former South Islander who trained there as a school dental nurse, I have a lot of affection for that city.

We believe it is appropriate to set aside a day for the people of Christchurch to mourn for the staggering loss of life. I would, however, have to say that it would feel better if we knew the names of all those who had passed away; if we knew whom we were commemorating. There is a feeling of emptiness that there are only so many who at this stage are identified as having passed away. I understand the terrible work that has to be done in identification, and I say to the Minister of Police that the work the police have been doing is absolutely amazing. They will do it thoroughly, and they will ensure people know that the remains returned to them are those of their loved ones. I understand that, but I feel that a commemoration should really be about commemorating those who have lost their lives, and we will be in a situation where this day is held but we will not know whom they all are.

It makes one wonder whether at some stage there needs to be some other commemoration. Perhaps a year after this event, when we have been able to identify everybody and when people’s minds have been put at rest, we will be able to commemorate this terrible disaster together as a country. We also believe that by having a day to commemorate, people will be able to stop and reflect on what they would like to see happen in their own region and their own city. That is important.

One of the things that was said to me over and over again when I was in Canterbury is that Cantabrians must have a say in what happens to their city. They must have a say in how it is developed, designed, and built. I think that is a strong message we must all take on board when we think of the people of Christchurch. If we think about it as if it were our own back yard, it would be wrong and we would feel aggrieved if somebody said to us: “This is how you are going to have your own domain, and we’re going to make the decision for you.” So I think there is a clear message to us.

Those of us who would like to see a rebuild happen quickly should stop, take a breath, and think about how we can ensure that there will be a rebuild of that city that has the buy-in of many, many of those people. We know that the economic cost to us as a country, along with the social cost, is huge if we do not get the rebuild of that city right. So when we commemorate and when people are thinking about it, I urge us to consider the views of Cantabrians and what they would like to see happen.

There is no need for us to panic in terms of the re-establishment of the city. We, with the people of Canterbury, have an opportunity to ensure we build a city of the 21st century, the like of which we do not have in this country. We will look back in 100 years’ time and say that out of that devastation and rubble has grown a city that is providing economic and social sustenance to many, many people over many generations.

Labour members support this bill, and we will ensure we are represented at that memorial service. I know that the Leader of the Opposition, alongside the Prime Minister, will be speaking on that day, making it a parliamentary occasion. I think that is important, because it is not a Government occasion. We have been talking across this House about how we stand together. It is important, I think, that the commemoration service represents political parties across the political divide, so people can see we are together in commemorating with them the loss of so many lives. We stand here to support this bill and commend it to the House.

Hon JUDITH COLLINS (Minister of Police) : It is truly humbling to speak on the Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill. I have just returned from Christchurch for the third time since the earthquake of 22 February.

I will just take a moment to deal with an issue raised by the member who has just resumed her seat, the Hon Annette King, in relation to the memorial day and the fact that it is a shame we do not yet have all the names released of those who have lost their lives. I am fully aware that we may never have all the names of those who have lost their lives, such has been the devastation that has been wreaked on some individuals. As that member has acknowledged, the police are doing their very best to get absolute accuracy in relation to the remains of those deceased, so that they are returned to the correct families.

We should not underestimate the dreadful job the disaster victim identification teams—DVI teams—have had to endure. I visited them at their work. I can say only that it is quite clearly the Lord’s work, because I am not sure that too many other people would do it. They do the most amazing job. When the bushfires in Victoria went through and killed over 200 people, it took over 6 weeks before any victims could be returned to their families. Our team was returning them after a few days. It is a stunning effort from some fantastic people. I take this opportunity to thank all of the people who have been part of that team—all of the people from around the world and from around New Zealand who have been part of it.

This memorial day is about those who have died, but it is also about the many survivors in Canterbury who have to live with the fact that they have lost loved ones or friends, and about those who have had their businesses and everything else destroyed around them. All they have left is their own lives. When we look for anything positive we can take from that terrible day of 22 February, it would have to be the absolute value of human life. Those who are still alive, even those who have lost limbs, have had their backs or necks broken, and have the most dreadful injuries, still have life. They cling to life and they wish to live it as fully as they can.

I know there will also be people who think this service will make things harder for many businesses, because it is another paid day off. But I say that we should think about those people who have continued to go to work through all of this. Think about our police, our corrections officers, our nurses, our doctors, and our council workers, who have been collecting the rubbish off the streets to try to do what they can. Think about the people who have been trying to reconnect sewers. Think about the people who have been doing all the plumbing and the electricians—the people who have been working all the way through. This is a day that we owe to Canterbury.

I recently read a comment in an Australian magazine about this quake. It said, for all Australians, today they are Kiwis. I say to this House, for all New Zealanders, today we are Cantabrians.

Hon TREVOR MALLARD (Labour—Hutt South) : I want to join with members in the tone of this discussion. I was not going to start this way, but I will acknowledge the work of the police and the teams they have developed. It must be pushing 15 years since I spent a number of nights out with the police in my city. On one of those early occasions I was with a team that was involved in cleaning up after a road accident where someone had hit a power pole and the remains were spread over 50 to 70 metres down a road. The police officers were using tweezers to pick up body parts. The police and others in Christchurch—our other heroes—are doing that on a scale 200 times bigger, and that is an enormous effort. It is almost like under every boulder, brick, and stone they lift there is a possibility of finding part of a loved one. It must be so hard, yet in a way I am sure for many of them it is rewarding.

I have had discussions with people from my area who have been involved in the work, just as there are people in Christchurch from every area in New Zealand. Police from my area have been in Christchurch and have come back. In a funny way it must also be enormously rewarding work to be part of a team that is now doing the best that can be done in returning as much of the remains of the loved ones to the families as they can for appropriate burial or other disposal. It is hard work, and we thank them for it.

Perhaps a related question is that of whether there can be some informal arrangement or direction from the commissioner that the police and others who have already worked in Canterbury and who are now back home can be taken off active duty at least for the period of the memorial service, so that they can be part of the commemoration. I think that would be a good thing, and it should probably apply to the fire service and other personnel as well.

I say to the Minister of Labour—again, I think it will be a matter of the discretion of individual employers—that there are now tens of thousands of Cantabrians who are outside Canterbury. Many of them are working for nationwide employers or are outside their normal place of work in the Canterbury province. I wonder whether the Minister—and she might like to report at a later stage—could encourage employers to treat those Cantabrians who are now outside Canterbury as if they were in Canterbury for the purposes of the commemoration. Because of the television aspect to the commemoration, I think a lot of us will want to feel a part of it, and it would be good for those who were in Christchurch or who would normally be there to be allowed to be part of it. I think gentle encouragement could be given for that to occur. I do not think we need an amendment to the legislation, but if we can get some leadership from the Government on arrangements for the police and paid firefighters at least, then that would be a good way of progressing.

As well as acknowledging those people, an enormous number of public servants in Wellington as well as in Canterbury have been burning the candle at both ends in order to best respond to this terrible event. We need to acknowledge them and thank them. In fact, I tell the Minister of Police that the police with whom I have had a discussion have had some arrangement where they have been down in Christchurch for a week. I think they went down on the ferry late in the afternoon of the earthquake and stayed there for a week. There was not one occasion when they had their normal 9-hour break between shifts. They were always working for at least 16 hours a day, and often longer. There will be some issues that flow from that.

Before I get back to that point, I acknowledge Anne Tolley and the work she and her team have done. Through informal links—and occasionally some tears on Lambton Quay—I have caught up with what has been going on within the Ministry of Education and its team. I acknowledge her leadership and the work that Karen Sewell and those people have done in order to make the best possible arrangements at this stage. It is an enormous job. It could have gone much worse. There will be enormous challenges to come, and I am sure that at the appropriate time we will give Anne Tolley some hurry-up to make sure the good work continues. She has taken some leadership there, and part of it has involved some additional funding, some double funding, in order to make sure that arrangements are appropriately in place for schools.

I want to go back to the Minister of Police and ask that she too looks at the effect on police district budgets as a result of the earthquake work arrangements. Because of briefings on the Rugby World Cup, I know that this year will be enormously tight for the police. As far as leave is concerned, there are large blocks of time when the police will not be able to take leave, and therefore there will be an accumulation of leave. I understand that the Minister of Police has already approved the paying out of time in lieu for police who have been in Christchurch where that is appropriate. I say to the Minister of Police that I think we should not pressure some of the police who have been in that situation to take payment of time in lieu unless they choose to. As a result of working for a week, some of them have effectively accrued about 3 weeks’ leave. If some of that is paid out, that is good. But I just want to make sure that the funding arrangements are in place in the same way that the Minister of Education has arranged it for schools, so that policing is not reduced as a result of the exercise.

I know it is a matter that will take some working through and it is not as urgent as it is in respect of education, but it is a very real question. Having been involved in some negotiations in the past as the Associate Minister of Finance—if I take off my shoes and do some counting on my fingers—I think about a week’s policing from the Hutt was the loss of an officer for about 6 months. That gets to be quite a serious drop in a relatively small community, so we need to work though it.

I will finish on what might be a slightly discordant note and say, as our leader and deputy leader have indicated, that because the Government has made this decision, and because of our determination to stand beside the Government in these matters, we will be supportive of it. I just want to toss in my personal view now, which is that it might have been better to wait a little while until we had an understanding of who died, or at least as good an understanding as we will ever get. I know of a relatively close friend and acquaintance who has died, but whose name has not been released. I do not know whether others are on that list. I think that all of New Zealand is thinking about that at the moment. At Pike River we knew who the 29 were. But we do not know in this case, and my view is that it might have been better to leave it a bit longer until we do.

But what is clear out of all of this is that at some stage in the future we have to work out what a proper memorial is. I want to think in grander ways than the physical for that sort of memorial. Thank you.

KEITH LOCKE (Green) : The Green Party will be fully supporting the Canterbury Earthquake Commemoration Day Bill. Since the horrific 22 February earthquake New Zealand has been mourning those who tragically were killed in that earthquake, and our hearts are very much with their families at this very difficult time. In a myriad of ways, New Zealanders have been extending their support to those who survived and those whose lives have been very badly affected. This support takes many different forms, more than I think I have seen in New Zealand in as long as I can remember. It includes everything, from the official response by civil defence—and I see that John Carter is with us today, and civil defence has done a tremendous job—right through to the very grassroots things such as people having a little sausage sizzle in their street in suburban Auckland and sending the money as their contribution to those who have suffered. We particularly feel for those at the front line, the medics and search and rescue people who are uncovering such horrible sights that none of us would ever want to experience. I pay tribute to people from all levels of society, on behalf of the Green Party, for the wonderful response across New Zealand.

It is appropriate that we have a more formal opportunity as a nation to mourn what has happened and those who have died, and the ceremony on 18 March will provide that, but I must admit that when I first heard the Prime Minister’s announcement of the date, my instinctual reaction was that it was too soon. Many of the bodies of the victims have not yet been identified and returned to their families. As of today, only 65 of the 166 bodies have been identified, and the families of most of the deceased have not had the opportunity to properly grieve with their loved ones. However, I think this memorial service will help those families, including the families of the overseas citizens who died, mainly in the CTV building. They will all get to see that the whole of the New Zealand community is behind them and with them in their grief.

I hope that the ceremony, to be held a week tomorrow, is more down-to-earth than many of the ceremonies that we as parliamentarians are involved in. An image in my mind is of a whole lot of dignitaries in suits, ties, and formal regalia, and that clashes with another image that is in my mind from the time I spent going door-to-door in the suburb of Aranui, of people who have not been able to wash their clothes for more than 2 weeks, whose toilet is a bucket, and who will find it quite difficult to travel over the cracked and bumpy roads to even get to the Hagley Park event. I certainly hope that they do get there.

It is unfortunate that having a public ceremony so soon and declaring a public holiday has provoked quite a bit of angst and controversy amongst the good citizens of Christchurch, which can be seen in the blogs and in the comments on the TV3 website at the moment, for example. All the points made by people in Christchurch on those websites and blogs are relevant, even if they do clash a bit with each other. Some business owners who are already on their knees have complained about missing a day’s work, or the cost to them of a paid holiday. Others have replied in the comments section in blogs that the additional cost to business should not override the importance of collectively remembering the tragedy and huge loss of life. Both sides have a point, and we should welcome such public discussion not only now but also in terms of the future of Christchurch.

I think that whether it be on such matters as the memorial day, which heritage buildings to reconstruct, or whether to continue with the Rugby World Cup at AMI Stadium in Christchurch, we should be open to that discussion. It worried me a little bit to read today that reports on the state of AMI Stadium would be shared with the International Rugby Board before the people of Christchurch were told. It seems to me that there needs to be a good public discussion about whether the Rugby World Cup games should go ahead at that Christchurch stadium, weighing up all the pluses and minuses, and there are a lot on either side. I am worried that there might be a fait accompli announcement by the Government, the International Rugby Board, or the Rugby Union. I hope I am wrong about that, because although it is likely that there will be a reduction in the financial return if the games are held in Christchurch and there would certainly be many other difficulties with holding the games there, this needs to be balanced against whether the games would be an important morale boost for the people of Christchurch.

Although this memorial service is dedicated to remembering those who died in the earthquake, it will inevitably be to some degree an opportunity to express our feelings towards all those in Christchurch who have suffered, and who are still suffering, as a result of the earthquake. Just about everyone living in the eastern half of Christchurch has suffered some degree of trauma, and their nightmare has not ended. Nerves are frayed from not knowing just how big the next aftershock will be, how much extra damage it might cause, and whether they will be hurt. Then there is the sadness at the loss of precious items in the big quake, worries about when homes will be repaired—if they can be—and where the money might come from. Add to that the daily grind for the many who are living without water and sewerage for the indefinite future. The memorial day will be an opportunity to remember those whose lives were lost, but we will also be thinking of those whose lives are now badly damaged and who are living in their own version of hell.

However, the story of Christchurch is also the story of great resilience, of community spirit and togetherness. When going door-to-door in Aranui offering food and support, I came across a front lawn with a substantial fireplace and a pile of wood on it. The neighbours had been cooking food together. The next-door neighbour commented that it should not take a disaster to bring people together. But together they now are, and this story is repeated across the affected suburbs of Christchurch. Earlier that day I had been at Canterbury University watching students mobilising with their shovels for another hard day attacking the liquefaction. It brought tears to my eyes because, along with the Farmy Army, the students have shifted tonnes and tonnes of silt that was covering the lawns and drives of so many householders who did not know what to do next. Much of the food that we were distributing on that day in Aranui was home-baked goodies. I was particularly touched by a set of buns covered with white icing. The person who had baked them had taken the trouble to add lettering in pink icing to each bun, reading simply “We love you”. That, to me, is the spirit of Christchurch today.

I agree with what Annette King said. We have to take time to grieve; we have to take a breath. The memorial service on 18 March is only part of the process; there is a lot more to be done. Everyone in this House, particularly the Christchurch MPs—including the Green MP Kennedy Graham—are doing their utmost to offer assistance. That is to be commended. We are working very much in a cross-party spirit, as we should in such a tragedy, and I think we will all be together next week on 18 March with the people of Christchurch. Thank you.

Ko ngā mea o Hapanihi, te hunga o Ahitereiria, ngā pirihimana i hara mai i Ahitereia ki te tautoko. Ngā mea o roto o Amerika i hara mai i tawhiti kia eke mai ki konei, tautoko mai ai i a tātou.

Ko tāku he mihi ki te hunga ope tūao, arā, te voluntary army. Me mihi nui ki a rātou ka tika, ngā wātene Māori o te motu, Salvation Army, te Rōpū Hāpai Rū Whenua i Rangiora, Student Volunteer Army, Farmy Army, Red Cross, me ngā marae o te motu. I whakawātea mai ngā marae o te motu i ō rātou kūaha ki te hunga i noho raruraru nei, arā, kai raro iho nei, kei Pipitea tētahi i whakatuwhera nei i ngā kūaha o tō rātou marae.

Engari, me pēnei rawa te mihi nui, me kī, ki a Ngāi Tahu. Ko Ngāi Tahu tērā e kaha tonu nei ki te hāpai, ki te āwhina, ki te noho hai pou, hai tuarā mō te hunga e rongo nei i te ngau o mamae, Pākehā mai, Māori mai. Kai Wigram kē rātou i tēnei wā tonu nei, kai a rātou te nama waea hai āwhina i te hunga e kimi kōrero nei, e kimi āwhina nei mai i ngā moutere o Te Moana-nui-a-Kiwa, me ētahi atu. Nō reira, ka nui te mihi ki a rātou me ngā pūtea ka tukuna ki te kai, ki te āwhina. Nō rātou te whakaaro nui ki te hāpai, me kī, i ngā mea e noho mai ra rā i Te Wai Pounamu. Ngā Hau E Whā Marae, ko ia tētahi kua whakatuwhera nei i tōna kūaha, me kī, ki Te Manatū Whakahiato Ora, ka mutu, kua noho tērā hei wāhi hāpai i te whakaoranga o te tangata. Me mihi rā ki Te Puni Kōkiri me ngā non-governmental organisations, ngā rōpū āwhina Kāwanatanga.

Nā reira, ko taku ko te mihi ki ngā iwi katoa o te motu i roto i a au, anā, kua whakatuwherahia ngā kura kaupapa Māori i te kūaha ki ngā tamariki o roto o Ngāi Tahu, kia haere ki roto i a au o Te Arawa.

Hai kupu kōrero whakamutunga māku, i te whakaaro ake au mō tētahi aituā nui i roto i a au, anā ko te hū o Tarawera i te tau 1890. I tērā wā, e ai kī ngā kōrero, e 150 o Ngāti Rangitihi-Tūhourangi i mate. Nā, i whakawātea mai a Te Arawa i ngā kākahu, i ngā whenua, i ngā kai ki te hunga mōrehu, anā, kei reira tonu tērā here ki roto o te Whakarewarewa ko Ngāti Wāhiao, ki Ngāpuna ko Ngāti Hurunga, ki Waitangi ko Tapuika, ki Matatā ko Ngāti Rangitihi, anā, kei reira ngā uri i tēnei wā, nā te whakaaro nui o ngā wāhi nei o roto i a au o Te Arawa ki te hunga i raruraru i tērā o ngā rū nui. Kai te pērā anō hoki i tēnei wā tonu nei, kua whakatuwherahia te ngākau ki te āwhina, ki te tautoko i te hunga kua parekuratia nā runga i te āhuatanga o te rū nei.

Me mihi ki a Ngāti Porou, me kī, ngā whanaunga o te Hōnore Hekia Parata, ki te reo irirangi i kohi moni ki te āwhina, ki te Pouaka Whakaata Māori mō Hyundai CODE i tuku i a Glen Osborne rāua ko Reuben Wiki ki te waiata, ahakoa koretake rāua ki te waiata, te kōhanga reo o tēnei takiwā, Te Kōhanga Reo o Ngā Mokopuna, me Te Kōhanga Reo o Te Kaahui, me kī, i mahi i te kapa haka i Lambton Quay i nanahi nei ki taku mōhio, ki ngā nēhi o roto i a au o Te Arawa, a Waikato-Tainui i tuku i wā rātou kaihanga whare ki tērā takiwā, me tētahi tangata tērā pea kāore anō koe kia rongo mō te tangata nei Mr Assistant Speaker, a Henare O’Keefe. Nō roto o Heretaunga tēnei tangata. Ko tāna mahi he heri i āna mīhini tunu kai. Koi nā te ingoa o tana kaupapa Tunutunu. Kua heria ki reira ki te tunu kai mō te hapori.

Ka mutu, me waiho pea ki reira engari, arā anō ngā marae; Takahanga ki Kaikōura, Tuahiwi Marae ki Kaiapoi, Te Aitarakihi Marae ki Te Tihi-o-Maru, rātou katoa kua āwhina mai. Nō reira, ko tāku he mihi nui, whakaharahara o te ngākau, otirā, o te Pāti Māori me te Pāremata, taku kī atu ki a rātou katoa i kaha ki te tautoko i te hapori e tangi nei, e auwē nei, me te tūmanako ia nā runga i te āhuatanga kua whakawāteahia mai tēnei rā, me whakanui i te hunga kua ngaro, waiho rātou kia moe, kia okioki. Kua ea te wāhi ki a rātou. Ko tā tātou he tuku i te roimata me te hūpē kia ea ai te āhuatanga o mate. Huri noa, kia ora tātou.

[Greetings, Mr Assistant Speaker. Mention has been made of the Māori World’s view on the Christchurch earthquake, so I will not take that further. Let me begin by talking about a man from Waikato by the name of Tetaki Tairākena. Recently he celebrated his 60th birthday. To most, he was plain Wally. To my knowledge, for over 25 years he taught the Māori language. He was very good at playing the guitar and was even in a band, according to some, alongside Steve Apirana. This Sunday he will lie in state on his marae at Tauwhare, close to Morrinsville. He is one of the ones who is about to be buried. So, yes, the most important thing is to acknowledge those who are lost from sight. Death is something that cannot be avoided.

Is it a holiday that is ahead of us? I say it is not. A holiday is a time to rest and recuperate, to catch up on things that one is not able to do on a working day. Is this a holiday? No. It is a time to give thought to those who have suffered the loss of loved ones, to offer help to those who are still grieving, and to grieve for those who have passed away. We must stand shoulder to shoulder with the people over there, be it non-Māori, Māori, people from beyond our shores, or migrants. That is the most important thing ahead of us. The principle cannot be faulted. Set aside a day for the people of that region of ours. I simply say that the Māori Party supports this emphatically.

Personally, I believe that it is an opportunity for us to acknowledge the rescue workers, the helpers, the ones who have worked tirelessly for that one of our communities, and indeed the nation—not just the ones from here in New Zealand, but those from overseas as well, particularly the rescue workers from Japan, the police from Australia, and those all the way from the United States of America, who came to support us.

I particularly want to acknowledge all the voluntary organisations. It is most appropriate that we thank them immensely—Māori wardens of the country, the Salvation Army, the Rangiora Express, the Student Volunteer Army, the Farmy Army, the Red Cross, and the marae of the country. The marae opened up their doors to those with difficulties. Pipitea Marae just down the street along from us was one. It opened up its doors.

But Ngāi Tahu must be accorded the greatest accolade. They were resolute in their efforts to lift morale and provide assistance and be a pillar and backbone for those who suffered hardship, whether they were non-Māori or Māori. They are currently set up at Wigram airbase. They have a telephone number to assist those seeking information and assistance relating to Pasifika people and other people. So a huge accolade is due to them and the financial help they provided for food and assistance. Ngāi Tahu have enormous foresight to help those living in the South Island. Ngā Hau e Whā Marae is one that has opened up its doors to the Ministry of Social Development, which has become a recovery assistance centre. Thanks indeed to the Ministry of Māori Development and the non-governmental organisations.

I acknowledge all the tribes of the country that are part of me. Māori-medium schools have opened their doors to children within Ngāi Tahu to go and stay within me in Te Arawa.

To conclude this address, I recall a huge disaster that occurred in my area. It was the eruption of Tarawera in the year 1890. At that time, and according to the stories, 150 of Ngāti Rangitihi-Tūhourangi perished. Te Arawa made clothing, land, and food available to the survivors, and that bond with Ngāti Wāhiao of W.hakarewarewa, Ngāti Hurunga of Ngāpuna, Tapuika of Waitangi, and Ngāti Rangitihi of Matatā still remains. The descendants are still there today, due to the great regard of these places within me in Te Arawa for those who suffered that huge earthquake. We have a similar situation again today, where the heart is opened up to assist and support those who have become victims of this disaster.

I need to acknowledge Ngāti Porou; relatives of the Hon Hekia Parata; radio stations that raised money to help; Māori Television, for Hyundai CODE, releasing Glen Osborne and Reuben Wiki to sing, even if their efforts were not very flash; Māori-medium schools and language nurseries of this locality, such as Te Kohanga Reo o Ngā Mokopuna and Te Kohanga Reo o Te Kaahui, which performed cultural items on Lambton Quay yesterday, as I was told; the nurses from within me of Te Arawa; Waikato-Tainui, who sent their carpenters to that place; and a person whom you may not know anything about, Mr Assistant Speaker, Henare O’Keefe. This person is from Hastings. He owns a mobile barbecue kitchen. Its name is Tunutunu. He has taken it down there to barbecue food for the community.

Perhaps I should stop there, but before I do I need to mention the marae: the one in Kaikōura, the Takahanga Marae; the Tuahiwi Marae in Kaiapoi; and the Te Aitarakihi Marae at Timaru—all have offered help. I extend my immense and heartfelt accolade, and that of the Māori Party and Parliament, as well, to all those who supported the community here, which is grieving and hurting so strongly, in the hope that this day will be set aside. Celebrate those who have perished. Allow them to sleep and rest. The part concerning them has been fulfilled. Ours is to shed tears and mucus, to acquit our losses through death. Thank you to all of us, throughout the Chamber .]
