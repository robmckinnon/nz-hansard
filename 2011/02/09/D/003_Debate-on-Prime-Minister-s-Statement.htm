Debate on Prime Minister’s Statement

  • Debate resumed from 8 February.

Dr CAM CALDER (National) : This Government is committed to helping and protecting the most vulnerable in our society and creating an environment where all New Zealanders can dare to dream our dreams, unlock the potential that lies within us all, and achieve our goals and aspirations in a better society. We have a considered, logical plan to grow our economy. We are delivering world-class infrastructure to unblock the arteries of the economy, and we are demanding better, more efficient public services. We have introduced a number of regulatory changes to make it easier to do business, and we are fostering better skills in the workforce and a research climate that encourages innovation. We are pursuing an ambitious free-trade agenda, led by the very able Minister of Trade to my right, to expand our markets for our products, and we have introduced a growth-friendly tax system that rewards effort. Alongside that we are hauling back on new spending allowances and confronting the challenges of lifting our national savings rates so that we can reduce our debt to overseas lenders—a fiscally prudent, economically literate plan.

Contrast our plan with the Labour spiral of spending promises, costed so far at $5 billion and still climbing steadily towards the stratosphere. Although many New Zealand families are being prudent, being careful with their money, and paying down debt, Labour is talking of doing exactly the opposite. It is behaving fecklessly, recklessly, and irresponsibly. Where is the money coming from? So far the search for answers to this fundamental question has been singularly unrewarding; no answers have been forthcoming because Labour does not have an answer. How will Labour grow the economy? Labour is offering a pot-pourri of policies made on the hoof; it is a case of fruit-loop thinking leading to fruitcake policies with absolutely no sound economic foundation. Maybe that is an explanation for why Labour would remove GST on fruit and vegetables, even though this policy has long been discredited, even by its own caucus, as a meaningful intervention at any level, let alone as a driver of a nation’s economy.

What exactly is Labour’s mechanism to encourage economic growth? Sadly, it is a continuation of Labour’s old tax, borrow, spend, and hope philosophy that served New Zealanders so poorly in the past. Perhaps it is using a carrot and stick philosophy: reduce GST on carrots, and whack with a big stick—in the form of increased taxes—many of those helping to create jobs. Labour’s recipe of loose spending, more borrowing, and higher taxes is a clear contrast to this Government’s responsible programme to keep debt under control, promote savings, and get the economy working faster to create a better society for all New Zealanders.

I would like to talk about education. Education is at the root of our belief in the need to unlock the potential that lies within all New Zealanders. We have never spent more than we are spending now on education.

Hon ANNETTE KING (Deputy Leader—Labour) : The Prime Minister’s speech at the beginning of a political year is often eagerly awaited by commentators, the media, politicians, and even by some of the public, and I suppose even more so in an election year. The speech is seen as the Prime Minister’s chance to put forward his Government’s vision, to outline his plan and programme to make New Zealand a better place in which to live, to show that he has command of the issues that we face in the very unstable world that we live in, to give reassurance to the vulnerable members of our society—children, disabled people, the sick, the elderly—and to show that he is in touch with the real issues that New Zealanders face today. There is always a certain amount of expectation about what will be in the speech, and, of course, for this speech we had the usual hoopla and hype about this showcase piece. In fact, one commentator went so far as to predict that the Prime Minister’s speech would plot the course that the Government would take and acquire a strategic direction.

I have to say to that commentator that he must be feeling very, very disappointed. We did not get a vision, we did not get a plan, and we did not get a strategic direction that would, perhaps, give us a brighter future, maybe. Or what about a direction that would turbocharge the economy, or perhaps something that would be ambitious for New Zealand, or maybe even something about catching up with Australia—let alone something that would give us a step change for New Zealand? What did we get? We got a hastily cobbled-together announcement that the way forward, the breakthrough, the way to cut through the issues was to restructure the Public Service. We were told there are too many employees and a bloated Public Service, so National will get rid of a few public servants and replace them with a few consultants—in fact, a lot of consultants. You see, consultants do not count when we are counting up public servants. It is the old National Party trick: smoke and mirrors; smoke and mirrors. It is the usual merry-go-round that National runs when it is in Government.

This morning, on Morning Report, John Key could not even name which departments were on the block, how many jobs would be lost, how much would be saved, how much the restructuring would cost, or even when it would be done. We then had to listen to a chorus of the usual sycophants saying it was an amazing plan, and we had an amazing Prime Minister. If I have ever heard anything like the emperor’s new clothes, it was listening to all those people. They had been rung up and told to put out statements that said how wonderful the plan was.

Well, I say that what we got from John Key was more like a political circus. He walked the tightrope between lies, damned lies, and statistics. He juggled the latest unemployment figures. He even dropped the ball on job creation. The unemployment figures are ballooning. They are now at 6.8 percent, with 265,000—I will say it again: 265,000—people in New Zealand officially jobless, according to the latest household labour force survey. What did John Key say about that? He said people should relax. He said they should not give up hope as it is just a survey, the information is old, and the survey is notoriously volatile. Then we had the doozy of them all, which came from the “Minister of Unemployment”. She chimed in and said a slow recovery fits the Government’s focus on the economy. Do members know what we say? Hear, hear! That is what we say. The Government’s focus on the economy has been about gimmicks and slogans, and it is no wonder that the economy has been slow to recover.

Unemployment in New Zealand is going up; unemployment in Australia is going down. Today, just before we came into this House—in fact, as we came into this House—the Minister released the latest unemployment and benefit numbers. She has had them for 5 days. She slipped them out as we came into the House, and what do they tell us? Almost 100,000 more New Zealanders are now on all main benefits since 2008 under that Government.

Hon Phil Goff: That’s their welfare reform policy.

Hon ANNETTE KING: That is the Government’s welfare reform policy: to put more people on the benefit.

John Key did acrobatics over the rising cost of living and the drop in real wages that has occurred under his regime. He said people are getting more, when they are getting less. He said prices are going down, when they are going up. He said people are better off, when they are going backwards. I am very, very sure it is a long time since he walked down McGehan Close and talked to the people there. If he did, he could have heard what they had to say about this National Government. They said it has done little to help the poor: “He’s just making everything better for high earners and not the low-income ones … Bread, milk, everything that we need that is a basic necessity for us is going to be more expensive. It’s going to be harder for us to feed our kids.” So whom do we believe? Do we believe John Key, or do we believe the ordinary struggling family that lives in the street that he made a feature of at the last election?

Then John Key did the high wire act over his plans to sell off our State assets. He told us we would own more of our own country if we sold a few shares to mum and dad investors, who already own those assets. I do not quite understand that. How will we own more if we sell to mum and dad investors something that we already own? I would really like him to tell us how that works. Mind you, it is a risky move. It is a risky move by the Prime Minister, because he has removed his usual safety net: the U-turn that he does when things get a bit politically hot. He has said he does not give a damn whether New Zealanders care about selling the assets; he is going to sell them.

To complete the political circus, John Key has acted like the clown. I am not talking about his mincing down the catwalk; I am talking about the poor joke that he has played on the most vulnerable members of our society: children in this country of New Zealand. They received a fleeting mention as a postscript, a sort of afterthought, in his speech. Someone said to him that he had better throw a few lines into the speech about some kids, because Labour has set out a comprehensive policy to lift our children and our families out of poverty; to give every child in this country a good start—

Hon Dr Nick Smith: All mush.

Hon ANNETTE KING: —to put support at the beginning of their lives, I say to Dr Smith, and not to waste millions of dollars on putting them into boot camps 14 and 15 years later; and to value caring for children. We value those who care for, nurture, and look after children, and we do not reduce that to only the rich, those who can afford to stay home and look after their children, while struggling low and middle income families and sole parents are told that they do not count.

If this Prime Minister truly believed in a real future, and if he had put forward a vision for this country, it would have been about growing the economy, and growing jobs so that people could go into them and gain the dignity that comes from work and opportunity, and it would have been about giving a future to our children. It is not about just the economy; it is about the economy and giving people a future in this country. He would have taken notice of his Chief Science Adviser, Professor Gluckman. He would have gone to Anne Tolley and told her to reverse the cuts in early childhood education. The $250 million - odd he is stripping out of early childhood education must go back in, because our young children matter in this country. It is a right for them to have education.

Why is it that when this Government made its first cut, it picked on the smallest, most vulnerable kids, who have no voice in this country in that Government? They have no voice at the Cabinet table in this Government. The first moves that it made were against those who have no voice. John Key should have listened to his adviser, Dr Gluckman. He should have addressed child poverty in New Zealand. The shame of that Government and of that Prime Minister was to say in this House before Christmas that they had no intention of eradicating child poverty in this country. Child poverty is a shame for this country, and when we were in Government we lifted 130,000 families out of poverty through Working for Families, which John Key called “communism by stealth”. But I notice that he is scared to get rid of it.

HILARY CALVERT (ACT) : It is good to be back in the debating chamber for what promises to be an interesting year. I hope all members had a great Christmas and New Year break and are feeling refreshed. The parliamentary year has barely begun but we have already witnessed plenty of drama and sideshows. We cannot let what the media deem important distract us from the reasons we are here. We should not worry about catwalks and campaigns but about how to enable the conditions whereby all New Zealanders can get ahead.

ACT and National have worked well together in many areas. We in ACT are pleased to have ensured a stable centre-right Government since 2008. We are a party of principles, and when we see National stray from its principles it is our duty to call it out on that. A key principle for ACT is one law for all New Zealand—that is, one law for all, regardless of race, religion, income, or location. We thought that that was National’s view too, but since 2008 we have seen National, under the influence of the Māori Party, move us further down the path to separatism.

One such example is the Marine and Coastal Area (Takutai Moana) Bill, which is scheduled to replace one racist piece of legislation with another. The Prime Minister promised to replace the Foreshore and Seabed Act only if there was widespread support for doing so, but it is plainly obvious that the new bill is massively unpopular with almost everyone. The bill divides up our coastline, minerals, and access along tribal lines.

Hon Trevor Mallard: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I know this is slightly unusual. The Marine and Coastal Area (Takutai Moana) Bill is not actually on the Order Paper, but we have had an assurance from the Speaker that the bill is a matter that is now set down because it has been reported back. If that is the case, then it is a breach of the Standing Orders for the speaker to anticipate an order of the day.

The ASSISTANT SPEAKER (Hon Rick Barker): The member is technically correct, but it is a breach only if it can be reasonably expected that debate on the bill will be reached on the same sitting day.

Hon Trevor Mallard: Well, come on.

The ASSISTANT SPEAKER (Hon Rick Barker): Excuse me. There is no chance that we will get to that bill today, so it is perfectly in order for the member to make reference to that particular legislation. I invite Hilary Calvert to continue.

HILARY CALVERT: If one is not in the right iwi, one misses out. National says that up to 10 percent of our coastline could wind up under customary title. We could ultimately have a situation where our entire coastline—just like our longest river—is under co-governance. Some may ask what is wrong with that. The Treaty of Waitangi, which was signed a little over 171 years ago, promised that all—Māori and non-Māori—would be equal before the law. Everyone would have the same rights and responsibilities as one another. That promise has long since fallen by the wayside. The Treaty has been twisted into a form that pits them versus us. No longer is it about equality of opportunity for all; instead, it is now focused on taking what we have and divvying it up along racial lines.

For ACT, one law for all goes beyond race. It is about empowering all New Zealanders to get ahead and better ourselves. It is about acknowledging that focusing on equality of outcome is impossible and irresponsible, but that we can and must work to ensure equality of opportunity. We see children trapped in bad schools, ill people forced to linger on public waiting lists, and the jobless lost in an archaic welfare system. As our leader, Rodney Hide, said, ACT finds unacceptable the thought of 12,000 new entrants per year ultimately leaving school functionally illiterate. It is abhorrent that those in the central North Island cannot get the same level of chemotherapy treatment as those in other parts of New Zealand, and locking our young people out of the workforce through impossibly high minimum wages borders on criminal.

The current system locks too many into a lifetime of inferiority and missed opportunities. ACT has policies that will enable people to choose the education and health systems that suit them best. We are desperate to lower barriers into the workforce that past Governments have been only too happy to erect. We want an economy that rewards those who raise our living standards through hard work and innovation. We will fight against the divisive Marine and Coastal Area (Takutai Moana) Bill, and fight to restore the sanctity of property rights for all, regardless of colour and as determined by centuries of common law. That is what one law for all New Zealanders means to me and to the ACT Party. We look forward to continuing to work with National where our principles match, and to speaking out when they do not.

Hon HEATHER ROY (ACT) : Welfare reform is something the ACT Party has spoken passionately about since its inception. I congratulate John Key on his focus in his speech yesterday on welfare reform. He said that a Government is judged by how it cares for the sick and vulnerable. That is too true. He said that 200,000 children are trapped in welfare homes—that is 200,000 children too many. He said that those who can work should work, and he is right. But it is easy—very easy, in fact—to stand up in this Chamber and say these things, and much harder to do the real work and to act.

I was very disappointed, I have to say, in the Hon Annette King’s address. She decided to spend 10 minutes whacking Mr Key around the chops for what he said yesterday, yet whenever she is given the opportunity she talks about the wonderful policy work she is doing in the area of welfare reform. Well, where was the talk of that in her speech? She had 10 minutes to positively put forward what she wants to do. The truth of the matter is that in 9 long years in Government, Labour did not manage to get on top of the issue. Here is the situation: in 1970 we had 37 workers for every person on a benefit. In 2000 there were five workers for every person on a benefit. In December 2010 there were six workers for every person on a benefit. Under the current Government at least things have improved slightly, but it is a shameful position to be in. We have 2.2 million people employed in this country, but 353,000 people are on welfare benefits—a ratio of 6:1. Welfare dependency amongst working-age New Zealanders is reaching unsustainable levels financially and it has done huge damage to this country socially. Far from reducing the number of people on welfare, subsequent welfare policies from National and Labour Governments have taken the opposite path. They have increased the number.

We are seeing at the moment an increase in the minimum wage; both National and Labour Governments are responsible for that. National has steadfastly refused to reinstate youth rates, which pushes people out of work and on to welfare. This has been coupled with the entrenching of middle-class welfare by refusing to alter the Working for Families policies, which make more people than ever dependent on the State.

The Welfare Working Group has put forward some very sound proposals. There are a lot of them. Some of the proposals are excellent and are things that the ACT Party has proposed for a long time. Some are considered to be a tough love approach. That may well be the case, but it is time that we got tough. It is time that we had the courage to do the things that will make a difference. This might mean lifting the age at which a young woman can get the domestic purposes benefit. It might mean having time limits for benefits. These things might be tough, but they put the incentives in the right place and allow people to take ownership of their lives. These are the decisions that we have to make.

When we talk about what is tough, and again I congratulate the Prime Minister on focusing on this issue in his speech yesterday, it is too tough to stomach the environment in which far too many of our precious young children live, where they are abused and in some cases—too many—are killed. The Lillybings, the James Whakarurus, the Nia Glassies. Welfare dependency has created an environment where there are abusive relationships and children suffering, and none of us in this House should be comfortable with that.

ACT is the only party committed to true welfare reform and we will push the Government as far as we can to get the incentives right. There are many things we could do to send the right signals. We could be purchasing our own accident and unemployment insurance, which would put the emphasis in the right place. We need to get the economy to be in a position to support the truly vulnerable, where welfare functions as a safety net, not a hammock that is so distended that we cannot tell the truly vulnerable from those who would like to climb out on their own. Michael Joseph Savage would turn in his grave. He was popular because he offered people hope. He said that we should let people govern themselves, and when we think about welfare reform, that should be foremost in our minds.

Hon BILL ENGLISH (Deputy Prime Minister) : In the House yesterday the Prime Minister outlined the next steps in the Government’s programme to get the economy on track, to strengthen it, and to raise incomes. All of us need reminding every now and again of the scale of the change and adjustment that the New Zealand economy needs to go through, and we are by no means alone. Most of the countries that followed the same policy prescriptions as New Zealand over the last 10 years have found themselves in the same position.

I will use for example the discussion about debt levels, because there will clearly be an important public debate this year between the Government’s interest in getting New Zealand’s debt—

Hon Lianne Dalziel: Talk about public debt.

Hon BILL ENGLISH: —and Government debt down, and the Labour Party, which takes a different view. Labour seems to think that if debt is cheap, then we should have endless amounts of it.

The average household in New Zealand owed $61,000 in 2003. That amount is an average, and it includes all types of credit, of which mortgages are obviously the largest, but it also includes credit card debt, consumer debt, and overdrafts. Of course, debt is not distributed evenly across households. Some households are debt-free. Older households tend to be debt-free; younger households tend to have a lot of debt. But in 2003, on average, household debt was $61,000. By 2008, just 5 short years later, it was $107,000. That is by far the fastest rise in debt burden that New Zealand has seen over any reasonable period. The average household now owes $107,000.

New Zealanders have, fortunately, understood the risks of that debt. They understand that if interest rates are rising, it will eat into their standard of living, because they will have to pay more interest on what is now a much larger stock of debt than they had before. Much of that debt has been incurred in buying houses from each other or buying consumer goods, none of which generates a return to the economy. New Zealanders are now being careful with their spending and are trying to pay off debt, but we have barely made a dent in that $107,000. In the meantime, the people who lent the money to us still want us to pay interest, and we have to keep going back to them through the banks to roll over that debt.

If we thought that $61,000 was enough debt per household, then every household in New Zealand would have to pay off $40,000 of debt. That would have an enormous impact on this economy, because it is money that would not be going into the job-rich areas such as the retail sector, or into building new houses. Even if we think $107,000 is about the right amount of debt, that figure still represents a lot of constraint in this economy. The burden of debt that households have built up has now become quite a considerable drag on economic growth. Until households believe that they have their debt at the right level and that they can cover the risks of interest rates rising on that debt as the economy recovers, they will not go out and spend up.

In the agricultural sector it is worse. The agricultural sector debt per farm more than doubled over the period from 2003 to 2008. Despite the fact that we have record commodity prices, we are not seeing that money flowing into the shops in Hamilton and Invercargill, because people in the farming community have to pay down the debt that they ran up, some on productive investment, but mostly on bidding up the prices of farms and buying them from each other. Again, although conditions are favourable, with very high commodity prices, it will take some time for those conditions to filter back into the economy because the debt hole on our farms is so large. They will be pouring their profits into that hole for some time yet.

The same kind of scale of rebalancing is evident when it comes to job growth. We can think of the economy as being 80 percent non-tradable—that is, domestic, which is not competing with the rest of the world—and about 20 percent making up the export sector and the tradable economy. The non-tradable sector was delivering a lot of new jobs over the last decade. But far too many of them were funded by the debt that I have just talked about and by big increases in Government spending. That spending simply cannot continue, and it is not continuing. New Zealanders have decided that it will not continue. The Government has been a bit slower, and we are deciding that we will have to tighten up.

Substantial job growth will not come from that 80 percent of the economy. There will be some growth, and there has been. In the last 15 months there have been 35,000 new jobs in the non-tradable sector, but that is 80 percent of the economy. The sustainable job growth has to occur in the other 20 percent. In the export sector in the 5 years to 2009 the number of jobs shrank by 55,000. We had 55,000 fewer people in the export sector 5 years on from 2003. That trend has just turned round, and with high commodity prices it should keep turning round. So 20 percent of the economy needs to grow very fast to offset the fact that the other 80 percent of the economy, which generated all the jobs, is now heavily constrained by debt and will not be growing nearly as many jobs as it used to grow. There is a long road ahead of us to get a vibrant, job-generating export sector up and going when it has been on its knees for a fair bit of the last decade, shrinking in production and shrinking in jobs.

That brings me to the issue of the public sector, which the Prime Minister addressed in his speech. We are very keen to see an enthusiastic, professional, and innovative public sector. Not only are we keen to see it; it is an absolute necessity. New Zealanders’ demands and expectations for public services have not shrunk because of a recession; those demands and expectations have continued to grow. In fact, those expectations are probably lifting: people expect better health care, they expect better education, and they expect more effective law and order from the police and prison system. So the Government is focusing on producing more and better public services, knowing that it will have to do so for less.

I expect Opposition members will rant on about cuts, as they usually do, but in the time since they left office we have reprioritised $4 billion—

Sue Moroney: Cuts.

Hon Member: He means cuts.

Hon BILL ENGLISH: There we go: they call it cuts. Any dollar spent in the public sector under Labour is a dollar that cannot go anywhere else. They are the only people in New Zealand who think that. Every household and every business in New Zealand has had to reorganise its affairs, and the public sector will need to be reorganised in order to produce more and better services. We are determined to do that.

The Government will go about this task as it has so far: in a very considered and thoughtful manner. We have already initiated quite a lot of change across the public sector. Increasingly the public sector is taking leadership of that change because its members can see that to have satisfying professional lives, and to meet their strong ethic of public service, things will need to be done a bit differently. It will be the Government’s role to support them in that task. They are aware of the financial constraints; they have heard that message for a couple of years. The rest of the community is aware of the financial constraints, which is why people take no notice of Labour’s ranting about cuts. They know, actually, that Labour oversaw a pretty bloated public sector, which is now being brought into line.

I, along with the team in the National-led Government, look forward to participating in a process that sees improvements in public services at the same time as we get Government finances into line, reduce our record-breaking liabilities to overseas lenders, and get this economy up and running and producing more jobs.

GRANT ROBERTSON (Labour—Wellington Central) : Yesterday when John Key spoke in Parliament he told us that a society is judged on how it protects the vulnerable. It perhaps will not surprise members in the House that he did not get the quote quite right. The quote comes from an American politician named Hubert Humphrey, who said that the test of the morality of a society was the way in which it looked after those in the dawn of life, the twilight of life, and the shadows of life. If we put the National Government’s record to that test in just one sector of our society, the health sector, we can see a total failure by the National Government. We can look at those in the dawn of life, the young. Today in question time I raised with Tony Ryall whether it is acceptable for someone living on the Kapiti coast to have to spend 20 percent of the weekly family grocery bill on a visit for after-hours treatment for their daughter’s cut lip. Is that acceptable?

Hon Nanaia Mahuta: How much?

GRANT ROBERTSON: Twenty percent of that family’s weekly grocery bill was spent on one visit for after-hours care, and this was from a Government that said that under-sixes and the treatment of our young children would be a priority. So those in the dawn of life are not receiving the kind of treatment in the health sector that we would expect.

Research last year told us that there were 2,000 more admissions of children to hospital in 2009 than there were in 2007. That is 2,000 extra admissions to hospital for diseases of which many would have been preventable if the Government were prepared to invest in primary health care, if the Government were prepared to invest in the well-being of our young people. The fact that we have rheumatic fever in New Zealand, at all, is a disgrace. We need investment in those who are in the dawn of life because that is a test of the morality of the Government, but we have seen that this Government is failing that test. Again, we see it with the cuts to early childhood education as well. Young people are being left behind in the health system because Mr Ryall is obsessed by his focus on elective surgery. He is obsessed by looking good in front of this House when it comes to the health sector, but the reality for some, like that young family on the Kapiti coast, is that the health costs they are facing end up putting them and other families in a situation where it is a choice between food on the table or health treatment.

Well, in this country we do have choices. That Government has choices, and it has chosen to give tax cuts weighted towards the top 10 percent of earners. The Government could have made a choice to invest in health and education and to ensure that care for under-sixes could be free for people all around New Zealand, but it made a choice to give those tax cuts to the highest-income earners.

If we take Hubert Humphrey’s quote further, we may consider those in the twilight of life, the elderly. The record on elder care in the health sector from this Government is appalling. I could not believe that in a country like New Zealand I would have a constituent come to me about her 92-year-old father, a World War II veteran, who had had his home-help hours cut over the phone. This man has hearing difficulties and sight difficulties, but his home-help hours were cut over the phone. That is not the kind of New Zealand I want to live in. I want to live in a New Zealand where we look after each other and where we make a priority of looking after one another, not a priority of rewarding the top 10 percent of earners with a tax cut that they did not need. So those in the twilight of life have not been treated well by this Government.

What about those in the shadows of life? I take that to mean people, for instance, who are receiving care under the mental health system. What kind of treatment have they seen from this Government? There have been cuts to funding right around New Zealand for mental health. Here in my own area, the Capital and Coast District Health Board in Wellington has cut mental health funding over the last 2 years. That means that two community clinics in Wellington city will close this year—two mental health community clinics will close this year. It means that right across the health sector, non-governmental organisations that provide vital support to people in our community are losing money.

An organisation like the Phobic Trust of New Zealand, run by a wonderful woman named Marcia Read, who has been lauded by parties on both sides of this House, is losing a funding contract here in Wellington to support people with phobic disorders. Is that the kind of country that has a good morality? That is the kind of message the Government is sending. It is failing the test of looking after the vulnerable. It is failing the moral test of a Government to look after its citizens in the dawn of life, the twilight of life, and the shadows of life. That is a shame upon this Government, and it is more evident in the health sector than anywhere else. It is a question of priorities.

The Government has already said that it will cut the spending allowance in the next Budget down to $800 million of extra spending. If the health budget stands still, it will take up $600 million of that $800 million—if it stands still. Actually, that is still not enough. In fact, over the 4-year Budget cycle that the Government likes to do its Budget on, there will be a $750 million hole in the health budget. One whole year’s worth of new health spending will have gone, across those 4 years. That will inevitably lead to more service cuts. It will inevitably lead to more pressure on district health boards not to provide those services to the young, to the elderly, and to those patients in mental health. That is not good enough, because the kind of country that I want to live in is one where we invest in health and education.
