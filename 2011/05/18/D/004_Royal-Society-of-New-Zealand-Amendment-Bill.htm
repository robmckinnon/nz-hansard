Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill

In Committee

  • Debate resumed from 4 May.

Clause 4 Purpose (continued)

GRANT ROBERTSON (Labour—Wellington Central) : I welcome back colleagues to the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill.

Chris Tremain: We’ve been waiting all night for this.

GRANT ROBERTSON: Absolutely. It is the highlight of Mr Tremain’s evening—and perhaps life—to be here for this.

When I was last speaking I was working my way through the memorandum of understanding between the Royal Society of New Zealand and the Council for the Humanities, because it lies at the heart of this purpose clause. Essentially, the memorandum of understanding is bringing together the Council for the Humanities and the Royal Society in a way in which both organisations will be able to work closely together and to ensure that the kaupapa, the philosophy and values, of the organisations are kept. I think I had come to the point of saying that the term “humanities” will be included in all places within the Royal Society’s mandate and on its website. We will be doing that as we work through the bill that is before us. Also, in making its representations to the Government and agencies, the Royal Society will be including humanities wherever it is relevant, and particular representations will be made about humanities. Obviously in collaboration with the Council for the Humanities, which will become the Humanities Committee of the Royal Society, there will be a particular focus on promotion and celebration of the humanities.

I thought it was important to put on the record what the memorandum of understanding between the Royal Society and the Council for the Humanities means in terms of this purpose clause. I think it is a very good document that gives a solid foundation to the way in which the two organisations will now work together and it will allow the purpose clause to be enacted. I think this is a very important document. It shows that the level of collaboration between the Council for the Humanities and the Royal Society has been high for some time. They are committed to bringing this together. In fact, they have largely incorporated most of the work required to bring it together and this legislation is merely the legal requirement on top of that.

In one last point on this memorandum of understanding, as I said in the last intervention, one aspect of the memorandum of understanding is the statement that within 5 years the Royal Society will use its best endeavours to have its Act reflect the inclusion of the humanities. We are well ahead of time, because this was negotiated in February 2010, so we have until February 2015 to pass this legislation. As I said the other day, it might take that long.

Hon Pete Hodgson: It won’t take that long.

GRANT ROBERTSON: I say to Mr Hodgson that it probably will not, but we certainly want to give it a thorough consideration this evening as we work our way through the clauses. At the end of this call I will seek the leave of the Committee to table this memorandum of understanding because I believe that it is an important part of the debate. I seek the leave of the Committee to table the memorandum of understanding between the Royal Society of New Zealand and the Council for the Humanities.

The CHAIRPERSON (Eric Roy): Leave is sought for that purpose. Is anyone opposed to that course of action? It appears not. Leave is granted.

  • Document, by leave, laid on the Table of the House.

Hon PETE HODGSON (Labour—Dunedin North) : I think the memorandum of understanding will be pretty useful, and I hope it will be made available to members soon. I was going to ask three questions about the memorandum of understanding, but one of them has been answered, and that is the date it is being written in. The second question relates to whether the memorandum of understanding was itself subject to any scrutiny by the academy council or the academy executive committee. The member in the chair, Grant Robertson, may want to reflect on that. I would assume that there has been consultation. I would further assume that in the course of the memorandum of understanding there would have been—I do not want to use the term “argy-bargy”; that is not appropriate for the Royal Society—carefully considered variances of view as people trolled through the vicissitudes of their various disciplines to see whether incorporating the humanities into the memorandum of understanding, and therefore into the purpose of the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill, was a legitimate thing to do. The member may choose at his leisure to give us some insight into the process by which the memorandum of understanding is drafted.

I think the more important thing for members of the Committee in general is to reflect on the words in the memorandum of understanding that the member referred to when he said that where it was appropriate the Royal Society, in making representations to Government, would consider whether the humanities had something to offer. That is an area that I think we need to look at a little more deeply.

Later on in the bill—and I appreciate that we are talking only on clause 4—when we get to section 6(e) in clause 7, it states that it is a function of the Royal Society “to provide expert advice on important public issues to the Government and the community:”. From now on, that expert advice will include the advice that we can divine from the humanities. Let us reflect on what that might be. A good way to do that is to go to the British humanities scholarship group—the British humanities council, the body of people in Britain who involve themselves with humanities. I suppose in an effort to sell humanities to the British public, it produced a book about the sort of information that humanities might offer a Government of the day that might be helpful to the Government of the day. The book came out at about the time of the London bombings, so it inevitably included a piece of research in which some sociologists and those who wanted to study the phenomenology of religion spoke at length with the imams of different parts of Britain—with the geographical distribution of imams, but also with imams from different parts of the world; for example, those who had come from Pakistan, from Afghanistan, northern Africa, and so on. The researchers were able to give, by way of this scholarship, quite a lot of insight into a nation that desperately needed that insight. It is a good example, I think, of the humanities being of immediate value to the Government of the day.

Another example, just to get our appetites whetted—because I am sure other members will be able to offer the Committee further examples—is from New Zealand, not England. It is not an immediate example, it is historical, and it is occasioned by the sad death of Judith Binney, who, of course, through the latter part of her life wrote extensively on Tūhoe. I do not want to make any politics out of this, but the Tūhoe claim is not advancing, or it at least has got itself into trouble. Although I do not know where the Tūhoe claim will go in the future—others will know more than me—it is my assertion that when that claim is settled, people will look back on Judith Binney’s work and say to themselves that had she not lived, it would have been a different settlement. My assertion is that that is an example whereby one person’s scholarship, quite narrowly defined but quite extensively done and most exhaustively written about, will actively influence public policy that is in front of us in this country.

So let us think now about other examples in the area called the humanities in which we, the Government, including local government, those of us who are in the policy elite, those of us who get to do the decision making, will be informed by the humanities in the future and have been informed by humanities until now. I am asserting that we have been ignoring the humanities; that we have been absorbing their lessons almost in an indirect way, in an unconscious manner, and that the humanities influence public policy a great deal.

This comes from a scientist. If members want to know where I have been greatly influenced by science, they should refer to the Royal Commission on Genetic Modification. That is where I drew my public policy from. It was not from any opening or closing prejudice or predilection or from a general view of the world, but from the science alone. Why? Because I had nothing else to go on. This was new. I needed to know what the science said. It was a very good example of where science effected a huge impact on public policy. But very often it does not, and the Royal Society would much rather that it did more often than it does. I am making the case, as a scientist, not only for science, but I am beginning to make the case for the role of humanities in public policy. This purpose clause is a good place to do it.

The member in charge of the bill has reminded us of the memorandum of understanding that exists between the humanities of New Zealand and the Royal Society of New Zealand to bring them together and spoke of the role of humanities when the Royal Society was making representations to the Government or was involved in some educative process. Those of us who think that humanities are nice only, that they are just poetry, should think again. Poetry may not directly affect public policy a lot, but history does and geography does. Just think of the Geographic Board—people who go around renaming things. Think of the role of the humanities in our trying to get the nomenclature of our own place in order. It takes a bit of time, and we do not do it without expert advice. So there is another example. I am sure many members will be able to leap to their feet, when I return to my seat, to give examples of where the humanities have been instructive or have been, in some cases, perhaps even inspirational in determining Government policy. We do not have to always rely on what the health professionals say, what the physicists say, what the educationalists say, or what the social scientists or political scientists say. We can reach to the humanities. We can ask historians what they have to say about life.

Hon Member: We all have feelings.

Hon PETE HODGSON: We all have feelings. We can ask people whose skill is with language, who are skilled linguists, what they have to say about that. We all know that this is the one country in the world where there is a particular language, known as te reo, which if it does not exist here, it will not exist anywhere. We are absolutely beholden to the humanities for the maintenance of that, the teaching of language to our mokopuna. There are a bunch of things to which the humanities make a contribution that I think have probably been underrated.

I am coming to the view that I soon need to resume my seat. I have enjoyed this contribution and I do thank members for their close consideration and careful attention to my modest contribution. Thank you.

DAVID SHEARER (Labour—Mt Albert) : What I would like to do is to follow on from Pete Hodgson’s contribution. I think he made a really important point that cuts to the very heart of the purpose of the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill, which is the incorporation of the humanities into the Royal Society. I was reflecting on his contribution as he was speaking.

The Prime Minister’s Chief Science Adviser, Peter Gluckman, is looking very much at the issue of evidence, and scientific evidence in particular, in terms of the contribution that that can make to policy. Although we support that—and, of course, we should be making decisions on the basis of evidence—Peter Gluckman makes it quite clear that we should at least have the evidence presented before us, and as a result of that evidence we should make the decision. We may decide to take that evidence into consideration in the decision on policy, or we may, for political reasons, decide to ignore it or take only some of it into consideration. But nevertheless that evidence is before us. Peter Gluckman makes a very good point in that regard.

However, I think what Pete Hodgson was also saying is that it is not just scientific evidence that is important in the consideration of policy but also history. He mentioned te reo, the Māori language. That too is important when we are looking at policy in this particular place.

I will go back to clause 4, the purpose clause, to reflect on what the Royal Society itself says about the inclusion of the humanities in this bill. Clause 4(a) recognises that incorporating the humanities is a big step forward and an important step forward for the Royal Society to make. The explanatory note of the bill also notes that in November 2008—and Grant Robertson talked about this before, in terms of the date of this particular legislation and how it came into being—the council of the Royal Society resolved that there was a need to expand the object and the functions of the society to include humanities. So we are looking at this legislation 2½ years on from when it was first considered.

The Royal Society went on to say—and this is important—“The decision to extend and enhance the Royal Society’s Academy to include researchers from across all disciplines of science, technology, social sciences and the humanities is a landmark decision.” It recognises that this is not just a decision that was suddenly dreamed up by the Royal Society and the academy of the Royal Society. It was considered over a long period of time by the Royal Society, and I think that was one thing that we recognised and valued when we were in the Education and Science Committee hearings—that this was not an ad hoc decision. Again, I will quote from the Royal Society’s submission: “There is a growing appreciation, at both national and international levels, of the need for greater cross disciplinary dialogue to address the global problems that now occupy much of the research agenda.”

That really comes back to what Pete Hodgson said in his contribution. In fact, his contribution chimes very well with what the Royal Society was trying to get at and why it wanted to make this landmark change. The submission goes on to say: “The enhanced Fellowship at the centre of the Royal Society mirrors the situation in many of the world’s leading academies. This strengthening of capacity will enable the Royal Society to provide a significant contribution to present and emerging debates …”. I think that sums up the reason that we are here tonight debating this bill. I say “debating”, but in many ways it is a traversing of the issues. [Interruption]

Hon David Carter: Let Parekura have a go. We need some entertainment.

DAVID SHEARER: We will wait for the Hon Parekura Horomia. I did not want to be unkind; I probably should have sat down. I feel somewhat guilty now for seeking a second call, to be honest.

To come back on track with regard to the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill, there were about 13 submissions on the bill, which we heard very carefully. A number of those submissions were made in person and were made very passionately by people who were very concerned that the object of the Royal Society was to be eroded. I just want to reflect on the contribution of Bob Brockie—

Hon Parekura Horomia: Bob who?

DAVID SHEARER: —Bob Brockie—who is a weekly columnist for the Dominion Post and is also a scientist in his own right. What I would like to do is to note what he stated quite articulately about his opposition to the inclusion of the humanities into the Royal Society bill. He said: “The Royal Society might think that it is doing God’s work by bringing the Sciences and the Arts together. I don’t think this is the role of a Scientific Society which should stick to its knitting. From my observations there is very little traffic between the worlds of Science and the Arts and that is all in one direction. Science inspires a lot of artists but Artists rarely if ever inspire Science (science fiction writers are the exception). The Arts and Humanities can have their own Academy but finance it not from the Science Budget but from the Creative Arts and Heritage budget.”

Although I think his point is probably slightly overstated compared with a lot of the other opposition that we have heard on this bill, it does reflect the feeling, quite strongly held, that the Royal Society is going backwards in terms of bringing the humanities into the Royal Society. I think that that view is mistaken. What the Royal Society said in its submission was right. In fact, the humanities are an extraordinarily important part of policy dialogue and other dialogues. As I quoted before, bringing that in is enormously important. I will just wrap up on that particular point and hand over to the Hon Parekura Horomia, and I will give him plenty of time to get to his feet.

ALLAN PEACHEY (National—Tāmaki) : In speaking to clause 4 of the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill, I will reflect for a moment on the comments of the previous speaker, David Shearer. The only bit of merit in his speech was the way he opened his second call, when he said, in effect, that he should not have taken it. It is time that this nonsense stopped. There is support across the Committee for this bill. As chairman of the Education and Science Committee that conducted the hearings into the bill, I think it is time that I at least, on behalf of members on this side of the Chamber, apologised to the Royal Society for the way in which this legislation, which is very, very important to the society, is being made into a farce by the tactics of the Opposition. The Opposition is trying to delay debate on—

Dr Rajen Prasad: I raise a point of order, Mr Chairperson. I fail to see how the member is addressing clause 4 of the bill in anything he has said so far.

The CHAIRPERSON (H V Ross Robertson): No, the member has a few minutes in which to respond to any debate from the other side. But the member should come to the point.

ALLAN PEACHEY: I am indeed trying to respond to the comments that have been made by the Opposition on clause 4. The difficulty is, of course, that there is actually very little to respond to. So on behalf of the Royal Society and members on this side of the Chamber, I appeal to the Opposition to stop this nonsense. It is costing the taxpayer $31,000 an hour—

Colin King: How much?

ALLAN PEACHEY: —$31,000 an hour—while the Opposition unnecessarily delays the progress of this bill. It is a bill on which there is agreement across the Committee. It should be passed, the Royal Society has asked for it to be passed, and it is incumbent on this House to do that in a timely and diligent manner. But all the Opposition is doing, in prolonging the debate on clause 4 and in prolonging the debate generally on this bill, is avoiding confronting the issues of the Education (Freedom of Association) Amendment Bill.

I think that approach brings no credit to that party, at all. I say to the member in the chair, Grant Robertson, who has spoken on clause 4 endlessly, that he should live up to his responsibilities as the sponsor of the bill. He has that responsibility and that honour as the member for Wellington Central, and it will not be lost on his constituents that he is disabusing that responsibility with a smirk on his face because of a greater agenda on the part of the Labour Party. That brings no credit, at all, to that member. I appeal to that member to take a moment to reflect on the comments he made on clause 4, to reflect on the strategy that is being used, and to question whether he is meeting his responsibilities to his constituents as the member sponsoring this bill.

Hon Steve Chadwick: This isn’t the classroom or the schoolhouse; this is the House of Parliament.

ALLAN PEACHEY: I heard the junior Opposition whip shouting at me a moment ago, and I say to that whip that she should exercise some responsibility, some leadership, and some control over her team. The behaviour of the Opposition in the debate on clause 4, as in other aspects of this debate, brings no credit to this Chamber and even less credit to the Labour Party.

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA (Labour—Ikaroa-Rāwhiti) : I have much pride in rising in this House of democracy and debate. I will certainly put to rest the notions of the previous speaker, Allan Peachey. I have just watched someone who has taught the ABC to pupils over generations in the classroom.

The Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill is a serious bill, as my learned friend from Wellington Central has pointed out. I wonder when I listen to the debate between those sciences that were supposed to be exact—only those sciences recognised through research, and whatever else—and the other parts, suggested as the humanities, that have been put aside. I was the most uneducated Associate Minister of Education I think this Parliament has had, because I was evicted from school at 16. But I learnt over 9 years as one of the longest-serving Associate Ministers of Education in this House that snobbery pervades in relation to unreal differentiations between the humanities and those supposed exact sciences.

You see, when Captain Cook came here, he used cartography. He would put the line up, take the angle, and strike it. He used researched science and proof. Yet he did not know where he was going—he did not know where he was going. Generations before that, my ancestors used their wills, their hearts, and the stars, and they got there—

Hon Pete Hodgson: And back again.

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA: They came back again. I think there has been a fair bit of snobbery by people like the last speaker, who have tried to be harbingers of the isolation of those sciences. I think this is a wonderful and great opportunity for merging and recognising things.

When I went to school we learnt about Shakespeare. There would be talk about a pound of flesh, or there would be talk about Puck coming down at midnight, or some damn thing. I was always astounded, as a Māori living in a Māori environment, and wondered why the hell we were thrust into that long-breadth learning about somebody who lived in Italy, wrote stanzas, and whatever else. We had great stories of our own. I believe marginalisation, in the sense of educational progress, came out of that isolation. What has survived? What has survived? Italian songs and stanzas that are 4,000 years old—the arts!

We can go into the Louvre and look around at things. Gee, it is bigger than the Tolaga Bay community hall. We can look at things and stand in front of the Mona Lisa. The eyes follow us, and we think it is great stuff. But it is not science; it is the humanities. Yet all those mugs go over there and pay money to go and look at it.

In 1867 the Royal Society was started—a long time ago. That is why, with respect, we need to give this debate time. In 1856 the Māori chiefs went to Pūkawa, and they decided they would mirror those great scientists. They would get together collectively and form things not too dissimilar to the King, and everything would be put right. But what they missed, and what the old professors—Mr Hodgson’s ancestors—kept in their hip pockets was this great thing about science.

At the end of the day I commend the member in the chair, Grant Robertson, for his effort in putting forward this bill, and in getting general agreement for it across the Committee. It is like the great statement about the person who is about to inflict on us a lot of doom and gloom, in about 2 hours and 54 minutes of sitting time—that is, Budget day. I am not too sure what size they have used, but there are “nice-to-haves”, like psychology, English literature, arts, and Māori. Who were the fools in the past who believed that those subjects do not have a place in any society, especially the Royal Society, which was mirrored by our people?

It is like wine tasting: there is a science of fermentation, and there is an exactness about prolonging tasting over a period of time, so one quits the taste of the wine. But then people get pissed if they drink too much of it. Or they get a bit wayward. I am sorry, Mr Chair—they get a bit wayward if they drink too much. Which is the right science? The one that is researched and states that we should ferment the grapes, plant them, try to avoid botrytis, and keep the sugar levels up, or the one that makes us happy? Which is right?

I say to Mr Peachey that there is a very, very serious case for merging these great studies and sciences. We have to capture our young people’s mindsets in relation to how they should be part of the Royal Society. They should be more than inspired; they should actually understand.

Imagine a young Māori sitting in Mr Brown’s biology class in Tolaga Bay. There are two things they like: the Bunsen burner, because they can burn something, and the subject of unicellular animals, such as paramecium and amoeba. They are fascinated because this is unlearned stuff. It is outside the sciences. They are attracted because they learn that those organisms breed, and that their sexuality is carried out through the central cells splitting. The cell is surrounded by cilia, which means fine hairs. Then that person understands that there are two little creatures, unicellular things, flying around in the air somewhere. That person starts to sit down with their mates and wonder about how they might trap some.

Hon Paula Bennett: Wouldn’t this be more interesting if you said it in Māori?

Hon Simon Power: In te reo.

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA: Well, that is another point. The Māori language is not recognised. Shame on you—

The CHAIRPERSON (H V Ross Robertson): Not shame on me.

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA: Shame on the people who kicked it out. It is like being in Italy, and their language being put under the mat. It is like being in Greece, and their language being put under the mat. Why are these humanities not important? There is only one place where the Māori language is really important, and it is here. Why? Because the Māoris are not going away. Captain Cook’s progeny and all those people who came from Australia, such as my ancestors the Rutherfords, have a lot to answer for at the end of the day.

What we are doing here, in fine community spirit, is ensuring that these two great functions are merged, and that neither is greater than the other. It is like the tax take at the moment. What is it about giving tax favours to the more well-off when the people at the bottom suffer? It is like not recognising the humanities. If we want to disconnect from the humanities, let me tell the Scots to get rid of their bagpipes and take those skirts off, or tell the Irish to stop drinking green beer. Yes, that is right. It is like telling the English to stop going to big weddings—all of those things. Stop doing that sort of stuff.

This has been a whole lot of nonsense. I am really, really proud of my colleague for bringing this bill together, for making history, and for ensuring that simple people like me who were evicted from school at 16 will have a chance, or my grandchildren will have a chance, to learn and to become great scholars, and will not have to worry whether they are in the humanities or the true sciences.

If we are to say that everything has to be stood up and the impact and outcomes of years of research and information measured, then I say stop feeling good, stop believing we can do things, and stop dreaming. What is that? Is it the humanities or the sciences?

David Carter is the Minister of Agriculture. We have this great science that says that if we put so many tonnes of urea on the paddock, the grass will grow. We have this science that says if we pour copper on cattle, they will survive better and get heavier. But at the same time, the cows piss in the paddock, it leaches into the river and turns our rivers into dirty, uninhabitable environments—and that is through science. Only people’s feeling that we must keep things green and clean will correct that issue, because nobody puts effort into the science needed to correct it. So I am a strong supporter of the humanities.

Jo Goodhew: Did the member get the wrong notes?

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA: I am not worried, unlike those people who have no culture, who bastardise everybody else’s dreams and joys, and who sit on the other side of the Chamber.

Hon Simon Power: Steady, steady.

Hon PAREKURA HOROMIA: That member is skiving off. He is not a bad bloke, though. Simon Power has been a great man. He has run the justice portfolio with an iron hand. He has used science, he has measured everything, and he has ensured that the countables are counted, but he has used his heart. He has a heart, this Simon Power. I am trying to get him in trouble with his leader. He has a good heart, and that is the truth. That is why Paula Bennett is sitting next to him. She is trying to get from him the ways to feel about the heart.

IAIN LEES-GALLOWAY (Labour—Palmerston North) : It is not easy to follow a great orator like the Hon Parekura Horomia, but I will give it my very best shot. I have to say that Mr Horomia showed exactly what this Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill is all about, which is the bringing together of science and the humanities, through the poetry with which he described some of the environmental challenges that we face through the degradation of our rivers in this country. Of course there is a lot of science involved in monitoring our rivers, for the nitrate levels and the other things that we might find in them, but could we have heard them more poetically described than we did when the Hon Parekura Horomia spoke this evening?

I fully support what other members have been saying tonight. I support this bill. As someone who has taken papers in such diverse subjects as biochemistry, the history of New Zealand politics, inorganic chemistry, and the English language, I have great interest in this coming together of the humanities and science. Although there seems to be—and Mr Peachey pointed this out—a pretty uniform agreement around the Chamber on this bill, I am not sure that the same can be said of the members of the Royal Society itself, and of some of the practitioners of science out there in New Zealand. That was borne out, I think, in some of the submissions at the Education and Science Committee. I wonder whether the member in charge of the bill might be able to address some of those issues.

When I first looked at this bill I reflected on a number of the meetings I have had with Royal Society members in Palmerston North, many of whom are involved with Massey University. On a number of occasions I have heard those members lament the fact that, in their opinion, not enough young people in New Zealand these days are engaging with the sciences or feel so connected to the sciences through their time at secondary school that they wish to continue with a career of studying science and technology at university, and maybe go on to undertake some research in science and technology. In fact, they lament the fact that too many young people are perhaps taking off to the humanities and studying subjects such as English, French, geography, and subjects like that. I wonder just how much support there is amongst the rank and file members of the Royal Society for this bill. My concern about that, I think, is borne out in some of the submissions that the select committee heard.

I have to admit that I come to this debate rather belatedly. This is the first opportunity I have had to participate in the debate, but while looking at some of the submissions I noted that some submitters said the exact definition of “humanities” was difficult to provide, and that “humanities” was a vague term. I know we will come to that matter later in the debate on clause 5, where there is a definition of the humanities, but it seems rather limited, and I wonder whether it is too limited. I think some of the submitters are right, as the humanities are difficult to define, and I wonder whether the member in the chair has some reflections on that. There was even a submission that said there was no need to change the current Act, because the Royal Society of New Zealand Act could be interpreted to include humanities fellows. Obviously there is some concern out there amongst the science fraternity that this bill, in some ways, waters down what they do.

Although the Royal Society proposed this bill and asked the member Grant Robertson to bring it to Parliament, I wonder about the process that it used to engage with its members. I am sure other members who were on the select committee might be able to discuss that issue. This matter is central to the purpose of the bill. Clause 4(a) states that the purpose of the bill is to “incorporate humanities into the object and functions of the Royal Society of New Zealand:”. David Shearer reflected on the submission of Dr Bob Brockie, who made comments that science and the humanities were incompatible.

CAROL BEAUMONT (Labour) : It is a great pleasure to rise and speak to the Royal Society of New Zealand Amendment Bill and to acknowledge my colleague Grant Robertson, who I believe has taken this matter very seriously in bringing forward this bill. I acknowledge his work on it and his representation of this organisation, which is based in the electorate that he so ably represents, Wellington Central.

As others have said, the purpose of this bill—I thought I would remind people—is to amend the Royal Society of New Zealand Act 1997 to “(a) incorporate humanities into the object and functions of the Royal Society of New Zealand: (b) rename the Academy Council: (c) amend the standard for the election of Companions of the Society: (d) amend the election process for Councillors of the Society.” I will talk about one of those purposes in this call, although later on I will come back to others.

Incorporating the humanities into the object and functions of the society is the core part of this bill, and I think it is very, very important that it is done. It makes a great deal of sense. Others have talked about their areas of studies. I did a degree in social sciences. Arguably, it would already fall under the authority of the Royal Society of New Zealand. Social sciences would be seen as science for the purpose of currently belonging to the Royal Society.

However, when we look at a subject or a series of subjects, we can see that a number of them are made up of different components. Take the subject of geography, which is the subject I majored in. Human geography is certainly a social science. Physical geography is, clearly, a science. And there is something in between all of that when we look at people’s relationship with the land and how they organise and place themselves on the land, which is what the study of geography is really about. That can be conveyed through poems, music, art, and a whole range of mechanisms that one might call the humanities. So, in fact, many subjects are effectively interdisciplinary in nature. It seems to me that one of the advantages of this change is to recognise that many, many subjects touch across a whole range of areas, and that makes that traditional division no longer useful. Take environmental studies; again, we can see it has physical, social, and humanities elements in it.

I want to give a particular example, because it is one that I have recently been looking at. It is the issue of violence against women. That issue can be studied at a university, for example, as part of women’s studies, sociology, or history, which is one of the humanities. The reality is that it is interdisciplinary. The fact of the matter is that creating this new object and function of the Royal Society is entirely appropriate.

I want to illustrate that a little bit more. At a conference I was at over the weekend we were talking about the issue of violence against women. As many in this Chamber know, it is a very serious, troubling, and pervasive problem. We can look at the problem from the point of view of the history of it. What is it that society has experienced over generations in relation to violence against women, and where has it come from? What has been the underlying reason for the sexual and other forms of physical violence that women face? What has created that? Is it an issue of power, perhaps? Is it because of the view that men own women and can treat them in any way that they want? Certainly, if we look at the relationship between men and women in a historical context, we might come to that conclusion.

At the conference I was at in Australia over the weekend, we were shown a T-shirt. It was illustrative of what one might loosely call art—one of the humanities that under this provision is now being incorporated into the objects of the Royal Society. This T-shirt had a very ugly drawing of a woman who was bound and bleeding, and the words that went with that were “Relax, it’s just sex”. That is a deeply troubling image. I think that image is quite powerful; the words are powerful and the image is powerful. My point is that up to that point in the conversation at the conference we had been talking about numbers, about the number of women who have violence inflicted upon them. We had been talking about politics and what the political response was.

While I am talking about that, I note that $86 million of new money has gone into the issue of violence against women and girls in Australia—A$86 million of new money—and there is a 12-year plan to tackle the issue. Sitting there, I could only compare that with what is going on in New Zealand, where we are cutting funding in the area of violence against women, where refuges have had their funding cut by $700,000, and where Te Rito and child advocates have had their funding cut.

I come back to my point, which is that we were having a political discussion on the issue of violence against women and children. At that point, the T-shirt was shown.

Clause 5 Interpretation

GRANT ROBERTSON (Labour—Wellington Central) : Clause 5 is the interpretation clause. It is where a number of quite significant changes are being made. As members know, Te Ururoa Flavell has an amendment to clause 5(2), which we will come to in time. I note, before we do, that his amendment has been consulted on across the House and there is agreement to it. The Royal Society itself is comfortable with the change and supports it going through. So I hope that all parties in the Chamber will be able to find their way to support it. It will be helpful to have an explanation of the process and how we arrived at it.

Before we come to that, though, there is clause 5(1), which amends the Act by “repealing the definitions of ‘Academy bylaws’ and ‘Academy Council’ ” and substituting the definitions included there. The important point, as was raised earlier, was the confusion that could occur by having an Academy Council and a separate council of the Royal Society. The Academy Council is essentially the science scholarship committee of the Royal Society, and the council is the governance body. But by having one called the Academy Council and the other called the council, there was scope for some confusion. Therefore, the decision was taken to rename the Academy Council as the Academy Executive Committee.

Some might say that is a minor point, but it is important. Those two bodies represent the different strands of work of the Royal Society. The council has the overall governance role to ensure that the Royal Society is performing the functions outlined in clause 6, which we will come to forthwith. It is about fostering the culture of science and presenting arguments to the Government. There needs to be a body that can govern that work and provide the overall stewardship and leadership. That body does not necessarily have to include just scientists, and it does not. Currently it includes someone like Phil O’Reilly from Business New Zealand. He brings an outside perspective. He would not consider himself to be a scientist, I think, by any means, but he has a valid perspective that is worthy of being included on that council. The Academy Executive Committee, on the other hand, is the harder science end of the operation. It is the part of the organisation that decides who wins fellowships and scholarships. It is at the hard edge of deciding what work the Royal Society will undertake within its scientific fields. That is what clause 5(1) focuses on.

Clause 5(2), as I mentioned, defines “humanities”. This has gone backwards and forwards a little bit. The original definition that came before the House included English and other languages, and American studies at the end of it. That is a slight hotchpotch of subject areas. It was largely defined by subject matter that the Council for the Humanities tended to note when it described itself. I think the Royal Society made clear from the very beginning of this process that it was relaxed about that definition. It wanted a definition that was inclusive but that highlighted areas that people thought were important. The Education and Science Committee withdrew “English and other languages”—perhaps a member of the select committee might want to discuss that; I was not on the committee myself—“and American studies.” Te Ururoa Flavell has introduced an amendment that will include te reo, specifically, and also Māori studies.

As I noted when I read out the memorandum of understanding, I think that that falls in line with what the two organisations, the Council for the Humanities and the Royal Society, were trying to achieve in bringing themselves together. Right at the start of the memorandum of understanding, the third point refers to the importance and value of Te Whanga Māori. It was recognised right off the bat that that was part of the bringing together of those two organisations. I actually think Mr Flavell’s amendment picks up that spirit; it includes te reo and Māori studies. There could have been an argument to re-include other things, but the Royal Society is happy with where it has ended up. As I have stated before, I am here on behalf of the Royal Society sponsoring this bill, and it is comfortable with the amendment that is on the Table in respect of clause 5(2), but it would be useful to hear from someone on the select committee at some point about why the change was made to withdraw English and American studies originally. But I do not think that we should concern ourselves that the amendment is in any way hostile; it is in fact very supportive of the original agreement of the Council for the Humanities and the Royal Society to bring this bill to the House.

I think clause 5 is an important one because what it does is define two specific changes that have been made: the change to the structural part of the organisation and also the change to the definition around the bringing in of humanities. Just to clarify once and for all, I say that the social sciences are already there, so my own subject of political studies is covered already, and humanities is defined as it is here in the bill and in the amendment from Mr Flavell.
