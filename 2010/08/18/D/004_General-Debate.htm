General Debate

Hon BILL ENGLISH (Deputy Prime Minister) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. So the party that is mired in splits and divisions calls a snap debate on splits and divisions. Now, that is a great strategy, in the same way as the party that is responsible for all the damage to the New Zealand economy is trying to front-end the economic policy as its strongest point! It is a bit like Trevor Mallard saying that other people are unbalanced. That is the strategy of a party that is still struggling, and understandably so, with the challenges of being in Opposition. The one hurdle that members opposite must get over—there are a lot of hurdles in Opposition, and we know a bit about that—is to admit the voters were right.

Hon Members: Ah!

Hon BILL ENGLISH: They have to admit the voters were right. How close do we think the Labour Party is to admitting that the voters of west Auckland were right to throw Labour out? It is no closer than it was a week before the last election—no closer. With all the other machinations—all the little tactics, all the very odd strategies such as we have seen today—that is the one hurdle members opposite are nowhere near getting over. I think that will happen in about year 4 or year 5, probably, which is a long way to go.

The interesting thing is that the voters know when a party has got over that hurdle. It does not matter how clever a party is, or whether it is the most cohesive and smartest political machine that was ever invented—and still run from New York by the sound of it—the voters know that the Labour Party still thinks the voters were wrong. Guess what? The voters think they are right, I have found. The voters have not changed their mind. Actually they think they are in charge. I know the Labour Party does not understand that, yet. It still thinks that Darren Hughes is in charge. It still thinks that Phil Goff is in charge. Actually, Chris Carter is in charge. He is the one who is really telling the voters what the voters believe—that Labour cannot win, there are splits and divisions, and they cannot make up their mind about their policy.

The Government, fortunately, has the opportunity—given to us by those same voters—to grasp the challenges, and particularly the long-term challenges for the economy. Those are considerable. They have always been a bit of a challenge in this small, open economy at the end of the world but they have been made much larger, much more challenging by the actions of the last Government, particularly since 2005, where its policies effectively wrecked the tradable sector of a trading nation. If a Government is going to damage a trading economy, it could not have done more than the last Government did. There has been a 5-year recession in our export sector, in the best economic conditions—that decade was called the “great moderation”—where other countries grew very strongly. Just by way of example, in the 4 years to March 2010 the Australian economy grew 11.6 percent; we grew 2 percent. Then we found out in recent days that if one takes the statistics that Governments use to calculate national superannuation—that is, the average wage series that is used to calculate national superannuation—and one adjusts it, takes the after-tax numbers, which the Government takes when it is adjusting national superannuation, which is the after-tax average wage, and adjusts it for inflation, guess how much New Zealand incomes grew on that series—which is used for national superannuation, so it is not one randomly selected? For 9 years it grew by 0.3 percent. [Interruption] It grew by 0.3 percent. I thought that meant 0.3 percent a year, but it does not. No, most of that growth happened while Labour was still enjoying the benefits of the previous National Government’s policies. From 2004 on it was almost zero. I think 0.3 percent was the actual result. From 2005 on, real after-tax incomes, as measured by Statistics New Zealand with the tool it uses for calculating national superannuation, had almost zero growth.

Hon DAVID CUNLIFFE (Labour—New Lynn) : The previous speaker, the Hon Bill English, sat down in a hurry, and well he might. On the subject of splits and divisions, let us call to mind the record of John Key and Bill English. Let us think about policies that matter to real New Zealanders. Here is the top of the list. Bill English said that the Overseas Investment Office should be open slather, that we should bring in the buyers for Crafar farms and let Sammy Wong and the others have their way with it. Guess what? John Key said no, we would be tenants in our own country. He said that Mr English could not do that.

Let us talk about KiwiSaver, shall we? Bill English said that KiwiSaver was a rotten system that was not working. Bill English has been doing some polling and he now says it is time we had a policy. After 2 years the Government has set up a committee, but Government members cannot agree amongst themselves. On tax policy, John Key said we should make tax better and we would not increase GST. Bill English said that we would—we are putting up GST on 1 October. Every New Zealander will suffer.

In his speech Bill English said that the voters were right. I do not know about that, but I know that voters gave National a chance. Kiwis are fair-minded people. They said that Labour had had three terms and they should give National a chance to see what it could do. Do members know what the voters have found out? National promised them we would catch up with Australia. We went backwards by 50 bucks a week for the average worker, and we are falling further behind. National said it would stop the brain drain across the Tasman. What have we got? The June-on-June number—the number of Kiwis leaving—has doubled from a year ago.

National said it had a plan—wrong. A plan is not a goal. A goal is not a strategy. A plan has a map: we know where to start, we know where we want to get to, we work out the milestones, and we have a compass to get us along the way. Is that what this National Government has given New Zealand? It does not have a plan. It does not have any more than a series of slogans, and it is running from poll-driven fruitcakes to flip-flops—flip-flops.

In the last 3 months 19,000 more New Zealanders have lost their jobs and the number of unemployed has risen to 159,000. I want to talk about just two of those New Zealanders, both from my electorate, whom I have seen recently. “Mrs U” worked for 14 years in a rest home in Epsom—14 years. She was sacked without notice. She was rung up and told not to come to work, because her employer had ended up hiring a younger, newer person on a lower wage rate. “Mrs U” got a reference after 14 years that had four lines in it—after 14 years of hard work. That is one example.

I will give members another example. “Mr E” lives in Kelston. He is a Polynesian worker at a factory in Penrose. He is an honest man. He has four children and a wife. He lost his job in the recession because this Government did not have a plan for jobs. Now he cannot afford a State house, and he and his family have moved in with his brother. There are eight kids and four adults in one 2½-bedroom house. That Government has no plan. There are 19,000 more New Zealanders like those 12. “Mrs U” was sacked at will because this Government’s latest fad is firing workers and cranking down employment conditions.

The list of National’s reversals and short-term flip-flops has got so long that it is a joke. Those members called it a rolling maul, but it looks to me like a ruck up. Catching up with Australia? The 2025 Taskforce was dead on arrival. The Job Summit and the national cycleway were both scaled back. The 9-day working fortnight was cancelled in the middle of a recession. Budget 2009 deferred pre-funding superannuation and lied to New Zealanders about it. It created a $50 billion fiscal hole. It reduced stimulus and it raised unemployment. Budget 2010 broke a promise by raising the rate of GST. Most New Zealanders will be worse off next year when they face 5.9 percent inflation.

Who knows where the review of the Overseas Investment Office will come out? Bill English wants to liberalise it and John Key wants to tighten it. What about the latest random walks on their map: mining national parks, firing at will, so-called compulsory superannuation, and—here is the newest stuff—savings?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: Labour members knew what they needed to do. He knew that in 1998. Those members knew from research that was undertaken, like the Iterative Best Evidence Synthesis and Strengthening Education in Mangere and Otara, what needed to be done to lift the numeracy and literacy skills of young children in our schools. But what did those members do? They ignored that, and let those kids fail. For 10 years they stood by and watched as the unions stopped them—the unions stopped them—from implementing what Trevor Mallard knew had to be done: setting benchmarks so that we clearly understand what children need to know and by when.

So what was the first thing that this National Government did? It introduced the national standards that Trevor Mallard talked about. “So last century.”, the member said. What about the kids who are suffering in this century because they did not get that help from the so-last-century Labour Government? Well, this Government has acted, and, quite frankly, the previous Labour Government should hang its head in shame for failing up to one in five New Zealand children in our schools—up to one in five. That is 150,000-odd kids. How many of them have been cast out on to the streets in this recession not only with no skills or qualifications but not able to read, write, or do maths at anywhere near the level they need to get an apprenticeship or even a semi-skilled job? When we talk to some of the employers they shake their heads. They ask how a compulsory education system could allow kids to go through school for 8 or 9 years and come out unable to write a sentence or fill in a simple employment form. They ask how that could happen. The previous Government knew what to do. There was good research put out on the Progress in International Reading Literacy Study of 2001.

National members’ latest acknowledgment is that they have been wrong about savings. We have news for National members: we have been saying that for years. That is why we developed the KiwiSaver plan, that is why we started contributions to the Cullen fund, and that is why, since the election, we have been saying National has got it wrong. The big difference between New Zealand and Australia is in the level of savings, and National members have just agreed with us about that. They have just agreed with us, but will they deliver in that area? No, they still have no vision; all that they are planning is to establish a committee. After two Budgets, 2 years into this National Government, its big announcement and its big plan last week, when it was on the ropes in respect of the gap with Australia widening instead of narrowing, was to establish a committee. The Government does not even have members for a committee and it has no terms of reference for it yet, but its plan is to establish a committee.

It continues to be amateur hour on the National Government benches, at a time when the economy is tanking. We have more and more people going overseas—

Hon DAVID PARKER: Steven Joyce laughs, but Bernard Hickey says “We are losing a whole generation of workers before our eyes”. I say to Mr Joyce that if he wanted to do something about that, he could have done something at the Hillside railway workshops. He had the justification for doing so in an independent report from Business and Economic Research Ltd, and he has ignored it. As a consequence there will be 300 fewer jobs in Dunedin.

This country was in recession before the global financial crisis. It also had a pathetically poor level of increase in after-tax wages. We also inherited a projected decade of deficits, because when the recession arrived and the tide went out the high levels of Government expenditure were projecting a decade of deficits. We are lowering company taxes down to a rate of 28 cents in the dollar, which will give us a lift over the Australians. There is no plan. There is no way to join any of the critical elements into a plan that could develop this country, provide jobs, and move the economy forward. GRANT ROBERTSON (Labour—Wellington Central) : Last week the Labour Party held its caucus in Whangarei. It is a beautiful city. Dr KENNEDY GRAHAM (Green) : I rise to challenge the Government to maintain the dignity and honour of the Crown in respect of the actions of the SAS in Afghanistan. Reports that the SAS may be witnessing the handing over of detainees to the Afghan National Directorate of Security raised serious concerns as to whether New Zealand is violating its obligations in international law.

This matter goes beyond purely State responsibility. If those legal obligations are violated, and if they have been translated into New Zealand domestic law—and they have—our individual soldiers may be guilty of criminal misconduct in our courts as a result of their action in Afghanistan. Because of our adherence to the International Criminal Court statute and the Geneva conventions, New Zealand nationals are subject to criminal liability for any acts of genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and inhumane treatment of prisoners. As a party to the Geneva conventions—in particular, to Common Article 3—New Zealanders must apply humane treatment to all persons in enemy hands. We are specifically prohibited from murder, mutilation, torture, and cruel, humiliating, or degrading treatment. There may be prima facie evidence that our SAS soldiers are violating these obligations, and they may be individually accountable in New Zealand criminal law.

This is not the first time such suspicions have been raised. In 2002 our SAS troops were involved in actions where over 50 individuals were detained in order to capture Taliban and al-Qaeda suspects believed to be among them. The previous Government addressed these concerns in 2007. According to the then foreign Minister, Phil Goff, “The NZDF understands that no person who has been held, even temporarily, in New Zealand custody is currently in the hands of the United States or other nations represented in the international forces.” That statement is characteristic of Government statements on such morally and legally sensitive issues. Its significance lies more in what it does not say than in what it does say. It did not identify the dates on which such detentions occurred, and it did not discount the possibility of coalition forces having held such detainees at any time before February 2007, or provide information on the manner of treatment during any such detention.

The Minister also said: “The Chief of Defence has put in place procedures for the notification of the International Committee of the Red Cross of the fact that persons have been captured and/or transferred to another power … New Zealand has, from an early stage, made clear our expectation that all detainees would be treated humanely and in accordance with international law.” That statement is almost meaningless. Conveying an expectation does not constitute a verifiable assurance that an illegal action has not occurred or may not occur in the future. Statements of expectation of another party’s behaviour do not absolve one of criminal responsibility.

Three years later this National Government is also dissembling. Yesterday the Minister of Defence acknowledged that the SAS worked with the Afghan Crisis Response Unit to capture insurgents, and that it was likely they were being transferred to the National Directorate of Security. At question time yesterday the Minister of Foreign Affairs assured this country that “appropriate steps” will be taken, and “relevant inquiries” will be made in respect of SAS operations in which detainees have been handed to the National Directorate of Security. Yet, true to form, the Prime Minister blandly assured the public not to worry. The SAS, he said yesterday, was not involved in the torture of prisoners, and where the SAS worked alongside the Afghan unit, it was not the detaining force. In that instance, he said, it was not our responsibility when it came to those people who were detained.

I tell him that that is completely wrong. It is astonishing that the Prime Minister has such an erroneous knowledge of the international obligations of the country he purports to lead. I say to the Prime Minister that if our troops jointly capture people, we are not absolved of responsibility for their humane treatment simply because our soldiers do not lay hands directly upon them. The Prime Minister has the obligation under the Geneva conventions to ensure that those who do the act of detaining do not torture. An international act can consist of actions, but also of omission—the recognised doctrine of positive obligation. If we do not take steps to ensure that, we can be criminally liable in international law, and in New Zealand domestic law. We have not done those things, and the Prime Minister has the temerity to say that we are not responsible.

I call on the Prime Minister to get his knowledge of the law right, and on his Government to immediately suspend operations with any military units in Afghanistan that hand over detainees to the National Directorate of Security.

DAVID GARRETT (ACT) : I wish to speak today about the detail of the new foreshore and seabed legislation—at least, I would if I could. Despite the Government’s intention—

Hon Tau Henare: Have you seen it?

DAVID GARRETT: No, I have not seen it, I tell Mr Henare. Despite the Government’s intention to pass the legislation by Christmas, as of today there is no draft bill in the public arena that I can speak on. It is, however, likely to be a law that will give billions of dollars worth of minerals to a few select iwi. It is a bill that could quite likely limit free public access to beaches. I note that despite many invitations to do so, neither the Prime Minister nor the Attorney-General will confirm in this House the fact that the legislation will forbid charging for access to a beach. This bill will be upon us just in time for summer, if National and the Māori Party have their way.

It is incorrect to claim, as the Government does, that the bill will restore the right to go to court to claim customary rights. Such rights were preserved in the Foreshore and Seabed Act 2004. It is more correct to say that the proposed bill allows claims to the courts for greater ownership rights than were available under the 2004 legislation. The bill will also say, however, that iwi will be able to negotiate customary title with the Government. That is why many, many New Zealanders are gravely concerned. Just today the Coastal Coalition, representing over 8,000 Kiwis, has started a billboard campaign highlighting its real fears of having a New Zealand where people are excluded from the foreshore because they are not Māori or, for that matter, they are not from the right iwi.

Looking at this Government’s record of giving away rights to Māori covertly, it is no wonder that people are frightened. There have been veto rights over the Waikato River, and we had a Minister sneak off to New York to sign a taxpayer-funded separate Māori governance that even Miss Clark’s Government would not sign. The ACT Party shares those people’s concerns. Once again, one group of New Zealanders is getting special rights over the rest. This is not what the Treaty of Waitangi intended, as can be seen by anyone reading it without a vested interest in distorting its meaning. In fact, it is not even what the decision in Attorney-General v Ngāti Apa, the case that started all this, said. Herein lies the problem with letting Governments do a job that is properly the role of the judiciary. Despite the claims of many, including my colleagues in the Māori Party, the Ngāti Apa verdict did not give Māori the foreshore. It merely gave the iwi the right to have their case heard in the Māori Land Court.

The judges stated in that case that the chances were slim. They stated: “any customary property in the areas vested seem unlikely to survive.” That is a direct quote from the Ngāti Apa decision. We can contrast that with what the Attorney-General, Chris Finlayson, has been saying. He said that 10 percent of the foreshore and seabed is likely to end up in exclusively Māori hands—2,000 kilometres of the coastline. He even touts the direct negotiation route as the better option, and why would iwi not go for the direct option? Mr Finlayson says that it will “negotiate a settlement outside the courts to eliminate excessive cost or uncertainty”. Well, if I was an iwi leader who wanted to get my hands on a whole lot of foreshore and minerals, I would be a moron to go through the courts when I can negotiate with such an accommodating Minister.

If Mr Finlayson concludes that giving large tracts of Ninety Mile Beach to the five iwi that make up the Te Hiku Forum will keep the Māori Party onside, then no legal precedent will stop him from doing so. If the Prime Minister decides that it will be less strife to give Te Ātiawa the 20 kilometres of foreshore and seabed in Taranaki that they are after, then what the courts say is immaterial. If they succeed, who would blame the other six iwi with coastal boundaries in the region from having a go themselves? Successive Governments, both National and Labour, have shown that political expediency is far more important than doing what is right. Labour did this when it overreacted in 2004 to the Ngāti Apa decision, and National is doing it again now. The only place to determine those property rights is the High Court.
