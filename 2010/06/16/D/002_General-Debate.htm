Hon NANAIA MAHUTA (Labour—Hauraki-Waikato) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. Seventy-three percent of people in my electorate earn $40,000 or less. The tax cuts announced in National’s Budget 2010 will mean very little—in fact, nothing at all—to the great majority of workers in my electorate and many other Māori electorates throughout the country. Not one constituent has expressed optimism about the impacts of tax cuts in places like Te Kūiti, Tuakau, Pukekohe, Tokoroa, Tairua, or Thames—not one. They know that Budget 2010 will not deliver to them. They know that the sharp edges of the recession may have been softened for the small percentage of Kiwis, but not for the large majority. Those same tax cuts are exacerbated by rising inflation, making the cost of living pressures a real issue for far too many families in my electorate.

National has promoted this Budget as positive for growth and jobs. Meanwhile, 60,000 young people are unemployed. Job Ops has been promoted as an ambitious solution to solve that problem, but it is a short-term fix to a long-term challenge. There is nothing in the Budget to increase the uptake of Modern Apprenticeships placements. I was in Raglan last week and heard a story of a young man who was keen to take up an apprenticeship. He did OK at school, but his own teachers, by their own admission, would say that he was better learning with his hands. He wanted to do an apprenticeship and take up a trade. He was no slouch, but he was not able to take up a Modern Apprenticeship placement. There is nothing in this Budget to encourage many more young people like him throughout the country who want to earn while they learn. There was no opportunity for him to take up a Modern Apprenticeships placement. The Government has not recognised that there are heaps and heaps of young people throughout the country who want to work, but 60,000 of them are currently out of work. There is nothing in the Budget to address that particular challenge. National is throwing a lifeline of a 6-month training opportunity to them.

Hon Darren Hughes: What’s that going to do?

Hon NANAIA MAHUTA: Very little. Young people do not want a training opportunity; they want to work. They want a job, and to earn while they learn. There is no vision and no ambition for young people in National’s Budget 2010.

Families are bracing themselves for the impact of GST increases. It will hurt. There will be increased costs to the weekly kai bill. Take, for example, a weekly kai bill of $261. Add to that 15 percent, which means an extra $39.15 per week on that whānau’s kai bill. It will hurt. There will be increased costs for mortgage or rental payments, increased costs for school uniforms for the kids, increased costs for household items, and for rates. Let us look at rates. Take an annual bill of $2006.23, and add to that 15 percent. That will cost $300.93 extra a year.

David Garrett: It must be a nice house.

Hon NANAIA MAHUTA: I heard a comment over there that it must be a nice house. In fact, rates in Kāwhia are more than that, and those are humble baches, so let us be very clear that the member for ACT has no idea what everyday families are paying in rates. He should be ashamed. Far too many—[Interruption]. What did he say?

David Garrett: I know Kāwhia very well.

Hon NANAIA MAHUTA: He knows Kāwhia very well. He obviously does not know its rates bills very well at all. He should be ashamed that he is supporting a 15 percent increase to rates bills, which will add around about $300 a year to them. Pensioners in Kāwhia will not be getting any more out of the Rates Rebate Scheme, because that has gone under National’s Budget 2010. GST hikes will hurt, no matter how National slices and dices its message. They will hurt many households, many Māori households, and many whānau, and I know that Hone Harawira does not like that particular decision.

These are the types of changes that Māori families and individuals will feel, because they will hurt them in the pocket. Five members of the Māori Party voted for these changes on the basis that they have made far too many gains to turn back now. Well, let us look at the checklist. First there was flying a flag on one day of the year. Yes, they delivered that—woo hoo! That is done, but it means very little. The repeal of the Foreshore and Seabed Act is done, but the solution is not what was promoted out in the communities.

RAHUI KATENE (Māori Party—Te Tai Tonga) : The headline that greeted us yesterday, “Peace deal on beaches”, was worlds apart from a tumultuous period of our history. It is only right that as MP for Te Tai Tonga I mihi to the eight iwi of Te Tau Ihu who first sought declaration from the Maori Land Court that the foreshore and seabed within the Marlborough Sounds are Māori customary land. They had the precedent of the mid-1860s, when the Native Land Court accepted applications to determine title of customary land—land held by Māori in terms of tikanga Māori. In 1997 Judge Hingston ruled that the Maori Land Court had jurisdiction to consider whether foreshore and seabed are Māori customary land. His finding was appealed to the Māori Appellate Court, to the High Court, and then to the Court of Appeal. Exactly 7 years ago, on 19 June 2003, the Court of Appeal unanimously held that the Maori Land Court could determine the status of the foreshore and seabed under Te Ture Whenua Maori Act 1993.

The reaction of the then Prime Minister and the then Attorney-General was rapid beyond belief. The day after the Court of Appeal decision, Helen Clark informed the country that steps would “be taken to confirm absolute Crown title over the foreshore …”. She was instantly backed up by Margaret Wilson, who claimed that “Ownership of the foreshore and seabed had long been considered to lie with the Crown”, conveniently neglecting to say who, exactly, thought that way, other than themselves. From then on, peace would not prevail for 7 long years.

Within days of the Labour Government’s knee-jerk response, the movement was ready to act. Lawyer Moana Jackson spoke for us all when he said that the Government had denied Māori the right to a fair trial, and that it would create a festering sore. John Key reflected that concept just this week in addressing what he described as the weeping sore of the 2004 Foreshore and Seabed Act.

The Treaty Tribes Coalition—representing most of the North Island iwi, the Taranaki Māori Trust Board, and Ngāi Tahu—laid a complaint with the United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination, stating that the act of confiscating the foreshore extinguished Māori rights. The committee upheld the complaint, confirming that the legislation and the extinguishment were indeed racially discriminatory.

So many acts of brave opposition and courageous protest have led to this day. No one can erase the memory of 40,000 New Zealanders marching on this Parliament calling all of us to account. But there are other unique events. Hauraki kaumātua Toko Rēnata Te Taniwha returned his Queen’s Service Medal as a protest against the Government’s handling of the issue. The Rātana movement made its first ever submission to Parliament, arguing that the foreshore and seabed legislation contributed to the legislative erosion of the Treaty. Some 3,946 submissions were presented to the Fisheries and Other Sea-related Legislation Committee; 94 percent of them opposed the legislation.

Will peace be retained? The Māori Party campaigned to repeal the 2004 Act, and to restore due access to justice. On both counts, we have done well. But that does not mean the fight is over. Māori have the right to seek customary foreshore and seabed title, which would bring benefits similar to freehold title rights, including commercial development rights, and veto over other developments. Iwi and hapū are now able to claim customary title through the High Court or negotiations with the Crown. There is now an opportunity for universal recognition, mana tuku iho. That is significant. The Crown acknowledges the mana of iwi and hapū as enduring and inalienable. A line in the sand has been drawn. The 2004 Act will go down as appalling law.

Our rights to justice have been restored, but there is still much to watch over in the drafting of the legislation to ensure it expresses the Treaty relationship. We must see hapū rights protected alongside those of iwi. The legislation must set out the nature of the development, occupation, and usage rights that Māori will enjoy—the actual rights. We will be actively putting up the case for those whose relationships with the foreshore were affected by raupatu, fraudulent land transactions, or other breaches of the Treaty by the Crown to be recognised. Māori did not march just to repeal this discriminatory legislation; Māori marched for justice. We as the Māori Party will continue to do all that we can to ensure that justice is done.

CHARLES CHAUVEL (Labour) : This is my first opportunity to speak in the House since I was given the role of Labour’s spokesperson on the environment. I would like to begin my contribution by thanking and acknowledging all those members from across the House, and of course in my own party, who have been good enough to congratulate me on that new responsibility. I am looking forward to working in the portfolio.

When I spoke on the Budget in the last week Parliament sat before the adjournment, I said that it failed to mention the environment once, and that is a major omission. I also said that the Budget was a dangerous gamble, because it contains revenue assumptions that will not survive any further worsening of international economic conditions. But there is another major problem with the Budget, and with the economic policies that accompany it. Those policies mean that for fixed, low, and middle-income earners, everyday Kiwis, day-to-day living costs will rise steeply.

The National Government knows this, because it has tried to hide it by saying that no New Zealanders will be left worse off than they were before the Budget. But on this side of the House we can see through that, because once we do the maths, taking into account price rises, interest rate rises like the official cash rate rise yesterday, accident compensation levy rises, the emissions trading scheme’s effect on power and petrol price rises, and the GST increase, all at a time of nil wage growth, it is obvious that the very people who need and deserve help will not get it from this Government.

The Budget is not fair. Despite all the rhetoric, it is a Budget that simply caters to the few. It harms our many hard-working families. Let us look at where most of the money is going. Fully a third of the value of the tax cuts will go to the top 5 percent of the population in New Zealand, and fully 15 percent of the value of those tax cuts will go to the top 1 percent of income earners. How will the National Government fund that? It is doing two things, and they are both unfair and unwise.

First, it is borrowing to fund these tax cuts. There will be an extra $50 million of debt for all New Zealanders, just to give a big boost to the very best-off. That is just not sensible, and it is not fair to saddle ordinary, hard-working people with millions of dollars of debt to reward just an elite few. Borrowing to fund tax cuts is at best stupid, but when they are as inequitable as these tax cuts, it is also immoral. The Government knows this, because in the Minister’s Executive Summary in the Budget documents is found the following quote: “it would be irresponsible to saddle the next generation with further debt to pay for tax cuts now.”—even though that is exactly what the resulting legislation does.

The Budget is also unfair because the Government claims on the one hand not to leave anyone worse off, whereas in reality it has just hidden the many extra costs with which it will burden the majority of New Zealanders. The Budget will disadvantage those already struggling, and reward those who need the least help. Let us take the example of an average male worker who earns $50,000 a year. The Budget claims at face value that this worker will receive an extra $29 a week, leaving him $16 better off each week when the extra $13 a week in GST is taken into account. But in claiming that, the Government has ignored some very obvious factors—pointed out by Treasury—in order to create an illusion. The Government knows that this worker will not be better off. It knows that it is making him, and the many other workers like him, worse off.

There has been no taking into account the estimated 6 percent inflation that the country will face in the coming year—and that is not something that slips the mind too easily. If it has been omitted, it is safe to say that that omission is intentional. The reason the National Government has failed to take inflation into account in the information given to ordinary Kiwis, is that this would expose how an average worker would end up losing $19 a week, thanks to higher inflationary prices. That is just not fair. If we take into account increased accident compensation levies, costs to the worker if he has a student loan outstanding, early childhood education cost increases, higher power prices, and higher taxes on things like rent and tobacco, our average worker could, in reality, end up $30 a week worse off than he was before the Budget. And the worker will be getting worse off still, because as we saw last year we are in times of relatively low or nil real wage growth. I cannot for the life of me see what is fair or good about that.

The Budget and the propaganda surrounding it have been deceptive. I say this because the Government knows that what it is doing is wrong, and because it knows that taking from the many to give to the few cannot be morally justified. It is a Budget of broken promises, and it is one on which the Government will be judged.

Dr KENNEDY GRAHAM (Green) : I rise to draw the attention of the House to the quite extraordinary breakthrough that came out of the review conference at the International Criminal Court in Kampala last Saturday. At that conference the States parties agreed, against all expectations, to adopt aggression as a justiciable crime in international law. There are many caveats entered into the agreement and there is a considerable time delay before it becomes justiciable, but the fact that agreement has been reached on this most critical of all issues of international law is nothing short of remarkable. I believe that it will come to be seen as the most important advance in international peace and security since 1945, when the United Nations Charter was adopted.

The meeting agreed that aggression would become a crime under the Rome Statute once a decision has been made by a two-thirds majority of the 111 States parties. This decision will be made only after January 2017, but if 7 years ahead seems far away, we would do well to recall that work towards making aggression a crime has been ongoing since 1920—exactly 90 years ago. In that historical light, 7 years is a fleeting period. The definition of the crime and its associated elements have been agreed on, and the most intractable issue of all, the relationship between the court and the UN Security Council, has been sorted. In short, the council may determine an act of aggression, as it is empowered to do under the Charter, and refer such a case to the court for investigation and possible prosecution. This will apply to any country, whether or not it is a member of the court. Even in the event that the Security Council does not make such a determination, the International Criminal Court prosecutor may investigate a case of aggression, provided a pre-trial chamber gives the green light. In such a case, however, he may proceed only against those countries that are members of the court and that have accepted the jurisdiction of the court over the crime. Notwithstanding these shortcomings, the fact that agreement was reached on aggression is little short of astounding. We can now foresee the day when aggression will become a crime in international law.

This House will recall the member’s bill that I submitted last year: the International Non-Aggression and Lawful Use of Force Bill. My bill was debated in August and September and it was voted down on its first reading, although not by a large majority—only 64 to 58. It could have become law. The aim of that bill as described at the time was to put into domestic law a provision that would have had the same effect in New Zealand as the emerging international law now in the International Criminal Court will have. It would have criminalised in New Zealand law aggression by a New Zealand leader if he or she illegally committed our armed forces overseas.

The Government’s opposition to that bill rested on the complaint that it would have rendered this country’s foreign policy subject to a Security Council veto, preventing New Zealand forces from collaborating with others in a humanitarian intervention of the Kosovo type. That reasoning was simply wrong. The responsibility to protect doctrine, as was made clear by the UN Secretary-General in his seminal report of 2004, is always subject to a Security Council veto. As we predicted last September, the day has come when the New Zealand Government will be obliged to fall into line and agree with the notion of aggression becoming a crime. The fact that it is adopted by the International Criminal Court does not absolve New Zealand of responsibility to make aggression a crime in domestic law. Indeed, it will not only be obliged to implement aggression as a crime in our domestic law; it will acknowledge that our domestic courts are expected to prosecute in the first instance before such a matter is referred to the international court in the Hague. So when the time comes for the Government to implement the international agreement making aggression a crime, it will effectively be doing what it was invited to do last year and found disingenuous reasons for not proceeding. I wish the Government well in assisting the court to hasten the day when this occurs.
