Hamilton City Council (Parana Park) Land Vesting Bill

First Reading

DAVID BENNETT (National—Hamilton East) : I move, That the Hamilton City Council (Parana Park) Land Vesting Bill be now read a first time. We intend to send this bill to the great Local Government and Environment Committee to deliberate on and to hear any submissions that there may be from the public. This bill is brought to the House on behalf of the Hamilton City Council, which approached me as the local member for Hamilton. The bill is in relation to an area of land known as Parana Park. If people have been to Hamilton they will know where Parana Park is. The park is aptly named because it is right on the edge of the Waikato River and is beside Memorial Park, which is where we have our great memorials for fallen soldiers and those who have served this country. It is the centre place for our Anzac Day celebrations and commemorations. It is also a public area and space used along the riverside and is very much part of the community of Hamilton and Hamilton East, so we have great pleasure in presenting this bill to this House today in regard to Parana Park.

I will give members of this House and the public some understanding of the history around Parana Park. Parana Park was gifted to the city by George Parr, who died on 26 February 1929. By his last will he gifted the house situated at 3 River Road to Hamilton City to be used for a children’s convalescent home for patients from the Waikato Hospital. The land surrounding the house was to be used as a children’s playground. In the 1950s it became apparent that the house was most unsuited for such a use and the 1958 Hamilton City (Parana Park) Empowering Act was established to give the Hamilton City Council more effective administration and management of the Parana Park area.

It is interesting to look the obituary of Mr George Parr on 26 February 1929 from the Waikato Times. It describes him as an old and highly respected resident of Hamilton, at the age of 60 years—nowadays 60 years would not be old. He was the son of the late Mr John Parr, a former Mayor of Hamilton, and the Parr family had a grocery business on Hood Street in Hamilton. If members have been to Hamilton they will realise that Hood Street is now the nightlife capital of Hamilton, and I imagine the building with Parr Bros on is still there.

George Parr was an active person and took an active interest in public life. He was president of the Hamilton Chamber of Commerce. He actually advocated for a few issues that are not dissimilar to many issues that are being advocated in Hamilton at the moment: the connection of the Waikato River with Waiuku by means of a canal, and a train service from Kāwhia and Raglan to Hamilton. Those issues are interesting when we look at what is being advocated in the city at the moment. He was a member of the Hamilton licensing branch, and the most telling thing was that he was a very strong Liberal supporter and had a great active interest in politics. He was somebody who was known to be very successful and community-orientated and not to have any enemies in the city.

Unfortunately, Mr Parr never married and had no family that would have taken over the property in normal circumstances, so he gifted it to the city council. When it was vested in the city council in 1958, as I said before, the Hamilton City (Parana Park) Empowering Act gave Hamilton City Council the ability to effectively administer the park. I will read from a speech on that legislation by the Hon Dame Hilda Ross, who was a member for Hamilton, and a member of the National Party as well—a very fine member. She told us some history of the property. She said: “In 1929 on the death of the late George Parr, his property and his house were left to the Hamilton City Council. In his will he expressed his wish that the property be held as a children’s playground and the house as a convalescent home for children from the hospital. The house has been found most unsuitable for that purpose—”

DAVID BENNETT: It is a very short extract from Hansard from those times, as members can see. I was going to read only a small part of it to the member but I am sure he will be most diligent, and look for it and read it later when he goes back to his office. Essentially, Dame Hilda Ross echoed the thoughts we spoke of earlier about the hospital not finding the land able to be used for the intended purpose, so the council used it as a park. The council found that it could use the property only for rental and it was not used for the specific purpose for which it was intended in the original vesting with the Hamilton City Council.

She noted: “There is a paddling pool and a barbecue, which the Girl Guides use a great deal. An outdoor stage has been built so that people can sit and watch. Trees are planted so that people can look at this attractive part of the city and take advantage of it.” I was reading that out for a purpose in the sense that Hamiltonians use that part of the city for its aesthetic qualities often and many a time. It is appropriate that Hamilton City Council now be able to deliver to the residents the ability to use that land in such a way that they have better control over the property so that it can deliver exactly what the residents want and have wanted for many years in the way they use that property.

So the purpose of this bill is to assist the Hamilton City Council to provide the amenities that Hamiltonians want. Hamiltonians use that land now for those purposes, and through this bill we give the city council the full extent of the powers that they desire so that they can implement and deliver those services to the community. As Dame Hilda Ross said, it is a very pleasant site but brings in no revenue. The city council wants to run the ground and use whatever proceeds there may be as a means of maintaining it. So things have not changed terribly in the last 50 years.

In the end, we are providing this bill to the House on behalf of the city council. The land was gifted by an individual for a children’s convalescent home for patients of the hospital. The land has been used as a playground in the past. In the 1950s it became apparent it was unsuitable, so was vested in the city council through the Hamilton City (Parana Park) Empowering Act. Parana Park is currently used for recreational purposes, and the Hamilton City Council considers it is appropriate to update and modernise the legislation by which it holds Parana Park, to enable it to more effectively manage and administer the park. The repeal of the 1958 Act will allow Hamilton City Council to do just that and will enable it to carry out any plans for development of that area.

In essence what we are doing here is making the vesting of this land into the Hamilton City Council a reality so that it can use it for the most appropriate community purposes, as it is currently used by the community now, and we recommend this legislation to this House. National supports Hamilton City Council in its desire to maintain this land and Parana Park in a way they can serve the community interests. Thank you.

SUE MORONEY (Labour) : It is my pleasure to rise to support the Hamilton City Council (Parana Park) Land Vesting Bill 2010 at its first reading. I want to say at the outset that Labour will be supporting this bill through to select committee because Labour members will be very interested to hear whether there are any issues with this bill. Currently, it is Labour’s perspective that this bill is not controversial. However, we do not know if there are any descendants—it appears that there may not be. I think the previous speaker, David Bennett, who is sponsoring this bill, let us know that the original owner of the land was not married. However, that is not to say we do not know that there are descendants lurking about somewhere in Hamilton who may want to have something to say about this.

I thank the member David Bennett for bringing forward this bill on behalf of the Hamilton City Council. My only wish is that the member would also agree with the Hamilton City Council over pursuing a train service between Hamilton and Auckland. It would be great to see him support that initiative from the Hamilton City Council, as well as this one. Although this is a useful bill, I do not think it is quite as important to the local population of Hamilton today as the initiative to establish a passenger train service between Hamilton and Auckland. I take this opportunity to ask him to consider that initiative from the Hamilton City Council.

The member is quite right about the history of Parana Park, but I would have liked to hear a bit more passion about the beauty of Parana Park. It is a stunning part of our city. It is on the east side of the river, right up against the river. I think the important thing about the history of this piece of land is that it is now back in public ownership. Now, everyone in Hamilton and visitors to Hamilton can make use of that very beautiful piece of land. That was not always the case, as it was in private hands during the course of its history. It is really wonderful to see that the Hamilton City Council has the ownership of that land and that it makes the land available to the good people of Hamilton because it is such a special part of our city. It is alongside and adjoins Memorial Park, where so many special occasions such as the Anzac dawn parade and a civic ceremony on Anzac Day occur every year in our city. It hosts two major Anzac ceremonies on Anzac Day every year. It is also a place where many other activities take place. A lot of weddings take place there, many picnics take place there, and I have attended even union protests at Parana Park. So it is a very, very important part of our city. It is open to the public for a whole range of activities, which very important.

The bill that we are addressing ensures that the Hamilton City Council can use the park for all of the many diverse ways in which it is currently used. The original intention of George Parr in his will, when he gifted the park to Hamilton in 1929, was for it to be used as a playground for children, and it is, but with the plan that the house on that land was to be used as a children’s convalescent home for patients from Waikato Hospital. Clearly, George Parr was a man who loved children, because this was at the forefront of his mind when he gifted this land; he was gifting it to the children of Hamilton. I think that is such a fantastic thing to do. I wonder what he would think if he were alive today about the way the current Government is taking away rights from children, cutting the funding to early childhood education, and doing those sorts of things. I know that this playground is a place where many local early childhood education services take children along to for a bit of a day trip. Those are the very sorts of things that will be under threat because of the cuts to funding that this Government has put in place. It is the field trips and the excursions that many of those centres are looking at cutting out. I think that that would be a great shame, because the playground at Parana Park is a wonderful site for people and young children to visit.

Earlier this year there was a very significant event that took place on that very bank of the Waikato River. Local members of Parliament, local city councillors, and people involved with restoring and keeping Hamilton’s history came together to celebrate the restoration of a gunboat that is pretty important to Hamilton’s history: the PS Rangiriri. It was a very interesting event because we gathered on the opposite bank of the Waikato River to get on board what I would describe as a barge—that is probably even overstating what we were getting on to. It was like a floating platform. We got on, on one side of the river, and motored across the other side of the river to land right by Parana Park. The fascinating thing that happened was we were having rotten peaches thrown at us while we were doing that. Members may ask why that was—and this is a very important part, a very interesting part, of New Zealand history. It was apparently what happened when the PS Rangiriri first trawled up the mighty Waikato River. Local Māori were so concerned—members must remember that this was the period just following the Land Wars—and worried about the presence of that boat on their river that they threw rotten peaches at it, because it was the time of the year when peaches were abundant.

So there we were, earlier this year in 2010, recreating that moment, but without as much grandeur. We were celebrating the restoration of the PS Rangiriri, which now resides, in its restored state, under cover, and not at risk of having the flood plains of the Waikato River causing any more damage to it. Much money from the New Zealand Government and from the Hamilton City Council has been put towards the restoration, but the PS Rangiriri also has all the stories associated with it around it. I think that another important part of Parana Park, and its associated Memorial Park, is that it contains the stories of Hamilton. There are now stories there, which have been written for everyone to go and see, about the people who were involved with the PS Rangiriri and its arrival. I was particularly fascinated by a story of a woman who arrived on the PS Rangiriri, when it first came up the Waikato River. She was a particularly gutsy woman for those days, and she decided she was not going to wait for the captain of the boat to have the opportunity to make land first. She wanted to be the first one, so she threw off her shoes—and goodness’ knows what else—to leap into the waters of the mighty Waikato, run up on to the bank, and be the first person from that boat to land. She would have stepped on to land not too far away from Parana Park.

SUE MORONEY: No, I do not believe that David Bennett was there at the time; this was many, many years ago. That woman claimed her place in history, and I think that is a jolly fine story to read when visiting Parana Park.

I will not talk too much more about the importance of this piece of land to our city and my support for this bill, but before I came down to speak on the bill I had a quick google of Parana Park. I found a really interesting site with a lovely photo, a beautiful photo, of Parana Park. Unfortunately, the person who had put that up on the Internet had misspelt it as “Pirana” Park, so many of the contributions and comments about that beautiful photo are telling the person to get the spelling right—Parana Park. However, there are many interesting stories that people have added, with their very fond memories of Parana Park. I have to say that supporting this bill has been so popular among Labour members that I had to fight off some of my colleagues to take a call on this bill. So many Labour MPs have very fond memories of this particular piece of land and they will enjoy putting those forward in this debate, as well. I look forward to the select committee process on this bill and to see whether any descendents of George Parr might want to come forward. I wish the select committee all the best in its deliberations.

Hon NANAIA MAHUTA (Labour—Hauraki-Waikato) : I speak in favour of sending the Hamilton City Council (Parana Park) Land Vesting Bill to a select committee, to ensure that in the fullness of time those more expert in the history of this property can bring that history out, because I know that that could be a local concern.

I will offer some of my own childhood recollections. We used to live on Boundary Road when I was a child, and often would walk our dog down to Parana Park. It has been rightly said that this is a children’s playground. There were really two places my family went in Hamilton either as children or to take our children. One was Hamilton Lake, which had a walkway around it, and the other was Parana Park. We went to Parana Park mainly, I suspect—for my mother and father in summer—because we were able to swim in the little paddling pool there without too much problem. I have been there recently—just last summer, in fact—with my grandson. We took him there and he enjoyed the area. Sue was right to say that a lot of early childhood centres go there because it is a small and confined space. Children who go to that particular park are probably no older than 8 because of the nature of the small playground. It is a very confined space, and there is a small paddling pool for children to use. I am sure that the benefits of the park as a children’s playground will not be lost. This bill is just about the transfer of the management and future management of the playground.

I will comment on some of the remarks made by members in respect of the history of Parana Park. It is true that there is a post-raupatu history of Parana Park. Indeed, the land would not have been owned by George Parr were it not for the confiscations within Waikato. But Hamilton City is very rich in history and if anyone wants to find out all the different pā sites in the area, there is a well-indexed history at Waikato Museum, to which Ngāti Wairere and Wīremu Puke, in particular, contributed. It was really a means of trying within the city to ensure that those special places in the pre-raupatu history of Hamilton City would be retained. In the fullness of time through the select committee process a case may well be put by Māori community members to have both those historical accounts coexisting within the Parana Park story.

The gift by George Parr later on to ensure that the land would be retained for the city and the benefit of all citizens within Hamilton was a great gift to all members of the community, and it should stay as such. The little house there is a really nice little house, and in summer we see the full beauty of Parana Park. I am concerned, because there is an encroachment to the north of Parana Park. We are seeing more developments along Opōia Road and more apartments being put up. There is council housing in that area. I would hate to see in time Hamilton City Council choose to redesignate special characteristics of that whole area as a result of the changing residential profile of Opōia Road, which is just abutting Parana Park.

The select committee process will be important. It is an opportunity for those who have concerns about the future management and classification of this particular reserve to put their concerns to the select committee so that they can be heard by committee members. Not all Hamilton members will be on that particular select committee, but I am sure anyone who has an interest in Hamilton will know that any reserve around our city would be protected by the citizens there. That is really important. The other important thing about the select committee process is that the Hamilton City Council itself may, in its submission—and I hope that it will—set out its future management for that particular reserve.

I support the bill being referred to a select committee and then coming back to the House. I am not sure whether there will be a number of amendments, but it is an opportunity, perhaps, for the Hamilton City Council to consider its future management. It could have a member on the management committee of reserves in Hamilton. If that is a strong enough wish, it should be put to the select committee.

Lastly, the fondest memory I have of Parana Park was of the first slide that I slid down as a child. Kia ora koutou.

RAHUI KATENE (Māori Party—Te Tai Tonga) : Just over 6 weeks ago a historic bill, the Waikato-Tainui Raupatu Claims (Waikato River) Settlement Bill, was passed. It was the framework for a new era in the relationship between the Crown and Waikato-Tainui. That bill focused on restoring and protecting the health and well-being of the Waikato River for future generations. It is timely now to recall the words of the late Sir Robert Māhuta. He said: “The river belongs to us just as we belong to the river. The Waikato tribe and the river are inseparable. It is a gift left to us by our ancestors and we believe we have a duty to protect that gift for future generations.”

I make reference to that important precedent established by Waikato-Tainui as I believe it is directly relevant to the Hamilton City Council (Parana Park) Land Vesting Bill. To the people of Waikato-Tainui, the river is indeed a taonga tuku iho, the treasured legacy that their tūpuna have entrusted in their care. It is of the utmost relevance to this bill, yet today the local member for Hamilton East, in introducing this bill, spent a long time talking about Mr Parr and his relationship with the land and the park itself. Unfortunately, in bringing this bill into the House, he and the bill failed to even mention Māori, let alone the profound importance that Waikato-Tainui accord to the need to protect, enhance, and restore all waterways. So it was really good to be able to listen to the Hon Nanaia Mahuta talk a little bit about the history of Māori and this area.

Just to be clear: Parana Park is immediately adjacent to the Waikato River in Hamilton. If members have any doubt about the connection, they might talk to Environment Waikato scientist Bill Vant, who has confirmed that as recently as 50 years ago septic tanks at Parana Park were discharged directly into the river. Think about it: that is the place to which Waikato-Tainui would go to swim, to wash, to pray, to be cleansed, and to be healed. Pollution was being directly dumped into that pristine pool. It does not make sense.

We understand that the purpose of this legislation, in vesting Parana Park in the Hamilton City Council, falls within a chain of legislation that should be updated and modernised. This bill is no longer subject to the trusts of the last will and testament of George Parr, a farmer who died back in 1929. It also supersedes the 1958 legislation, which was introduced to ensure the land was protected from inappropriate use, bearing in mind that, up until that time, it had been used for a children’s convalescent home for patients from Waikato Hospital. That history is important, but equally important is the history and authority of mana whenua, who take seriously their obligations as tangata tiaki to care for their environment.

The recent history is especially important in terms of the role of the Waikato River Authority. The co-governance approach associated with that authority shapes a new era of resource management in which accredited commissioners appointed by Waikato-Tainui, Raukawa, Ngāti Tūwharetoa, Maniapoto, and Te Arawa river iwi will be appointed to Waikato Regional Council hearing committees and boards of inquiry for applications for resource consents. It is a very positive approach, the authority comprising equal numbers of Crown and iwi-appointed members sitting together at the table overseeing the management of the health and well-being of the river. In doing so, the authority gives life to concepts of te mana o te awa, the respect for the tūpuna awa, and mana whakahaere—the exercise of control over access to, and management of, the river and its resources in accordance with tikanga.

In undertaking my research into this bill, I came across a study in which 150 marked farm-reared juveniles of the threatened giant kōkopu were released into streams in the urban Kirikiriroa, Parana Park, and Mangakōtukutuku catchments in Hamilton. Survival of the kōkopu species is of vital interest as an aspect of our environmental protection and management, and reminds us again of the need to care for the health and well-being of the Waikato River, the river that runs directly adjacent to Parana Park.

Other aspects of the history of the land may come up in the select committee’s deliberations, particularly the historic association that Ngāti Wairere have with this area, which is known as Pūtikitiki. There may well be many, many more stories that need to be heard. It is therefore in the interests of the iwi and mana whenua that we support this bill to bring their stories to the fore.
