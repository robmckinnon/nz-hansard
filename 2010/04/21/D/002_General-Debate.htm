HONE HARAWIRA (Māori Party—Te Tai Tokerau) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. [Interruption]

Mr SPEAKER: From time to time all of us make the odd mistake in this place, including me. That is no reason to make so much noise.

Hon Trevor Mallard: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I wonder whether we should take another 30 seconds to allow the instructions to come in from New York.

Mr SPEAKER: I choose to ignore that.

HONE HARAWIRA: It was with a sense of enduring shame that when the rest of the world was signing up to the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples in 2007, the Labour Government of the time, without consultation with its own Māori caucus or with the wider Māori population, decided to oppose the declaration, thus sending a very clear statement to the world that as long as Labour was in power, New Zealand would oppose the fundamental rights and aspirations of Māori people. Labour openly declared that opposition, even though Māori had attended meetings all around the world for more than 20 years in support of the declaration, and even though the draft declaration had received the unqualified support of such national Māori icons as Sir James Hēnare, Dame Whina Cooper, Dame Mira Szaszy, Te Atairangikaahu, Sir Robert Māhuta, Hui Vercoe, Sir Paul Reeves, and Sir Hepi te Heuheu, as well as the Māori Women’s Welfare League and the New Zealand Māori Council.

So it was with a deep sense of satisfaction from within the whole of Māoridom that Labour was dumped from the Government benches 12 months later, and lost four of its precious Māori seats to an upstart party whose people had finally woken up to the fact that Māori aspirations were being strangled to death in a party of indifference. This cleared the way for the brand new Māori Party to open negotiations with the National Government, which led to yesterday’s historic announcement to support the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples—a document opposed vociferously by the previous Labour Government in 2007, and still opposed by Labour in 2010.

Today I stand with pride to congratulate the Māori Party co-leader Dr Pita Sharples and his staff on the months of negotiation in the lead-up to yesterday’s announcement that New Zealand would be supporting, without caveat, the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples at the opening session of the Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues in New York. On behalf of the Māori Party I congratulate Prime Minister John Key and National on the boldness of the Government’s decision to work with the Māori Party in recognising that Māori do hold a special status as the indigenous people of Aotearoa, and that indigenous rights and indigenous culture are of profound importance to this country and are fundamental to our identity as a nation.

Again, on behalf of the Māori Party I acknowledge the longstanding support of the Green Party for the adoption of the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, and its commitment to the principles of Te Tiriti o Waitangi. I also acknowledge United Future for its support of the Government’s decision to adopt the declaration. For the rest of this House—those who would speak against this document—I have some very simple questions. Why is it that opponents in this House can so comfortably sign up to international covenants on the rights of women, children, gay people, and even dogs, but be so small-minded, petty, and mean-spirited in their opposition to an international covenant on the rights of indigenous people?

This declaration is aspirational in its wording, positive in its vision, and uplifting in its nature. The adoption of this declaration deserves a greater conclusion than I can deliver, so I leave my final words to the Hon Sir Edward Taihākūrei Durie, who emailed us this morning, stating: “My congratulations to the Māori Party caucus for the Party’s role in securing New Zealand support for the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. Were nothing else done during the Party’s lifetime, this one thing would be enough to secure for it a treasured place in Maori history.

Notwithstanding the progress made through all the tribunal reports and court cases from the 1980s, and the consequential changes in legislation and official policy, I would still rank the day that New Zealand gave support to the Declaration as the most significant day, in advancing Maori rights, since 6th February 1840.

I do not overlook that the Declaration has only moral force. The same is said of the Treaty. Important statements of principle established through international negotiation and acclamation, filter into the law in time, through both governments and the courts, which look constantly for universal statements of principle in developing policy or deciding cases.

Most significant for the present is the statement that recurs throughout the Declaration that indigenous people should be dealt with through their own institutions, a matter with potent implications for the Office of Treaty Settlements”—

Hon SIMON POWER (Minister of Justice) : The major disadvantage about being speaking fourth in the general debate line-up is that the third speaker gets to use the material before I do.

It is worth taking just a bit of time to look at this Labour brand survey. It starts like this: “We want to hear your views on the Labour brand. What follows are a series of questions aimed at discovering: what you think is essential to the Labour brand, how you’d like to see the brand represented, and your views on a selection of existing brands. Thanks again for taking part. 1. Pick the values you’d like to see associated with Labour. Choose as many as you like.” What should be in brackets after that sentence is: “because we don’t have a clue.”

My colleague has set out a range of options, but she missed out a few that puzzled me. We have heard that some of the options are “Futuristic”, “Feel good”, “Bold”, “Inspiring”, and “Cheeky”. I thought “Cheeky” was an interesting option, as were “Dynamic”, “Cool”, “Modern”, and “Happy”. “Unusual” is one of the options, as are “Artistic”, “Edgy”, “Out there”, and “Funky”. But the one that got me absolutely flabbergasted, and that made it in halfway through the list, is “Sober”. It asked whether members think Labour is sober. I do not know what to make of that option or why on earth any organisation would put it in as one of its core messages for consideration as part of its branding.

When we look closely at this survey, we see between the various categories a direct contrast between issues relating to the past, such as “Retro”, and matters relating to the future, such as “Futuristic”, “Edgy”, “Out there”, and “Unusual”. Are they “Sophisticated”? I particularly liked “Memorable”. Is the Labour Party memorable? Other options are “Integrity”, “Innovation”, “Caring”, “People”, “Dependable”, and “Approachable”.

What does this tell us? It tells us that halfway through the term of this Government, there is a sense of deepening malaise in the Opposition ranks. Watching their faces during question time today when their leader was on his feet was a sad indictment. Clayton Cosgrove is a good little scrapper—he likes to get his teeth into the odd issue and is known for putting forward a view every now and then—but he was totally disengaged. His head was up; he was looking at the ceiling during question time. His eyes were wandering, he was looking at the top of the roof, and he was not the slightest bit interested in what was going on. I do not mind reading about Trevor Mallard’s cycling exploits from time to time; I just wish that he would not write about them during question time when his leader is on his feet trying to hold the Government to account.

One of the things that I found interesting about Mr Brownlee’s contribution was his mention of Phil Goff praising the political skills of the Prime Minister. Phil Goff at the moment reminds me of one of those dad characters from a 1980s sitcom, and not just because Family Ties first aired when he was in his first term in Parliament. He is following a well-used formula called the “centra eccentrics” formula, where one straight character is the anchor for all of the other characters, who ultimately upstage him. While Mr Goff is nervously describing his unpopularity, we see a range of characters appearing in larger-than-life formats.

David Cunliffe was understandably moved by his visit to the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum, but his Dominion Post column yesterday suggests that he is forgoing his finance role for one presenting the History Channel. Some of his prose in that article reminded me of some of his earlier work from “I am Harvard”. I have also come across another delightful piece, entitled simply “Seasons”. I am no literary critic—I will leave those matters to the House—but it goes like this: “Almost without warning, branches that just a few weeks before were dead, dry twigs, are crowned with the lushest green foliage. Spring is brief. As a delicate balance between irresistible forces wrestling for control—it gives way, explosively, to Summer.” It sounds decidedly like the leadership crisis in the middle of the Labour Party. He is a lead actor in his own show.

As for old Mr Jones over here, his continual calls of “hōhā” in the House every time he gets to his feet remind me of Al Pacino in Scent of a Woman. He has refused to rule out a leadership challenge, stating: “that lies somewhere between our caucus and providence,”. Providence is also known as the work of God, so I guess that is his natural modesty continuing to come through!

Hon PETE HODGSON (Labour—Dunedin North) : In June of last year Environment Canterbury paid a visit, accompanied by the police, to the property of Mr Tim Smith, and 21 charges were subsequently brought against Mr Smith, who has subsequently pleaded guilty on all counts. Mr Smith’s brother is the Hon Dr Nick Smith, the Minister for the Environment, who has recently sponsored legislation through this House sacking Environment Canterbury. A conflict of interest, therefore, arises. The conflict arises because the Cabinet Manual says so. It says so very clearly.

Let me quote paragraph 2.60. Paragraph 2.60 states: “A conflict of interest may be pecuniary … or non-pecuniary (concerning, for example, a member of the Minister’s family.) A conflict of interest may be direct or indirect.” So a conflict of interest is there all right, but the question is how the conflict was handled. Again, the Cabinet Manual gives us some guidance. It states that when a conflict of interest arises and it does not impinge on the Minister’s own portfolio, then the Minister should declare the conflict of interest. It then states: “Having declared the interest, the Minister should either withdraw from the discussion or seek the agreement of colleagues to continue to take part.” So if it is not within the Minister’s own portfolio that the conflict of interest arises, the conflict should still be declared, and the Minister may or may not choose to leave the Cabinet meeting.

In this case, the conflict of interest directly impinged on the Minister for the Environment’s portfolio. Indeed, it was probably the most important thing on his plate at the time. At the time there was probably no bigger issue in the environment portfolio than whether to sack a rather large regional council. Again, the Cabinet Manual gives guidance as to what to do in those circumstances. Paragraph 2.70(c) states: “A Minister with a conflict of interest concerning a particular issue within his or her portfolio may, with the agreement of the Prime Minister, transfer responsibility for that issue to another Minister.” I know, because I have had issues transferred from me or to me on many different occasions—more than a dozen, I would think.

I do not think that that happened in this case. I do not think that the Minister for the Environment transferred the portfolio; I will say why in a minute. It is important to note that a conflict of interest does not cause the Minister to behave badly. The fact that a conflict of interest arises does not mean that the Minister necessarily performs badly in pursuit of that conflict of interest, but what does matter is that the Minister cannot say that he did not behave badly because he did not take himself out of the action in respect of this conflict of interest. That is bad Cabinet practice—pure and simple.

How can I be sure, when I do not sit around the Cabinet table, that the Minister did not transfer responsibility to another Minister? Well, because legislation resulted, and the legislation was in the name of the Minister, and the Minister involved was the Minister in the chair in this Chamber during the Committee stage and was the predominant Minister shepherding the legislation through. That is really bad practice. It is not just a sort of bad manners thing; it leaves the Minister vulnerable to the accusation that he did behave badly, and it leaves him vulnerable to not being able to prove that he did not.

That sort of thing is becoming a feature of this Government. We saw it just yesterday in the House with the Minister of Agriculture and the response that the Prime Minister gave to a series of questions from a member of the Green Party. I am putting on the table my concern about the slackness of Cabinet processes in this Government. I do not think that conflicts of interest have been managed at all well in this Government. They can be very awkward. They can be very awkward. When dealing with Māori land issues, sometimes half the frigging Cabinet used to get up and leave, including a bunch of Pākehās. That certainly was the case with the South Island Landless Maoris legislation I was responsible for. Certainly, if something involving Brierley’s arose 10 years ago, there would be a bit of a railway station effect as Cabinet Ministers left. I do not think that is happening in this Cabinet.

I think that this Cabinet is taking an easy approach to managing conflicts of interest, and I am putting this on the record now because I intend to question the Prime Minister as time goes on about the management of conflicts of interest in this Government. I do not think it is up to scratch. I do not think enough attention is being paid to the propriety of governance decisions.

Hon RODNEY HIDE (Leader—ACT) : We clearly have a problem in New Zealand when we have just announced the Government’s ratifying of the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples but even within the Government, even within this Parliament, we have such a widely divergent view of what it means. On the one hand, the Prime Minister says that it is of no practical effect, and that it is aspirational. On the other hand, MP Hone Harawira says that it is of enormous impact because, as Mai Chen says, it will inform the courts, it will inform the Waitangi Tribunal, and it will inform political discourse for years to come.

I believe that Mr Harawira is right, because words have to mean something. The words of the articles of the declaration are very, very clear in what they say, but I do not believe for a minute that the majority of New Zealanders agree with the sentiment set out in that declaration. I do not believe they want to live in a country where we are divided into two: those who are indigenous and have a separate set of rights and privileges, and everyone else. I certainly do not want to live in such a country. It is very clear when we read the articles that they say that indigenous people should be able to have political autonomy. Well, the Prime Minister says that that is an aspiration, but is it truly an aspiration for New Zealand that Māori people within New Zealand can have their own political structures, their own State? I say no.

Article 26 says that indigenous people have the right to the land and the resources that they traditionally owned or occupied. Clearly, if we look at that, we see that that is all the land and resources of New Zealand. We have followed a process where that land has been alienated, sometimes unjustly, and we are working towards fixing that through the Treaty. But this article does not allow for that; it says all the land and resources traditionally owned or occupied should be returned to the indigenous people. That is why Helen Clark as Prime Minister refused to ratify the declaration. She would sign up to anything from the UN that was put in from of her, but not this declaration; Parekura Horomia said it was incompatible with the democratic processes and the fundamental laws of New Zealand.

We cannot get around this by saying: “Don’t worry. Nod, nod; wink, wink. KEVIN HAGUE (Green) : When I need to buy laundry powder, I go to the supermarket. I go to the right aisle and I check out all of the products that are available. I look at the effectiveness, the environmental and ethical dimensions, and the price of each product, and I choose the one that best meets my needs. How dumb would it be if all of the laundry powder manufacturers queued up to offer me their products one by one? If the first one came and I said yes, then all of the others could go away; if I said no, then the next one would come, and I could continue that way until I bought something. It would not make sense for soap powder and it does not make sense for power generation schemes, yet that is precisely the method that we use to determine power generation schemes.

The problem in the soap powder example is that I would have no way of knowing which is the best of the products that are being offered to me and no way of knowing what the total range would be. That is precisely the problem for power generation. The only schemes that get to be considered are those that best meet the purpose of profit maximisation of the power companies, not those that best meet the public good. Very often those two purposes will not be aligned. Furthermore, it is currently forbidden rather than encouraged for the merits of, for example, two competing schemes to be compared.

It is a ridiculous situation and it is currently well illustrated on the West Coast and northern Buller, where a company called Hydro Developments Ltd has received resource consent for an ingenious power generation scheme that involves dropping water from the Stockton Plateau down to sea level and using the drop to generate electricity. The scheme that is proposed by Hydro Developments would have a net positive environmental impact. If that scheme goes ahead, along with the Arnold Valley scheme, which is already consented, and the existing generation the West Coast would be self-sufficient in electricity.

It is a worthy goal, but the consents have been appealed, most notably by two State-owned enterprises, Meridian Energy and Solid Energy. In both cases the grounds of the appeals look spuriously weak. Understandably, members of the public have come to the conclusion that both of the appeals may have more to do with rival schemes owned by those two State-owned enterprises. Solid Energy’s scheme is broadly similar to that of Hydro Developments, and it is a matter of extreme regret that the State-owned enterprise has refused offers of mediation from the chief executive of the Buller District Council. I would like to hear from the Minister for State Owned Enterprises as to what mechanisms he has in place to rein in the apparently unethical behaviour of both of these State-owned enterprises.

Its plan is to desecrate one of the most valuable and treasured places in the entire country, the Mōkihinui River. Meridian Energy intends to build an 85-metre-high dam and a 14-kilometre-long lake. It intends to inundate around 300 hectares of our most precious forest ecosystem in New Zealand. According to Meridian Energy’s own assessment, the conservation values and recreation values are second to none in the New Zealand conservation estate and cannot be offset. The impact of this scheme can never be reversed.
