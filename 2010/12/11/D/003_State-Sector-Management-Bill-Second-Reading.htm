State Sector Management Bill

Second Reading

  • Debate resumed from 7 December.

COLIN KING (National—Kaikōura) : It is crystal clear to all New Zealanders that after 9 years of a wasteful Labour Government we need to rebalance our economy. We also need to redesign and have a first-class State services sector.

The State Sector Management Bill achieves all of those goals. It provides for the formation of a new Ministry of Science and Innovation, it harmonises the National Library and Archives New Zealand under the Department of Internal Affairs, and, very importantly, it preserves the constitutional independence of the Chief Archivist. On that basis, it gives me pleasure to commend this bill to the House.

Hon STEVE CHADWICK (Labour) : One has to wonder where that member, Colin King, was a decade ago, if he thinks this issue is all peace and light. Labour will definitely be opposing the State Sector Management Bill.

There is an element of this bill that we do not have a problem with at all, and that is in respect of the amalgamation of the Foundation for Research, Science and Technology and the Ministry of Research, Science and Technology to form the new Ministry of Science and Innovation. We think that is great. That is fine, and I think we will wait with great interest to see what will emerge from the new Ministry of Science and Innovation, especially with Minister Mapp at the helm. Great things are obviously—

Sue Moroney: Anything could happen.

Hon STEVE CHADWICK: Anything could happen, at all.

But the aspect of this bill that we are very opposed to, for very serious reasons, is the proposed merger of Archives New Zealand and the National Library with the Department of Internal Affairs. These institutions are a key part of our democracy—absolutely key. They are about keeping Government departments transparent and the information held in those departments protected. When the Minister of State Services gave his opening speech on this bill, it was the most limp speech that I have ever heard. It was quietly given, in a measured way, and it looked as though it would not panic the punters. But, actually, this measure shows the determination of National to continue an unrelenting attack on public servants, whom they see as just being bureaucrats and bean counters, not as people who value their jobs, do their jobs with passion and pride, and take with great seriousness the roles and the functions of their jobs. The Minister saw this merger in his speech as being simply a little merger that tucks Archives New Zealand and the National Library—including the iconic Alexander Turnbull Library—into the Department of Internal Affairs. National’s position is that we should not worry, because it has sorted out everything about the roles and functions of the National Librarian and of the Chief Archivist. It says we should trust it to know what it is doing.

This merger looks to be all very simple and sort of logical, but, actually, behind it there is an insidious and unrelenting lack of respect for public servants who have done their job.

I found it interesting that Colin King stood up and gave a silly little speech, just reading the opening paragraph of the commentary on the bill—National members have been told to speak just for about 10 seconds on every bill, so that they will be out of here by about midday. Mr King was not around a decade ago. A decade ago there was a failed experiment in which Archives New Zealand was put under the Department of Internal Affairs. What goes around comes around; now we are doing it again. Relationships were so bad between the Department of Internal Affairs and Archives New Zealand that they went to court, 10 years ago. So what a waste of time and money this merger is.

The Minister could only come up with the justification that this great merger, all in the name of increased efficiency, better services for the public, better accountability, and great improvements in the quality of service provision from the so-called bureaucrats—who are wonderful, respected professionals in their jobs—will save only $165,000. Archives New Zealand and the National Library have always had an absolutely clean audit from the Audit Office—no problems; nothing that needed to be tidied up. They do their job professionally as stand-alone entities, so why in the name of efficiency would one merge them into the Department of Internal Affairs, for the sake of saving the princely sum of $165,000, and risk that grievances will arise again in the future? The roles of the Chief Archivist and the National Librarian will be third-tier positions, and they will not even have face-to-face meetings on a weekly basis with the Chief Executive of the Department of Internal Affairs. I think that is an absolute insult.

If Chris Finlayson, who does know about the functions of the National Library and Archives New Zealand and about the importance of the material that they preserve, had been a little more aware of what was being proposed by stealth by Tony Ryall, if he had been a bit more aware and read his Cabinet papers in time, he would have jumped up and said this was a foolish move. An absolutely flawed move like this took us to court a decade ago. As a lawyer, Minister Finlayson knows that. He knows about the risks if the public record is not kept transparent and people are not held accountable for it. Minister Finlayson knows about the risks, but he failed to challenge Nathan Guy and Tony Ryall, so this cute little merger, this cute little amalgamation, went through. Members can look at the resulting effect: 15 jobs are lost. Mr Ryall would say “Only 15—what’s that? That’s great. It’s all in the name of efficiency.” These are not just jobs. These are people who are held accountable for, and who know how to manage, the public record of both libraries, and who know how to look after the body of historical archival material held by the Alexander Turnbull Library. These libraries are not something that can be merged with the Department of Internal Affairs quite easily, even though National says the merger does not affect their roles because it has sorted those out in the bill.

I think this situation is an absolute disgrace. The Chief Archivist and the National Librarian must have statutory independence, and their independence is limited if they become part of the Department of Internal Affairs. What happened a decade ago when this happened? It was logical: they struggled to find a voice. If they are put into the third-tier level of management in a huge department, a burgeoning, huge department, then how will they have a voice? How will they have their independence? How can the National Librarian and Chief Archivist go and meet their Minister? That will not happen. They will be swallowed up in the department, and will struggle to have a voice to advocate for the record that they manage. This is an absolutely stupid move. It is a flawed move. It has already caused great distress in the library and the archive sector of New Zealand. Those who work in the sector are not fuddy-duddy bureaucrats who feel threatened by the loss of a job; they are there to preserve the function of their job, and it has been shown that they have an excellent record. For the sake of $165,000 a year and the loss of 15 jobs, National thinks this is a great little cute move, but it is not.

In conclusion, I say the merger is not necessary at all. It will not generate better public services; it is driven purely by an ideological desire to reduce the number of Government agencies. This is flawed logic. The Minister for Arts, Culture and Heritage failed to represent and advocate for Archives New Zealand and the National Library as being very important functions of a creative country, in a sector that has a great sense of history. The merger will fail, and I ask members to watch to see what will happen with the next audit report. We will argue against this merger in more depth when we get into the Committee stage of this bill.

To begin with, the submitters to the select committee presented a great number of risks and reasons why we should not proceed with this bill. We heard that the National Library of New Zealand and Archives New Zealand have different roles: one is a library and one is responsible for archiving. We heard that their independence is at risk. Throughout the select committee process there were no facts and figures, and no robust arguments were presented. In that case, there remains simply slogans.

When it comes to the finance we are talking about maybe $165,000 a year. It seems crazy that we are doing this for literally $165,000 a year. It is as if the Government, looking for inspiration for its legislative programme for the year, went back and looked at the Shipley-Bolger playbook from the 1990s. When it comes to worker relations it has gone back to the Shipley-Bolger playbook, and when it comes to these wonderful Government departments, it has gone back to the Shipley-Bolger playbook. We have returned to the 1990s. In the select committee we once again heard that there were compelling reasons why these departments were separated by the Labour Government.

In summary, we oppose this bill for a considerable number of reasons. We have simply heard slogans as arguments for it, and it essentially amounts to an ideological solution in search of a problem. Kia ora.

But doing something for absolutely no real reason does not seem to be a particularly good use of our time here on a Saturday morning.

One thing that brings us together as parliamentarians on a Saturday morning is the feeling that we could have done this better, and that the Leader of the House could have organised this a bit better so that instead of being here in Parliament on a Saturday morning, we might have actually been spending our time with our constituents. As we all know, in our electorates there are many things we could be doing this morning. We do not have very much time before Christmas comes along.

From there, it could serve as an independent watchdog and a repository of information that benefited the whole of New Zealand—the whole of New Zealand.

Instead, we are burying both the Archives and the National Library inside the Department of Internal Affairs, which will make the heads of both of those departments third-tier positions. That means that they will not, despite what we were told in the select committee, have access to the responsible Minister. It seems like the Minister would not necessarily be doing anything even if they did have access to the Minister, so perhaps that is a pointless point to bring up.

In fact, I congratulate Minister Mapp on coming up with a good name: the Ministry of Science and Innovation [Interruption] That is true. Dr Mapp came up with a great name: the Ministry of Science and Innovation. We may ask what is in a name. Well, the answer is not a whole lot. If we dig below the name, we see the same old, same old approach coming out yet again.

I will give members two examples. First of all, putting two departments together will not save any money for at least 7 years, according to the Auditor-General’s report. That report supposes that no extra people have been taken on board as a result of new things that the new ministry comes up with. It will be 7 years.

The other thing that was interesting when the select committee looked at this bill was that I expected, perhaps in the preamble, something slightly more inspirational about what this bill is trying to do. It is bringing together two pieces of Government bureaucracy. The officials said that they had not had time to think about that, and that they had been too busy trying to merge the Ministry of Research, Science and Technology and the Foundation of Research, Science and Technology. In a sense, that says it all about this legislation. It starts off aspiring to be something that really tries to take us forward, but it does not. It falls at the first hurdle; it really does not move us forward any more.

I will give the House a second example, which is in respect of appointing the head of this new ministry. From the very beginning, I have had no issues or problems with the person who was chosen for this job.

Hon Darren Hughes: Was it Christine Rankin?

DAVID SHEARER: No, in this case it was not Christine Rankin. But I ask members this: if we were going to start a brand new ministry that really looked to be inspirational and aspirational about what we want to do, as the Minister has spoken about in every speech he has come out with, what would we do? I will tell the House what I would do. I would advertise across the world and try to get the best person for the job, from perhaps the other side of the world, perhaps from Finland or Denmark, which the Minister keeps mentioning as the example of where we want to be in the world. Maybe we should go to Denmark and see whether we can attract anybody from Denmark to New Zealand who can show us what happened there. We could have looked at Sweden or Singapore.

But we have not.

This is another example that permeates right through the Government of where we have sold ourselves short. We have lacked the drive to drive this ministry forward to the place it should be.

Here we are on a Saturday morning looking at a bill that puts the Archives and the National Library back into the Department of Internal Affairs, despite the best advice from just about everybody who has ever worked in both of those areas. In the research, science and technology area we could have made a real difference and really lived up to the great name that the Minister has come up with, but instead we have fallen sadly short.

MOANA MACKEY (Labour) : If ever there was an example of Government legislation that is a triumph of ideology over common sense, then the State Sector Management Bill is that legislation. It has the people of Aotearoa New Zealand at heart!

Hon Darren Hughes: It was time for a change.

MOANA MACKEY: It was absolutely time for a change in the way we manage our National Library and our archives.

The merger of Archives New Zealand and the National Library absolutely defies belief. It was only 10 years ago that we separated them out because of all the problems they were having when they were administered by the Department of Internal Affairs and the Ministry of Education.

On the subject of Archives New Zealand, interestingly enough I think there is quite a lot of correlation between the National Government’s attempts to rewrite history and its desire to run down the role of Archives New Zealand. Archives New Zealand plays a key constitutional role in New Zealand. It ensures that the documents of Government are retained and are available for scrutiny by the people of New Zealand. Should we really be surprised that this National Government has shown no respect for the processes under the Official Information Act? When we put in our Official Information Act requests to the Government, the information comes back late. The Government refuses to release anything, but if it is forced to release anything, we get blank pages or words crossed out, because the Government does not want the people of New Zealand to know that the advice it has been getting from its officials shows that the path we are on is not good for our country. So I do not think we should be particularly surprised that the Government would like to undermine the role of Archives New Zealand, because that would make it a lot easier for John Key and his team to rewrite history, which they have tried to do many, many times already.

Members across the House have sat on select committees that both these entities have come before for their financial reviews. Both of them are very, very well run. They are very efficiently run. The cost savings that the Government has been trumpeting are simply not there.

One of the things Government Ministers have said is that if they bring these entities together, the big information and communication technology project to digitalise all of this information will be easier to do. Well, according to people who actually know about that digitisation, it is doomed to fail because of the complexity of this merger and the way that the new entities will be set up. In fact, that information and communication technology project is under threat from this merger.

So why is the Government doing it, other than out of blind ideology—blind ideology—and a feeling that it hates Government departments and nameless, faceless bureaucrats, despite the fact that they are preserving our history for future generations of New Zealanders? The Government has no plan for our economy, so instead it will say: “Aren’t we great? We fired a whole lot of people in the State sector. Aren’t we doing a really good job? Look at us managing public funds properly.”, even though there will be no savings from this merger, and even though it puts at risk very expensive projects that have been ongoing for a long time. More important, it undermines the very independence of these two constitutional roles, which this House should be protecting.

I will follow on from my colleague David Shearer and his comments about the other part of this legislation, which is the combining of the Ministry of Research, Science and Technology and the Foundation for Research, Science and Technology. Labour members do not oppose that. I do not think it will make any great change, to be perfectly honest. In fact, when Dr Mapp came to the select committee for his first appropriation—

We do not think it will do a whole lot of damage. It is really just shuffling the deck chairs.

It does not answer the more important questions in the area of science and research and development, which are why the Government slashed the funding the previous Labour Government had put in place, and why it removed the research and development tax credit, which was unanimously supported by the sector as a tool it needed to lift private-sector investment in research and development, and replaced it with a bureaucratic grants system. If Labour had put that in place, members opposite would have been all over us about how bureaucratic it was, how inefficient it was, and why we had not introduced a tax credit. DAVID SHEARER (Labour—Mt Albert) : Some of the early provisions of the State Sector Management Bill cut to the heart of what the bill is really about in terms of research, science, and technology issues. First, I want to come to the aspect of the definitions contained in this bill.

A number of submitters made some comment on that matter—in particular, on clauses 7(1) and 7(2). One or two of the submitters wanted to try to put into the bill, and to have much more fleshed out, the definitions of what constitutes science, research, technological development, innovation, and benefit to New Zealand. They felt that the bill as it stood at that point did not go into that sufficiently. They wanted to see those terms expressed more fully, explained and defined more fully, in the legislation, and I think there was some sympathy for that view.

In particular, the submitters cut to the definitions that are applied by the OECD in terms of science, research, and technology. Those definitions, which are widely accepted as being the authority on research and development, are the Frascati definitions. They define basic research as “experimental or theoretical work undertaken primarily to acquire new knowledge of the underlying foundation of phenomena and observable facts, without any particular application or use in view.” In those terms, basic research is what one would call the foundation work that we do in science. Applied research—and I quote from the Frascati definitions again—“is also original investigation undertaken in order to acquire new knowledge. It is, however, directed primarily towards a specific practical aim or objective.” We can see the difference between the two definitions.

Our science in New Zealand obviously straddles both of those areas. We have fundamental research taking place, particularly in our universities. The work of the Crown research institutes tends to be more of the applied nature, although I have to say that even in those institutes a significant amount of good science is happening, particularly in the basic earth sciences. But I think the feeling was that those definitions, in a sense, set the bill up, so there was no problem in understanding what was being referred to in clauses 7(1) and (2).

The applied and the more experimental research is somewhat different from those two areas. Again, I will quote from the Frascati definitions: “Experimental development is systematic work, drawing on existing knowledge gained from research and/or practical experience, which is directed to producing new materials, products or devices, to installing new processes, systems and services, or to improving substantially those already produced or installed.” We can think of many, many examples of that, one of which is the development of the red kiwifruit. That is really a development of something that currently exists, but the new knowledge and new science that was applied to kiwifruit enabled a new product to come about.

In many ways, that example cuts to the heart of what this bill looks to do, which is to look for innovation in terms of our science, research, and technology. The bill tries to build on that aspect of what we do in our science and research institutions. That is where we get the greatest amount of economic benefit; the economic benefit comes from improving what already exists. There is nothing wrong with that. Obviously, a lot of things come out of blue-sky science, and we do some extremely good blue-sky research in our science institutions. But not much of it—or very little of it—actually goes on to produce real economic benefit. The core of the economic benefit comes from the area covered by the Frascati definition that I just read out, which applies to experimental development that builds on and develops processes and aspects of our science system that are already in place.

We discussed at some length in the Education and Science Committee whether it would be worth putting the Frascati definitions in the bill. I think we came to the basic conclusion that with regard to the use of the phrase “RS&T” in clauses 7(2)(e) and (f), although the Frascati definitions are good and comprehensive, they are really already embodied inside that definition. They are embodied in the bill already. It is well known what they mean and they are well accepted throughout New Zealand, so we thought it was probably unnecessary to draw that definition out any further. However, we agreed to amend the phrase “RS&T’ used in clauses 7(2)(e) and (f) to read “research, science, or technology,” but we did not change the definition of “RS&T funding” in clause 4.

I come to the second issue raised by submitters on this part of this bill, which was the phrase “the benefit of New Zealand” in clause 7(1). Again, it was felt by some submitters that we should have that specifically brought into the bill and stated up front. The committee did not discuss that in great depth, but we felt that the phrase “the benefit of New Zealand” is probably intended to have its ordinary, natural meaning. In fact, most of us would believe that it is pretty self-explanatory, and that to expand it and try to define it would do the opposite to what the submitters wanted, which was to make that definition clear. If we had defined it, that might have made it much more restrictive in terms of the future interpretation of the legislation. So we left that particular part of the definition, “the benefit of New Zealand”, in place.

In conclusion, I think this particular issue of the definitions is really important, as we are moving towards a bill that, in a sense, develops a new title of research and innovation, and that was well discussed. I think we have now started off this new legislation with a full understanding of exactly what the various definitions mean. We are clear about that, and it is based in the commentary on the bill. I will leave it at that. Thank you.

After a period of time, people have a right to know what is going on and to have access to whatever is contained within such papers, however inconvenient the content may be. Archives New Zealand records significant volumes of information regarding ministerial decisions, Government decisions, members of Parliament, and public records. That information may become inconvenient over time and Ministers and members of Parliament may not want it to see the light of day. It would be very concerning if we created an environment in which any political pressure could be brought to bear very subtly behind the scenes on the Chief Archivist.
