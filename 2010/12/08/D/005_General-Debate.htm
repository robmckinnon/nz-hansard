Dr RUSSEL NORMAN (Co-Leader—Green) : Today I will talk very briefly about the Trans-Pacific Partnership negotiations—talks that have been under way in Auckland. Most people think that the Trans-Pacific Partnership is about trade; in fact, very little of it is about trade. Most of it is about trying to restrict the ability of Governments to make regulations.

When we look at some of the issues that are on the table in the negotiations around the Trans-Pacific Partnership, we see that they are issues like restricting the ability to control foreign investment. When Australia negotiated its trade deal with the United States, it gave away the right to have any restrictions on incoming investment from the United States into Australia up to a level of A$800 million, which effectively gutted Australia’s entire foreign investment review process for US investment. That is one of the issues that the United States has put on the table.

A second issue it has put on the table is our GE food labelling laws. Biotech companies in the United States do not like our GE food labelling laws, or the Australian ones, and they are trying to get them removed as part of the trade negotiations.

Another part of the negotiations that I think is critically important is the investor-State disputes mechanism. As part of these free-trade deals, a disputes mechanism is established whereby corporations can sue Governments in international tribunals. These tribunals are generally set up at the World Bank. They are three-person tribunals that make binding decisions on Governments. These tribunals have in the past required Canada to allow a toxic additive into its petrol. Canada tried to remove a toxic additive from its petrol, but was sued by Ethyl Corporation, which was the producer. Ethyl Corporation argued that Canada had expropriated Ethyl Corporation by banning the toxic additive, and it was able to win its case.

Recently, Argentina lost a case when it tried to intervene to protect water supply. Argentina argued that it had to intervene because water is a basic human necessity. The tribunal established at the World Bank said that just because something was a human right did not mean that it overrode trade rules. Trade rules actually trump the human right to water.

Labour and National are very keen for us to sign up to these agreements. Investor-State disputes resolution provisions were in the New Zealand - China free-trade agreement that the former Labour Government negotiated. These kinds of issues are on the table.

How does the New Zealand public find out what is part of these negotiations? We know that the New Zealand Government has produced a series of position papers outlining the position of the New Zealand Government, and they have been given to all the other negotiating countries. For example, the Sultan of Brunei knows what the New Zealand Government’s position is on, let us say, intellectual property. But the New Zealand people do not know what the New Zealand Government’s position is on intellectual property, because the New Zealand Government has not released the position papers that it has given to the other negotiating parties.

When we asked the Government about this, it said it could not release the position papers, because it would undermine our negotiating position. That is not a rational argument. The other countries already know New Zealand’s negotiating position because we have given them the position papers outlining our negotiating position. The only people who do not know New Zealand’s negotiating position are the people of New Zealand. The other countries are entirely aware of our negotiating position because we have given them our position papers. It is a matter of public record that our Government has presented these position papers to the other Governments in the negotiations, but has not publicly released them.

The Government cannot argue that it would undermine the negotiating strategy when New Zealand’s position is already known to the other negotiating parties. How can it be right that the New Zealand public is not told what New Zealand is telling the other Governments in these negotiations? There needs to be greater transparency so that we, the public—and even members of this Parliament—have a right to see what New Zealand is telling the other parties to the negotiations.

I argue that the reason the New Zealand public is not being told is that what the New Zealand Government is offering would be very unpopular. If the New Zealand Government was offering to establish investor-State disputes mechanisms whereby other corporations can sue our Government in international tribunals, that would be very unpopular amongst the New Zealand public. If the New Zealand Government was offering to get rid of our GE food labelling laws, that would be very unpopular. If the New Zealand Government was offering to get rid of our investment rules, that would be very unpopular. That is why the Government is not releasing the position papers to the New Zealand public. It should. These negotiations need transparency.

RAHUI KATENE (Māori Party—Te Tai Tonga) : Today is a really auspicious day for the Māori Party. It is an auspicious day for Māori and it is an auspicious day for the country. I am really disappointed that none of the other speakers in this debate so far have even mentioned the fact that this morning our co-leader Pita Sharples stood alongside the Deputy Prime Minister and announced a new phase of public debate to be generated around New Zealand’s constitutional arrangements.

This is the day we have been leading up to since the agreement was signed with National, and we note that other political parties said they have been looking towards this day, asking that they be part of this debate. Well, today is it. This is the day when our constitutional review is starting, and it is a fantastic day.

In his book The Treaty of Waitangi in New Zealand’s Law and Constitution, Matthew Palmer expresses his view that Te Tiriti o Waitangi contributes greatly to the objective of healthy relationships between the Crown, Māori, and other New Zealanders. Mr Palmer concludes that in order to better achieve that objective, change is desirable. Change is good—change that stabilises the place of Te Tiriti o Waitangi in New Zealand’s law and constitution. Today the drive to stabilise the place of the Treaty of Waitangi in New Zealand’s law and constitution enters new terrain.

I wish that other members of the House were as excited as our party is about this, because this is the day that we have been looking forward to for so long and that Māori have been working towards for decades. As a first-term MP and a lawyer by trade and by inclination, it is almost beyond my wildest dreams that in such a short time we, the Māori Party, have been able to place the constitutional significance of Te Tiriti o Waitangi firmly on the agenda.

The second anniversary of my maiden statement in this Parliament is just days away. In that speech I referred to the many hours I had spent in the Court of Appeal being educated in the practice and impact of constitutional law. I will remind members of the challenge this Parliament has until now failed to live up to. I am referring, of course, to the ruling of the Court of Appeal in New Zealand Maori Council v Attorney-General. In his summary the President of the Court of Appeal, Sir Robin Cooke, stated: “We have reached two major conclusions. First, that the principles of the Treaty of Waitangi overrides everything else in the State Owned Enterprises Act. Second, that those principles require the Pakeha and Maori partners to act towards each other reasonably and with the utmost good faith.”

Those words are pivotal in our pathway to nationhood. For too long our nation has suffered the injustice of being one of only three countries in the entire world to have no written constitution. We are one of three in the whole world with no written constitution. Commentators refer to a veritable patchwork quilt of significant legislation, several legal documents, common law derived from court decisions, and established constitutional practices known as conventions.

A vital thread running through all these documents has been the status of Te Tiriti o Waitangi as a founding document of government in New Zealand. Today in this House we appreciate that article 1 of the Treaty is in itself the basis for the current government structures in New Zealand, expressing the right of the Crown to govern. We know that article 2 of the Treaty guarantees to Māori te tino rangatiratanga over our lands, estates, forests, fisheries, and taonga—the full, exclusive, and undisturbed possession of all that we treasure as the ultimate expression of our identity. Article 3 of the Treaty affirms the equal citizenship rights of all New Zealanders, including Māori. Article 3 also promises the Queen’s royal protection to Māori. A measure of this protection would be the well-being of Māori.

Such a rudimentary summary of ideals and aspirations emerging from our foundation document, Te Tiriti o Waitangi, is not, regrettably, something that all New Zealanders feel equipped to express. Surveys have been carried out over the years. One survey that was carried out 5 years ago by UMR Research found that levels of awareness varied: 71 percent of Māori are aware of the document, but only 51 percent of Pākehā are aware of it. This needs to change. It will change through this constitutional review. Thank you.

Hon PETE HODGSON (Labour—Dunedin North) : Aaron Gilmore has just given us verse, not cleverly done and certainly not terse. The joke is on him, for today’s speaking list are Cabinet wannabes who regrettably missed.

The Prime Minister must refer the Pansy Wong issue to the Auditor-General for investigation, and must do so soon. That is what the Prime Minister did with Phil Heatley, it is what happened in the case of Bill English—after a period of delay, I acknowledge—and it should happen in the case of Pansy Wong. Should the Prime Minister do that, an investigation into an area of possible misuse of public moneys can be conducted by an office that is respected and exists to look into the use and misuse of public moneys.

That is why we have an Auditor-General’s office. It is an office capable of measured judgments. In the case of Phil Heatley, for example, it was capable of giving him a rapid re-entry straight back into Cabinet. In the case of Pansy Wong, that door was closed by the appointment earlier this week of Hekia Parata. But it does not close the door for ever.

Indeed, I think Pansy Wong would welcome an investigation for a couple of different reasons. The first is that she might find herself getting the Phil Heatley treatment, where the matter was found to be minor. No instances beyond it were found in the McPhail report. There was nothing more to discover. It was a $500 job. The money has been paid back. It was not deliberate, systemic, systematic, or anything like that. That is a possibility.

Another reason she might be pleased for the Auditor-General to look at her case is that the information flow to us—certainly, to me—will stop. It stopped when the McPhail report got under way. For 2 or 3 weeks, the information that had been flowing in to me and that I had been putting into the public arena as it came to pass stopped. I stopped putting anything into the public arena; there was nothing to put in.

On Friday the McPhail report came out, and it was panned by Friday evening as being an inadequate report. It does not mean that it is wrong, but it does not draw a line under the case, because it does not have credibility. There is no significant certainty of its being right—so say the media, and so say I. I do not think it is a thorough report. So, of course, as Friday evening turned into the weekend, the information started to flow again. It just comes, and it is time-consuming. Much of it has to be translated from Mandarin. I continue to put it in the public arena, as I have done today, and I may well do tomorrow. Who knows? I may well do it next week. Again, who knows? Pansy Wong has to put up with that, and I do not think it is fair on her.

Of course, I could stop, but I am not going to, because my job and my duty—there is no need to grin about it, Mr Assistant Speaker Roy—is to hold the Government to account. That is the job of Her Majesty’s loyal Opposition. People can call me a muckraker, but that is the reason why the Westminster system is set up as it is. I will continue to put this information into the public arena, as long as I can prove using written evidence that it is true. I get an awful lot of rumour, which does not go any further, because I do not know whether it is true. But when I can show that something is true or very likely to be true, I will put it into the public arena. I will continue to do so.

It is not good for Pansy Wong. I think she would want it stopped, and I think the Prime Minister can stop it by taking her case and ringing up the Auditor-General and asking, just like with the Phil Heatley case, that she please look at it.

TIM MACINDOE (National—Hamilton West) : It has been fascinating to witness the Labour Opposition’s rudderless and insipid performance during the general debate this afternoon. It will not surprise many people who have followed Labour members’ confused and contradictory announcements of recent times that they were so reluctant to engage with Government members over current policies, or to defend their own irresponsible economic record when they were in Government, which contributed so significantly to New Zealand’s slide into recession.

Labour’s record in Government is now roundly and rightly condemned by economists, commentators, and the electorate at large. Yet what has Labour learnt? It has learnt that Ed Miliband writes better speeches than Phil Goff. Labour members have learnt from their embattled leader that David Caygill has more credibility than their current spokesman on finance, presumably because Mr Caygill played no role in the economic recklessness that Labour inflicted upon New Zealand for 9 long years. They do not seem to have learnt that Trevor Mallard should behave himself during question time on days when he is expected to lead Labour’s “Charge of the Ultra-light Brigade” in two measures on the same day’s Order Paper. And, on the day when we welcome to the House the new member for the marginal seat of Mana, they have recently learnt that even their previously strongest supporters are deserting them.

They are probably learning that their commitment to voodoo economics is about as popular as their recent opposition to the package to save the Hobbit movies. It is as credible as their commitment to higher taxes, uncontrolled Government spending, and welfare for millionaires.

Members on this side of the House and members of the public have learnt a great deal from Labour’s sorry and embarrassing performance both in this debate and during today’s question time, which preceded it. We have learnt that less than a year out from a general election Labour is in freefall. We have learnt that Labour members lack confidence in their leadership, their philosophical direction, and in one another. They are not just working on the basis that pixies at the bottom of the garden will jump up and finance their policies—as our new Poet Laureate, Aaron Gilmore, observed earlier—but also they are increasingly turning to the pixies to write their policies. Judging by how many people turn up to any of their public meetings nowadays, or take seriously any of their utterances, it can be only a matter of time before their front bench is replaced by five pixies, three goblins, and an orc. Actually, I think the orc has already joined them, which may explain why there are so many headless chooks on their middle and backbenches.

I am looking forward to campaigning in Hamilton West next year on the importance of National’s six-point plan to growing the economy, especially when it is contrasted with Labour’s six-point plan to, one, change their leader; two, lose the few electorates they hold at the moment; and, three, increase Government borrowing to levels that will reduce New Zealand to a banana-less republic. One has to wonder what they are going to feed to the poor pixies and goblins, but I digress. The fourth point is to tax anything that moves, which means their Mana campaign team will be all right but everyone else is in big trouble; the fifth is to bring back David Caygill, Fraser Colman, and Arnold Nordmeyer; and the sixth is to hope that endless treks around the country on the magical mystery tour, with Phil playing the ukulele and Annette warbling “As Long as He Needs Me”, will at least keep them away from any gathering where they might be expected to say something credible and remotely compelling.

How ironic that David Parker should want to highlight a recent poll that put Labour on 28 percent. How fascinating that Labour is starting to hope that if it adds its support to the support for New Zealand First, the Greens, Jim Anderton, and the Monster Raving Loony Party, it might just cobble together sufficient votes to get across the 5 percent MMP threshold next year. How insightful that Mr Parker spoke of losing the plot. If I had dug a hole as deep and wide as the one he and his mates are still digging, I would want to lose it too.

This is the last general debate of the year. In my usual humble fashion and in the manner to which I have become accustomed, I am its final speaker. So it falls to me to perform the last rites for the lacklustre, leaderless, limp, and lamentable Labour Party. I take the responsibility seriously, knowing that as Michael Joseph Savage, Peter Fraser, and Norman Kirk have spun in their graves in recent times at the shambolic efforts of their modern day successors, the kindest act we can all perform is to find Mr Parker’s lost plot, ease the whole Labour caucus into it gently, and wait for some decent opposition from the pixies, fairies, and goblins who will so surely have replaced them by this time next year. Merry Christmas.
