Hon ANNETTE KING (Deputy Leader—Labour) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. As New Zealanders, we like to think that our country is the best place in the world to bring up children. We call New Zealand God’s own. We say things like: “The future of New Zealand is with our children.”, “Our children are our greatest assets.”, “Every child deserves a decent standard of living.”, “Every child should have the ability to reach their full potential.”, and “We value our children, and children matter.” How many times have we heard those words spoken in this House? I believe that all those statements are true, and I doubt that any member in the House does not believe they are true. But are they true for every child in this country? That is the question that we should be asking ourselves in this House.

Today we heard the compassionate and soft face of National! We heard Judith Collins answer a question about child poverty and she made it very clear National has no interest in what happens to children in this country. She has no interest in working across this House, in line with the request of Every Child Counts that parties in this House work together for the good of children. The organisation made that request in all sincerity to this House. Whom does Every Child Counts represent? It represents the major organisations in New Zealand that look after, and care for, children and families, from Barnardos and Plunket to kindergartens, iwi organisations, whānau organisations, and so on.

Every Child Counts is a major organisation, and it has pleaded with this House for us to form a cross-party group to work for the benefit of children. In good faith I took its request to the Prime Minister of this country, 1 year ago. He turned it down. I have that letter here and I will seek leave to table it later on. He turned it down and said he was not interested. He was not interested, and I have waited 1 year to see what this Government would do in terms of children in this country. I waited to see whether it would put children first and whether children would become a major focus of this Government’s expenditure and policy development.

Have we heard about children? As the Child Poverty Action Group said, children have become invisible in New Zealand. They are invisible to this Government. The Government will say it has done so much for children. It had an advertising campaign to stop parents from shaking babies. It has done so much for children that it finds it hard to write it on a postage stamp!

Well, the truth is New Zealand is not doing well for our children; we are not doing as well as comparable countries. We sit at the bottom third of the OECD rankings in terms of most child indicators. Many of our children in this country are left behind. Their early life experiences are harmful and ongoing. Many Māori and Pasifika children have poorer life chances than other children of the same age.

Contrary to Mrs Collins’ statements in this House, the Labour Government did make some progress on child poverty in this country. We made it with positive actions like Working for Families, which took 132,000 families out of poverty in this country. We brought in affordable primary health care to reduce the cost of going to the doctor. We restored income-related rents and introduced 20 free hours of early childhood education. And we reduced the number on the unemployment benefit to 22,000, compared with the 65,000 we have now, and that number is growing.

But I have to say that is not enough. As far as Labour is concerned, there is unfinished business when it comes to child poverty in this country. We ask the Government whether it will do something about it. It has had 2 years to address child poverty. We heard today that it will not make a commitment to reducing child poverty in this country. I need to tell those members opposite who sit on the front bench and make out that they care about children that child poverty in this country is growing yet again. The numbers are going up right now and they will continue to go up, as long as the Government continues to follow its current policies. I recently read Plunket’s annual report in which it stated: “You need to invest in the first 3 years of life.”

Dr KENNEDY GRAHAM (Green) : I rise to address the issue of climate change and this Government’s failure to develop adequate national policy to combat it.

Climate change has slipped below the threshold of daily media focus, and that is the way that this Government seems to want it. The failure at Copenhagen to tackle the global threat head on has sent the international community into a state of collective catatonia. We see this in the lack of leadership from the UN, in the actions of national Governments around the world, and in the attitude of much of the public around the world.

The problem we have is that nature is not disposed to wait for humanity to iron itself out morally and get its political act together. The poor countries rail against us for historical responsibility and insufficient reduction targets. The rich countries fear the projected population growth among the poor and insist that they enter binding commitments before we sign on to medium-term cuts.

Humanity probably faces only two global threats: immolation through nuclear conflict, or suffocation through global warming. The first is the product of traditional enmity; the enemy was the other tribe or the other nation. Climate change is the product of a new enemy: us. We try to cut nuclear arsenals by changing the enemy’s behaviour; we are required to cut carbon emissions by changing our own behaviour. It is no surprise that we are not succeeding. Most Governments lack the political courage to convey the magnitude of the climate change threat to their peoples, and they lack the political insight to prescribe the required global and national policies that are necessary.

Before, during, and since Copenhagen the threat of serious unpredictable climate change has grown. Our scientists do not know when non-linear change might occur, but they warn that tipping points exist. If the precautionary principle is to mean anything, we must all move with speedy purpose and resolve. Translated politically, that means we must act not as an international community of States, but as a global community of peoples who are represented by Governments. If the difference seems vanishingly small, then we do well to act on it none the less, lest our prospects of survival prove to be the same.

Our professional negotiators are rearranging the deckchairs contemplating whether we shall have one or two legal agreements, and whether it will be next year, or 3 or 10 years from now. Our political leaders dampen our expectations with appeals to realism. We all suffer from cognitive dissonance. Every so often we see the magnitude and imminence of the threat, but it is simply too frightening to accept individually and politically, so we basically return to business and government as usual.

As the Minister made clear recently in question time, the state of play is the Copenhagen Accord, with voluntary commitments to national cuts. These are demonstrably inadequate to the science-based judgment of what is required to avert failure, but we pretend that it is a useful start to greater things. We are told that global emissions must peak within about 7 years, and we know that the accord is way, way short of achieving that, so we mumble about bigger cuts later and avoid looking into our children’s eyes.

Let us address some facts. To achieve a 2 degrees Celsius threshold, we must reduce our global carbon budget from 50 gigatonnes today to 36 by 2020, and to seven by 2050. The rich countries must cut from about 40 today to 11 by 2020 and to one by 2050. That is correct: we in the rich world must emit only one gigatonne in 2050, out of the seven emitted by the world that year. It is called contraction and convergence, and it is the only way humanity will successfully deal with climate change. That is when our moral and political standards merge at the global level. After 10 millennia, especially the past two centuries, it is the moment of truth.

For our part, New Zealand has to agree through treaty or by voluntary declaration in advance to cut our national emissions proportionately. That means we must cut from 78 million tonnes today to 56 million tonnes in 2020, down to 1.6 million in 2050. That is the scale of the challenge before New Zealand. It is as well that we face up to it now, not when it is too late.

Hon PHIL HEATLEY (Minister of Housing) : I appreciate this opportunity to address the House. It would be fair to say that the National Government team, the MP team and certainly the team in Cabinet, are very, very positive about the progress that we have made in the last 2 years, while going through a very, very difficult time. In the area of my portfolio I could not be happier with the way that Housing New Zealand Corporation has managed its work during this recession. There has been more pressure on the State house waiting list and on the stock than we have seen for a couple of decades. Why? Because during the worldwide recession many, many families have come under pressure, either because of the loss of jobs or because of reduced hours being worked. Because of the difficulties in a worldwide recession, families have had to close their cheque books, and their ability to spend on simple items such as rent, mortgages, and those types of things has become quite difficult. During that time Housing New Zealand Corporation has managed to maintain the waiting list at a level that has been typical of the last decade. We have not seen the waiting list shoot up by 3,000 or anything like that; it has tended to oscillate at around the 10,000 mark.

I have to commend the corporation, because it has done this through a variety of means. One, of course, is the Options and Advice Service, which the National Government introduced. When people rock up to Housing New Zealand Corporation and say they need a State home, instead of the corporation writing down their names and putting them on the waiting list it now takes them through some options. One option is to remain where they are. The corporation asks whether it can help them to remain where they are, and outlines subsidies like the accommodation supplement. Another option is private rental—the possibility that they could rent privately. The corporation outlines subsidies for them to do that.

Another option, of course, is homeownership. Here is a good one; this will fascinate members. In the last 18 months about 400 people have walked into Housing New Zealand Corporation—so that members do not misunderstand this, I say thousands of people have walked into Housing New Zealand Corporation, but they have included 400 people in particular who would, under the previous Labour Government, have been put on the waiting list. Can members guess what those people did after talking to the Options and Advice Service? Members will not believe this: they went to buy a home. The previous Government would have put them on the waiting list. They got some advice, turned round on their heels, went out, and it was their intention to buy a home. The Labour Government would have put them on the waiting list. It is unbelievable. All that Housing New Zealand Corporation did was to ask them whether they were sure that they wanted to go on the waiting list. Those people asked whether there were other options, and the corporation said yes, there were options like staying where they were, and the corporation would help them; renting privately; renting with another community housing provider; or even buying a home. They said: “Oh, we could buy a home?”. We said that if they wanted to do that, we could even help them to do so.

The previous Labour Government would have told those people to go on the waiting list. They loved it for people like them to become welfare beneficiaries, because then they would become dependent on the great Labour Party and vote for it for the rest of their lives. Well, here is a crack-up: 400 people turned on their heels and decided that rather than going into a State house, they preferred to go and buy a home. Many of them, I understand, have pursued that.

For the very same reason we believe that it is worthwhile to look at the almost 70,000 houses that we now have under the corporation’s management, and to ask whether we are always housing the people who are most in need—because the answer is no. The reality is that when we have a couple in Māngere at this very moment who have a four-bedroom home, and who originally had one adult child and two dependent children—so that is five people in a four-bedroom house—but who now live alone with three spare bedrooms, those tenants could be matched to a one or two-bedroom home. We have another single mother with three children boarding with relatives in a garage in Māngere, and they should be in the home that has three spare bedrooms. That is why we are not ashamed to make this conversation public and have a debate on the issue.

Dr PAUL HUTCHISON (National—Hunua) : I heard only a few weeks ago from one of New Zealand’s leading children’s organisations that the former, failed Labour Government, under its Working for Families policy, failed thousands of New Zealand children.

We notched up yet another success last Friday with the opening of Waiuku Health Plus, a primary integrated medical health centre, by the Minister of Health and me in the excellent Hunua electorate, which is, by chance, the very electorate that I am privileged to represent. It is yet another sign of the significant gains to health services under the National Government. Firstly, I congratulate Dr Mark Eustace, his wife, Jo, the many medical staff and other colleagues, and the developer. The other thing that the New Zealand Public Health and Disability Amendment Bill will do is ensure that back-office bureaucracy is minimised and the resources moved to the front line, which is something that will be more and more prevalent under this excellent National Government.

Hon Sir ROGER DOUGLAS (ACT) : For the past 15 years New Zealand has been slipping slowly—some people would say, not so slowly—into economic difficulty; some might even say that it is headed towards chaos. This has happened over the past 15 years because both Labour and National have been prepared to compromise any principles they claim to have. We have seen a preparedness to write a cheque for whatever demands the latest focus-group puts out there. The worst case of this was probably at the 2005 election when Labour was re-elected, probably thanks to interest-free student loans. That did the trick. This year it is putting up the idea that it would take GST off some items of food. That is why New Zealand is in economic difficulty. I must say that it is not helped by a National Government that says: “These things are sacred. We will not touch the interest-free student loans.”, even when it knows it is madness; “We will not look at the age of retirement of 65.”, when it knows that the big issue that will confront New Zealand over the next 10 or 20 years is the clash between young and old, as the young start to say that they are either leaving this country, or they are not prepared to pay for the benefits at the current level. The National Government will not touch Working for Families and it will not even look at the issue of privatisation, even if it means higher productivity.

The fact is that we as a nation are borrowing $250 million a week, or $13 billion a year. For a family of four, we are borrowing about $240 a week. We have had a situation where over the last 11 years this country has increased Government expenditure out of all proportion to what is reasonable. We have seen a situation whereby Government spending per person under Labour increased over 9 years by $5,500 in real terms, and in 2 years under National it has increased by $1,500 in real terms. Government spending has increased by $7,000 per person in real terms, or by $525 a week for a family of four.

We might well ask what we as a nation get for that increased spending of $7,000 per person, or $525 a week for a family of four. Do all children receive a decent education as a result of this massive increase in public spending? No. Do most people retire with enough money to live in comfort in this country? No. Does everyone receive health treatment when they need it? We have increased public expenditure by $7,000 per person, or by $525 a week for a family of four. Do they get health treatment when they need it? No. Have we eradicated poverty? No. On the very goals that the welfare State has sought to achieve, no one in this country can genuinely argue that we have succeeded. Even the modern-day proponents of the welfare State, be it National, Labour, the Greens, or the Māori Party know deep down that it has failed. But they think they have the solution, that all we need to do is increase spending; but increasing Government spending by $7,000 per person in real terms over 11 years has not done it—maybe $10,000 will! The fact is that National and Labour should apologise to the people of this country. They do not really care. The only thing they care about is re-election.
