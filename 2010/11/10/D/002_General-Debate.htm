General Debate

Hon GERRY BROWNLEE (Minister for Economic Development) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. I am able to confirm to the House today that the Labour Party is a rift of splits and divisions, and that the statements made at the time that the Hon Chris Carter exited the party were absolutely correct. There has been a lot of speculation about that magic number, 17. Who were the 17 people signed up, ready to go, wanting to give Phil Goff the shove? It has been a mystery up until now. We knew that Charles Chauvel was there and we knew that George Hawkins was there, but we had an amazing revelation last night in the Chamber. When the Labour Party was voting on the Rugby World Cup 2011 (Empowering) Bill it managed to split its vote into four different categories—those who were in favour, those who were against, those who were abstaining, and those who just did not know what to do. The interesting thing is that the Noes, the abstentions, and the “I don’t know what to do’s” come to 17.

One would think that on a bill such as the Rugby World Cup 2011 (Empowering) Bill, which is designed to make sure this is a very successful event for New Zealand, the Labour members, who have been through contortions at the Government Administration Committee for months, would have followed the advice of Trevor Mallard—a major factional leader—and said to him: “We’re with you, Trevor.” But, no, they did not do that. They split themselves into all sorts of factional camps.

In just 2 short weeks the Labour Party has voted against the success of TheHobbit and against the success of the Rugby World Cup. Who are these people? It will not come as any surprise that Brendon Burns is in that camp. It will not come as any surprise that Lianne Dalziel and Ruth Dyson are in that camp. Ruth Dyson may well be the leader of that camp now; I am not sure. There is also the Hon Nanaia Mahuta. There is also the Hon—no, he is not—there is also Ross Robertson. That is a true shock to me. That man regularly comes to Parliament adorned in all sorts of clothing, one of which he wears proudly is the blazer belonging to the Parliamentary Rugby Team, yet he is voting against the bill. It is a factional decision on his part, but I suspect it is also about securing his future. There is also William Sio, which is no surprise, and David Shearer, which is no surprise, although how disappointing for Mr Goff that a man he has been so close to has lost his confidence. Then, of course, there is Maryan Street. She is also tipped to be deputy to Ruth Dyson in this new faction.

But there are a couple of names here that are very surprising. Firstly, there is the Hon Damien O’Connor. That is a true surprise, but here is the biggie: Clayton Cosgrove. Clayton Cosgrove was not a yes or a no, he was an abstention. Actually, he may ruin my theory because I suspect that he is out there, wanting to set up his own faction, and he would be a faction of one.

There are two other interesting names. One is Lynne Pillay. I suspect that she just lost her way to the Chamber, which would be nothing new. Then, finally, we have Shane Jones.

Hon Maurice Williamson: How did he vote?

Hon GERRY BROWNLEE: He did not vote at all.

Hon Tau Henare: He wants to be the video ref!

Hon GERRY BROWNLEE: No, I think Shane Jones was out there, probably consulting the Ayes, he probably consulted the Noes, went off to have a talk to the abstainers, and then just got lost on the way to the Chamber. He has no direction whatsoever.

So there we have it: the 17 fingered by Chris Carter. These are the people who are all signed up and all set to go to say goodbye to Mr Goff. It is no surprise. Mr Goff has spent most of the last 6 months trying to find out what John Key’s appointments were around the country in order that he might turn up on those occasions to get a photograph opportunity with the Prime Minister. It is great to be able to confirm to the House that these splits, these divisions, and these factions are live and well in the Labour Party.

Hon ANNETTE KING (Deputy Leader—Labour) : An amusing little speech there from the Leader of the House, a man of substance who always makes a major contribution on the issues of the day. An old saying comes to mind when we look at the flurry of activity of the National Party around the Mana by-election. That old saying that comes to mind is “lies, damned lies, and statistics”, and we have had a liberal dose of all three over the past month. The bemused people of Mana have watched the passing parade of Ministers, including the Prime Minister, swanning through their electorate. Well, that was a place those Ministers would not have been seen dead in a few weeks ago. They thought of places like Porirua and Tītahi Bay very much in the same way that John Banks, their man in Auckland, thought of South Auckland. But the by-election has ignited a sense of panic in the National ranks, because those members know that they must have a strong endorsement of their policies on 20 November. It will not be good enough to get somewhere near their last result; they will need a ringing endorsement of their policies in the by-election. They will have to really perform on 20 November.

You know, it was not so important at the time of the Mt Albert by-election; in fact, perfunctory attention was paid to the outcome of the Mt Albert by-election. After all, National put “The Brains Trust” in charge of the election campaign—Jonathan Coleman—the person who knows as much about campaigning as he does about giving somebody else’s speech in Parliament and not knowing that it is not National’s policy. National did not really worry about the Mt Albert by-election. John Key made a couple of visits and had his photograph taken, and then he abandoned the National candidate for Mt Albert. He did not even bother to turn up on election night to be there with the defeated candidate. For me, that was the moment when I got the measure of the man—the measure of this Prime Minister. He did not pass a fundamental test of leadership. A good leader is there in the good times and the bad, and a good leader has loyalty. John Key was not there at Mt Albert.

Two years on, and there is a by-election in Mana. Well, we no longer have a new Government, as those members like to call themselves. They have been in power for 2 years and it is time they took responsibility for the decisions that they are making. We have had two Budgets since the election—two Budgets. All those decisions are Bill English’s decisions, and he does not take any responsibility for them. We can look at him in the House, day after day, blaming everybody else except himself and taking no responsibility.

In Mana we have also seen major changes being made to the lives of New Zealanders. Many old people, young people, and low-income and middle-income people in the Mana electorate have been detrimentally affected by the Government. It has passed many pieces of legislation. It had a Job Summit that did not produce any jobs. There have been countless reviews, task force inquiries, rising unemployment rates, a rising cost of living, and no plan for the future. There is no brighter future, just slogans. All we have nowadays is the politics of “smile and walk away”. How will National convince the people of Mana to give it a strong endorsement? It must get a better vote than in 2008, and that will be very difficult.

We can take one issue: health. The old people of Mana have just been told that on 1 December their home help will be cut unless they have the community services card. That means if older people receive over $399 a week, they will not be able to get home help because they are over the limit for the community services card. That means they will pay for their home help. When we take off the cost of living for an old person, he or she has about $124 a week to live on. We would need to take $30 from that to pay for that person’s home care. That is what National is doing to the old people of Mana, and it believes that those people will turn out to vote for it. I say that National members are dreaming. They are in cloud-cuckoo-land.

Apart from the fact that Phil Goff seems to talk about everything and know very little, there is no leadership in the Labour Party.

I bet Chris Carter was sitting in the back of the room. It is outrageous that we should see Labour in such a mess that it has a split vote on a closure motion.

How a former Minister for the Rugby World Cup can abstain—abstain—from voting on a bill that will ensure that the Rugby World Cup is a success is beyond me.

While the Opposition is in disarray, this National Government is getting on with the things that matter to New Zealand. Absolutely! The important things that matter to this—[Interruption]

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: I will take the opportunity this afternoon, whilst the Opposition is in such disarray, to talk about what is happening in the rest of the world. I recently attended a meeting of OECD Ministers of Education. It was amazing, when we are in the middle of industrial negotiations with teacher unions, to realise what is happening in the rest of the world and to realise how good we have it here under this National-led Government. In Italy 42,000 teaching jobs are being lost—42,000. In France next year 13,500 teaching jobs will go, and they are looking at losing 40,000-odd teachers. In Russia they are talking about losing 200,000 teachers out of their system in order to balance their education budgets—200,000 teachers. In Ireland there is a 2-year pay freeze for public servants, which of course includes teachers. In England they have just announced their Budget. There is growth of 0.1 percent in real terms each year for the education budget in the next few years. That means that schools will have to save about a billion dollars; they will have to make savings of around a billion dollars.

That is extraordinary, compared with this country, where we put a new $300 million into education last year and the year before that. I read on a website today that schools in England will be forced to publish teachers’ salaries, qualifications, and sickness rates in order to determine whether they offer value for money.

When we look at what the National Government is doing here in New Zealand, we see that we are focused on lifting long-term economic growth, in which we know education plays a key part. We want to make sure that all our children learn to read, write, and do maths while they are in primary school. We want to make sure that every child leaves school with a qualification that will enable them to succeed in this modern world. We know that we had to rebalance our tax system. We know we need better, smarter public services.

Hon DARREN HUGHES (Labour) : If any member of the executive greeted with great delight the announcement made by the Prime Minister this week that there will not be a major Cabinet reshuffle before the general election, it would have been the Hon Anne Tolley. I think her lack of passion in describing her education portfolio, at the tail end of her speech, just shows why, when everyone thought the skids would be put under her before the election, she must have been so happy at John Key’s announcement that there would be no change in Cabinet. The national standards slogan that the Minister is trying to turn into a policy is a disaster, up hill and down dale. Anybody who delves into the issue to understand it gets to see that it was a slogan for an election; it was never meant to be a policy for a country. That is what all the people tell us who sit in meetings and look at the issue in a clever way—be they parents, students, or teachers. The same thing comes through time and time again: the slogan does not work as a policy. But then, of course, it is part of the whole mantra of “smile and wave”.

I want to follow on from what the deputy leader of the Labour Party, the Hon Annette King, had to say about the Mana by-election. There are some really important issues for that by-election, to be held on 20 November, that we believe are worth exploring. Annette King has talked about the health issues in that electorate. I want to touch on some of the transport issues in the Mana electorate.

At the last general election, residents of the Mana and Ōtaki electorates thought there was a bipartisan transport policy between Labour and National when it came to transport. They thought that both parties were saying the same thing. In fact, the National candidates went quite some way to wrap themselves in the cloak of the Labour Government of that time. They said they believed in Transmission Gully, and they said they believed in the 90 percent Government subsidy for a local two-lane Western Link Road, like the one that the Labour Party had proposed and funded. They said they believed in the further electrification and double tracking of rail. Those were the things they said at the time of the general election.

Then, about a year or so ago, in the dead of night, Steven Joyce, the Minister of Transport, who runs large swathes of the Government behind closed doors, decided he had a better idea than everybody else—a bit of a theme there, actually. He thought he knew better than absolutely everybody else. He turned up and said he had lived in the community 35 years ago, so therefore he knew what the answer should be. He said a four-lane, 110 kilometre an hour motorway, an autobahn, should go right through the whole of the Kapiti coast. That is what he said he was going to do, but that was not what the National Party had campaigned on, and the community was taken by huge surprise because of that.

Of course, during the consultation period the big, bold, decisive, brave, and courageous National MPs in the area, when asked what their opinion was, said nothing. They had no opinion on the preferred option in the consultation period. They had absolutely nothing to say about it at all. They sat on the fence, waiting and waiting to see what the Minister of Transport would decide. As soon as he made a decision, they were gung-ho behind him—the greatest cheerleaders ever to have walked along the Kapiti coast. That is how bold and brave they were.

On Sunday this week the people of Kapiti were meant to find out where the expressway will be going. The New Zealand Transport Agency spent $22,000 on inviting people from all around the region to come and see the plans. As we heard in question time yesterday and today, Steven Joyce, the Harry Potter equivalent of Lord Voldemort in this Government, discovered that this issue would be coming up before the by-election, and he decided—

Hon Tau Henare: Harry Potter saves the day.

Hon DARREN HUGHES: If only Tau Henare had gone to Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry, he might be in a better state than he is in. But he would join any old organisation, which is why he is sitting over there on the National benches at the present time. As soon as Steven Joyce found out that this issue was coming up too close to the Mana by-election, he cancelled the consultation. On 2 November the Government cancelled the consultation, due to take place on 7 November, until after the Mana by-election.

Do members not think that the National MPs for the area would be outraged at this disenfranchisement of the people of Kapiti? Do they not think the National MPs would be waving around pieces of paper, saying that it was not good enough, and that there comes a time when MPs have to put their community before their party, so they were breaking ranks with their Government in order to make sure that their people had a say before the by-election? But can members guess what? There was not a word, not a complaint, not a criticism, and not a demand for a new series of dates. The National MPs sat there boldly, saying absolutely nothing. They are lambs in Parliament and lambs in the electorate. It is absolutely unbelievable. Now we know that these plans will remain secret, because the Government is scared to release them to the Mana electorate. It is a disgrace. Government members should be upfront with the public about the situation. They should go back to Labour’s policy at the last election, which they said they were in favour of, as well.

PAUL QUINN (National) : I want to return to the theme that the mover of the motion started with, and that is the chaos that currently prevails within the Labour Party. We can have a look at one of the names that the Associate Minister and Leader of the House mentioned—Charles Chauvel. I do not know whether members noticed, but Charles was having a “Secret Squirrel” session with Chris Carter this afternoon, at the back of the House. Perhaps Charles has decided that the mood for change has re-emerged, and no doubt Charles wants to be at the front of the queue. Charles likes the baubles of office; there is no question about that. He wants to be where the winner will be. It is no wonder he was up there.

Grant Robertson spent most of the debate last night speaking against the motion. He rose three or four times last night to speak against the motion. I looked at the list. What does it show? It shows that he voted in favour of the motion. Grant cannot actually make up his mind. He naturally, probably, belongs with the Chris Carter faction—the 17 faction—but he has obviously decided that currently, as he has moved up in the ranks to the front of the 2008 fraternity, he clearly owes some patronage, some payback, to the current leader. So he obviously decided in the end that he is making better grass where he is at the moment. Clearly, though, he is ready to jump ship.

We then move to Iain Lees-Galloway. He spoke vigorously and regularly against the bill, but he voted for it. I am not quite sure, but he clearly was a mirror image of Grant. He is really in Phil’s camp, and unfortunately he got a bit betwixt and between. But that is not unusual. He proceeded to tell us how the annual Rugby Sevens tournament should be run, but he then admitted he had never been to one. But that is not unusual behaviour from Labour members. They spend most of their time lecturing people on things they have never known and never experienced.

I come now to Trevor Mallard. Here is a guy who has fought this particular bill, tooth and nail. He lost every battle—but I look at the voting sheet, and what has he done? He has voted in favour. Why did he do that? I suspect that it is because he wants to make sure that he gets invitations to the corporate box. He is a bit scared, and I know for a fact that he has already had a conversation with the New Zealand Rugby Football Union about getting free tickets to the Rugby World Cup. I know that for a fact. On the one hand he wants to fight the bill but on the other hand he knows that if he does not vote for it, the New Zealand Rugby Football Union will not give him tickets.

Shane Jones, my friend at the back, did not know what to do.

A point of order should not be used to interrupt the speaker.

PAUL QUINN: Thank you, Mr Speaker. I come now to Shane. Shane did not vote, at all. He wanted a foot in both camps. Do members know why he wants a foot in both camps?

Hon Tau Henare: He wasn’t here.

PAUL QUINN: Well, he wants to be the TMO. He wants to watch the video.

Hon Tau Henare: Wants to be the video ref.

PAUL QUINN: He wants to be the video ref—the TMO—but he is also a bit worried about making sure he gets the corporate hospitality. What he does not know is that he is, I think, off the list for corporate hospitality, because people do not want to host people who watch bad movies.

Let me finish by mentioning Sue Moroney.

Mr SPEAKER: The member’s time has expired.

SUE MORONEY (Labour) : The speech made by the member who has just resumed his seat, Paul Quinn, is evidence that the Government is clearly struggling. Not only has it put up Paul Quinn for the general debate but it is talking about a personal vote and how the Opposition voted on a personal vote. The Government is clearly struggling, and that was evidenced by Paul Quinn, who suffers from being two inches shorter that he needed to be to become an All Black, according to the rugby fraternity. But I think that sad speech he made is just more evidence that he is suffering from short man’s legacy.

I want to focus on the poll that is coming up, and we will see whether the Government is struggling. I refer to the poll that is about to happen in Mana. We know that it will be a poll on the Government’s policies. It has to be, because Hekia Parata said she does not do second place. So if she comes second in that by-election, it must be a commentary on the Government’s policies. John Key knows this.

Hekia Parata: It’s a highly aspirational electorate that’s been held for 60 years by a low-ambition Government.

SUE MORONEY: She is very loud here in the House, but we will see how she gets on in the electorate.

John Key knows that the Mana by-election will be about the Government’s policies. The New Zealand Herald of 24 September said we should not blame him for chasing every potential voter in Porirua and Whitby. Then it went on to state: “It was reminiscent of a scene in The Simpsons movie where the US Government, having lost its credibility, gets super-nice-guy and actor Tom Hanks to announce the destruction of the town of Springfield while gently rustling a boy’s hair.” That is what the New Zealand Herald had to say about John Key and his campaign style, out in the electorate of Mana.

Of course, John Key has gone to a few early childhood education centres. He was very brave to do that, because we all know that the National Government has cut funding to early childhood education, thereby increasing costs to families and making it extremely difficult for the parents of Mana to have their children participating in early childhood education. John Key visited Mana Montessori, a centre that has 60 children, and, yes, the quality of education for those children is in doubt because it is one of the centres where funding has been cut by John Key. He cut the funding in the Budget, then he visited the early childhood centres and stroked a small boy’s hair, just like Tom Hanks in The Simpsons. But it did not work very well. The school head, Nicola Dickie, said she wished there had been no funding cuts to early childhood education and that the school was likely to introduce a fee increase for parents. What will that do for the young children of Mana and their future? We know that families really struggle when fees go up.

What about First Five Community Childhood Centre? I do not know whether Hekia Parata has been there, but I know that Kris Fa’afoi has. He is an excellent candidate for Labour, and he has been to visit the centre. That centre has 34 children, and it will also face a funding cut. In May, its treasurer, David Conwell, said there was no choice but to increase fees for care over the 20 hours scheme. He said that parents using that centre would be facing increases of $40 to $50 per week per child. He said that the parents were shocked but they understood.

One of the parents actually knows about this because she is also on the management committee, and her name is Niki Alefosio. She has two children at the centre, and she knows that she now has to find $100 extra per week to keep sending her two children to that centre. She is part of the centre’s cooperative management group, and says that if she takes into account other factors like rising GST—another National Government policy—she is looking at an extra $10 per day per child. She says she may need to look at cutting back on family outings or savings. That is the sort of impact that this Government’s policies are having on the families of Mana. Mrs Alefosio works 30 hours a week, but she said part-time work may not now be viable. She said she may now need to look at going to full-time work or looking at another form of childcare, just to make ends meet.

Then there is Jane Wild, who also has two children at the same centre. She said that the funding cuts were “really stink”—that is what she had to say about it. Her son Max receives 20 free hours of childcare and she pays for an extra 4 hours. She also has a 1-year-old by the name of Cameron, and he goes to that centre for 16 hours. She will have to pay extra for him. That is the effect of the Government’s policies in Mana.

CHRIS AUCHINVOLE (National—West Coast - Tasman) : We have just heard from my parliamentary colleague Sue Moroney. Yet again she concentrated on Mana. It seems to me that Labour members are concerned about Mana, and indeed they should be. If they do not get more than their current majority, then it says a lot about Labour.

It seems that Labour cannot even run its own caucus; I dread to think how it would try to run the country. [Interruption] Yes, it really does pose questions. One need look only at the votes last night to see that Labour is at sixes and sevens as a party.

Hon Member: Six and 17.

CHRIS AUCHINVOLE: Yes, six and 17. On a matter of national significance, a bill that ascertains how we are seen internationally, Labour splits its vote. It seems it is as divided on this matter as it is on the party leadership. The Labour Party cast 23 votes in favour and 11 votes against, and four abstentions—abstentions. These members of Parliament are paid to make decisions, not to sit on the proverbial fence, twiddling their thumbs, leaving the hard thinking to the rest of Parliament. If they abstain on this one, one wonders what else they might abstain on.

Hon Trevor Mallard: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. There have been previous Speakers’ rulings about tedious repetition. There is tedious repetition from one speech to another. Maybe they should just table the research unit notes.

Mr SPEAKER: We will not have any further points of order on this. The member should not interrupt the member’s speech like that; that is not fair.

Hon John Carter: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I am sorry to interrupt. There have also been Speakers’ rulings about idiot interruptions through points of order. The member should be brought to order; it is not good enough.

Mr SPEAKER: I do not know where the member has been for lunch, but it is not hard to see that I am on my feet. He will be silent. Points of order should not be used to call a member what that member just called him; I will not repeat it. I have already reprimanded the Hon Trevor Mallard for frivolously interrupting a member’s speech. At least it was in moderate humour and it was not nasty. The member raised this point of order in quite a nasty way, and that is not helpful. I go back to the first point that members should not interrupt, especially in the general debate.

Hon John Carter: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I am quite embarrassed by the comment you made about my lunch. It was quite inappropriate, actually. For lunch, I was not very well and I had a piece of chicken, if that is of any use to the House.

Mr SPEAKER: The member will resume his seat immediately. If the member was not aware what he said, I say that he called another member of this House an idiot under a point of order. That is totally unacceptable. He continued to interject when I was on my feet. He will not do that.

CHRIS AUCHINVOLE: Thinking of the point of order that was raised by the Hon Trevor Mallard, it is interesting that he talked about repetition. Goodness me! After wondering who the shadow Leader of the House is, one has to wonder at the repetition of his points of order—it is all the time, and it goes on.

Let us come back to the point that I know minds are seized with, and that is the fact that the country cannot afford to be mired in the sort of prevarication that Labour members have been. In military parlance, yesterday’s performance by Labour can be seen as the “Charge of the Labour Extremely Light Brigade”. Those members not only charged in the wrong direction—the wrong way—but they went in all directions at once. My parliamentary colleague Mr Damien O’Connor—back in the saddle, I am told—leapt on to the horse and went in completely the wrong direction altogether. To top it all off, we had a number of desertions from the ranks.

On a slightly more serious note, why do Labour members hate success? They hate success. It seems that everything that might benefit New Zealand, such as The Hobbit and the cycleways, Labour is dead against—dead against—and it will do everything it can to vote against it. Is Labour seriously trying? Is this perhaps a cunning Labour plot to seriously damage New Zealand’s tourism potential? We in National fortunately are more focused and we are working hard on the real issues that affect New Zealanders on a daily basis. The National-led Government has maintained a clear and unrelenting focus on employment in the midst of a recession.

I was very taken by the very beginning of the Prime Minister’s statement, which stated that we would protect the entitlements of beneficiaries. We have done that. When we have made changes to the cost of living, when we have made changes to GST, we have compensated for that. I do not remember Labour engaging in any such activities.

Let us go through a list of the things we have done. We have a growth-enhancing tax system and we have better, smarter public services. With respect, the resolution of the neurosurgical questions in Dunedin came to me as a surprise during question time. I think the answer was fully deserving—fully deserving—of strong and sustained spontaneous applause, because that is what it merited. We are lifting education, we are lifting skills, and no matter how rude Labour might be towards our Minister of Education, she is doing a great job.
