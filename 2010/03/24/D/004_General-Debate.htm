Hon Dr JONATHAN COLEMAN (Minister of Immigration) : I move, That the House take note of miscellaneous business. News just in from Great Barrier Island: Phil Goff is there on yet another tour. I do not know how many tours he has made this year, but I recall that he has been on the “Axe the Tax” tour and the Harley Davidson tour. Of course, we are looking forward to the valedictory tour soon, because Labour members cannot wait to see that happen. They know that they are in a dire situation. They know that they have not got a chance with Phil Goff at the helm.

It will be interesting to hear what is actually happening on Great Barrier Island today, because I predict that Mr Goff will not be talking about the 74 mines on public land that he was part of authorising under the previous Government. He will not be telling the good people of Great Barrier Island about the Pike River mine. He will not be talking about gold and gem concessions in Paparoa National Park. No, I think he will be turning on one of his usual acts where he pretends to be at one with the local people.

But, you know, the reality of it all is that Phil Goff, I believe, does not have any real problem with mining. I do not believe that he has any objection at all. What he does not like is the idea of New Zealanders becoming wealthier and doing better, because he is the leader of the party of zero aspiration. The problem for Labour members is that if New Zealanders do better and become wealthier, their party does not get in. Fundamentally, they do not want to see New Zealand doing better, because while New Zealand is doing better there is no way that Labour will get a run on the Treasury benches from the electorate. There is something fundamental in everything that the Labour Party says: it is against everything, but we do not know what it is for. Phil Goff went on the “Axe the Tax” bus, but he could not say whether he would reverse a rise in GST. He cannot tell us whether he will reverse any of the changes to the welfare system that will make New Zealanders get out there, work harder, and further their lot in life.

If the New Zealand public decides that mining on conservation land can go ahead, I bet that if Phil Goff gets the chance, he will not turn that decision round, because Phil Goff is an opportunist who is running out of opportunities. Darren Hughes over there is nodding—he is quite happy. Members opposite know that things are getting worse and worse for them. They have seen what has happened in Australia with Tony Abbott, who is someone in Opposition who actually believes in something, and they are thinking that if they put Trevor Mallard up as leader, they might start getting some traction. The public cannot stand Trevor Mallard, but at least they know what he stands for. With Phil Goff, they are in real danger. With him they have someone who has been all over the paddock. When he is on Great Barrier Island today, I do not think he will be talking about the time when he was part of a hard-right Labour Government in the 1980s, when he was demanding hard-right reforms. He likes to distance himself from Roger Douglas these days, but that is his political pedigree. When we look at Phil Goff’s record over the years we see that, yes, he has gone back and forth many times on many things.

The one thing he has not managed to do while he has been the Leader of the Opposition is outline a plan for growing the economy. What our Prime Minister has done is say that New Zealand has to do better as a country. We have to earn more. We have to provide incentives for people to get off welfare and better themselves in life. There is a coherent plan. Every week the Government is rolling out policies that will make New Zealanders better off and wealthier, and give us the income we need to pay for social services, hospitals, and the police force. Labour members want to keep the people in South Auckland subjugated and below the radar. They want to maintain the huge welfare spend. Their basic tactic is to tell people on a benefit not to worry, because Labour will look after them; all they have to do is vote Labour, not get above their station in life, and not do a little bit better. They should not listen to Mr Key. They should not get their kids educated properly so that they can get a university degree and realise their potential. No, Labour wants to keep them there on the bottom level.

I do not think Mr Goff will today be telling the people of Great Barrier Island how last year he was very happy to take the Labour caucus down to Blackball to celebrate Labour’s mining roots, because he says one thing and does another, depending on whom he is talking to.

The previous speaker, the Hon Dr Jonathan Coleman, was the member who destroyed the by-election campaign in Mt Albert, and he was offering advice about leadership. He is the “Maestro of Self-defeat.” He is also the man who distinguished himself recently by being kicked out of this Chamber for refusing to apologise. Second, it has decided that the quick-fix remedy is to dig up the countryside. The jobs will be scarce and the degradation in those very sensitive areas will be very long term, and, I would think, irreversible. The third and most egregious failure afflicting the Government lies in Auckland. That is where the vast majority of voters are settled. They know that over 50 to 60 percent of their current democratic influence has been handed over to shadowy organisations, hand-picked by Rodney Hide. This is a travesty. The environment is being destroyed, along with the Government’s political reputation.

JOHN BOSCAWEN (ACT) : I rise this afternoon on behalf of all New Zealanders: farmers, business people, employers, and families. On 1 July—in just over 3 months—this Government will force upon the people of New Zealand an experiment, a grand, new experiment, unlike anything launched anywhere else in the world. [Interruption] It is not surprising that there is so much banter and discussion in this House. All of the political parties in this House bar the ACT Party have been part of this huge fraud, this emissions trading scheme tax, that starts on 1 July.

Why is the ACT Party is alone in opposing this tax? From 1 July we can expect an immediate increase of 5 percent in electricity and 4 percent in petrol, and we can expect those increases to be doubled on 1 January 2013. The increased costs of electricity and petrol will flow through the whole economy. They will flow through the costs of everything—food, housing, and clothing—everything. Just a fortnight ago Alan Bollard, the Governor of the Reserve Bank, confirmed to the Finance and Expenditure Committee that his latest estimates for inflation take into account a 5 percent increase in electricity prices and a 4c per litre increase in petrol costs, as forecast by Treasury last November. Our families will pay, our businesses will pay, and our farmers will pay.

What makes this such a tragedy is that our emissions trading scheme tax goes further than similar taxes in any other country in the world. I say again that we are conducting an experiment unlike any other country in the world. We will be the first country to tax our farmers—that is right. Our farmers have enough to compete with in an unevenly trade-balanced world, but we are the first country in the world to tax our farmers.

Just last November the Prime Minister proudly told the Federated Farmers national executive that agriculture would not be coming into the emissions trading scheme until 2015. He said that we would not be taxing methane from animals until 2015. Farmers might have felt some sense of comfort from that. Well, they should not have done, because if we take average dairy farms, we see that no less than three-quarters of the tax that farmers will pay will come as a consequence of their use of electricity and petrol and through the emissions of dairy processing factories. Although it is all very well for the Prime Minister to tell farmers that they will not have to pay for the cost of their animals’ methane until 2015, farmers will be paying from 1 July this year. If any farmers are listening to this debate this afternoon, they might be surprised to find out that they are paying from 1 July, but it is a fact. It is a fact. Of the average $10,000 cost for dairy farmers, no less than three-quarters of what they will pay relates to petrol, electricity, and the cost of emissions.

I ask why we are doing this to ourselves. Our three major trading partners are Australia, China, and the United States. I ask what our three major trading partners are doing. Are they taxing their people or increasing the price of electricity for their citizens? No, they are not. The ACT Party stands alone in opposing this move. It is not surprising that the House has fallen into silence, because other members should be ashamed.

I ask why we are conducting this experiment. It is not too late to change. Overnight—12 hours ago—President Sarkozy of France announced that France is abandoning its intention to introduce a carbon tax. Why is that? It is because the French people have risen up in protest. Finally, they can see that France would be foolish to tax its people when other countries in Europe are not doing that. If Nicolas Sarkozy can show leadership and abandon the French carbon tax, as he has done in the past 24 hours, then I call upon the Prime Minister of New Zealand, John Key, to show the same leadership and show the people of New Zealand that things have changed in the last 5 months.

This House was told time and time again last year that we would not lead the world; we were to be a fast follower and our emissions trading scheme tax was to be based on that in Australia. What has happened is that there is no Australian emissions trading scheme tax. There is no United States emissions trading scheme tax and there is no such tax in China. Why do we allow the power generators to earn hidden profits and why do we tax our people this way?

Dr KENNEDY GRAHAM (Green) : This House is expecting Government legislation soon on regional governance in Canterbury. It arises from the ongoing tension that exists in Canterbury over the vexed issue of water. Canterbury’s water has become the bell-wether for the current political debate in New Zealand. This is between two alternative philosophies embraced by different sections of the New Zealand public and also in this House. The first postulates economic growth as the paramount value, yet in the primal mists of the political jungle this Government has an instinctive awareness that not all is right with the environment. It intuits that the people also are concerned, so it utters soothing noises to assuage the masses: “We shall balance our economic opportunities with our environmental responsibilities, and we have a subgroup—the Bluegreens—to salve your conscience. The Prime Minister himself is a member, so nothing to worry about.” The second philosophy embraces sustainable development: the economy is forever subordinate to the environment, whether or not humans choose to acknowledge it.

These alternative world views play out before our eyes over Canterbury water. The growth mania is at its height in Canterbury today, which comprises almost a quarter of New Zealand’s farmland. Dairy fever has hit its peak in my province; it has grown by 61 percent in 6 years, which is almost half the national increase. This is in a region where the natural climate is dry, but “not to worry; human technology has freed us from Nature’s primitive grasp”. So the thirst for irrigation is unquenchable.

The Canterbury region accounts for 70 percent of this nation’s irrigation, yet the dairy industry wants more, so the developers urge Environment Canterbury to grant consents, and Environment Canterbury responds with untrammelled accuracy that its job is to protect the environment. This enrages the developers, who conspire with the local authorities to instigate a ministerial review. This is conducted with great solemnity, involving a former Deputy Prime Minister as the chief independent reviewer. It is delivered with the sonorous tones of analytical dispassion and professional insight. The independent reviewers themselves assemble in the Minister’s room to brief MPs—an odd procedural arrangement in itself, but who is counting around here?

The Creech report recommends the replacement of the Environment Canterbury council with a temporary commission and a semi-permanent water authority, both appointed by, and answerable to, central government. Through a carefully calibrated form of tough love, local democracy is throttled and dies. In fact, the Creech report is a shoddy piece of work that fails rudimentary tests of professional standards.

First, the report lacks intellectual integrity. It criticises Environment Canterbury for being science-driven and not science-informed. The Creech report is politically driven and not politically informed. If it were politically informed, it would acknowledge that democracy is bigger than business; that the subsidiarity principle is bigger than Government; and that one does not replace elected councillors with appointees of central government, just because they are making decisions one might not like. That is political arrogance of the highest order.

Second, the report purports to be an organisational review but it sets no clear criteria for judging institutional efficiency that allows for contestable judgment. It cites a selective range of what it calls “external opinion”. I could assemble a selected range of external opinion that would say the opposite. It would have equal claim to partial truth.

Third, the report rests its views on what it calls the “enormous and unprecedented gap between Environment Canterbury’s responsibility and capability”, but although it makes a great play of advancing three options, it fails to include the obvious one—that is, to give Environment Canterbury the resources to do the job. That would retain local democracy and meet the Government’s stated concerns, but, of course, the report does not include that option; to do so would not meet its underlying purpose.

Fourth, the report is a shameless exercise in prejudgment. Its starting premise is also its conclusion. The report seeks to identify what it describes in its terms of reference as “ECan’s poor performance record”. It then concludes, 67 pages later, that Environment Canterbury’s performance has been poor and that it needs to be replaced. That was a brilliant exercise in circular logic, which would fail a level 101 Master of Business Administration test.

This crisis is not over Canterbury water, though Canterbury water is in crisis. This crisis is over a particular world view that is driven by central government arrogance. The Creech report is part of a juggernaut that is gathering speed in Wellington—from local democracy to centralised corporatism, from Environment Canterbury to “New Zealand Incorporated”. The Key Government is making the fatal mistake that humans can balance the economy with the environment. We cannot do that; the economy is a subset, a wholly owned subsidiary of the environment. If we ravage the environment, we destroy the economy and human society with it. I say: “Mr Prime Minister, give us back our province and retain your integrity as a national leader.”

Hon KATE WILKINSON (Minister of Conservation) : What is it about the Labour Opposition that does not want New Zealanders and New Zealand to succeed? What is it about the Labour Opposition that seems so anti economic growth and does not want us to make the most of our economic opportunities? What is it about the Labour Opposition that does not seem to realise that if we encourage economic growth in a sensible and rational way, then we are better placed to have better health, better education, and better welfare?

What is it about the Labour Opposition that has such a dislike of our mining industry; an industry that can contribute sensibly, significantly, reasonably, and responsibly to our economic growth, and can actually contribute to jobs? Finally, what is it about the Labour Opposition that wants to so deliberately damage our international reputation and our “100% Pure New Zealand” image with mischievous and irresponsible scaremongering?

The Government released a discussion document—and look at the reaction. It is a discussion document; it is not policy. It is not a decision; it is a discussion document. Immediately after we released that document, the mischievous Royal Forest and Bird Protection Society went on a membership drive. After the release of the document, the society said the Government was going to target Tongariro National Park, Mount Aspiring National Park, and Kahurangi National Park. Yet the discussion document is specific. It states that there will be no change to Tongariro National Park, no change to Kahurangi National Park, no change to Mount Aspiring National Park.

It is possible to mine responsibly on conservation land, and there are examples of it now. As the Minister of Conservation I am more than aware of the need to protect our iconic conservation land. I saw a quote this week from Jane Clifton in the Listener, and it sums up the reaction to a T. She said: “It has been the accepted convention that when Labour grants a mining licence, it is a sensitively handled, best-practice maximising of opportunities, and when National grants a mining licence, it is a barbaric exploitation of our priceless heritage to the benefit of its big-business mates.” I have to say that there is a word for that, and I am not allowed to use it in this House. We already have sensible mining.

I quote Chris Carter, the previous Minister of Conservation. He said: “the Pike River coal company had to come up with a host of environmental protections and meet very stringent safeguards.” That is a good quote; we like that. We will be doing the same. We will have the same protections and the same safeguards. Even Mr Goff has said: “I think most New Zealanders want to see a balance. They want to see some areas that are less sensitive in the conversation estate available to environmentally sensitive mining.” We agree. Annette King has said that if mining was done in areas that are not key conservation areas on the conservation estate and if it is done well, she does not think that people would have a lot of difficulty with it. Well, we will do it and we will do it well, and we agree with what Annette King said.

Chris Carter, the previous Minister of Conservation said in relation to Pike River Coal: “This mine does represent an intrusion into an area of high conservation values”—in fact, it is a national park—“and a decision on whether to allow it to go ahead has been a very difficult one to make because of this.” We agree; some of these decisions are difficult, but they are balancing economic opportunities very seriously with our environmental responsibilities.

Even the Greens have been mischievous on this. Metiria Turei said: “This case clearly shows that it is possible to balance the economic concerns of miners and the conservation concerns of protecting endangered species”. But what do the Greens say now? They say it is different when National wants to be responsible; it is different when we want to make the most of New Zealand’s economic opportunities for the betterment of New Zealand, and it is different when we want to be responsible with the conservation estate. It is totally different than under Labour! As I have said, there is a word for that.

What annoys me is the damning of the mining companies. Yes, Pike River Coal is in Paparoa National Park—and it contributes to the endangered blue duck programme. It contributes to pest control, and it has paid over $100,000 for conservation work. It paid for a public road in a national park. Mining and conservation can go hand in hand responsibly.

PHIL TWYFORD (Labour) : I have been doing some research into ACT’s local government policy, which is very relevant to the discussion in the House today because the ACT Party appears to be driving the Government’s local government policy in Auckland. I will read to the House a couple of very relevant pieces from the ACT Party’s local government policy. ACT’s local government policy describes the current situation in relation to local government. What does it say? It says that local government is vulnerable to interest group capture: special interest groups get money for programmes, yet everyone ends up paying for them. Actually, that is a description of Rodney Hide’s super-city plan. He says that everyone pays—the Auckland public pays—but a special interest group gets the benefit. Who is he talking about? He is talking about National and ACT’s business mates, who are about to inherit the entire Auckland super-city, thanks to these reforms.

The ACT policy also describes dictatorial powers that remove freedom and choice. Actually, that is a description of the super-city, as well. Those are exactly the powers that Rodney Hide is exercising right now, in taking freedom and choice away from Aucklanders—Aucklanders who have never been consulted on the super-city. They have had no say in the matter, but they will have to pick up the tab for the super-city fiasco.

Further on down, ACT’s local government policy also states that councils get captured by narrow interest groups, which is unfair to everybody. Again, that is a great description of Rodney Hide’s super-city. I do not know if members have noticed, but only three organisations have come out in support of the Government’s council-controlled model, and they are the Employers and Manufacturers Association, the Council for Infrastructure Development, and the Property Council. Everybody else in Auckland is against the council-controlled organisation model, and against the corporatisation of local government in Auckland. Here is a classic example in ACT’s local government policy of how a narrow-interest group of big-business lobbyists can seize control of local government, and can get its hands on assets that Auckland ratepayers have paid for over generations.

We saw in question time this afternoon another example of the shoddy and rushed handling of the super-city, when the Minister of Local Government explained that in spite of his third super-city bill, no decisions had been made yet. That bill is currently before a select committee, and it makes it absolutely clear that the council-controlled organisations are taxable entities, which is why the New Zealand Herald reported today on its front page that Aucklanders would be up for tens of millions of dollars in taxes. But Rodney Hide said: “Oh, we have not made any decisions yet. We are still talking about it.” What an absolute joke!

It is clear to members on this side of the House, to the New Zealand Herald, and to Aucklanders, that the costs are mounting.
