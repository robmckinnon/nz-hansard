Corrections (Contract Management of Prisons) Amendment Bill — First Reading

[Advance Copy - Subject to minor change before inclusion in Bound Volume.]

Corrections (Contract Management of Prisons) Amendment Bill

First Reading

Hon JUDITH COLLINS (Minister of Corrections) : I move, That the Corrections (Contract Management of Prisons) Amendment Bill be now read a first time. At the appropriate time, I intend to move that the bill be referred to the Law and Order Committee.

This bill reflects Government policy to allow for the competitive tendering for contract management of prisons on a case by case basis. Legislation providing for the private management of prisons in 1995 was repealed in 2004 by the Labour-led Government. That saw the end of the private management of Auckland Central Remand Prison, and the Corrections Act 2004 expressly prohibits the Crown from entering into contracts for the management of prisons. Labour’s decision was purely ideological. It had nothing to do with the management of Auckland Central Remand Prison, which was widely acknowledged as being highly successful. In fact, it was so successful that the Department of Corrections adopted many of its innovations when it took over the prison in July 2005. Those improvements included education, crime prevention programmes, physical training, and staff dedicated to behavioural assessment. Local iwi also supported the partnership with Auckland Central Remand Prison.

The legislation that governed the contract management of Auckland Central Remand Prison provided a sound basis for an effective and productive relationship between the contract managers of the prison and the Crown. This Government will, therefore, re-establish the legislative framework for contract management of prisons based on the previous legislation. We have made some changes that reflect the environment in which prison management must be carried out in the 21st century. Past provisions have been preserved where they remain sound, and amended where new provisions that have arisen in the intervening 15 years have made it prudent to do so.

The Department of Corrections is facing significant pressure on its existing prison capacity. Prisoner numbers over the next 10 years are forecast to grow to the point where an additional 5,000 prison beds may be needed by 2018. Demand for prison beds is being driven by an increasing prison population and the need to replace obsolete facilities. The Government must, therefore, look for innovative solutions, efficiency, and cost-effectiveness in prisoner management. Allowing private companies to provide custodial services creates an opportunity to benefit from private sector initiative.

The Corrections (Contract Management of Prisons) Amendment Bill is consistent with the objectives and purpose of the Corrections Act 2004. The Corrections Act requires the corrections system to improve public safety and to contribute to the maintenance of a just society. This must be done through the safe, secure, humane, and effective containment of people who are subject to custodial sentences. The maintenance of public safety is the paramount consideration in all decisions made about the management of offenders. The Government believes that this bill provides an opportunity for innovation in safe and effective offender management that is consistent with the guiding principles of the Corrections Act.

Another key feature of the Corrections Act is the accountability that it sheets home to the chief executive of the Department of Corrections. Prisoners remain in the legal custody of the chief executive at all times. This will not change when the prisoner is held in a prison managed under contract. The chief executive remains ultimately accountable for the acts or omissions of the contractor. This will drive a rigorous performance management regime. Contractors’ performance will be regulated and monitored through the use of prison monitors and the rigorous reporting requirements imposed by this legislation.

The bill clearly identifies those things that must be provided for in every contract for the management of any prison. These include performance objectives and standards, particularly regarding the secure containment of prisoners; staff selection and training; the provision of rehabilitative programmes to address the causes of prisoners’ offending; obligations to manage the interface with other Government agencies, such as the police and the courts; and termination provisions governing the right of the Crown to step in, in the event that a contract manager is failing to meet the terms of the contract.

The bill provides for all prison management contracts to be tabled in Parliament 12 sitting days after they have been entered into. It establishes a specific role for prison monitors. Monitors will be based within specific contract-managed prisons. They will be tasked with assessing and reporting on the management of each prison for which they are responsible. The bill sets up a number of significant reporting obligations with which the contractor must comply. These include reports on all serious incidents within the prison involving escapes, escape attempts, or prisoner deaths; reports detailing the use of disciplinary powers within the prison and the outcome of all disciplinary proceedings taken against prisoners; and reports providing the details and results of all random drug-testing undertaken within the prison.

The bill establishes clear rules around Crown liability. It also imposes a duty on the contractor to indemnify the Crown if the actions or omissions of its staff cause loss to the Crown. The bill requires the contractor to carry sufficient indemnity insurance to cover these liabilities.

The bill does not place any statutory limitation on the duration of the contract that can be entered into. It leaves this to be negotiated between the parties. This approach, providing the parties with the freedom to negotiate on duration, is one of several key terms and conditions, alongside cost performance objectives and a return on investment over time.

The bill requires contract prison managers to comply with the requirements of several relevant New Zealand laws. These include the Corrections Act and its regulations, the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act, and those parts of the State Sector Act that invoke the requirement to be a good employer. The bill also requires the contractor to comply with all relevant international obligations and standards. Because all these requirements must be expressed as terms within the management contract, failure to comply with any of them will place the contractor in breach of its contract with the Crown. These conditions may seem onerous, but it is no less than we expect from our public prison system. This Government will expect both public and private providers to deliver efficient and effective custodial services, and to be accountable for the way in which they do so.

Finally, the bill protects the interests of staff who are members of the Government Superannuation Fund. It ensures that existing staff who transfer their employment to contract prison managers are still deemed to be in Government employment for the purposes of their Government Superannuation Fund entitlements.

One matter that remains to be addressed is around the flow of information between contract prison managers, the department, and other agencies, such as the police and the courts. This is necessary to enable every agency involved to carry out its appropriate tasks, duties, and functions effectively. The Department of Corrections is in discussion with the Office of the Privacy Commissioner as to how this should be managed. Advice will be provided to the select committee before the bill is considered.

Allowing for the competitive tendering of prison management on a case by case basis provides an opportunity for innovation, efficiency, and cost-effectiveness. I consider that this bill will provide a sound legislative framework through which the Government will be able to realise those benefits. Therefore, I commend this bill to the House.

Hon PHIL GOFF (Leader of the Opposition) : That was a written speech read word for word by the Minister Judith Collins, without any passion, conviction, or belief in what she was saying.

We have to ask why the National Government is moving to privatise prisons. I guess the first answer is that essentially this National Government believes in privatisation. National would not admit to it during the election campaign, but now it has already signalled that it will take the profit sectors of the accident compensation scheme and give them to Australian insurance companies to make profit from New Zealanders and provide fewer services. Now it is talking about doing the same thing in the prison system.

Why is National doing it? The first reason is simply blind ideology. Those members have faith that somehow the private sector will manage prisons better, will deliver those services for a lower cost, and at the same time will provide a very healthy profit to, again, the overseas companies that will run these prison services. That is an act of faith on the part of National, but in that speech from the Minister there was not one iota of evidence that the private sector could provide any of the things that she suggested. The first reason National is privatising prisons is ideology.

I will say this about ideology, if ideology means values. On this side of the House we believe that running a corrections system is a core public service, in exactly the same way that running the Defence Force or running the police force is. Those are core public services. In none of those instances have we ever believed that turning those services over to the commercial sector to make a profit is appropriate. This I would suggest, is the top of a slippery slope. If we are to privatise the corrections system, then I ask why we would not move to do that to other core public services. Of course, National members never had the courage to say that before the election. They said that in this term of office there would not be any privatisation. Mr Coleman, the Minister of Broadcasting, keeps saying that he will not privatise Television New Zealand this term, but the message very clearly is that this is the inherent agenda of National—to go back to its roots, to privatise everything that it can, and to turn over these core public services to the commercial sector so that a profit can be made.

But the real motivation for this Minister lies elsewhere. This Minister likes to be known as “Crusher Collins”, but after the fiasco with Barry Matthews she became known as “Chihuahua Collins”, and that is a little bit more like it. I remember seeing a cartoon in a newspaper. There was the prison, the barbed wire, the big kennel with “Rottweiler” on it, and there was this little chihuahua with the Minister’s face and a little pink ribbon on it. That said it all. Heads were going to roll. She failed. She backed down. She talked tough, she did not deliver.

Then she comes to this House complaining that prison numbers over the next 10 years are going up by 5,000. This is the same Minister who said that Labour was soft on law and order. Now she is saying that the legislation is too tough, as it means that more people are in prison, and she cannot cope. In Opposition she was the first person to get up and tell the Minister to take responsibility. Now she will not touch it with a bargepole. She will not take responsibility, and she believes that by privatising prisons she can avoid responsibility. It will be the private sector’s fault that it has not delivered what it promised and what she promised. But I tell the Minister that she can run but she cannot hide. She is still accountable, and by privatising she will not remove the accountability, because we will hold her accountable for what experience tells us will happen in the private sector.

I will just say that there is one other motivation—and let us be very clear about this—as to why the Minister wants to privatise. Privatisation is a way of forcing down wages and bringing down working conditions. Every private prison in the world has made its profit by reducing staff levels and reducing services. I will give the Minister some examples of what has happened overseas and as to why there is absolutely no evidence for her claim that somehow a superior service at a better cost will be delivered.

In 2001 the United States National Council on Crime and Delinquency produced a report. It stated that there was “no data to support the contention that privately operated facilities offer cost savings over publicly managed facilities”. That is what the US National Council on Crime and Delinquency said. It said that there was no evidence that services to prisons and conditions of imprisonment were significantly improved in privately operated facilities. In fact, it came out with some concrete findings. The report stated that privatisation led to a 15 percent lower ratio of staffing to inmates. That is supposed to make our society safer—a reduction in the number of staff who are running the prisons. The report stated that the management information systems were less well organised in the private sector. It stated that the number of serious incidents was higher in the privately run system than in the public system. It stated that there was a higher rate of assaults by prisoner on prisoner, and by prisoner on staff, in the private sector. I ask where the evidence is in that report that privatisation of our prisons in New Zealand will produce better conditions, better standards, and lower costs. There is no evidence there.

Canada, just 2 years ago, ended the privatisation experiment in that country. It returned the one private prison it had to the public sector, saying that after 5 years it could find no appreciable benefits from privatisation. Canada, I say for the Minister’s information, has a very similar system to that of New Zealand.

Then, in the United Kingdom, there was a report by the National Audit Office in 2003—just over 5 years ago. It stated that the performance was mixed, but it pointed out that of the bottom-ranked prisons, almost all of them were privately managed prisons. The worst-performing prisons in the United Kingdom were in the private sector.

I ask this Minister where the evidence is that we will get better results, a safer and better society, through the privatisation of our prisons. This bill is blind ideology. This is blind faith by the National Government that somehow privatisation will produce better-run prisons at a lower cost. It was private sector people who brought about the financial crisis of the developed world at the present time.

I want to say that under the previous Labour Government the prison system in this country—the public prison system—appreciably improved. The number of escapes was down by 84 percent under a Labour Government. Drug taking was down by two-thirds. The Minister is very fortunate to have inherited a situation where cellphones can no longer be used in prisons, because every prison in this country is jammed. She is very fortunate to have inherited a situation where, annually, 600 inmates are now getting drug and alcohol rehabilitation, where over 1,800 inmates a year are getting literacy and numeracy training, and where 60 percent of sentenced prisoners are getting work in prison. I say to the Minister that those advances will be compromised under privatisation, because the private sector has no vested interest at all in producing better results. It has a vested interest in making a greater profit. If members want to see the ultimate corruption in the private sector, then they will see it in the United States, where judges were recently convicted of corruption for sending young prisoners to privately managed prisons to up their profit margins. That is the judiciary in the United States. There is clear evidence that if we turn this sector over to private management, then there is a vested interest in profit making—

SANDRA GOUDIE (National—Coromandel) : I thank Opposition members for their applause, knowing that the Hon Phil Goff has just finished his speech in the House, and that I am about to begin mine. So I say thank you for that wonderful applause.

The Opposition member who has just resumed his seat was the Minister of Corrections—”was”, being in the past tense—with responsibility for parole, and what a mess he made of that. Yet he was here in the House sounding like the be-all and end-all—the ultimate authority. Well, I believe that he is in absolute fantasyland. If he actually looked at the objectives of the bill, he might develop a little bit of understanding. Perhaps Opposition members might like to refer to the explanatory note, where they will find the objectives of the bill, which allow the Department of Corrections to tender and contract prison management on a case by case basis. That will in turn allow contracted prison managers to introduce innovation, improve service performance, and be more cost-effective. And what did that Opposition member say? He said it is wrong that the running of prisons should offer the potential for profit. Members opposite find that so scary. The Opposition cannot stand the idea that anybody might make even a dollar from offering good services to the public of New Zealand.

I am delighted to follow my most excellent colleague the Minister of Police and the Minister of Corrections, Judith Collins, who is making some outstanding changes in regard to the Government, and providing services for the continued safety of the New Zealand public. Without a doubt, that is being achieved here. The Opposition’s argument that the running of prisons should not offer the potential for growth is an argument without substance. It is simply a catchy cliché that does not stand up when analysed. This bill is about providing for the private management of prisons; the ownership and the overseeing of prisons will remain in the hands of the Crown. It is about management.

One has to wonder how much Opposition members really understand this legislation. I wonder whether they have even read it. I ask whether any members of the Opposition would like to put their hand up to say that they have read the bill. Well, look at that: no hands went up. Not a single hand went up. They have not read the bill. That is quite clear. Look at that! And there are only 8 clauses! What does that tell us? They have a complete lack of understanding. They do not even have an adequate overview of the legislation in the House. They have not read the bill. They do not know what it is about, yet they think that they can stand up in the House and criticise a really worthwhile initiative that will serve the public of New Zealand well, and that will provide them with the security and safety that they rightly deserve. This Opposition—weak as it is—cannot even appreciate that fact. None of those members seems to have even read the bill.

One of the other arguments that has been put forward relates to the idea that corrections is a core State function. This is the Opposition’s absolute nanny State control ideology. Labour lost the Government benches because the New Zealand public were sick of that absolute nanny State control where Labour could not see any other option or choice, other than it being the absolute controller, ruler, and arbiter of all.

That is why those members are now in Opposition.

Let us face it, most people would agree that just as the private provision of some health and education services is effective, efficient, and provides choice for the New Zealand public, so too can the private management of prisons, and that is what this bill is all about. The core standard of care and conditions is set by the State. Even the buildings will be owned by the State. All that is actually happening—and maybe Opposition members might like to listen up or read the bill—is that we are allowing the management of prisons by the private sector. One could not argue about the ability to have some choice in that regard. I would love to hear any further arguments that Opposition members might be able to dredge up.

Another argument is that there will not be incentives to reform prisoners and reduce the prison population. That argument is wrong, as well, because this is all based on a contractual arrangement. Opposition members should understand, although I doubt that they do, that this can be quite clearly determined by the robustness of the contractual arrangement in place in the private provision of prison management. The management problems that have occurred with privatised prisons can usually be linked to poorly drafted contracts, which is quite true. These matters can be addressed if good, robust contracts are established and put into place. Let us face it: the previous Minister made a mess of corrections and parole. If we look at the report by the Auditor-General we can see that the previous Government has a lot to answer for in terms of the robustness of the services being provided.

The previous speaker, Phil Goff, talked about the disadvantages for workers because private sector prisons provide lower pay and fewer incentives, in order to increase profits. As I have already outlined, the privatisation of health and education services can benefit workers and provide benefits that the public sector may not be able to provide. Employees have the opportunity to negotiate with their employer. Past experience has proven that private prison service providers are more open to direct Māori and iwi involvement, which is fantastic.

The Iwi Whānui o Tāmaki-makau-rau—a committee of six Auckland iwi—was heavily involved in the Auckland Central Remand Prison while it was privately managed. The managers and the iwi whānui had a very strong partnership that allowed for extensive involvement by iwi in the prison. The iwi whānui publicly criticised the Labour Government’s decision to return the prison to public management. Maybe the Opposition would like to reflect on that. The iwi whānui chair, Te Wārena Taua, said, in 2003, that the Auckland Central Remand Prison was quite rightly regarded as the most innovative, effective, and culturally safe prison in New Zealand’s history.

Mr Taua also said that the iwi whānui strongly believed that removing the Auckland Central Remand Prison management contract, and preventing further like it, represents the biggest step backwards in terms of prison management, particularly in the areas of inmate management and rehabilitation, and that is exactly what the Labour Government did. It took a huge retrograde step when it closed down the opportunity for private prison management and removed the legislative ability for that to happen. It removed that choice from the statute book. We are now rectifying that gross omission by the previous Government, and we will satisfy some of the concerns expressed by Mr Taua, the iwi whānui chair, at that time in 2003.

Discussions around the private management of Ngāwhā prison, before the Labour Government removed the provision for private management, resulted in agreements with northern iwi Ngāti Hine for Corrections Corporation of Australia to run the prison for 5 years with Ngāti Hine’s involvement. The two were going to work together. I see that as providing much more successful outcomes for the rehabilitation of prisoners—for their going back into the community and not reoffending. I would like to see Opposition members try to argue their way out of that one, because I do not think they have a leg to stand on. Their nanny State ideology is not wanted by the New Zealand public, and the sooner they wake up to that, the better.

Hon DAVID PARKER (Labour) : What that member calls nanny State, I call principle. There are certain functions of the State that ought not to be contracted out. One of those functions is taking away a person’s liberty. That ought not to be contracted out. Where to next? As Phil Goff said earlier, where will it go to next? Will it be a private army? Will it be a private police force? Again, they are functions that ought not to be contracted out. The National Government’s policy on this fails the most basic principle that this is one of those core State functions that cannot legitimately be contracted out.

There are some, and National Party members are amongst them—and it was not put very eloquently by the person who spoke before me—who try to drive a fine distinction between the allocation of a penalty that deprives someone of their liberty and the deprivation of liberty following sentence. But such fine distinctions cannot be made, because it is the private contractor in that prison who makes decisions that affect the liberty of the person who is the prisoner. It is the report of the private contractor that is relevant at the parole hearing, which determines how long that person stays in custody. Those are the functions of the State, not the functions of private enterprise.

Shane Ardern: Why?

Hon DAVID PARKER: It is for the same reason that we ought not to contract out the army to mercenaries, or the police force to a private police force. They are proper functions of the Crown, not private enterprise.

There is an important principle at stake here, and it is that some things ought not to be put out of the control and responsibility of the State and into the private sector. The State is held accountable for prison policy, through elections. Private enterprise has no such control. Private enterprise is not held accountable for its actions in depriving people of their liberty, through elections. There is no control on private enterprise in that way. One might think that if there was not some reason for the basic fundamental principle that one ought not to contract out core State functions, then some good should be achieved through contracting out. The international experience on contracting out correctional facilities is now some decades old. We heard Phil Goff recount that the experiment in Canada failed. Canada reverted to State control of prisons. He also adverted to some of the literature in the United States.

I thought it was notable that the Minister of Corrections did not make any reference to any studies overseas as to the effectiveness of private prisons—because she cannot. She said that we will have an increase in the prison population, on the Government’s forecast, of 5,000 prisoners over the next 10 years. That statement most clearly illustrates that the biggest issue in respect of corrections policy and whether it is succeeding or failing is rates of recidivism—rates of reoffending.

For there to be any justification for a move to private prisons—and I think there is none on the principled basis, but let us ignore that basic principle and look just at efficacy—we would have to be looking at overseas studies that have shown that private prisons have reduced rates of recidivism. But there are no such studies. There are none in Australia. A recent report from New South Wales in Australia on privatisation in the corrections industry, which is published in the journal Corrections Criminology, concludes that there are few detailed studies on the effects that privatisation has on recidivism. There is an update to that, and it notes that that remains the case in Australia. There is no evidence in Australia that some of their experiments with privatisation have worked.

In the United States it is even worse. There are two peer-reviewed studies in the United States on the effects on recidivism of private correctional facilities. I will quote for members the first one, entitled The Effectiveness of Juvenile Correctional Facilities: Public versus Private Management. The very learned authors are Patrick Bayer from Yale University and David Pozen from Oxford University.

David Garrett: There are only two in the whole United States?

Hon DAVID PARKER: The peer-reviewed ones? Mr Garrett is correct that there are only two. The member should know that, if he has done his study, as I have done. The study concludes that full profit management has statistically significant impacts on recidivism. It has statistically significant impacts on recidivism. The unfortunate fact for Mr Garrett is that the statistics go the wrong way. In 1 year, recidivism rates are 5 to 8 percent higher for those juveniles who have been in privately run facilities.

The other study is one called the Bales report. Again, it is a peer-reviewed study of the experiments in Florida on private prisons. That study found no empirical justification for the policy argument that private prisons reduce recidivism more than public prisons do.

That is why the Government has not produced any evidence by which it can justify contracting out this core State function—there is no evidence that it improves rates of recidivism. The peer-reviewed articles in fact go the other way. We also heard from Phil Goff, and I will reinforce his points about the experiment in the UK. Again, there was very poor analysis as to whether overall there have been good or bad results from prisons, but the results that are available show that the poor prisons are the ones that have been contracted out to private providers.

David Garrett: Would you like to table those two studies? I’d like to see them.

Hon DAVID PARKER: I can give the member the references.

David Garrett: Just table them.

Hon DAVID PARKER: I am happy to table the information I got from the Parliamentary Library, and it is a quote from there. I will give the member the reference and he can look it up. The reference is www.econ.yale.edu/growth. The PDF is cdp863. The member can look that up for himself. There is no doubt that there is no case for this on the basis of recidivism.

Mr Goff makes a fair point. The only way prisons can make a profit is by cutting the cost of their services. The prisons themselves are provided by the State, we are told by Sandra Goudie. Those running the prisons will not be able to reduce the cost of the bricks and mortar, so the only thing they can do is cut the cost of services. The only way they can do that is either by paying people less, having fewer people, or reducing the level of services available to inmates. That last one is the one that concerns me. The other two concern me, as well. A reduced number of corrections officers obviously would be poor, but if there is not as much money spent on alcohol and drug programmes, and if there is not as much money spent on literacy and numeracy programmes, and anger management issues and violence issues are not dealt with, we will have increasing rates of recidivism. I suspect that that is why the international studies do not show a decrease in recidivism as a consequence of privatisation of correctional facilities, but, rather, an increase. It is because the private prisons cut costs where they can, and one area where they cut costs is the programmes that would reduce recidivism.

The Government, instead of privatising prisons, should be focusing on how it can reduce rates of recidivism. A move towards private prisons will increase rates of recidivism. New Zealand already has the second-highest rate of imprisonment in the Western World, behind the United States. We should not follow the United States further down that route. We certainly should not follow the United States into having privatised correctional facilities.

I have one last point. The other bit of news we had from the Minister of Corrections in recent weeks was a plan for a 1,200-person prison. A 1,200-person prison! What a ridiculous prison policy—a 1,200-person prison! Gang culture will prevail.

METIRIA TUREI (Green) : It is clear that the Green Party is opposed to the Corrections (Contract Management of Prisons) Amendment Bill. Tonight I want to set out a response to the economic arguments that have been put forward by Government members, but first I will address the issue of Māori imprisonment and the profiteering from that imprisonment.

Profiteering from the imprisonment of Māori is simply profiteering from institutional racism, regardless of who profits. On this issue the Green Party and the Māori Party diverge, and that saddens me terribly. I agree that the Department of Corrections has an appalling record with regard to involving Māori in prison issues, and that the 50 percent of the prison population who are Māori desperately need interventions and the restoration of their mauri ora and mana that tikanga Māori within the prison environment can provide. But that will not be the case when the cash is going into the back pocket of some US corporation, and when the cash is going into the back pocket of an iwi or rūnanga feeding off racism in the legal system.

Racism in the legal system is a proven fact in this country. A 2007 report, Over-representation of Māori in the criminal justice system, shows that the analysis of data from apprehension through to prosecution, conviction, and, finally, sentencing confirms that Māori are more likely to be apprehended and more severely punished than non-Māori. Judge Becroft of the Youth Court confirms that young Māori are more likely to receive a harsher sentence for lesser crimes than those committed by their Pākehā counterparts. So now we know why the Māori imprisonment rate is five to eight times higher than that of Pākehā. For Māori the cost of this legislation is that more of us will end up in jail.

Private profiteering provides no protection for Māori, and incentivises a massive increase in prison population by the use of the full range of racist filters in the system. Having Māori managers of a prison, at the end of systemic economic policy programmes specifically designed to put our people in jail, is pointless. We cannot protect our people from that systemic racism if key Māori organisations support the economic levers that put our people in there in the first place. We urge Māori across the motu to condemn this legislation.

The Government argues that private management will save public money; that is the key reason why it promotes this bill. Yet the facts prove the Government 100 percent wrong on that. The facts from the running of private prisons in the US and Australia clearly show that there are no real cost savings to the public purse. The United States Department of Justice report Emerging Issues on Privatized Prisons clearly showed that the privatisation model simply mimicked the public sector in every practical and critical way. The promises from private companies in the US of 20 percent savings simply did not eventuate. Any modest savings that were made came at the cost of reductions in staffing and other labour-related costs. The report concluded that “This amount of savings will not revolutionize modern correctional practices.”, which proves Judith Collins wrong. In other words, privatisation has no practical impact on the cost of running prisons or on the practice of running prisons.

An Australian paper, “Privatisation and New South Wales Prisons: Value for Money and Neo-liberal Regulation”, published just last year, also showed that the privatisation of prisons did not result in value for money or a significant reduction in cost. In fact, the paper showed that the rhetoric of cost-effectiveness undermined alternative criteria for assessment, such as safety, educational outcomes, and reduced offending. That is where the National Government is duping the public. By misleading the public on the efficiency of private prison management, it is diverting attention from the most important issue of all: the effective use of taxpayers’ money to keep our communities safe. Public scrutiny of private prisons will be severely limited. As the New South Wales example shows, the actual internal operating efficiencies of the private operator are not made public. An Australian Public Accounts Committee report showed that the actual operating costs for the private prison are not available, as GEO Group is a private company that is in competition with other operators in Australia, and that information is commercially sensitive.
