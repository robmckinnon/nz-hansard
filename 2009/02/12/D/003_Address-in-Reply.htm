Address in Reply

  • Debate resumed from 11 February.

JO GOODHEW (National—Rangitata) : When my contribution to the Address in Reply debate was interrupted I was reflecting on the reasons why New Zealanders voted resoundingly in November for change. After 9 years of opportunity and economic fair weather, Labour was found wanting. Some may say—and they have—that it was a good election to lose, but the National Government was certainly not thinking like that at the time. We believe in our vision. We will deliver to the people of New Zealand our promises, even though that task may have become more complicated by the dirty socks that were left by Labour. Yes, I am referring to the uncosted promises and unfunded commitment—things like the state of accident compensation funding.

It must be galling to sit on the Opposition benches and watch the National Government taking true action and bringing forward infrastructure spending to stimulate our economy. Opposition members have tried in vain to undermine the infrastructure package that was announced yesterday, although deep in their hearts I know they will be pleased to see jobs saved for New Zealanders. We saw education net five new schools, school refurbishments and maintenance, and an information and communications technology infrastructure improvement plan. We saw transport spending set to encompass five large State highway projects, and in housing 10,000 State houses will be upgraded and renovated and an additional 69 new State houses will be built—but, more important, built quickly. The Social Services Committee heard yesterday that the nation’s State housing stock has suffered from years of insufficient upgrades and maintenance. This deferred maintenance amounts to an estimated $2 billion. We were told that it will need 10 successive years of spending to address that. The neglect of the previous Labour Government has come home to roost for New Zealanders.

Last week’s small to medium sized enterprise package has been well received in my electorate. It is amazing how many people will ring your electorate office when they hear that things will get better and say “Thank goodness for common-sense orders.”

Hon Trevor Mallard: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. We had pedantic comment on “your” earlier in the day and again yesterday. This member has breached the Standing Orders in that way. I am sure she would not be referring to your office, Mr Deputy Speaker. Therefore, I ask that the rules be applied evenly.

Mr DEPUTY SPEAKER: It did come up again yesterday. Members cannot bring the Speaker into the debate, so future speakers should not use the words “you” or “your”, because they relate to the Speaker. Please continue.

JO GOODHEW: I give my apologies, Mr Deputy Speaker. These same people rang my office and said “Thank goodness we can see some common-sense measures coming from the Government.”

Resource Management Act reforms are filling New Zealanders with hope. Anyone who has encountered delays with consents is saying “Thank goodness!” again. It is amazing how such news triggers a whole lot of stories coming into our offices. Let me give some examples: a code of compliance that was not granted because the plugs for the sinks did not have chains attached; and bolts that had round washers, not square ones, and therefore did not comply. It beggars belief, does it not?

Then there was the story of the housebuyer who lived in his new house at No. 11. He was contacted by the local council and told that his number 11 on the fence was larger than 300 millimetres and therefore he needed a consent because it was a sign. Can we believe this? The $400 cost and the five forms to get that consent seemed ridiculously idiotic so the house owner just gave up, removed the number 11 from the outside of the fence, and put it on the inside of the fence where it has become a talking point for anyone who can find his house.

Changes to the Resource Management Act and to the culture that brings about idiotic and frequent examples like the ones I have just mentioned are absolutely essential for New Zealand’s future. For our country to weather the current economic storm and rise out and above the effects of the recession we need to work hard, preparing the way for our country to be more productive than it has ever been before. The National Government has the purpose, the drive, and the determination to steer New Zealand in the right direction through these tough times. But at the same time we must apply the building blocks.

Mr DEPUTY SPEAKER: I am informed that the next call is a National call and it will be split. I call Chris Tremain.

CHRIS TREMAIN (National—Napier) : Mr Deputy Speaker, I acknowledge you and congratulate you on election to your office. I would like to take this opportunity to thank the people of Napier and Wairoa for electing me as their member of Parliament. It is a privilege that I do not take lightly and a privilege that has to continue to be earned.

The Speech from the Throne highlighted two crises ravaging the belly of New Zealand communities: the financial crisis and the gang crisis. The financial crisis has been much debated in this House. John Key has a clear vision for this nation: blunt the edge of the recession and place New Zealand in the very best position to go forward and to recover and grow. There has been total focus in this regard. A coalition agreement was reached within 11 days, Cabinet was selected in 2 weeks, and Parliament was reconvened in just 30 days. Before Christmas the House sat under urgency from 9 a.m. until midnight in order to give effect to a tranche of measures to deliver on John Key’s vision to blunt the edge of the recession. Just 93 days following the election we are back in this House, in urgency again, delivering a further spread of initiatives to deliver on the Prime Minister’s vision for blunting the recession and preparing the economy for growth.

The small-business package has been practical and proactive. The infrastructure announcements are sensible. They are helping to protect jobs and laying the foundations for growth. I acknowledge the impact of the Matahōrua Gorge project in Hawke’s Bay. This project will shave significant time off the Napier to Wairoa route, improve safety, and drive economic growth to the region. This is an example of short-term impact and long-term benefit. On any measure, this package of initiatives is impressive but also measured. We are cognisant of the fact that any dollar borrowed today needs to be paid back tomorrow.

The second crisis I wish to speak about is the ravaging of our communities by gangs. Members should make no mistake: it is a crisis. When I was 18 a friend of mine, Vaughan Gooch, stood up for a young woman at a Napier party. He was punched senseless by gang members, to the point where he was unrecognisable in hospital. Twenty-four years on, the situation is simply worse. Now people are not only punched senseless but murdered. The social experiment with gangs has failed.

Whether it isthe killing in Murupara, which we read about in this morning’s Dominion Post, or the murder in Hawke’s Bay of 34-year-old Mark McCutcheon—who went to the aid of a woman and who leaves behind a wife and three young daughters—the cup of evidence is indeed overflowing. Every day stories such as those go below the radar. Before the Ōtāne murder, a gang member went into a Waipukurau cafe, grabbed food, walked out, came back 3 hours later, grabbed more food, and sat down. The proprietor, a woman, confronted the gang member, who spat at her and head-butted her for good measure as he walked out.

These people are no longer part of our community, yet we continue to treat them as though they are. We feed them, we house them, we pander to them in our prisons, and what do we get in return? We are repaid with horrible crimes. We condone their existence. We condone the gangs to such an extent that many Kiwis and international tourists see them as the face of Māori culture. Yet they are a stain on Māori culture. Gangs hide behind tikanga, of which they know nothing. Most cannot recite their whakapapa, except back to ngā kurī—the dogs—and they wear disfigured moko as some alien connection with a culture of which they have no idea.

Māori need to break free from the shackles of gang thuggery, to disown gangs, and to stop making excuses for them. Rape, murder, burglary, and intimidation are not part of tikanga. While Māori leaders continue to make excuses for gangs, Māoridom, in my opinion, will be the loser.

Hon Parekura Horomia: What about the Pākehā gangs in Timaru?

CHRIS TREMAIN: And those too. In that respect, I congratulate Ngāti Kahungunu. At their Clive Waitangi Day celebrations they banned gangs and they banned patches. This is the leadership that needs to be taken. The Māori Party needs to lead the way in that respect; all Māori MPs need to lead the way. Māori culture will not advance to where it should be while these thugs continue to drag on our coat-tails.

In that regard, I totally support the legislation of Simon Power. Would we allow 3,000 people who wore Hitler’s swastika to parade in our streets, to commit murder, rape, and burglary, to feed our kids P, and then to turn up in the courts barking like dogs? No, we would not. Yet we condone a private army of people wearing Mongrel Mob and Black Power insignia. We condone a private army where rape is an act that qualifies one for the patch. I no longer condone these patches, and I join my Hawke’s Bay colleague Craig Foss in doing all that I can to wipe out this menace from the face of New Zealand society. Thank you, Mr Deputy Speaker.

NATHAN GUY (National—Ōtaki) : Mr Deputy Speaker, can I congratulate you on your position and, indeed, Mr Speaker Lockwood Smith and the Assistant Speakers, Eric Roy and Rick Barker. Indeed, it is a privilege to be in this Parliament, sitting on this side of the House. I also need to acknowledge the fantastic maiden speeches and maiden statements that have been delivered in the House in this term. There was a very high quality of speeches right across the House.

I want to talk about the fantastic result in this election and how John Key is progressing extremely well as our Prime Minister through difficult economic times. In record time he has pulled together a very stable Government, with the support of the ACT Party, the Māori Party, and United Future. I believe that it will be a long-serving and very stable arrangement as we go forward. Of course there will be times when we will have disagreements, but that is part of our democracy, and we have some tough decisions to make going forward.

But the biggest issue in 2009 is indeed the economy, and that is why the Prime Minister has taken the steps he has taken in recent times to take the sharpest edge off the economy, to try to protect jobs, and to try, indeed, to get us out of this recession, which I believe we were in in 2008 but many people did not realise it. I believe that with the packages that have been announced over the last couple of months, we will track out of the recession a lot more quickly. I will talk a little about those packages, and also about what a privilege it is to be representing the people of Horowhenua and Kapiti in the Ōtaki electorate, and the fantastic result those people gave me, to enable me to serve as the Ōtaki MP in this House—a huge honour. In fact, we had the highest voter turn-out in the whole of the country, which just goes to show what a close contest it was out there in the Ōtaki electorate.

I alluded before to the packages that will take the sharpest edge off the recession. They include the tax cuts that will be rolling out on 1 April. They will put more money in people’s pockets. The ReStart package will help those people, the most vulnerable, who may in fact lose their jobs. The announcement made by the Prime Minister of $480 million for small and medium sized enterprises will help those small businesses. Indeed, in my electorate there are 7,700 of them, and they employ about 20,000 people. The package that has just been rolled out in our infrastructure announcements made yesterday, of $500 million across about 4¼ years, will also help to kick-start our economy.

One of the important things is the reform of the Resource Management Act. It has allowed vexatious and frivolous objections that have slowed down projects of national significance. There are about 100 clause changes, and they will help to streamline and speed up this process. I am excited about that initiative, because I believe that it will also help us get through this economic recession. It will create more jobs. It is important that we get started on some of these big projects, rather than the process taking as long as it does now. Transit openly told a select committee just a couple of years ago that it takes as long now to get resource consent as it does to build about 1 kilometre of road, and most times that can take up to 3 years.

We also have important initiatives around law and order, which Chris Tremain, the previous speaker, has so rightly just spoken about. Those initiatives will be really important in allowing our communities to be safer.

It is interesting, when we sit on this side of the House and move around Parliament, to see the different factions currently in the Labour Party all starting to form up and jostle for different leadership bids as we progress to the next election. Indeed, we have Mr Cunliffe’s camp there, talking about how they do not actually have a plan for the economy now, but they will deliver it for the next election. We are interested to see who will run in the Mt Albert by-election. Will it be Phil Twyford? Will young Darren Hughes head up north to run in Mt Albert? We also have the different camps for Jones, Horomia, and Cosgrove. We are interested to see who will come into Parliament over the next few months, from the list. The water cooler conversations have already started amongst Labour Party members. They are not very settled, at all. We are waiting to see whether Phil Goff will survive the 90-day probation period.

Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE (Labour—Waimakariri) : As is appropriate, Mr Deputy Speaker, I congratulate you—and your fellow office holders—on your appointment and also the Speaker, Dr Lockwood Smith. I also pay my compliments to the Governor-General.

This debate is occurring in the biggest global economic crisis we have ever seen—certainly in the lifetime of members here. I suspect that no one in this House, nor in the great countries of the world—the UK, Australia, and the US—knows how deep it will cut or the impact it will ultimately have on our people in New Zealand and those in other countries. The leadership of the UK, the US, and Australia has been called upon to do everything they can to try to stem the tide, as it were, of the negative impact of the recession.

We can look at and compare the approach here—and I mentioned a couple of these points when talking on the tax bill—with what Barack Obama, the President of the United States, whom Mr Key takes great relish in likening himself to, has done in the United States. Even before Mr Key and Mr Obama were elected roughly at the same time, and even before President-elect Obama became President, he had publicly put out a plan. He had put together a strategy, he had published that strategy, and he had talked and jawboned about his economic package in the House and the Senate. He cajoled people and he gained support for his economic package, over and above the previous President’s package, so that when he was elected and actually had the power to do something about it, the wheels were turning, the support was there, and the package was moved to practical implementation.

If we look at Australia we see Kevin Rudd’s package—and a second package has been announced today. The National Party tends to debate this argument by comparing dollar figures. But I say that we should compare National’s approach with the aggressive nature of the package in Australia, where the Australian Prime Minister and the Australian Government, together with the state Governments, have pledged to do everything they can—not to drip-feed or have rolling mauls. We know that Mr Brownlee—I think he made the third XV at St Bede’s College—knows all about rolling mauls. There should not be rolling mauls or drip-feeds, because they are based upon the political cynicism of the National Government, which just drips it out so that it has something to put in its Budget and to try to lengthen its honeymoon. Those are the motivations in relation to the rolling maul. It is not about actually saying “To hell with it! We’re going to put a stake in the ground, we’re going to have a strategy, and we’re going to do everything now. And if we don’t have anything to announce in the Budget, well, we will have tried everything we could have to protect the people of New Zealand.”

If we look at Gordon Brown in the UK we see that he is doing everything that he and his Government can do. They are not drip-feeding for political purposes but they are saying that they will try to stem the tide of the negative economic impact that this recession will have. But the game plan of members over there on the Government benches—and I hope the journalists are listening, because a few of them have latched on to it—is to try to convince the people of New Zealand that the Government inherited a mess, that it inherited hell in a hand basket, and that it now has to clean it up. Well, let us look at a couple of very clear facts that are evidenced throughout the world.

That crew over there inherited from the Labour Government the lowest unemployment rate for 20, 30, or 40 years, but in the previous 2 years especially the lowest unemployment rate in the OECD and the world. Those are clear OECD figures. The National Government inherited from the previous Government the third-lowest level of debt in the OECD. Gross debt was 20 percent of GDP, and net debt was almost zero. That is what the National-led Government inherited from the previous Government.

It inherited a 4 percent unemployment rate. The present Government can look at Labour’s record over 9 years, during which each week 1,000 jobs were created. A thousand jobs were created each week over 9 years. Now we know the big bang package, the next scrum or maul, the next movement of the forward pack in National—the summit of five Ministers going up the line.

We had an admission today by Mr Joyce that the total amount of relief that New Zealanders can expect is, perhaps—maybe, if they are lucky—the creation or saving of 2,000 jobs in total. But as Phil Goff said, in the last 3 months 10,000 jobs have gone. Treasury predicts, and we know, that every week for the next 15 months 1,000 jobs will go, but this Government is trying to say that its package is the big bang that will maybe, as Mr Joyce said, save 2,000 jobs. Well, I say that that is grotesque and irresponsible, and that National—this rolling maul with its drip-feeding—is motivated by one thing, which is to generate an announcement once a week or once every few days that tries to hoodwink people.

I heard Mr Heatley interject on me earlier about State houses. He has said that the Government will build 69 new State houses, and he calls that an increase, when on the same day he has cut 1,500 houses—the construction build—out of Project Hope. Even with my limited mathematics I know that that works out to be a cut, and a deep one. That member stands up and has the audacity to bleat and to accuse the previous Labour Government of being slum landlords, when the previous National Government sold, I believe, 13,000-plus State houses in its time in office.

I will quote Brian Gaynor, as I did the other day. Members opposite rubbish Brian Gaynor today, but he is an eminent business person. I know Mr Gaynor; I had some interactions with him in the minerals industry some years ago. He is an eminent business person and an eminent commentator. In today’s New Zealand Herald he compared the trans-Tasman package with our own and said “our transtasman neighbours are planning to take the direct and aggressive route to the try line while we have a slow moving maul with little idea who is carrying the ball and which way the maul will move next.” That is what he said about New Zealand’s package. The Australians are taking direct and aggressive action. It was Kevin Rudd who stood up, after he delivered—

Paul Quinn: That’s why the All Blacks win, because the Aussies don’t know which way we’re going.

Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE: I think we have a parrot or something chirping away at the back of the Chamber. Maybe he should have a few more sunflower seeds.

Kevin Rudd said his first package was not enough, so he said “We’re going to go again.” This National Government is gambling with New Zealanders’ lives. It is saying to New Zealanders “Well, we’re going to drip-feed a bit here and a bit there.” National members are saying today, by their own admission, that even though the country has lost 10,000 jobs, and even though the Government inherited the lowest unemployment rate in the OECD, National’s package will save maybe, if it is lucky, 2,000 jobs but will bleed 2,000 New Zealanders’ jobs per week for the next 15 months. That is irresponsible.

Colin King: What about the 43,000 who were leaving for Australia under your lot?

Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE: That is the sort of arrogance coming from the back row. That member might want to reflect on the impact of 1,000 job losses a week in his own electorate of Kaikōura. [Interruption] That member does not have an electorate; he is a list MP. Well, where he lives there will be 1,000 job losses a week. Those members should reflect on that rather than stand up and try to trivialise what is a serious economic crisis.

People know the economic facts. They know that this crew in the National Government inherited one of the best economies in the world, with low debt and the lowest unemployment in the world. But National’s political tactic is to try to convince people that the economy was a mess when it got in. That is rubbish. It will not wash. I ask members opposite whether the best they can do is to have a summit of five Ministers, then a Māori employment hui, and then a Job Summit chaired by Mark Weldon, Chief Executive of the New Zealand Stock Exchange, who is an odd choice of individual to have chairing a summit, I would have thought, given the economic crisis in the capital markets.

National members’ strategy is to go to a Job Summit but not say “This is our plan that we were elected on, this is our strategy that we were elected on, and we want the commerce sector to help us implement it.” Oh no! It is a bit like the case with Judith Collins. She wanders around this country and says “We’ve been elected, but what should we do? What is the plan?”. The plan is to go to a Job Summit but not present a plan. Bill English will not get up and present a strategy, vision, or plan like Obama did, like Rudd did, and like Brown did; Bill English will go to a Job Summit and say “Can you give me a plan? What should I do? I’m going to take the edge off.” We say that if the sharp edge of the knife adds up to only 2,000 jobs, when 10,000 have been lost and we will lose 1,000 a week—thanks to Mr Joyce—then that proves that, today, National has no plan and no vision and is grossly irresponsible in using this recession as a political tactic to drip-feed little announcements.

At the end of all those announcements, New Zealanders will say “Well, why didn’t you do it aggressively like the Australians? Why didn’t you do it aggressively and responsibly like the Australians and the British? Why didn’t you throw everything at it?”. Every one of those 1,000 who will lose his or her job each week will ask why the Government did not act to do everything it could to save his or her job. I look forward to hearing Mr Brownlee, Mr English, and Mr Key giving a robust explanation for that.

DAVID BENNETT (National—Hamilton East) : Mr Deputy Speaker, I congratulate you on being a person from the Waikato who now holds the position of Deputy Speaker. We look forward to that continuing in the future.

When we look at what is happening around the world, we see that it is pretty obvious that all of the major economies are developing a plan based on three key factors. Those key factors are to reduce interest rates, to have stimulus packages based around infrastructure development, and to support those who will be in need of help during this crisis. The reason why all those countries are basically engaging in the same plan is that they want to kick-start the economic system of the world. They want a situation where people can go out there and invest in businesses, and have confidence that the economic environment will provide a future. That stimulus will lead to economic growth around the world.

People need low interest rates because they are an incentive for businesses to take the risk of borrowing money to try to make a go of it. They need stimulus packages, so countries invest in infrastructure. Major infrastructure programmes are the norm for most countries around the world. The question then becomes: which infrastructure projects are ready? That was one of the failings of the previous Labour Government—it did not have the programmes ready. The National Government has to bridge that gap by getting programmes through that we can actually deliver this year, when unemployment is due to rise. It is not good enough to wait 2 or 3 years for the design and implementation that are required for the work. The Labour Party did not do those things while it was in Government. The previous Government did not set the New Zealand economy up for this situation; it left us in a situation where we did not have those infrastructure projects ready to go. The National Government in the last week has tried to push through some infrastructure projects that are ready to go; it has more to do in this area. As a political party, we have led the charge in providing support for those in need. We have the ReStart package for people who will be in difficulty over this time.

Those three key factors are universal around the world: to reduce interest rates, to have a stimulus package, and to have a support package for those who will be in need. That is the base of most economic planning at this stage. When Labour members talk of Obama and Brown, and use the terminology of grand plans, they forget the reality and the fundamentals of what those political parties are pushing for. They are pushing for these three key things, which the National Government has done in New Zealand. They are pushing to get that economic base so that we can grow this economy.

When we look at what the National Government has done, we see that it has delivered on its 100-day plan, it has given some direction to the New Zealand public, it has given direction to the economy, and it has indicated to the world that National is leading this country in a way that is possible in these tough economic times. I ask members to compare that with the 90-day plan of Phil Goff, which is coming to an end very soon. He may not be there in the long term of the Labour Party.

The jobs and growth plan of this Government is about keeping this economy running so that it can deliver as strong a solution as possible to the recession the country will face. The infrastructure programme that was released gives a $500 million boost to this economy. The infrastructure is based around areas of education, transport, and housing and is spread geographically through New Zealand. It is also spread through different industries, and amongst different groups of the community that will be struggling. For those on low incomes a housing programme will provide some comfort coming into those cold winter months. For the economic growth that we need we have a transport programme, which will advance some of the larger State highway projects in some of our regions. And in education we are building the blocks that will deliver a stronger New Zealand, giving the strong base that the young people of New Zealand need to tackle the future economies of the world.

Those are the things the National Government has done that are practical and realistic, and that deliver. Let us compare that with what Labour would have done. Its infrastructure programme would have been to build embassies around the world. That would not have helped New Zealand workers, would it? No way. Labour was not delivering for New Zealanders; it was delivering an empire of embassies around the world. That would have been Labour’s plan if it had returned to Government. Labour does not understand what it means to invest in the New Zealand economy. It set up programme after programme investing in US stocks—stocks that have lost billions and billions of dollars. The heart of the New Zealand economy, which did so well in those good years, has been transferred to the US stock exchange, and that money has been lost to the public of New Zealand.

What else did Labour do? It felt it was better to put money into the hands of Australians—through Toll—by paying for a rail network rather than delivering the roading infrastructure that New Zealand needs. It was prepared to send billions of dollars overseas rather than keep it here. Well, National believes in New Zealand. We have a vision and a direction for New Zealand and we will invest in this country. We believe that New Zealanders can make the best of their money. We will not do what Labour was going to do: spend it on building embassies and send it to overseas companies. New Zealanders can build a stronger country—we will, and we have.

What will the Labour Government’s economic legacy be? Labour members always talk about what they have done for this country. Well, let us look at it. Labour put New Zealand into recession before the rest of the world. It destroyed the economy before the rest of the world hit the tidal wave. We are in a situation of recession before the rest of the world is. If we look at the TV channels we see that countries such as America and Australia are fearful of a recession. What about New Zealand? New Zealand has been in a recession for month after month because of the antics of the Labour members, their inability to determine how an economy should grow, and their spending priorities, which were not in the best interests of this country. That is the difference between the National Party and the Labour Party. The National Party has a plan that will deliver, unlike the Labour Party.

If we look at some of those plans we see that National has a business package for small and medium businesses. The package takes away some of the tax and regulatory impacts that have been stifling businesses for a number of years. They are simple measures, but measures that are very important and very well accepted by the business community. The tax cut package will come. New Zealanders will get a tax cut under a National-led Government. Let us compare that with the Labour Government. It took Labour 9 years to deliver a tax cut, and it had no intention of delivering the rest of the tax cuts. In December it was going to stop the next tranche of tax cuts. That was Labour’s grand plan of economic development. Its grand plan to deal with the recession was to stop the tax cuts that most economies are taking on board—about 40 percent of the US package is made up of tax cuts, and that country is led by the Democratic Party! Labour would have stopped the tax cut package. It only put it up for the election—that was it. It had no intention of carrying it through, because it does not understand the fundamentals of business. It does not understand that if businesses are given money they can use that money to build a stronger economy.

Labour would have taken money off New Zealanders because it thinks it knows best how to spend that money in these difficult times. On the other hand, National understands that New Zealanders need that money in their hands. We understand that New Zealanders can make the right choices. We will back them in these times so that they can deliver the kind of future that we need. Under a National Government we will have a growing economy. We will not leave this place in a recession, as the Labour Government did. We will not squander on political things the best economic conditions that this country has had—through international trade—for many years. We will look for a direction that will build a stronger New Zealand and a way forward. Thank you.

Hon MARYAN STREET (Labour) : I would like to add my congratulations to you, Mr Deputy Speaker, and your colleagues on your elevation to the speakership role. In particular, I congratulate the Hon Dr Lockwood Smith.

It is increasingly obvious to all that the world is caught in the grip of a profound recession, the like of which has not been seen since the 1930s, or even, in New Zealand’s history, since perhaps the 1880s. But it is well known both at home and abroad that New Zealand goes into this period of economic uncertainty in better condition that almost any other developed country, such is the legacy of the careful husbandry of Dr Cullen and the previous Labour administration. What the country needs at this point is bold and decisive leadership—boldness because unprecedented times call for unprecedented measures, and decisiveness because there are, and will continue to be, hard decisions to make about how best to protect Kiwi workers and their families from the worst effects of the recession.

We in the Labour team stand ready to support the Government when it takes such measures and makes such decisions in the best interests of all New Zealanders. We have said that from the start of our time in Opposition and it still holds true. Hon Dr MICHAEL CULLEN: —and I remind the member opposite that he attacked the Government every year for running too big a surplus. Every year National promised to spend the surplus. Now it can live off that surplus for a year or two, but within 3 years it will be looking at debt going back to being worse than it was in 1999, and within 5 or 6 years it will be back to where it was in the early 1990s. It will be a long haul through the next decade, in that regard.

To correct those imbalances, New Zealand needs more exports and more research and development, but the Government’s answer was to can the research and development incentive, to cancel the Fast Forward fund, and to cancel the Broadband Investment Fund, which was ready to roll. So this will not be a year for asking business what it wants, which is what Mr Joyce announced it would be today. By the time National decides that, the rest of the world will have moved forward to the next stage, and we will be catching up again in that regard.

What about the extraordinary claim from Mr Key that New Zealand does not have a debt problem? New Zealand has always had a debt problem. For a while the Government did not have a debt problem, but the new Government solved that pretty quickly. Now we have a household debt problem within New Zealand. Unfortunately, the Government’s obsession with tax cuts will not work, because the individual in the household acts pro-cyclically. When the economy is turning down and people are afraid of losing their jobs, they will actually start to save more, not spend, unless money is given to those at the bottom. What did the Government do? It introduced a tax cut programme that took away tax cuts from those at the bottom and redistributed them to those at the top, who will not be spending that money in stimulating the New Zealand economy.

What of the financial markets? There is no sign of a comprehensive understanding of what needs to be done. The Government is still locked into a right-wing agenda in that respect. New Zealand needs a new social contract around a market economy that states clearly that capitalism succeeds best not merely in producing wealth but when there is a fair sharing of that wealth. That was the contract that dominated the first three-quarters of the 20th century. It has been broken over the last quarter of a century, and that has led directly to the crisis that we are now in. Unless there is a move to have much greater disclosure in financial markets, both within countries and across countries, unless there is much greater commonality of rules, and unless there is the ability to intervene by regulators without tight, brightline rules that create means for financial sector people to move around them, we will simply see the next recession being worse than this. This recession is worse than the last one precisely because those issues have not been addressed.

What about the cushioning effects and taking advantage of them? It is much too little, much too timid, and it is constrained by National’s tax decisions. There should have been bold moves on sustainability, on public transport, and on skills, and these areas have not been mentioned at all so far in the Government’s announcements. A whole strategy has been agreed between Business New Zealand, the Council of Trade Unions, and the Government, and it is ready to roll out. It could be funded much more—Business New Zealand wants that. Where is the announcement around that? There is nothing so far. Where are we building the skills for when the economy takes up again? Luckily, the Government will get Kiwis coming back from Australia. Luckily, there will be fewer Kiwis going to Australia. That will be the great success in the next year. Why? Because Australia is in recession, and therefore there will not be so many people going. It is a great move, but it does not mean anything for the future of this country.

Where are the moves to retool industry while we have the chance to do so in this situation? Instead, there will be 69 new State houses, which is actually a reduction of 11, even on the Government’s own numbers. There will be a $42 million roading package. In terms of the cost of bringing it forward, half will be spent on a bridge designed merely to get people to and from their holidays faster. What will that do to productivity? Well, if the Government is going to take a week off people’s holidays, they will need to get to and from their holidays faster! Of course, the doozy concerns Transpower and the network—the Government is going to paint the pylons. So it is not a case of Fawlty Towers; rather, “Monty Pylon” is the Government’s answer to an infrastructure package. Painting pylons will be the answer to New Zealand’s economic problems! Well, it is slightly better than the situation in the 1930s—but not an awful lot.

It is no wonder that yesterday and today Mr English has looked overwhelmed by the problems. I know that the numbers he is getting from Treasury are enough to frighten any Minister of Finance. It is no wonder that he said yesterday, in a slip of the tongue, that it is the worst recession for 90 years. If that is true, the Minister of Finance yesterday told us that it is worse than the Great Depression. That was a frightening comment from a Minister of Finance. This Government does not have an answer, at all. It is fiddling and fiddling around, and it does not know what to do. It simply has spin lines to try to blame the previous Government. It will not work.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH (Minister for the Environment) : That speech was from a member whose legacy to New Zealand is a decade of deficits. That member said today that we should be spending more, but only 6 months ago he was accusing John Key of being reckless for wanting a $4.5 billion programme of infrastructure investment. What we have now is a Government that is committed to keeping its word to the public of New Zealand. We have a Government that is committed to responding properly to the global recession and to doing so in a way that is realistic and responsible.

We are 85 days into the first 100 days of the new Government. Is this a Government that is doing what it said it would do? Well, we said that in the first 100 days we would pass a tax package to reduce tax for all New Zealanders and to improve the incentives to create wealth. I note and compare the first act of the previous Labour Government when it came to office. It put taxes up. Members on this side of the House are proud of the fact that one of the first actions we have taken is to reduce the tax burden on those who earn the wealth for this country.

We have come to office with a huge infrastructure deficit. If there is any part that reflects that, it is in respect of the shambles of the Cook Strait cable. As a Minister in the previous National Government I remember being told that if there was an area where we would be attacked in the unlikely event of a military threat, then it would be the Cook Strait cable. Do members realise that the previous Labour Government, without any enemy, has created a set of infrastructure where half the Cook Strait cable is down? Whether we look at roading in Auckland, at electricity, at water, or at any other part of the infrastructure we will see that the last Government neglected New Zealand. National is on track to address that problem.

We said that during our 100-day programme there would be a Minister for Infrastructure. We said that we would be setting out a programme with a 20-year plan for infrastructure, and we said that we would bring those projects forward early so that we could get on and do them. The Government is doing just that.

The third thing we said we would do within that 100-day programme is to introduce and announce a set of reforms to the Resource Management Act. For 9 years there were pleas from every corner of New Zealand for the previous Government to make changes to that Act, which has acted as such a brake on the engine room of growth for the New Zealand economy, but the previous Minister for the Environment said that the Act was beautifully crafted and beautifully written, and steadfastly refused to make changes to that statute.

I was amazed to get a letter today from a former president of the Labour Party, Mr Bob Harvey. He has written to me to congratulate the Government on its Resource Management Act reforms and to say that they are excellent. He went one step further: he said that they should have occurred years ago. Bob Harvey is right; they should have occurred years ago. Next week this Government will be introducing a resource management reform bill so that we can build the infrastructure and have the engine room of the New Zealand economy growing.

We also said that we would be introducing a transitional relief tax package. I say to members opposite that there is almost a pretence from David Cunliffe that if only the Government spent enough, then it could somehow make the international recession go away in New Zealand. Members on this side of the House are far more realistic. Anybody who pretends that New Zealand can somehow magically—as David Cunliffe would have it—remove itself from the international events that are occurring is off in cuckoo land. We have seen from this Government a very realistic response to those things that we can do. We are aware that there will be New Zealanders out there, hard-working New Zealanders, who will lose their jobs through no fault of their own. I congratulate Paula Bennett on the package she has brought forward that will make it easier for people, particularly for low and middle income families, as they manage the effects of the recession.

I particularly compliment Simon Power on the work he has done in the justice area. If one looks at the 100-day programme and the challenges—[Interruption] Well, what does it say in the 100-day programme? It says that legislation will be introduced to deal with the parole issues for the worst repeat violent offenders. We have done that. I say well done to Simon Power. It says that we will introduce legislation to clamp down on criminal gangs and the drug trade. It says that we will make changes to the bail laws. It says that we will make changes around the DNA laws. It says that we will introduce a levy for those who commit crimes so that we can help victims of crime. It is a very ambitious agenda.

The interesting thing coming from members opposite is that every one of them has said that the Government is going too fast. Members on this side of the House say that we are keeping our word—and we will keep our word to the electorate.

I also commend Anne Tolley, the Minister of Education. She said that in her first 100 days she would introduce legislative change to provide for literacy and numeracy standards—and she has delivered on that. I say well done to her. New Zealanders up and down the country will also say well done to her.

Tonight I am holding a public meeting in my Nelson electorate around the issue of special education facilities. I have to say that the community in Nelson welcomes the extra money that has been brought forward for educational facilities. What does it mean for my community? It means that special-needs children in Richmond will have access to a newer facility so that they might get the education they deserve. I know that members on all sides of the House will say that that is right and proper. I just cannot see why members opposite would whinge about a plan that will see children in New Zealand have better facilities than they have had previously.

I look at the programme in terms of what the Government is planning in the area of health. The Government said it would instruct the Ministry of Health and the district health boards to pull back on their bureaucracy, and I congratulate the Minister of Health—my benchmate—on doing that. The Government campaigned on providing the full 12-month treatment for Herceptin. I have to say, the most amazing thing happened to me over the holiday period when I hopped down to my local Mitre 10. A lovely woman and her family came up to me—in a way that has never occurred in my 18 years as a parliamentarian—to express how grateful they are that the Government is providing that Herceptin. In fact, it was actually very emotional, in that the woman broke down in tears over the fact that no longer would they have to raise a mortgage on their house so that their daughter might be able to get the best chance of life. I say good on the Minister of Health for meeting that part of the 100-day programme. I know that families all over New Zealand are grateful for that.

Also in that 100-day programme John Key said that National would repeal the Electoral Finance Act. I know that there are New Zealanders up and down this country who are grateful for that. I look forward to members opposite apologising for that Draconian law, which was nothing other than an abuse of power by a desperate Labour Government that would do anything to hang on to power, even screw the electoral laws to its own advantage. The most amazing element has been the Leader of the Opposition admitting that Labour had that wrong.

This is a Government that is keeping its word. This is a Government that is responding responsibly to the recession. This is a Government that is on track to be able to be a responsible and good Government for the people of New Zealand.
