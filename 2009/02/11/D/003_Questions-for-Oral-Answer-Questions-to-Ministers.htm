Questions to Ministers

Ministers—Confidence

1. Hon PHIL GOFF (Leader of the Opposition) to the Prime Minister: Does he have confidence in his Ministers?

Hon JOHN KEY (Prime Minister) : Yes.

Hon Phil Goff: Why would the Prime Minister have confidence in a Minister whose behaviour is described by the Otago Daily Times as “blatantly partisan scapegoating” and as “having compromised any semblance of the due process,”—that Minister being the Minister of Health, Tony Ryall—and is the Prime Minister prepared to tolerate such behaviour in a Minister?

Hon JOHN KEY: Yes, I have great confidence in the Minister of Health, who is prepared to look for accountability when there is a $17 million fraud. While I am at it, I congratulate the Minister of Health on ensuring that those New Zealand women who have previously been funded for Herceptin for only 9 weeks now have a 52-week programme.

Hon Phil Goff: Does the Prime Minister support and have confidence in Tony Ryall’s efforts to sack Otago District Health Board’s elected chair, Richard Thomson, on the basis of a fraud initiated before Mr Thomson became chair, and when Mr Thomson was in fact responsible for helping catch the crooks; if so, why?

Hon JOHN KEY: I understand that the Minister of Health is following a proper process, and at this stage no decision has been made.

Hon Phil Goff: If the Minister is following proper process, why did the Otago Daily Times describe him as “having compromised any semblance of the due process,”? Is the Prime Minister confident that Mr Ryall is right in this matter, and that Otago’s chief medical officer, the senior doctors and staff, the entire Otago District Health Board, the Otago Daily Times, and the big majority of the voting public that re-elected Mr Thomson are wrong; if so, what evidence does he have for that view?

Hon JOHN KEY: I repeat that the Minister of Health is following due process. I am not responsible for what the Otago Daily Times writes, although I have noted that in recent times it has written some pretty terrible stuff about the Labour Party.

Hon Phil Goff: Is the Prime Minister confident that the approach of the Minister of Health, Mr Tony Ryall, in arrogantly trying to bully the elected chair of the district health board to resign, is consistent with the views of one Mr Tony Ryall MP, who earlier said that a well-founded dismissal of another board was “appalling political manipulation”?

Hon JOHN KEY: The Minister of Health is following due process, but I repeat again—[Interruption] Members opposite might not like it, but he is following due process. The members opposite need to realise that when there is a $17 million fraud this Government will stand up and make sure that there is accountability. The Government that Phil Goff was a member of did not do so.

Recession—Infrastructure Projects

2. CHRIS TREMAIN (National—Napier) to the Minister for Infrastructure: What steps has the Government recently taken to help address current economic challenges and contribute to driving up New Zealand’s long-term economic competitiveness?

Hon BILL ENGLISH (Minister for Infrastructure) : This morning I was pleased to join the Prime Minister and three other Ministers in confirming almost $500 million worth of publicly funded housing, transport, and education infrastructure projects. This is the first tranche of what will be a number of projects committing funding of up to $5 billion that has been allocated over the next 3 or 4 years.

Chris Tremain: Why were these projects chosen?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: These projects were chosen because they represent quality investments of public money, and also because they were among the few projects that were actually ready to go. I was surprised and disappointed to find that most of the announcements made by the previous Government about infrastructure spending related to projects that were unfunded and nowhere near ready to go.

Hon David Cunliffe: Has the Minister seen any reports on the comparative size of the stimulus injected by the so-called rolling maul of announcements, of which today’s infrastructure package was one; if so, can he confirm that although the Australian Government has introduced stimulus initiatives of about 4.5 percent of GDP, and the US of about 5.5 percent of GDP, this Government’s stimulus initiative in this package makes up less than 1 percent of GDP?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: As the member will know, New Zealand has a significant fiscal stimulus—at least in comparison with the Australian and the US Governments—of around 4 percent of GDP over the next couple of years. The difference is that New Zealand went into recession 12 months earlier than those Governments, under the previous Government. The 2008 Budget cut taxes increased spending significantly, which means that by now, when the recession is deepening, the New Zealand Government unfortunately has a lot fewer options than those Governments.

Chris Tremain: How will the projects announced this morning benefit the economy?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: The projects will benefit the economy in two ways. First of all, they will provide some short-term stimulus; they will soak up some jobs in a sector where jobs are being lost—that is, the construction sector. But secondly, and probably just as important, we need to get this economy ready for recovery. After 10 years of misdirected and complacent policy, these projects represent quality investments that will help lift the productivity of the New Zealand economy.

Hon David Cunliffe: What is the basis of the Government’s estimate of 2,000 jobs to be created by this package and retained over 2 years; and why, when Treasury has estimated that at least 68,000 New Zealanders will lose their jobs in the next 15 months, does he think that that is in any way an adequate response?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: These projects will have the effect of helping to retain some jobs and perhaps creating opportunities for those who have already lost their jobs. We have never said that we will be able to roll back the unemployment that is forecast for this year. The member quoted Australia; at the same time as the Australian Government announced its significant stimulus, it also announced that unemployment was likely to reach over 7 percent. This, along with other measures the Government has taken, will help protect people from the sharp edges of recession and send us along the road of lifting our economic capacity so this economy can grow when this recession is over.

Chris Tremain: What reports has the Minister seen on alternative approaches?

Hon Dr Michael Cullen: Roger Douglas’s latest package?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: Well, I have seen that report! Quite a lot of public discussion has been reflected in questions today about the difference between stimulus packages in New Zealand and in Australia. The net result of the 2008 Budget, tax cuts to come on 1 April, and the Government’s allocations to infrastructure spending, mean that the stimulus in the New Zealand economy is comparable to that in Australia and the US. It is just that it has not been announced in such a dramatic way, mainly because New Zealand started in recession 12 months ahead of those countries and we had an election around the time of the financial crisis.

Hon David Cunliffe: I seek leave to table an article from the New Zealand Herald on 19 December 2008 reporting Treasury forecasts of between 68,000 and 87,000 new unemployed in New Zealand over the next year.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that document being tabled? There is none.

  • Document, by leave, laid on the Table of the House.

Hon David Cunliffe: I seek leave to table a transcript of an article by the New Zealand Press Association on One News today reporting the Government’s estimate of only 2,000 jobs created by this so-called infrastructure package.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that document being tabled? There is objection to that.

Hon Sir Roger Douglas: Could the Minister explain to the House why, given the experience of the last 10 years, when the Labour Government reduced productivity in this country from 3 percent to less than 1 percent as a result of its policies of redistribution, the Government is planning to spend so much of the $500 million on infrastructure in what can only be described as low-quality, low-growth projects?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: I compliment the member on his analysis of the misdirected policies of the last 10 years, but I disagree with his description of these projects.

Research and Development Funding and KiwiSaver Reductions—Treasury Advice

3. Hon DAVID CUNLIFFE (Labour—New Lynn) to the Minister of Finance: What advice has he received from the Treasury on removing the research and development tax credit, removing the $700 million Fast Forward Fund, and reducing KiwiSaver contributions?

Hon BILL ENGLISH (Minister of Finance) : Treasury gave us the advice to keep the research and development tax credit, to maintain the Fast Forward Fund in the form that had been proposed by the previous Government, and to make no changes to KiwiSaver. However, prior to the election National had made a number of decisions, which were to remove the research and development tax credit, remove the Fast Forward Fund, and change KiwiSaver contributions, and we are sticking to our word.

Hon David Cunliffe: Why did the Minister suppress the Treasury briefing that was critical of these moves, and release it at a time designed to minimise public awareness?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: The Labour Party complaining about compliance with the Official Information Act is like Jack the Ripper complaining about community violence.

Craig Foss: What other reports has the Minister seen of advice offered by Treasury to finance Ministers?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: The Treasury advice is, as always, mixed. Some we agree with, some we disagree with. Treasury has given us other advice, such as the need for extensive review of the Resource Management Act, which the Government has followed, and a Resource Management Act Amendment Bill will be in Parliament within a few weeks. Treasury has also given us the advice that the performance of the Public Service needs to be improved significantly, and I would agree with that. After 10 years of bullying and manipulation, it can do a lot better and we will make sure it does.

Hon Dr Michael Cullen: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I raise a question about the relevance of that answer. The question was a patsy question designed to elicit the answer about my reference to an ideological burp, and I think the Minister read the wrong answer at that particular point.

Mr SPEAKER: That is not a particularly helpful point of order, I must say.

Hon David Cunliffe: Why did the Minister not have fortitude equal to that of the previous Minister of Finance and release the Treasury briefing at the same time as other briefings to incoming Ministers were released?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: The Treasury briefing to the incoming Government has been released. There are absolutely no secrets in it, and I am surprised that in the context of the most significant economic recession in 90 years, this is what the Labour Party is focused on.

Hon David Cunliffe: Did the Minister or any of his staff talk at any point to the Prime Minister or any of his staff about the date of releasing the Treasury briefing?

Hon BILL ENGLISH: The Prime Minister advises me that he read about it in the newspaper. It is unlikely that a matter of that kind of detail would have been discussed at the highest levels of Government.

Infrastructure Projects—Green-collar Jobs

4. JEANETTE FITZSIMONS (Co-Leader—Green) to the Prime Minister: How many jobs will his Government’s infrastructure package create; and, of those jobs, how many will be green-collar jobs?

Hon JOHN KEY (Prime Minister) : I have seen estimates that the State housing package could retain between 850 and 1,050 jobs, and that the State highway package could save up to 400 jobs. These figures are very imprecise, however, not least because the Government does not make individual hiring decisions on infrastructure projects. Regarding the second part of the member’s question, I say it is not my practice, nor is it the practice of Government departments, to classify jobs as being white-collar, blue-collar, green-collar, or any other type of collar—all jobs are important to the Government.

Jeanette Fitzsimons: Does he agree with the editorial in the New Zealand Herald that he should reinstate the Green Homes Fund insulation programme for low and middle income private homes, a programme that is particularly job rich?

Hon JOHN KEY: I agree that improving insulation in New Zealand homes will play an important part for social, health, and climate change reasons. The member can rest assured that the Government is working on that. I take issue with the fact that somehow the billion-dollar Green Homes Fund was anything other than a fantasy, because, unfortunately, it was unfunded, like so many other promises of the previous Government.

Nicky Wagner: Has the Prime Minister seen any reaction to the Government’s infrastructure announcement?

Hon JOHN KEY: Yes, I have seen a press release from the New Zealand Council of Trade Unions today, saying that: “The investment in schools and housing are exactly what is needed at this time”. I have also seen a very interesting report from Goldman Sachs, which said today it was unclear how much of the overall package was new work, but that it could see its 2009 forecast for national GDP to contract by 1.4 percent could improve by 0.6 percent. Lastly, I have seen a press release from the Leader of the Opposition, which states that education funding “might just paint a few classrooms”. Mr Goff might want to read just a little further on after his cornflakes this morning, and realise that five brand new schools are about to built and fast-tracked, a further seven schools will get funding to replace outdated and rundown buildings, 64 schools will receive funding for halls, gyms, libraries, and other buildings, and four Upper Hutt schools will get a major upgrade. I know that members out in that area, not the least of them being Mr Mallard, will be very appreciative that the National Government has finally fixed up the schools in Mr Mallard’s electorate.

Hon Phil Goff: What advice has the Prime Minister received on the number of jobs lost not just through the scrapping of the retrofitting of insulation but also through the biofuels legislation rammed through the House by Gerry Brownlee before Christmas—for example, at the Waharoa biofuels facility, which has now been mothballed as a result of that legislation?

Hon JOHN KEY: I have not received any advice on the former or the latter, except to say the following. Firstly, it was all very well for the previous Labour Government to promise things, but if there was no money allocated to them, then it was not going to go far. Secondly, even if one was to believe that the billion-dollar package was real, it equates to $60 million a year. Today we announced that by 30 June 2010 this Government, with real money, will actually be spending $220 million on the improvement of insulation and the improvement of State houses. This Government acts; that Government talked.

Sue Bradford: Does the Prime Minister really believe that building an extra 69 State houses over the next 6 months will met the increasing need, especially at the most urgent end of the State housing waiting list, and will he commit to accelerating the State house building programme over the full 3 years ahead, rather than just the next 6 months?

Hon JOHN KEY: No, I do not think anyone could practically believe that 69 extra State houses will fix the entire waiting list. There needs to be a recognition that there is a long waiting list, but there also needs to be a recognition that over the last few years the Government that was in place prior to the election totally failed to renovate and keep up to date those houses, and this Government is now being forced to fix that up.

Nikki Kaye: Have all the infrastructure projects announced this morning been fully funded?

Hon JOHN KEY: Yes. These projects have been costed and will be charged against Budget allowances. We have made provision for all those projects, and they will actually happen. That is completely different from the last Government’s practice of making lofty infrastructure announcements, with no idea of and no concern about how they would be funded.

Dr Russel Norman: Can the Prime Minister promise New Zealanders that the process for awarding these and future infrastructure contracts will be absolutely transparent; if so, will he ensure that all those who bid for these contracts are required to declare any donations to political parties?

Hon JOHN KEY: I am not aware of the operational process that is undertaken for the administration of such contracts, but if in any way, shape, or form the member is implying that somehow contracts will be issued and the upgrading of these State houses is because National is now the Government, then I find that offensive. The reason we are doing this is that we want to improve the State housing stock in New Zealand.

Metiria Turei: Will the Prime Minister match his excellent promise of more schools and school facilities with a commitment for more teachers and support staff—a commitment that would employ far more people per dollar invested than buildings will?

Hon JOHN KEY: I cannot comment on the need for more teachers; I can say that there has been a significant increase in teacher numbers recently. We are now looking to ensure that teaching practices are changed through the administration of things such as National’s literacy and numeracy plans, which will make a significant difference to the output of students in New Zealand.

Jeanette Fitzsimons: Can the Prime Minister tell the House what the difference is between announcing a programme today that is not funded but will be funded in next year’s Budget and announcing a programme last year to be funded out of the emissions trading scheme, which had already been legislated for?

Hon JOHN KEY: When we announced the projects this morning we intended to start paying for them as of tomorrow, when people start to work on them, as opposed to the announcement of the Greens’ fund, which effectively, as Dr Cullen pointed out when he asked the patsy question to David Parker last year, was all based on whether State-owned enterprises eventually gave some money back. As Mr Parker said at the end of the question, without that increased investment—in other words, unless the State-owned enterprises put up their money—there was not the slightest prospect of meeting the targets. In other words, there was no money.

Jeanette Fitzsimons: Did the Prime Minister, in deciding to spend more on new roads, take into account research quoted in The Economist that spending on buses and trains creates 19 percent more jobs than the same money spent on new roads, or research quoted by the Victoria Transport Policy Institute that each million dollars shifted away from purchasing motor fuel into anything else creates 4½ new jobs per million dollars?

Hon JOHN KEY: When reaching the decisions we reached today and the decisions we will be reaching over the months ahead about further infrastructure investment, we take on board the fact that our roading infrastructure has a need for improvement that will help the environment. That is not to say there is not the need for further investment in both rail and public transport. I assure the member that both will get additional funding.

Jeanette Fitzsimons: I seek leave to table the paper Smart Transportation Economic Stimulation from the Victoria Transport Policy Institute, which contains one of the figures I quoted.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that document being tabled? There is none.

  • Document, by leave, laid on the Table of the House.

Jeanette Fitzsimons: I seek leave to table the Economist article “Be careful what you wish for: spending on infrastructure could easily run amok”.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that document being tabled? There is none.

  • Document, by leave, laid on the Table of the House.

Corrections, Department—Confidence

5. Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE (Labour—Waimakariri) to the Minister of Corrections: Does she have confidence in the Department of Corrections; if so, why?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS (Minister of Corrections) : I am confident that the department understands that my expectations are significantly higher than those of the previous Minister of Corrections, Phil Goff.

Hon Clayton Cosgrove: Does the Minister agree with her colleague Simon Power when he said that two escapes in 5 days was “a worry”; if so, how would she characterise the six escapes in 7 days that occurred in January, and another one that occurred from North Waikato Prison this morning?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: In relation to the escapes the member has referred to, two escapes are, correctly, part of the corrections system; the others were actually escapes not from the corrections system but from the police. If the member would like to put down a question on my other portfolio, I would be very happy to give him the answer.

Melissa Lee: What action has the Minister taken so far in the corrections portfolio?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: Quite a lot, actually. We have had a very action-packed few months. So far I have visited three prisons, including the Auckland Prison (Maximum Security Division). I have spoken to and listened to prison staff and asked for their ideas on what improvements can be made to ensure they can do their jobs better. I have spoken to and listened to the prison unions and sought their ideas on practical ways the Department of Corrections can be improved. I have visited and listened to community, probation, and psychological services—something the previous Minister never did. I have reintroduced the Corrections Amendment Bill (No 2), which was left to languish by the previous Government. I have asked the Department of Corrections to review the legislation and remove roadblocks to the private management of prisons, and I have asked that that be done quickly. I have sought a review of security classifications for prisoners, to see whether there is any way that this can be fine-tuned to better protect the public and corrections staff. I have given my support to the construction of prefabricated prison buildings to achieve cost reductions in the vicinity of 25 to 40 percent, initially, compared with the cost of traditional bed and campus-style cells.

Mr SPEAKER: The Minister is—

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: There is just so much, Mr Speaker. There is just a little bit more. But, mostly, I have instructed the Department of Corrections to work with Vodafone and Telecom to ensure that cellphone towers built under the previous Government in prison grounds—in Auckland Prison—are relocated as quickly as possible. And, by the way, I have also asked that double-bunking proceed.

Mr SPEAKER: Before I call the honourable member, I remind Ministers again that giving answers to questions is not a time for making speeches. It is difficult to hold the Opposition to brief questions if the answers are very, very long.

Hon Clayton Cosgrove: Why is it that it took the Minister 8 days before she publicly responded to the issue of six escapes in a week, given the seriousness of the issue—and when she finally did respond, she dismissed this as “an operational matter”, and then tried to blame the previous Government rather than provide assurances to the affected victims and their families—and apart from engaging in a tiki tour around the country, prison to prison, what specific action has she taken on prison escapes, given that we have had another one from North Waikato this morning?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: Perhaps the member who has just resumed his seat did not listen to the answer. Of the six escapes he is talking about, only two are from the corrections system. Frankly, not one escape is acceptable, and that is why this Minister is going to hold people to account for their actions and inactions. Right now, I say to Mr Cosgrove, I am holding them to account—which is far more than 9 years of a Labour Government ever did; not one person lost his or her job.

Hon Phil Goff: Has the Minister, given that she has blamed this on the Minister of Police rather than on the Minister of Corrections, talked to her “colleague” about this?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: Of course in National we do not need to do that, because I am that same Minister. But I can tell that particular member—unlike the situation under Labour where members had to have a meeting—

Hon Member: What did she say to you?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: She said that the police have been asked right now to find out why they are not restraining prisoners by the same method the Department of Corrections uses, and they have been asked to look at the law changes that need to happen to change that. This Minister is not going to sit back for 9 years, as that member did, and wait for something else to happen, like Karl Kuchenbecker being killed.

Hon Clayton Cosgrove: Is the Minister concerned that the significant improvements within the Department of Corrections, which occurred over the last 9 years and which led to an 84 percent reduction—[Interruption]—I will say it again for the member at the back—which led to an 84 percent reduction in the number of prison escapes and a 90 percent reduction in the number of serious assaults on prison staff under the previous Government, despite the fact there was an increase in the prison population of 3,200, is now being compromised; if not, as she takes advice from the member beside her, why not?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: I can well understand that under the Labour Government people were in fact queuing up to get into prison, but I can tell them that under this Minister they might not want to be there.

David Garrett: What, if anything, is she doing about the incompetence and corruption at the higher levels of the Department of Corrections that was ignored by the previous Government; and by what date can we expect to see some significant action on those problems?

Hon JUDITH COLLINS: For a start, this Minister has been prepared, and will continue to be prepared, to listen to staff, unions, managers, and the public, and several steps are being considered. But I am not at liberty to further advise the House at this stage.

Schools—Building Projects

6. ALLAN PEACHEY (National—Tāmaki) to the Minister of Education: What steps is the Government taking to fast track school building projects?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY (Minister of Education) : The Government has announced today that it is bringing forward $216.7 million of spending on education infrastructure. Of this funding, $28 million will be an additional capital injection straight away in this financial year and a further $189 million will be used to bring forward school building projects from the middle of this year.

Allan Peachey: What types of school building projects will be fast tracked?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: There are a large number of projects. One example is the bringing forward of the construction of non-teaching spaces in over 60 schools across the country. Another example is a commitment to provide an initial investment of $34 million of information and communications technology infrastructure to schools that are most in need, in order to make them ready for the high-speed broadband roll-out.

Hon Chris Carter: How much of today’s school property funding announcement of $216 million is actually new money, and how much comes from the extra $3.4 billion that Labour had already allocated to school property?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: I am pleased that the member has asked me that question. Labour never promised money to build a trades academy in South Auckland; we have. Labour never committed an extra $30 million of funding to the Upper Hutt schools that it froze capital funding for; we have. Labour never committed an additional $28 million of funding in this financial year to taking advantage of spare capacity; we have. Labour never committed extra capital funding for special schools and satellite schools; we have. The real school property legacy that we have from Labour is a comment in the pre-election fiscal update that told us that the previous administration, under that member, had not set aside any funding for the $429 million of capital injection that schools needed over the next 3 years to accommodate roll growth and regional population changes.

Hon Chris Carter: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I would like to ask for your guidance. Yesterday we had an issue when the Minister of Labour was asked a very simple question, and she was asked to give a simple answer and lay it out clearly, but she did not. I ask the Minister how much—

Mr SPEAKER: I listened carefully to the Minister’s answer. The Minister gave a very full answer to the member’s question.

Hon Chris Carter: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. My question was a simple one; it asked how much of it is new money. The Minister never answered that question.

Mr SPEAKER: I have already ruled on the member’s point of order.

Chris Hipkins: Can she confirm that the $30 million promised to Upper Hutt secondary and intermediate schools today will be additional to the $14.6 million committed by the previous Labour-led Government, as the schools were led to believe during the election campaign; if not, why not?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: I am delighted to refer the member to National’s 21st Century School Building Programme, announced in October last year, which made it very clear that there was an additional $30 million to go to the Upper Hutt schools. That is what we have said in all the documentation we have released today. That is considerably more than what the previous Labour Government spent and offered to those schools after freezing capital funding under three education Ministers, destroying the hopes of a whole cohort of school pupils in Upper Hutt.

Hon Chris Carter: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. Again, the Minister was asked a simple question: does it include the $14 million, or not?

Mr SPEAKER: The member will resume his seat. The Minister gave a very clear answer to the question asked, and the member will resist making those points of order.

Hon Dr Michael Cullen: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. The answer referred to a National Party policy document. You ruled before Christmas that we were not allowed to ask questions about National Party policy documents from before the election. I assume that once a Minister refers to such a document in the House, it becomes a current matter and we can ask further questions about it, contrary to your previous ruling.

Mr SPEAKER: The honourable member has raised a very interesting point of order. It is traditional practice in the House that material the Minister brings into his or her answer can be followed up in further supplementary questions.

Hon Trevor Mallard: I raise a point of order, Mr Speaker. I appreciate your advice to the previous point of order, bar one point—that is, that the Minister’s answer was very clear. It was not very clear.

Mr SPEAKER: The member will resume his seat because he is questioning my ruling. [Interruption] The honourable member may just reflect a little on how his behaviour is contributing to good order in the House. I have demonstrated that where clear questions are asked, as they were yesterday, I will support the member in obtaining clear answers. I believe the Minister gave a clear answer, and that is my judgment. The Minister referred, in fact, to dollar amounts in relation to the previous question by Chris Hipkins. I have made my ruling, and the member will not question it.

Allan Peachey: What steps will the Government take as part of this package to put trades training back into schools?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: We committed today to provide $6 million of funding from the middle of this year to allow construction to commence on a trades academy at the Southern Cross Campus in South Auckland. This will be the first of at least five trades academies that this Government will fund to boost trades training opportunities around New Zealand.

Hon Trevor Mallard: Was the additional $30 million for Upper Hutt schools over and above the $14 million previously committed?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: At the end of last year I introduced and passed through the House a bill that allowed me, as Minister, to set standards in literacy and numeracy. I say to the member that in anyone’s language an “extra” $30 million is an “additional” $30 million. With any normal numeracy skill, one would add $14 million and $30 million and get $44 million.

Peseta Sam Lotu-Iiga: Which new schools will have their construction brought forward?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: Almost $69 million will be spent on bringing construction of five new schools forward: Mt Wellington Primary School and Hingaia Primary School in Auckland, Papamoa East Primary School and Papamoa Secondary School in the Bay of Plenty, and Kerikeri Waipapa School in Northland. These five schools are being brought forward by between 1 and 2 years to help provide jobs in these areas.

Simon Bridges: What reports has the Minister received about the state of the schools that are receiving funding in the announcement today?

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: I have seen comments from a former member of Parliament stating that particular schools needed a “significant funding boost to help with the backlog of capital works that have been put on hold”. That comment was from the Hon Paul Swain, the previous Labour MP for Rimutaka, and was in relation to the four Upper Hutt schools that we have announced today will receive an additional $30 million in funding. Under the previous administration three successive Ministers of Education decided they would freeze capital funding for years while they conducted an education super-school Labour Party thought experiment. Our commitment to these schools today recognises that they and their community were done a tremendous wrong by the previous administration.

Hon Trevor Mallard: I seek leave to table the press statement of the Minister of Education, which indicates the total spend is $30 million and not $44 million.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that press statement being tabled? There appears to be none.

  • Document, by leave, laid on the Table of the House.

Hon Chris Carter: I seek leave to table a press statement stating “Education Stimulus Package mostly recycled material”.

Mr SPEAKER: Is there any objection to that press statement being tabled? There is objection.

Minimum Wage Increase—Real Income Gain

7. Hon TREVOR MALLARD (Labour—Hutt South) to the Minister of Labour: What will be the gain in real income per week, adjusted for inflation since 1 April 2008, of a full-time worker on the minimum wage, as a result of the Government’s decision on the 2009 minimum wage increase?

Hon KATE WILKINSON (Minister of Labour) : It will be $3.78.

Hon Trevor Mallard: Did the Minister consult the Minister of Māori Affairs or the Minister for the Community and Voluntary Sector before she took this issue to Cabinet?

Hon KATE WILKINSON: I am always open to receiving representations from the Government’s supply and confidence partners. I received a letter from the Minister of Māori Affairs. Te Puni Kōkiri also made a submission. We took those submissions into consideration when calculating a fair, balanced, and realistic minimum wage.

Hon Tau Henare: Has the Minister seen any reports regarding the minimum wage?

Treaty of Waitangi Settlements—Progress

8. Hon TAU HENARE (National) to the Minister for Treaty of Waitangi Negotiations: What progress has recently been made in Treaty settlements?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON (Minister for Treaty of Waitangi Negotiations) : Today the Government signed letters of agreement with three groupings representing eight iwi. Those three groups are the Kurahaupō ki Te Wai Pounamu, Tainui Taranaki ki te Tonga, and Ngāti Toa Rangatira. When finalised, the agreements will resolve all outstanding historical claims in the South Island and substantial claims in the lower North Island. They mark a significant milestone in achieving the Government’s objective of justly and durably settling all Treaty grievances by the end of 2014.

Hon Tau Henare: What does the agreement signed today between the Crown and Te Tau Ihu iwi represent?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: The agreements send a strong signal of the Government’s intention to maintain the current momentum in the Treaty settlements area. Te Tau Ihu agreements represent the first comprehensive regional settlements in an area where iwi interests are fully overlapped and highly contested.

Hekia Parata: What leadership has the Crown shown in reaching agreement with Te Tau Ihu iwi today?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: Today’s signing is a clear example of the strong political leadership that the Government is committed to bringing to this important area of work. I want to acknowledge the personal leadership of the Prime Minister, and support of the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Finance, in making the agreement possible. I also acknowledge the work of my predecessor, the Hon Dr Michael Cullen as Minister of Treaty Negotiations, who worked so hard on these settlements. I also want to make a special mention of the active involvement of the Minister of Māori Affairs, Dr Pita Sharples, who participated personally in talks with respective claimant groups, and who made a significant contribution towards making today’s signings possible.

Hon Dr Michael Cullen: Has the Minister seen a cartoon on the front page of the New Zealand Herald, which purports to imply the Government has agreed to allow Ngāti Toa to charge royalties for people performing Ka mate! Ka mate!; if so, would he care to comment on the veracity of that implied assertion?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: Yes, I have seen the cartoon. I regard the cartoon as puerile and inaccurate. The suggestion of Ka mate! Ka mate! dollar! dollar! is highly offensive to Ngāti Toa. We are not talking about that kind of redress, and as we move forward in Treaty settlements I think it would be very helpful—and the member would understand this because of his experience, as Minister, with agreements on the East Coast of the North Island—if journalists, and I hope I do not sound like Winston Peters, would get their facts right.

Rahui Katene: How will settling Treaty claims improve life for Māori; and will improving life for Māori improve life for other New Zealanders?

Hon CHRISTOPHER FINLAYSON: Settling Treaty claims will, among other things, increase economic opportunities for iwi and their members. I am particularly optimistic that the agreements signed today when finalised and legislated for will inject substantial funds into Te Tau Ihu, and that is good for iwi and very good for the regions.
