Address in Reply

Hon BILL ENGLISH (Deputy Prime Minister) : As we work through the Address in Reply debate, it has become clear that the state of the economy and the outlook for the economy have become the major issues that the New Zealand Government has to deal with. The Government has made its strategy quite clear. We are setting out to achieve two things over the next few years. The first is to protect people from the hard edge of recession, and the second is to get this economy ready for recovery. That means that any of the decisions we take in the short term to assist people through this recession need to be consistent with our long-term objective, which is to get the economy ready for recovery. Only an economy that recovers quickly will be able to generate jobs to replace the jobs that are being lost, and return New Zealand to a path where its incomes are rising.

In pursuit of those objectives, the Government has instituted a range of measures, starting with the Restart package and a bundle of legislation before Christmas to support people who were made redundant, through to the tax cuts that are going to be implemented on 1 April to put money in people’s pockets. There has been a sense among New Zealanders that the effects of the worldwide recession have not reached them, but 2009 is the year when those effects are going to wash up in New Zealand. We do not know just how severe the effects will be. One thing that accounts for the fact that people have not felt squeezed by the recession is that there has been a succession of events over the last 6 or 8 months that have had the effect of putting more cash in people’s pockets. This is part of the debate about whether the stimulus in New Zealand is comparable to that being applied in Australia and the US.

Let me just take members through the basics of the New Zealand story. New Zealand went into recession 12 months earlier than most other economies. We had a recession starting in January 2008 that was a product of quite a different set of circumstances from those that existed prior to the financial crisis and the coordinated world recession. Those circumstances included a high-inflation economy that was running out of capacity, and that was driven by misdirected and complacent policy undermining the productive capacity of New Zealanders. So we started in that recession.

At the same time there was considerable debate about tax cuts, and a good deal of pressure on a fading Government that was reaching for taxpayers’ pockets to try to spend its way through to a successful 2008 election. So the 2008 Budget was quite a big-spending Budget, which came on the heels of two previous even bigger-spending Budgets. It also included the previous Government’s tax cut programme. That amounted to a significant stimulus to the economy that started flowing through about the time of the financial crisis in the US in September and October. As the incoming Government, we instituted our own tax cut package before Christmas, which will result in significant tax cuts on 1 April and an ongoing programme with lesser tax cuts in the next 2 years.

When we add all those things together, the reality is that by the time the incoming Government was presented with the effects of the financial crisis, it had fewer opportunities than, say, the Australian Government to have a sugar punch—a one-off cheque-writing exercise to put money in people’s pockets. But we have taken what opportunities we had, and we had fewer opportunities because the previous Government had already reduced taxes and increased spending. The net effect of all that, though, is that the stimulus in the New Zealand economy is among those of the top half-dozen countries in the OECD. Treasury calculates that over the next 2 years—this is the most recent calculation—the effect of the fiscal stimulus will be 5.1 percent of GDP. That is directly comparable to the fiscal stimulus of the US, and is in fact more than the Australian fiscal stimulus, on the best measure we can make of it.

Of course, a discussion about fiscal stimulus is inherently short term. We can easily forget, as most do, that any dollar spent today stimulating the economy has to be repaid with interest tomorrow. This Government has worked hard to strike a careful balance between what it can do in the short term to protect New Zealanders from the sharpest edges of recession, and the burden of repaying the bill for that in future years. I will just give people some sense of the size of that bill, which should, I think, engender some caution in their calls for more fiscal stimulus. For instance, the operating balance—we can go into all the definitions but it is essentially the operating balance—of the New Zealand Government for 2007-08 was plus $5.6 billion. In 2008-09—the year we are in—it will be minus $1 billion. In 2009-10 it will be minus $6 billion, and in 2010-11 it will be minus $8 billion. That is a huge swing-round in the large surpluses that New Zealand has experienced over the previous 10 years. In fact, over the next 3 years we will see the operating surplus turn around in total by about $14 billion. It will go $14 billion backwards.

That, of course, will flow through into the amount of debt that New Zealand incurs and the amount of debt it will have to raise. Our best estimate of the net amount of debt the New Zealand Government will have to raise over the next 3 years is around $35 billion. That is a dramatic change in outlook. The record over the last 15 or so years has been of significant decreases in gross debt, even of getting to the position where New Zealand had positive net debt. Not much debt was paid off, but financial assets were significantly increased. Over the next few years we will have to raise very significant amounts of debt, and we will be out there raising that debt in the context of every other Government in the developed world also trying to raise debt. In the case of the US we are talking at the bare minimum of a trillion dollars, perhaps of a trillion and a half—who knows—but a vast amount of capital will be sucked back into Government coffers. So we have to make decisions now that keep in mind that with the current settings, growth outlook, and expenditure trap, New Zealand Government debt will consistently rise over the next 10 years, from its current levels of somewhere under 20 percent of GDP to somewhere in the region of 50 to 60 percent, depending on how one calculates it. That is a huge challenge for the Government; I might say it is a challenge for Parliament. It is also a challenge for this country, but we are satisfied that at the moment we have the right balance between those contending pressures.

A particular focus will be on jobs, and I point out to the House that the number of people on the unemployment benefit is starting to rise reasonably significantly. Every forecast tells us that unemployment will rise quite significantly over 2009. We start from a position of lower unemployment than almost any developed country, and it is certainly the hope of the Government that we will get through this recession without unemployment reaching the double figures it did, particularly in the recession of the early 1990s. I expect that these matters will be discussed in some detail at the job summit at the end of February, where the intention is not just to have a discussion about what policy measures the Government might take to mitigate unemployment but also to have a discussion about what role the private sector can play in containing unemployment. Many New Zealand businesses have experienced skill shortages in recent years, and we believe, as many of them believe, that it is vital through this period to retain those skilled people, so that we do not run again, in a fast-growing economy, into skill shortages when so many people have been lost from the workforce. Managing the fiscal and economic outlook will be by far the biggest challenge for the Government this year, but it is one that this Government is up to.

Hon ANNE TOLLEY (Minister of Education) : Making eloquent speeches from the benches on the other side of the House is what those members do best. We know that they are very good at talking and very poor at doing, so I want to take this opportunity to talk about some of the real action that has taken place in education as part of the 100 days of action.

When National came to office at the end of last year we had promised the early childhood sector that we would review the regulations the previous Government had put into place. Those regulations were going to put many early childhood centres out of business, put huge compliance costs on to some businesses, and put out of service a number of kōhanga that were unable to meet the regulations, which in general had caused a great deal of angst throughout the service. So we have done that. We are now almost ready to start on a consultation process to have another look at some of those onerous regulations, and we are looking to reduce costs in the early childhood sector so that it can get on with increasing the participation of our children who are able to take part in early childhood education—in particular Māori and Pacific children, who figure so poorly in the statistics when we look at who is attending and getting education prior to starting school.

Secondly, we introduced and passed through all stages in this House the Education (National Standards) Amendment Bill. I was interested to hear the previous speaker say that the legislation passed before Christmas was unimportant, because passing that bill is one of the most important things we can do if we are serious about lifting the achievement of our young New Zealand students. For children to be able to read and write, and do maths, as they leave primary school, at a level somewhere near their chronological age, gives those children the opportunity to go on to secondary school and get qualifications that will enable them to then live good, healthy, and wealthy lives, contributing to our New Zealand society. So that Education (National Standards) Amendment Act is a hugely important step as we work to lift the achievement of our young New Zealanders. It will enable the Minister to set standards in literacy and numeracy that are about what we expect children to have learnt by certain stages through their schooling. It requires schools to involve parents by telling them in plain language what the strengths and weaknesses are of their children, so that parents can get involved in their children’s education.

In addition to that, we increased the maximum penalties for our hard-core truants, because we, as a National Government, want to make it clear that attending school is absolutely critical if our young people are to get a good education. We take that seriously, and we want the courts and the boards of trustees to take recidivist truants seriously, too. So we have increased those maximum penalties for truancy. But that is not all we have done about truancy. We are currently setting out to review the effectiveness of our district truancy services; we are consulting widely across the schooling sector, and we are consulting with the truancy services themselves. I, unlike the previous Minister of Education, want to know how many truancy officers we have out there on the ground, and how effective they are in the services they provide.

We will also carry out the survey that the previous Minister of Education cancelled, so that we can know how many students are truant from our schools at any one time. Why on earth the previous Minister did not pursue that last year, so that we had up-to-date knowledge, could clearly identify where the bigger problem areas were, and could put some extra resources into them, I have no idea. In fact, I do have an idea. I do not believe that the previous Government cared about truancy. It was a nuisance to that Government, and its members did not pursue getting some data, at least, so that they could take some action. This Government is determined to make sure that every child gets the opportunity to learn, and we want to know where those extra resources that we have made available for truancy services can best be used.

In addition, we have had a look at the work of the Education Review Office. This was part of the election promise we made to refocus the Education Review Office, and I have instructed the office to focus more on those schools that are struggling and to have a lighter hand on those schools that have proved themselves to be successful over a period of time and that are capable of more self-assessment themselves. Those schools that are performing successfully will be able to move on to a 4 or 5-yearly cycle with the Education Review Office. That will allow the office to focus its resources and its attention on those schools that are struggling, for one reason or another. We are determined to make sure that we take action earlier in schools that are having difficulties, in order to support the young people attending them. It is absolutely critical that we know which schools they are, what their problems are, and how we can get in and help them to get back on their feet and provide an excellent education for young New Zealanders. We have refocused the Education Review Office into a tiered system of cycling their investigations into schools, starting from the top with a 4 or 5-year cycle. We will retain a section in the middle with a 3-year cycle, and those schools that are having difficulties will be on a 1 to 2-year cycle from the Education Review Office.

That is part of what we are attempting to do to give a more light-handed touch to our schools. We are talking about high trust. We have around the country boards of trustees that are elected by parents to manage schools. We think that the majority of those boards are doing a pretty good job, and we want to say to them that we back them, we support them, and we want to have a light-handed touch rather than look over their shoulders all the time, double guessing for them what they are doing in their schools.

Part of that is about trying to reduce bureaucracy for our schools, so last week I announced that we will be removing one of the National Administration Guidelines, commonly referred to as NAGS—and many principals told me that they felt the Government was indeed nagging them over this guideline—the one that turned schools into food police. Boards of trustees had to measure how many times their tuck shops served sausage rolls to kids. A board of trustees had to take account of, and report to the Education Review Office on, the number of times a school was serving certain classified foods. The issue is not about whether junk food is good or bad; that is not the issue. The issue is whose actual responsibility it is to make that decision. Is it the responsibility of the Government? No, that is Labour’s answer—the nanny State knows best. National believes that boards of trustees and schools should not be held accountable for those sorts of decisions. Parents are the ones who should be making those decisions, so we have put the responsibility back on to parents. We have lifted the responsibility from schools to make those decisions, but we have not lifted the responsibility from schools, under National Administration Guideline 5/2, that requires them still to promote healthy food. Of course we want healthy food and drink habits promoted by our schools, but we are not going to say to schools that they will be regulating what food is served in their canteens. The reality is that we know that kids eat pies, whether or not they are banned at school. What happens is that they jump over the fence—

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: Exactly! I talked to a dairy owner, who said he had doubled the number of pie warmers in his shop. He had bought extra pie warmers because of the increased business in pies that he was doing from his local school. The regulation did not have the effect of stopping kids from eating junk food. I can tell members that principals all over the country have reacted.

Finally in our 100 days of action, which has been a whirlwind in education, we have announced today the infrastructure we are bringing forward. There is $216.7 million worth of spending on education infrastructure. The Opposition tried to say that we were recycling. I ask members: where was the Labour Government’s investment in information and communications technology? Labour cancelled it; the National Government has put it in. There is $34 million in the next financial year for that. Where on earth was Labour’s $30 million for those Upper Hutt schools? Labour members could not even add up. They do not even know that “an additional” means plus. They cannot even add up that $44 million will be going into those Upper Hutt schools. As I say, there has been a whirlwind of action in education in the last 100 days.

Hon TARIANA TURIA (Co-Leader—Māori Party) : Tēnā koe, Mr Assistant Speaker. Tēnā tātou katoa. Within a fortnight of the ballot boxes closing, negotiations were being sealed for the formation of a new Government, strengthened with the tripartite confidence and supply agreements. For the first time in the history of this country, an agreement to govern was signed that recognised the importance of mana maintenance and enhancement for both parties. I acknowledge the courage of this Government to state upfront their commitment with the Māori Party to act in accordance with Te Tiriti o Waitangi. We are proud that our agreement establishes the opportunity to examine our constitutional arrangements and, significantly, to initiate as a priority a review of the Foreshore and Seabed Act. In this Parliament we must be brave enough to build on the foundation of change and challenge sweeping through our land. We must look anew at ways to achieve greater security and permanent peace, and to invest in the well-being of all.
