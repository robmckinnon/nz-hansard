Budget Debate

  • Debate resumed from 3 June on the Appropriation (2009/10 Estimates) Bill.

Hon JOHN CARTER (Minister of Civil Defence) : It is with great pleasure that I rise to speak on the Budget debate, because, first of all, I have the opportunity to congratulate the Minister of Finance, Bill English, on what has been recognised across the country—and, indeed, in countries around the world—as a fantastic job done in setting out this Budget. It was a job done under difficult circumstances. Nevertheless, he has come up absolute trumps. It is a great credit to his skill and also, I must say, to the Prime Minister’s, for the work they have done together, along with fellow Ministers, people of the caucus, and our coalition partners, in presenting this Budget to New Zealand at a time when the country knows we are going through global turmoil, from a financial perspective.

People in this country are saying “Thank goodness we have somebody like Bill English to lead us through this difficult period.” They are saying “Thank goodness we didn’t have to sit and listen to another Cullen Budget.” They are saying “Thank goodness the leaders of our country are in good control.” Right across the country there is a resounding first-class tick for the Budget presented by Bill English. He is to be sincerely congratulated on the presentation.

Let us look at some of the things this Budget does. The first thing it does is to cushion New Zealand from the sharpest edges of the global recession. That in itself is an amazing feat. Not only has it done that but also it has added to the opportunity and the future of this country.

Let us look at some of the other things in the Budget, not just the fact that it has cushioned New Zealand from the recession. There is also new spending in so many areas. At this time, we must respect the fact that Mr English had the ability to come forward and to make further funding available for some necessary areas. There is new core Crown spending of $2.9 billion that, combined with the recent tax cuts, will support this economy and get it back on its feet a lot quicker than we would have had otherwise, and a lot quicker than most other countries will be able to do.

Budget 2009 directly supports jobs through a $323.3 million home insulation and clean heating campaign. We have allocated $50 million for a national cycleway network. There will be a multibillion-dollar infrastructure boost. We all know that those things are sorely needed around this country. It is a shame that the previous Government took 9 years and did little, yet, in this Government’s first Budget, it has come up trumps and has been recognised for it.

One thing I will talk about, particularly from a Northland perspective, is the issue of home insulation and the clean heating campaign. Northland, as we know, is an area with serious housing problems. My friend and colleague Hone Harawira has been on about the issue of housing in Northland ever since, and before, he became a member of Parliament. He and I have worked to get this programme under way. The shame of it was that we had to wait until we got into Government to have these issues addressed, because the previous Government did little about it. My friend and colleague from Northland and I are proud of the fact that this Government will make this money available to the people of New Zealand. The impact it will have in Northland will be enormous. We estimate that about 180,000 households are eligible for grants of up to $1,800, regardless of income. On top of that, people who hold community service cards will also be eligible for grants of up to $3,000. That starts on 1 July 2009, a little under a month away.

It is also important to note that the impact of this scheme is twofold or threefold. Not only will it make the homes of the people in greatest need more comfortable but also it will cut down on heating costs. If we have properly insulated homes it means that the way in which people can heat those homes will be far more efficient. But there is more. Not only does it mean that there will be, therefore, savings for individuals but also it means that it will reduce the demand on health care. As we all know, a lot of sickness is generated by people who live in unhealthy homes. This Government decided to get involved in this initiative not just so that New Zealanders would have warmer homes, which is a great thing, but also so that people would be a lot healthier. I think particularly of our young children in Northland. They are great young kids who need the opportunity to have a good future, who need the opportunity to have an input into this country, and who need the opportunity to make a contribution for themselves. Those children will have a far better start in life as a consequence of this programme, and I am really proud to be part of that.

To those of us in Northland this initiative will mean that we will see a great step forward, right across the community. Right across the community Māori and non-Māori will benefit from this. The whole community will step up and we will be far better for that. It means lots of things. It means that we will get better productivity out of those who are working, because they will be less stressed about the fact that their children are unwell. It will mean that there will be more money to go around, so that people will be able to better provide for their children and their wider family. It means that in the community itself, people will be able to make a greater contribution to the community in which they live. So many positives have come out of this sort of initiative, and we are all proud, I am certain, that we are doing this sort of thing.

The second thing I will talk about is the matter of superannuation. I am pleased to say, and again I am proud, that we have a Government that has guaranteed that superannuation will continue. It has absolutely guaranteed that superannuation will continue. Firstly, the age of entitlement is at 65. We know that that age will continue to be 65. Secondly, we are keeping the level of superannuation at 66 percent of the average income for couples. It is important to recognise that for a couple the level is at 66 percent. Those are the sorts of figures that we are talking about. The issue around the Superannuation Fund is something that Opposition members like to talk about and frighten people with. But, of course, if they were honest enough to tell us about the facts of the fund, they would have to say that at its peak it was going to contribute only about 12 percent of the money—

Hon David Parker: 25!

Hon JOHN CARTER: There are different figures. But the point is that even if that member was right—and sadly he has never been before, so why would he be today—the fact is that the majority of the funding for superannuation is going to come from the consolidated fund.

One of the things that this Budget will do is ensure that we have a prosperous and buoyant economy so that we can afford the superannuation that so many of our people in New Zealand need. That is one of the reasons why this Budget is so positive: it gives the people in need the greatest opportunity, it gives them a good start-up, and, just as important, it ensures that an income from the State will be provided to those who need it and rely on it as they get older.

I have to say that I am proud of this Government. I am proud of Bill English and the Budget he has delivered. I am proud of the way in which John Key is giving leadership to this country. I have to say that I am proud to be part of this Government and to be a Minister in it, because this Government is taking steps and going forward, whereas in the past we have not seen that. This country needed leadership. It has it from John Key, it has it from Bill English, and it will continue to get it from this Government.

I say to Mr Carter that for Māori and non-Māori this Budget will go down in history, especially for those in their sunset years looking to retire, as a big loser for them and a great lie. I say to Mr Carter that this Budget is a typical Tory Budget. If we look at the last 40 years’ history of superannuation in this country—if we back to Robert Muldoon, and if we look at Hansard—we would see the same set of speeches. If we advance a few years to the early 1990s, we will see that Jim Bolger made the “no ifs, no buts, no maybes” speech. He said he would not raise the age of eligibility. There were similar speeches about the age of eligibility being raised from 60 to 65, and about how proud National members were of that pre-election commitment, which turned out to be one of the great lies of New Zealand history.

Let us move on and consider Bill English’s period, a few years later in the late 1990s, under the Shipley regime. The proud Budget speeches came out then, as well. And who was Minister of Finance? It was Mr English, and National cut superannuation three times. I think Mr Carter was in Government then. He got up and made another of those speeches about how proud he was of the leadership of National as it slashed national superannuation, as it refused to inflation-proof it, and as it lied to the people of New Zealand—as it did in Muldoon’s time, in Bolger’s time, and in English’s time, and as it is now doing in John Key’s time.

It was John Key who went to the country with a cast-iron guarantee, almost signed in his own blue blood, that National would not tamper with superannuation. What it has done is defer the pre-funding of the Cullen fund for 10 years, based on a lie. It is based on the premise that if we have debt we should not fund superannuation. Well, if that is the case, as John Pagani said on Q+A, that would rule out most New Zealanders if not all, or all those with a mortgage. Under that thesis they would never provide for superannuation ever, would they, because they have a 20, 25, or 30-year large debt called a mortgage. That is another great lie of the National Government, and 53 percent of New Zealanders, as I said, do not believe National. They do not believe that, because of the deferral, the age will be held, and they do not believe that their entitlements will not be cut.

I have looked at Mr Carter’s proud speeches about courage and leadership. Courage to Mr Carter is a self-congratulatory attitude about the fact that for the first time in 40 years National has held the age of eligibility and has not altered the current entitlements in superannuation, and, for the first time, has not slashed and burnt the current 66 percent entitlement. But what National members did do was go round to the back door and gut superannuation, through the deferral—

Do members know the greatest crime in this Budget with regard to superannuation? It is the fact that there is no longer the same certainty. For a decade prior to this crew getting into Government there was certainty about superannuation. Promises about entitlements were kept. It was, of course, a Labour Government that reversed the triple cut that Mr Carter and Mr Williamson put through in the 1990s, and it was a Labour Government that set up the Cullen fund, which acted as smartly as Kiwis do when they are managing their own domestic budgets.

What do Kiwis do if they know that there is a big expense coming up, like the need for a new washing machine in the house in another 6 months? Average Kiwis, smart people that they are, start to put money aside, because they know the expense is coming. Indeed, the Cullen fund operated on the same principle, given that we know the age of folk, when they will retire, and what the bill will be. The other great lie is that the fund will contribute only 12 percent. That is rubbish—it is more like 20 percent to 25 percent. Labour said that, given the age of entitlement, we knew when folk would retire and we would make provision for it. We said we would create certainty and take away the fear people have as they move towards retirement. The great sin in what the National Government has done is that there is again fear and uncertainty for every person in his or her 40s and 50s. All those people had 10 years of certainty about superannuation, because the Labour Government said: “Here are the rules, here’s the age, here’s how much you’re going to get, and we’ll guarantee it, because we’ve logically made provision for it.”, as Kiwis do every day in their domestic budgeting. But now fear and uncertainty have been unleashed. What do I mean by that? Well, commentators every day are saying that we need a debate about the age of eligibility. They are saying we need to look at what the Australians have done in terms of lessening it.

Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE: Well, some will be National Ministers, of course.

Some people will be sitting at home now and thinking “Hang on a minute! Is history going to repeat itself? In my 50s, as I move towards retirement, a decade away—which is simply too short a time to recalibrate—”

Paul Quinn: You will still be bludging off the taxpayer.

Hon CLAYTON COSGROVE: It is a pity about the fool on the second row opposite. If anybody knows about bludging, it is that member. The fool on the second row cares nothing about the elderly. Let him go and barrack at the Grey Power meeting in his patch—oh, he does not have one! Let him go to an Age Concern meeting and sit there, like a stuffed dummy, and barrack at the old folk. Let him go and sit in public meetings and barrack at those people in their 50s who do not have the time to recalibrate their arrangements because he, and his ilk, again, for the fourth time in 40 years, pulled the rug out. I know what the old boys and girls at the Rangiora Grey Power would do to him, and I know what the old boys and girls at the North Canterbury Age Concern would do to him: they would treat him with the disrespect that he deserves. All he can do is sit there and barrack away.

So the great sin in this debate is that Kiwis no longer have the certainty they once had. For 10 years Kiwis knew that if they did all the right things—pay the mortgage, look after the kids, and ensure that their debts and their affairs are in order—the Government would look after them. For 10 years Kiwis could say: “The Government has told me what I have to do, and the Government will look after me.” And we know that Kiwis are generous—we need only look at KiwiSaver. Kiwis stepped up to the plate and said they would make their contribution. They decided to contribute to their retirement. They said that as well as getting superannuation, they would up put their own money. They were generous, they were strong, and they did that.

Today, as the debate in the media and among political parties moves along, we see a headline in this morning’s New Zealand Herald—I do not mean the interesting individual on the front page. That headline says it all: “Super at age 65? Don’t bet on it”. Now that that debate has been ignited, and the consensus has been destroyed, folk in their 40s and 50s are rushing around trying to work out what they have to do to enjoy their retirement, for which they have worked all their working lives.

The great betrayal is what the National Government has done. Yet members opposite sit there and want to be congratulated because, for the first time in their history, they decided they would not move on the age of eligibility or cut the current levels of entitlement—yet. But we know what they will do at the next election. They will go to the country and say: “Look, it’s been 3 years. We haven’t put money into the Cullen fund, and we are billions of dollars behind.” They will tell New Zealanders: “You have a choice. We’ll up your taxes and put the money in, or we’ll have to look at changes to the age of entitlement.” But they will not say that before an election. I say to them, as they barrack away like the old bellows in the second row, that they should not take my word for it. They should look at the history of the National Party from Muldoon, to Bolger, to English, to Shipley, and to Key. They are condemned by their own history.

CHRIS TREMAIN: To the Budget. Since being fortunate in being elected to power in 2008, this Government has been about action. With the help of United Future, the ACT Party, and the Māori Party, and more recently with some support from the Greens, we have been about putting things into action and going forward. It has been absolutely fantastic. No more is that the case than with Budget 2009, which takes steps to ensure that New Zealanders are cushioned from the sharpest edges of the recession, but also, and more important, focuses on the future and on providing jobs as we go forward.

I will particularly focus on the investments we have made in this Budget that will create jobs. As I have sat through this Budget debate, I have heard challenges from the Opposition many, many times, asking how the investments will create jobs, what they will do, and where the jobs are in this. That is what we have heard from the other side of the House. So I will pick up on a couple of points in that regard. I want to focus on one of the unique ideas that came from the Job Summit, and that John Key is delivering on in this Budget. It is the cycleway. I recall yesterday that a lot of calls were made from the Opposition—

Sue Moroney: How many jobs?

CHRIS TREMAIN: And those calls are coming now. I will start with just a little analysis of the Otago Central Rail Trail, and a couple of quotes from some tourists who have enjoyed that cycleway. This quote is from Stephen Bigarelli from Australia, a tourist: “It is a really great trail and one I can see becoming more popular over time. I am sure I will be back to do the trail again. Keep up the good work, I wish we had trails in Australia that were as good.” This quote is from Mary Bennie from Australia: “We completed a return Trip … while holidaying at Wanaka in March. Fabulous weather, views, and experience. When next back we plan to do more. Your brochures are great and so too the web site. Congratulations”. These are examples of quotes from genuine tourists who are coming and utilising that trail, and that is providing a serious number of jobs. I have talked to Mr Parker, because I understand that the trail is in his former electorate, Otago. There are 16 tour operators. That is not just 16 jobs; that is literally tens if not hundreds of jobs. There are four bike and transport hire companies. Do members think that they employ people? Yes, they do. They employ a heap of people. There are 25 small towns and hamlets that provide accommodation, varying from basic to luxurious, and where cars, four-wheel drives, cycles, dirt bikes, and canoes can be rented. All of these are businesses employing people, so this cycleway is providing a product.

The $50 million cycleway package will extend that product throughout New Zealand. I say that, yes, it will start with iconic trails around the country. I know that in my own province of Hawke’s Bay a bid is being put in to have a trail going through it. This is exciting stuff. I visited Taupō and Rotorua, and I saw the trail that is starting to progress along the Waikato River. What an amazing opportunity that is! I say in response to challenges from the Opposition that the trail will provide jobs. It will provide jobs not only in terms of making the trail but also in terms of the hundreds of other businesses that will form around it. We will create not just one or two products; we will create a large number of products. When Australian tourists like the one I mentioned come here, they will look at the cycleway and see that they have a whole raft of opportunities to come and do. It will expand the product base, and expand the opportunity. I cannot understand why Opposition members shout asking how many jobs there will be, because hundreds if not thousands of jobs will be created over time from this cycleway. It is absolutely fantastic, and it is just one of the opportunities coming from this Budget.

There are hundreds of opportunities that I can speak about in this Budget. The home insulation package that we are getting together with the Greens on is a wonderful opportunity. That will create jobs. I cannot stand here and say that the package will create 50 or 100 jobs, or 223, but I can say that it will create jobs because it will provide businesses with contract work. You see, it is not Governments that create jobs, although I guess the previous one did through the bureaucracy; it is businesses that create jobs. This home insulation package will create hundreds of jobs, and it will retain them. Not only will we improve the environment and create healthier homes but we will create jobs, with $323 million in this particular part of the Budget alone. It is the second area creating jobs.

In the health area there will be a $3 billion investment in health services over the year. There will be 800 more health professionals, which will increase elective surgery capacity. That is 800 jobs in addition to all the jobs I have just talked about in respect of the home insulation package and the cycleway. We are already talking about thousands of jobs that this Budget will create. That is fantastic. The improvement of maternity services has gone down particularly well in my home electorate. The fact that fathers and mothers can stay in the maternity ward for a little longer than they were able to before will ensure that breastfeeding is going well, before they go home. How can anyone complain about that? That is absolutely wonderful.

Education will have $1.68 billion going into it from the Budget. I understand that that is one of the biggest spends in education going forward. Napier Intermediate School can now be sure that it can proceed with spending funds on its buildings, and that will provide jobs. A number of construction companies, sparkies, and plumbers in Napier will be able to get jobs there and retain people. Again, this is a wonderful opportunity.

The investment in standards for literacy and numeracy is also absolutely wonderful. I will spend my last 2 minutes speaking about the standards in literacy and numeracy because this gets to the heart of the concerns of a constituent who came into my office recently. She said that she wanted to understand more about numeracy and literacy, and that she had gone to one of the teachers at her school to ask for the progressive achievement test results. My constituent initially found it pretty tough to get the results out of the teacher. She finally got the progressive achievement test results back, which was good, and she found out that two of her children were not doing very flash in one particular area, based on the national levels. She asked why her children were not doing well, and what she could do to help with their improvement. We have to understand that this is not just the teachers’ responsibility; it is the parents’ responsibility as well. When parents understand that their child has an issue, they can ask what they can do to help. Do members know what that teacher’s answer was? It was that she should not worry about it, as it was a problem across the whole school.

National standards in literacy and numeracy will make sure that we understand where there are issues in literacy and numeracy across a whole school, and they will allow head teachers and teachers to be accountable in that regard so we can address those issues. That, for me, is critical. Parents are not saying that they do not want anything to do with it; they want to put their hands up.
