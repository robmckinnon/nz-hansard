Road User Charges Amendment Bill

Third Reading

Hon NATHAN GUY (Associate Minister of Transport) : I move, That the Road User Charges Amendment Bill be now read a third time. I thank members and officials for their work on this very important bill. The bill will achieve two things. Firstly, it will enable the exemption of light electric motor vehicles from the requirement to pay road-user charges, and, secondly, it will ensure adequate notice of road-user charge increases.

Over the years, motor vehicles have given New Zealanders opportunities to travel, and travel more freely now than at any other time in the past, and motor vehicles have brought huge improvements in economic growth and prosperity. However, part of the price of that freedom has been a contribution to global climate change, toxic emissions, and, indeed, noise. These problems are not due to the roads or our motorists; rather, they are due to the motive power of our vehicles. Recent advances in vehicle technology will see zero-emission or very low-emission vehicles in our market as early as 2010. These new vehicles will be powered, either wholly or partly, by electricity.

Electric vehicles are beneficial: while providing the choice of flexible and personalised transport, they are more efficient and cheaper to run than conventional vehicles. They will reduce our need to import fossil fuels and, when combined with smart metering technology, will increase road and electricity security against energy disruptions. Electric vehicles release little or no toxic emissions, and they have the potential to substantially reduce transport greenhouse gas emissions.

This bill seeks to allow exemptions for owners of light electric motor vehicles from paying road-user charges. The 4c per kilometre saving would provide an increase for those people who have bought light electric motor vehicles. The exemption is intended to operate until 2013, and is limited to light electric motor vehicles. In 2013 the exemption will cease, and owners of light electric motor vehicles will revert to paying the full cost they impose on the roading network—an important principle of our road-funding system.

The second important issue this bill addresses is the provision of notice for an increase to road-user charges. These charges are a significant cost to commercial transport operators. They are a tax, and no reasonable Government would increase a tax overnight without warning. The bill proposes a very reasonable period of 6 weeks’ notice before these charges are increased; the possibility of Government revenue loss through large-scale, pre-purchasing of road-user charges at cheaper rates is neatly averted. Road-user charge licences for heavy vehicles will expire 1 month after an increase comes into effect. Refunds of road-user charges for unused kilometres will be automatically provided at the time a new licence is purchased. No additional resources from Government or businesses will be required. The 400,000 owners of light diesel motor vehicles will not be subject to the expiry provision. The lower cost of road-user charge licenses for these vehicles substantially reduces the incentive for large-scale pre-purchasing.

Again, I thank the Transport and Industrial Relations Committee and officials for the very prompt work on this bill. The bill will help to encourage light electric motor vehicles on to our roads, which will lead to cleaner and quieter streets, lower fuel bills, and fewer greenhouse gases. The bill provides a transparent notification system for increases to road-user charges while safeguarding the Government’s revenue. This is a very good bill, and I commend it to the House.

The third reading of this bill is welcomed by Labour, although it is very timid legislation. Through the Committee stage we highlighted the concerns we had that the Minister and the Government have not used the chance of this legislation to be far bolder and show a little bit more vision with regard to the development of light electric motor vehicles in New Zealand. The bill, as the previous speaker said, has two parts: the first covers the exemption for light electric motor vehicles from road-user charges, and the second covers the period in which road-user charges may be purchased after the Government has announced an increase in those fees—and I will come to that point second. In dealing with the exemption for light electric motor vehicles, I say that the bill does not go far enough. It is disappointing that it is anticipating only 300 of these vehicles being on the road between now and 2013.

One of the issues that the Minister would not address in the Committee stage was why the opportunity was not taken to set a bolder target for those vehicles to be on our roads in order to send a very clear signal from Government that it was showing the leadership required to get a diversity of vehicles fuelled by different means through our motor vehicle fleet. I think that is a missed opportunity, and many questions were put to the Minister that he did not answer. For example, he was asked why a percentage figure of our fleet was not set rather than a number. When we consider how many cars each family has, we realise that 300 cars is a very, very small number indeed; 300 cars would pretty easily cover two or three streets in a small provincial town. But if the Minister had set a percentage figure, even one as low as 1 percent, that would have been a chance for the country to see how serious the Government was for light electric motor vehicles to be developed.

Light electric motor vehicles are such a great idea and reinforce all the work that the previous Government had done around the sustainability agenda to make sure that the single biggest area for most families in terms of fossil fuel emissions—that is, people’s participation in the transport sector—could be covered. For many families who think about how they can make a real difference to climate change, it will be in the area of transport that they are able to make the biggest difference to their emissions. Electric cars are obviously one way in which that could happen. We have to increase the proportion of these vehicles in our fleet on the roads. Combining both a greater quantity and the fact that New Zealand has such a strong supply of renewable energy would have brought together those great ideas. I hope that will happen with this bill, and that we will have 300 of these cars between now and 4 years’ time, but I fear that the wrong signals are being given, or that the signals are not being given strongly enough, with the passage of this bill here today. We know that cars that rely on oil engines or petrol and need to be filled up at the gas station are harmful sources of fuel because they are non-renewable sources, and emissions from cars are something that we are all concerned about. By flipping over to electric vehicles, we see that both of those things can be changed.

I want pick up on a point made by National member the Hon Tau Henare yesterday. He referred to a documentary called Who Killed the Electric Car? In politics it is very easy to have conspiracy theories put to members or to the community about how things can be affected, but I think that documentary clearly showed that not only Governments but corporations as well can send the wrong signals by the decisions they make. One thing that reminds me of that point is the style, shape, and design of the cars that were chosen in order to make them unattractive to consumers. A very deliberate decision was made to try to limit sales of that particular type of vehicle. I believe that the Government has a role to play in this area that can be quite influential, and I encourage it to take up that role in a stronger way.

We know that at the moment the availability of these cars in our market is currently very, very low. The purchase price is around double the cost of what a conventional vehicle would be, and that puts them out of the reach of many, many working families, particularly those who are facing harder times as unemployment rises at a seemingly unstoppable rate. Today it is at 6 percent of our labour market. Global manufacturers have not seen New Zealand as a priority to send cars down to, because they have not seen a market that is ready and willing to go. That is the tragedy of the bill.

The role of Government is to remove barriers. This bill partly does that, and that is why Labour is voting for it. We offer the Government congratulations on that part, but so often within this area of sustainability and climate change we are not seeing the Government being bold enough, which is what our country needs. New Zealand relies so heavily on a stable climate. Often the people who stand to lose the most from climate change are themselves the most reluctant to make a change, but I think it is the role of Parliament and the Government to look at that. I point the Government’s attention, as I did during the second reading, to Denmark. It has outlined a huge programme to try to kick-start a proper electric motor vehicle fleet, a US$135 million plan to try to get that going, and the New Zealand Government should be trying to focus on that as strongly as it can.

Finally, I turn to the area of road-user charges that the bill contains in its second part. The previous Government ordered a review of road-user charges because there are a number of legitimate questions in this area around fairness, where costs should fall, and the emerging types of vehicles that have environmental efficiency. We have just been discussing electric vehicles and they are, of course, covered by the bill, but there are also diesel-powered sedans and light diesel vehicles. They are becoming more and more popular on the consumer market. How those are all treated is a subject of some considerable debate and angst within the transport industry. The review was set up last year to try to come up with some potential options or solutions in that respect. I certainly welcomed that review.

The review came out of a period when the National Party had been very, very critical of an increase in those road-user charges. There was 18 years between increases in the fees, and when the increases were finally put forward we saw the then National transport spokesperson, Maurice Williamson, back when he was alive politically, lead a charge of National MPs around the country. They said that those were the most terrible, disgraceful increases they had ever seen, and that it was terrible that the Labour Government had raised road-user charges. Yet nowhere in this debate have National members got up and said that they believe road-user charges should be lowered to the level they were last year. I have been intrigued by that, because I thought that last year’s high principle would have been this year’s delivery. I thought that National members were so genuine in their outrage last year that they would have reduced those road-user charges. But—surprise, surprise—they have not done that. I would love to know from the next speaker why, all of a sudden, they believe that the road-user charges are now at an appropriate level, when last year those members joined protests all around the country because the charges were so outrageous. Dr JACKIE BLUE (National) : I am pleased to take a short call in the third reading of the Road User Charges Amendment Bill. There is a global movement towards the use of cleaner, greener cars, and that includes electric cars. The Government has a role to play in this change. It needs to take a lead, and through this bill the Government is making a start.

There is no doubt that road-user charges have been a barrier to the uptake of electric cars. An increase in the number of electric cars on our roads will reduce the greenhouse gases produced by the transport sector as well as the harmful emissions affecting air quality. It will also decrease reliance on imported fossil fuels and improve energy security.

This bill provides for 42 days’ notice of a road-user charge increase. That has been done to minimise the potential harm and revenue loss caused by the pre-purchasing of road-user charges by the heavy motor vehicle industry following notification of a road-user charge increase. The bill provides that heavy vehicle road-user charge licences will expire 1 month after an increase comes into effect. The changes in road-user charge rates that cannot be passed on to consumers in a timely manner impacts on their businesses. I commend this bill to the House.

Hon PETE HODGSON (Labour—Dunedin North) : I went over to the United States in 1992 on one of those “CIA” trips. I was there for a month and we were entitled to have a bit to do with putting our own agenda together. I found myself in Pasadena talking to a guy called Paul MacCready. Paul MacCready has since passed away, but before he did he managed a few things. He got a pterodactyl to fly. He took part in the Alice Springs to Darwin solar races each year, and continued to clean those up.

Paul MacCready was the guy who invented the Gossamer aircraft, the first aircraft that was human-powered and heavier than air, and he invented a thing called the Impact, which was an electric vehicle. I sat down with him for the day just to get a sense of where his head was at, and he taught me a really important lesson. It was that politicians should not pick technologies; politicians should back technologies.

In 1992 the Government of California had said—and as my colleague Darren Hughes said earlier—that there was a case for the Government to set a percentage target, so it did. It said that all vehicles to be sold from the year 2000, or thereabouts, in Los Angeles would be zero-emission vehicles. But Mr MacCready said that the politicians had made a mistake because they had picked a technology instead of backing it. They should have watched what technology was coming forward. It was not zero-emission vehicles back in 1992; it was hybrids. He could see them coming out of Japan, and he could see that that would be the next wave, to be followed maybe by plug-in hybrids, and then maybe by electric vehicles.

Now, 17 years later, Mr MacCready has been proved right, and the time for a Government to back this new technology—zero-emission vehicles; electric cars—has come. So Labour members support this legislation, but in the same breath we say that it is insufficient. We are not suggesting great subsidies, or anything of the sort, but we wonder whether taking road-user charges off zero-emission vehicles will make much of a difference when one considers not only the capital cost but the entire lack of infrastructure.

No planning is going on around the advent of electric vehicles into this country. There is no planning law about what type or types of recharging we might have—induction, plug-in, or whatever. There is no standard set for safety around those forms of recharging. We do not have any infrastructure in place for the advent of these vehicles, so the early movers will have to make it up as they go along. If we are to have 350, or something, electric vehicles in this country by a certain period—

Sue Moroney: 300 in the next 5 years.

Hon PETE HODGSON: Well, by the law of averages that will mean 10 in my home town of Dunedin, and those 10 electric vehicle users will not have a place to plug in, in the central city unless there is planning for that. The Government needs to do more in the electric vehicle space than it has done. I commend what the Government has done. It is a good step forward, but it is not a sufficient step forward by any means.

I wonder whether the Government would ever consider getting together a group of people who have an interest in this matter—potential users, potential services—and asking them when we should start training mechanics to look after these vehicles when they need servicing. We do not have that now, so will we be doing DIY maintenance on electric vehicles? Will people be phoning up some trained electrician who is very interested in electric cars?

The point is we will have to have that trained electrician, or have that knowledge, before people buy the vehicles. That is why I think the Government has not done enough. We need a skills infrastructure, we need a physical infrastructure, and we need a plan. But we do not have any of those; we simply have the removal of road-user charges.

I refer now to the other matter in this legislation. I well remember a bunch of truck drivers rolling into the streets of Dunedin, and elsewhere around the country, late last year. They were outraged by the fact that the then Minister of Transport had taken steps so they could not rort an increase in charges, as if such Government action was unusual. Has no one ever been in this House when, under urgency, tax changes have gone through in the dead of night? That is the precise action of a Government when it is looking to put up charges. It does so suddenly so that people have certainty about what they are going to pay—more or, in some cases, less—the following day. That is normal, but when the Minister of Transport did so it was an outrage. So a bunch of truck drivers came into town, blocked up the roads, and basically said: “We know that we don’t pay enough but we don’t want to pay any more.”

I acknowledge that road-user charges always bring debate around the margins, that road-user charges are never set perfectly, and that there is always room for debate about this or that aspect of them. I know that; I have been a Minister of Transport myself for a time. It is an ongoing thing, but when the Government decided that road-user charges needed to go up—and that decision was backed by quite careful analysis—and when the truck drivers of the nation started running into town, one did wonder whether a former National Party member for New Plymouth, who currently runs the Road Transport Forum, the trucking forum, was behind some political motivation. Hearing some of the chatter across the House today confirms for me that that was a political act designed to do damage to a Government rather than to protest an issue.

I think that this Government’s solution of a limited ability to pre-buy is a really good one, and I congratulate the Government on it. But let us not suggest that there is anything new or unreasonable about making a sudden decision to increase a charge or a tax. That is normal in democracies, and it is normal in this democracy. So I do not want to hear any of that from the National Government. With those remarks I welcome this legislation and I hope it makes its untrammelled progress through the House.

Hon TAU HENARE: Ten people blogged seven times, and they changed their names. One of them was called Flower. Oh, I am having fun. Seven people commented 10 times, they changed their names, and one of them is called Flower. What do we expect from a person with the name of Flower?

This bill is all about the future. It is all about making a statement about electricity and about the new technology we can look forward to. But members opposite want us to go backwards and put freight on trains that do not even exist.

Hon Maurice Williamson: Back to the future.

Hon TAU HENARE: Those members should tell that to the people of the north and the far north; we do not have any rails, let alone trains. So what that member is talking about is a load of old hogwash. It is about going back to the future for the Labour Party. But National is concerned with the future and with enhancing new technology.

SUE KEDGLEY (Green) : The Green Party is pleased to join in the consensus in the House in supporting the Road User Charges Amendment Bill, which will waive road-user charges for electric cars for 4 years. Why would anyone not support electric cars? After all, they will make our fleet more efficient, they do not have emissions, they will reduce our dependence on oil, and they will be a tiny step towards New Zealand becoming energy independent, and so forth. Of course we support electric cars, but we do have, as others have mentioned, some real questions about the underlying strategy behind the rather modest bill.

Others have asked why the bill has been rushed through Parliament and wondered what the great rush is. I would like to speculate. There are two reasons for it. The first one, and the previous speaker hinted at it, is that National members can go around, as David Bennett has done, claiming that National is now somehow taking leadership in clean, green transport. He can rush around saying “Look what we’re doing; we are giving incentives for using the electric car.” It is a great spin.

The second reason is that it justifies, in National members’ minds, business as usual when it comes to transport, which means building more and more roads for more and more cars. It does not matter, because in this wonderful vision that David Bennett spoke about, electric cars will be all over New Zealand. So everything will be fine! The presence of the cars will justify what National has just done. National has slashed $420 million out of the fund for alternative public transport, and it is pouring all that money into building new roads. This bill is like a fig leaf; somehow the Government can justify this incredibly short-sighted policy because we will have electric cars. It also enables David Bennett and the previous speaker, Tau Henare, to go on and on about it. Tau Henare said: “Look at it. We are embracing the future. We are embracing new technology.” That is great stuff!

There is one slight flaw: it is estimated that there will be only 30 electric cars by 2010, that within about 3 years, there will be about 300 electric cars, and, maybe, by 2020 there will be 30,000 electric cars on the road. That hardly is a panacea. The problem is that electric cars are expensive. They cost two to three times as much as an ordinary car. How will New Zealanders fork out $40,000 to $80,000 for an electric car? Yes, electric cars are great; we strongly support them. But let us not use them to provide a justification, a panacea, for increasing our focus on roads, and for running down public transport like rail, as previous speakers have mentioned.

The bill also deals with the ability to alter the rates of road-user charges, particularly referring to trucks. Trucks do enormous damage on our roads. A 40-tonne truck can easily cause as much damage to a typical road as 60,000 one-tonne cars. As a result of this, overweight trucks chronically underpay their fair share of road-user fees for the repair of roads and bridges. By damaging roads, large trucks further degrade highway safety. My estimate related to damage done by a 40-tonne truck. Imagine how much damage will be done by 53-tonne trucks, which National is about to unleash on our roads. These juggernauts are 72 feet long, which is 6½ feet longer. We can be quite certain they will destroy our roads. If 40-tonne trucks can cause as much damage as 60,000 cars, God knows what damage will be done by 53-tonne trucks! I can be absolutely certain that trucks will not pay their fair share of road-user charges, which are used to repair the damage they will do on our roads.

One thing that road-user charges do not take into account is the cost of all the accidents that these juggernaut trucks cause on our roads. Trucks make up only 4 percent of our vehicle fleet, yet cause 18 percent of the road toll. Despite knowing this, we will allow trucks that are much bigger and considerably longer on our roads. We know that that will inevitably cause more accidents on our roads. How do we know that? Am I just scaremongering? No, because the truth is that the longer the truck—6½ feet longer at 72 feet long—the longer it will take for people to pass it. It will be more difficult for these juggernaut trucks to go round the corners of our windy, hilly roads in New Zealand without passing over the centre line, as they do on many, many State highways in New Zealand. Huge 53-tonne trucks will take longer to brake. If one has the misfortune to be involved in an accident with a 53-tonne truck, one will come off even worse than if one was involved in an accident with a 40-tonne truck.

New Zealanders are completely opposed to the juggernaut trucks. The previous speaker from Labour, Sue Moroney, blamed National for them, but it must be said that Labour initiated the pilot study that is now being used as an excuse to allow these juggernaut trucks on our roads and to give them the green light. The stupidity of this proposal lies in the fact that our roads are far too narrow, far too windy, and far too hilly. They are just completely ill-suited for huge juggernaut trucks; that is why so many New Zealanders are alarmed. They are only just waking up to the fact that this is about to happen, and they are horrified. They know that these trucks will not only cause destruction to roads and bridges but also will contribute to more accidents. People are worried. Unfortunately, this will be one of those things that people wake up to only when it is a fait accompli. They will ask why on earth our Government allowed these huge juggernaut trucks on New Zealand roads that are completely ill-suited to them.

I am glad to see that the Minister of Transport, Steven Joyce, is here in the Chamber. He is the one who will make the decision as to whether these juggernaut trucks will be permitted on our roads. I suspect that he has already made the decision, and that the so-called consultation process was like most consultation processes—just a farce. The Green Party put in a submission. We wasted our time. We pointed out the sorts of things that I have pointed out in my speech. But we felt that making the submission was probably to no avail, and that the Minister, working hand in glove with the trucking industry, had probably already committed to this legislation. He had probably committed to it before the election, and it is just being rolled out now.

Having said that, there is one positive thing in this bill, which is why we are supporting it: it will give some incentive for electric cars. That is a good thing, but let us not pretend that having 30 electric cars on our roads next year and 300 more in a few years’ time will somehow justify pouring more and more money into roads. This will undermine rail. The other thing that I did not mention about those big juggernaut trucks is that heavy freight should be moved by rail. The Government owns the rail network. I ask why it is giving incentives so that these huge trucks will be carrying our heavy freight instead of the rail network. Why is the Government undermining the viability of the rail network in New Zealand?

CAROL BEAUMONT (Labour) : Tēnā koe, Mr Deputy Speaker. I rise again to speak in favour of the Road User Charges Amendment Bill. The bill proposes to amend the Road User Charges Act 1977 to achieve two main outcomes. The first is to enable the exemption of light electric motor vehicles from the requirement to pay road-user charges, and the second is to provide 42 days’ notice of increases to road-user charges. The road-user charge licences for heavy motor vehicles will expire 1 month after an increase comes into force.

I intend to talk about both of those matters in a little more detail, and perhaps recap some of the issues I raised in my previous contributions on the bill. But before I do that, I note, in relation to comments made earlier, that my colleague Sue Moroney was talking about the level of interest in the rail network between Hamilton and Auckland. Some comments were made by Tau Henare about that matter. I will just confirm that 71 people so far have responded to Sue Moroney’s blog on that issue.

First of all, I will explain the changes in the bill in perhaps a little more detail. In relation to light electric motor vehicles, certainly nobody could deny that it is a good idea to exempt those vehicles from road-user charges. We believe that the exemption reinforces the work Labour did in promoting electric vehicles. Electric vehicles will be the beginning of a new era of energy independence and low-emission transport. Increasing the proportion of electric vehicles in the fleet will decrease reliance on imported fossil fuels and improve energy security.

As a country we are very lucky that we have a competitive advantage in renewable electricity generation, which has the potential to reduce the amount of greenhouse gases produced by the transport sector. I intend to come back to that matter later. Light electric motor vehicles are also cheaper to operate per kilometre than conventional motor vehicles. However, as others have commented, there are some problems with them. First of all, availability is currently low. Secondly, their purchase price is at least double that of a conventional vehicle. Thirdly, manufacturers do not rate New Zealand as a priority market. Therefore, take-up rates are understandably low.

The Government’s responsibility in relation to this matter is clearly to remove barriers to the take-up of electric motor vehicles. Removal of the road-user charges is a positive step, but we all have to recognise that it is a minor one in the big picture. The Government needs to be much more adventurous in this area. It needs to look at some of the examples from overseas. For example, Denmark is currently installing a network of recharging stations hooked up to wind turbines. That would be a useful thing to look at.

We also had a discussion around how long the scheme would be extended for, in terms of the exemption from road-user charges, because clearly at the moment the exemption provides financial assistance to the owners of those cars. But given their price, for the reasons I outlined earlier the technology will be available only to those with significant financial resources. Poor people will miss out, and we certainly do not want to be in the situation where the poor are subsidising the rich.

The review on road-user charges was actually instigated by Labour and the previous Minister of Transport, Annette King, because there had not been a review in about 18 years. As I have noted previously, during that period the ratio of trucks to cars changed enormously, as did the weights being carried by those trucks. The upshot was an inequality between trucking firms and private motorists over who was paying for road maintenance. Operators of heavy trucks, which were causing substantial damage, were not paying their fair share.

We have all noted during the course of the debate on this bill that Labour faced a revolt by truck drivers when we put up road-user charges with no notice. But there was a reason no notice was given. On the previous occasion when notice was given, $17 million worth of pre-purchasing took place. That $17 million was $17 million of revenue lost to the Crown. So, as a sensible Government would do, we set up a process to work with the sector to put in place a regime that was fair to the Crown but also fair to the trucking industry.

Ultimately, the bill and the provisions around increasing road-user charges are the result. Effectively, it is a balanced outcome, whereby there will be 42 days’ notice of an increase. Truck drivers will be able to use road-user charges bought in advance for up to 1 month after any increase. Any pre-purchased road-user charges left over at that point will be able to be refunded. It is a good solution. As I said, we support the bill. We also support the very sensible Supplementary Order Paper 33, which clarified one of the points in the bill.

I will also comment on the process of passing the bill, because one of the things we have found somewhat perplexing is why there has been a need to rush it through. The report-back period was just under a month, with a 2-week adjournment included in that 1 month.

Hon Steven Joyce: Were you on holiday?

CAROL BEAUMONT: No, we were not on holiday; I do not know about Mr Joyce. The Opposition has been given no explanation or justification from that Minister as to why the bill has had to be dealt with so quickly. The submissions on the bill were received, and we did not get the opportunity to hear them orally. That is a huge mistake. One of the real strengths of our Parliament is our select committee process. It works very well. We have enormous numbers of people making submissions, taking the time to share their ideas and thoughts, and giving us feedback on the bills before the House. They add value to the legislation we pass. I do not know why on earth we could not hear from the submitters.

We are not seeing the proper scrutiny of legislation that one would expect to see from our Parliament. The Government is constantly undermining the process, reporting back bills faster than they need to be reported back, and pushing through under urgency legislation on many important issues. The process question is one that is very troubling. Why the haste? As a new member, I have absolute confidence in the select committee process and in the people of New Zealand having their opportunity to give us their feedback. The Government should seriously think about its behaviour in that regard.

I will follow up some points made by Sue Kedgley about trucks, because the significant part of the bill is in relation to road-user charges that apply to trucks. As Ms Kedgley has quite rightly pointed out, the Government is currently planning to allow larger trucks on the roads. We believe that there will be many serious consequences of that decision. It has the potential to make our roads less safe, and it will undoubtedly be bad for the environment. We agree with the Greens that rail and coastal shipping are better methods of moving heavy goods. Submissions have been heard on the matter, and I guess we have to wait to see the outcome as to whether the concerns that are being raised will be taken account of.

The heavy trucks potentially will not be paying their fair share of the cost. There are also safety and environmental issues. The possibility of road-user charges being replaced with a flat diesel tax that could exacerbate the already uneven playing field between roads, rail, and coastal is one that has been flagged up.

One thing I have not yet had a chance to talk about in the debate on the bill is coastal shipping, and I thought members would be quite interested in that. As well as talking about the problems with heavy trucks, let us look at one of the alternatives. We have had some discussion around rail, but let us look at coastal shipping. The previous Labour Government was developing coastal shipping because, within a relatively short time frame, our roads will be unable to cope with projected increases in total freight volume. An integration of roads with an upgraded rail and coastal shipping network is the only possible solution as we look to the future. One of the problems with the current Government is that it does not look forward and plan. Planning seems to be a problem for the Government.

We developed a programme called Sea Change, and $36 million was earmarked to help develop coastal shipping over the next 4 years. That programme has been cancelled by the National Government.

Grant Robertson: Shame!

CAROL BEAUMONT: It is an absolute shame. The target was for at least 30 percent of inter-regional freight to be carried by sea by 2040, up from the current 15 percent share. It is worth noting that in Japan, which has a very similar geography to New Zealand, domestic sea freight has more than twice the market share of sea freight here.

DAVID BENNETT (National—Hamilton East) : I take just a short call on the Road User Charges Amendment Bill. We have debated it at length over a number of days, and we have gone through all the big issues that could be debated. It is revolutionary legislation in the sense that we are supporting electric vehicles and showing leadership on environmental and transport issues.

Chris Hipkins: Revolutionary in that they’re supporting the environment for a change.

DAVID BENNETT: Some members of other parties have not been able to stomach that, but they do not realise the importance of showing that leadership on those issues. The National Government is quite happy to do that, and our Minister of Transport is quite happy to provide that direction. This bill will be for the benefit of New Zealanders going forward. It is well overdue, it is in our best interests, and we commend it to the House.
