Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill — First Reading

[Volume:653;Page:2810]

Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill

First Reading

Hon PETER DUNNE (Minister of Revenue) : I move, That the Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill be now read a first time. At the appropriate time I intend to move that the bill be referred to the Education and Science Committee, that the committee report finally to the House on or before 30 July 2009, and the committee have the authority to meet at any time while the House is sitting, except during oral questions, during any evening on a day on which there has been a sitting of the House, and on a Friday in a week in which there has been a sitting of the House, despite Standing Orders 187 and 190(1)(b) and (c).

The student loan scheme was introduced in 1992 to make it easier for people to gain access to tertiary education. It allows tertiary students to borrow money to pay their fees, and provides help with living costs for some of those students. Repayments for people who live in New Zealand are based on an individual borrower’s income after graduation. Although there have been some major changes since the scheme’s introduction, the fundamentals of the scheme have remained largely the same. It would be fair to acknowledge, however, the increasing concern across the political spectrum and the wider community about the mounting level of student debt and whether it will ever be repaid. That concern led the previous Government to introduce the policy of interest-free student loan repayments for borrowers resident in New Zealand, and led the National Party at the last election to commit to a repayment bonus scheme to incentivise early repayment.

The current student loan scheme is administered by a number of agencies, including the Ministry of Education and the Ministry of Social Development. The Inland Revenue Department is responsible for the collection of student loan repayments. Most repayments are made through the PAYE system by means of salary deductions.

Since the scheme began, nearly 800,000 people have taken out a student loan. Today, with over half a million borrowers and a nominal value of about $9.6 billion, the student loan scheme represents a significant Crown asset—one that has to be managed in an efficient and cost-effective manner.

One area of concern is that since the introduction of interest-free student loans in 2006, the number of voluntary repayments has been decreasing, with borrowers tending to take longer to repay their loans in full. Not surprisingly, this is because paying interest on the loan balance has been a big incentive to repay the loan as early as possible. Faster repayment of loans is of benefit not only to the individual borrower for obvious reasons, but also to the Crown, because the longer borrowers take to repay their loan, the more expensive that borrowing becomes to the Crown.

This bill introduces an incentive for faster repayment in the form of a 10 percent bonus for borrowers who voluntarily make extra repayments that total $500 or more in a tax year. Voluntary payments totalling $5,000, for example, would attract a bonus of $500. All borrowers, including those overseas, will be eligible for the bonus, provided they meet all student loan obligations, and have a loan balance with the Inland Revenue Department of $550 or more at the beginning of the tax year in which they make the extra payments. The bonus will be credited to their account at the end of the tax year. For those people who want to pay off their loan in full using a one-off payment, the maximum bonus payable will be one-eleventh of that balance. For example, if that balance is $1,100, the borrower will make an extra payment of $1,000, and the maximum amount of the bonus will be $100.

Once enacted, this bonus scheme will take effect from 1 April this year. The bonuses will be credited to borrowers’ loan accounts after 1 April 2010. This means that crediting will be retrospective for those who repay their loans in full before enactment and before the necessary systems changes have been made. Because there is no time limit on entitlement to the bonus, borrowers who may be in a better position to make extra payments later in their working lives will be able to take advantage of the incentive when it best suits their financial position.

I believe that the measures contained in this bill will provide a useful and welcome incentive to people who want to pay off their student loans early. I therefore commend the Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill to the House.

Hon MARYAN STREET (Labour) : It is a pleasure to speak on the Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill, because there are a number of points in the bill, and in the Minister’s introductory speech, that I think need challenging. I thought the Minister would have understood the difficulties this bill presents. At first glance the bill looks like the usual efforts of National to support the well-off. If someone with a student loan pays $500 extra off his or her loan, above the minimum requirement in any tax year, that student gets his or her loan total abated by 10 percent of the extra money that was paid off—for example, $50. The extra payments that qualify must be $500 and above.

The Government says that its purpose is to encourage student loan borrowers to make extra repayments to repay their loans faster. It looks like a typical National Government bill, which advantages those who can pay extra, and disadvantages those who cannot. But it is, on closer examination, clearly a con. Why would anyone go for a 10 percent discount when the loan is attracting no interest? Any students from wealthy backgrounds who, in practice, would be the only ones with the resources to increase repayments, would also be the ones most likely to have access to financial advice. This advice would point out to the students, very quickly, that they are better-off investing any extra money elsewhere, and keeping their interest-free loan in its current state. So why would the Government introduce such a measure when it will not work for the less well-off, which is to be expected from National, but it also does not work for the better-off, which one might have expected from National?

There are two possible answers to that question. The first is that the National Government did not realise what it was doing, in which case it was stupid. The second answer is that the Government knew exactly what it was doing but wanted to deflect attention away from a real agenda in the tertiary education portfolio, therefore making the Government duplicitous. In that case, the Government would be doing what it has done before, and that is telling only half of the story.

Just as an example from another portfolio, we might recall announcements about improvements in health reforms in the National Party manifesto last year, but the big missing feature, which Tony Ryall at that time blurted out, was that National’s intention was to lift the cap on general practitioners’ fees. He was very quickly slapped back into line for releasing something that National did not intend to release at that time. I use that as an example because I suspect that that is exactly what is happening in this case.

It is time to remind people that National when last in office did the same thing to students. Back in 1990 it promised to scrap Labour’s student loan policy in its entirety, but it failed to announce that it would be replacing it with a harsher one. What could the hidden agenda be? What could the real agenda for the tertiary education sector be? One possibility is that National might reintroduce interest on student loans, because this bill makes sense only if interest is being charged on student loans.

Another thing that National might do is lift the cap on student fees. It could consider doing that. Another thing it might consider is to stop the indexing of features of the student loan system, such as the thresholds for loan repayments, which are currently linked to the consumer price index and the movement in that. The Government could choose to take away the consumer price indexation and impose a freeze, which, as everybody knows, with any increase in the CPI amounts to a cut. National consistently opposed every single measure the Labour Government took over the last 9 years to improve the affordability of tertiary education. It now, quite frankly, beggars belief that it would offer a discount for early payment of an interest-free loan without also having a secret agenda of reintroducing interest, because that is the only circumstance in which such a bonus would make sense.

Labour’s history in this area is to make the student loan scheme and, more generally, the funding of tertiary education, more sustainable, more reliable, more stable, and more predictable. This bill, as it stands, could be more correctly entitled the “Saving Incentives Bill”, because it makes no financial sense to pay off more than is required of a loan that is generating no interest. Borrowers would be better off putting their extra dollars, if they have them, into an interest-bearing savings account for a deposit on a first home. When we take into account years of saving, interest on that, and compound interest on that, we see that it makes more sense for those who leave university or a tertiary education programme and go into the workforce, to put any extra money they have into an interest-bearing savings account for any other purpose than paying off an interest-free student loan. If they already have a house, then they could pay more off their mortgage or anything else, but the way this bill is structured makes no financial sense. If the Government has not been stupid about this bill, the only alternative is to conclude that it has, in fact, been duplicitous. Labour opposes this bill.

I cannot believe my ears when I sit in this House and listen to a Labour Opposition spokesperson exhorting the students of New Zealand not to pay off their student loans. The Labour Party talks about being the party of students, but its members cannot get past the politics of voting for something that will help students pay off their debts sooner. I am looking forward to going on to university campuses and telling students that Labour members stood in this House and would not support a bill that was designed to help students pay off their loans earlier. My email has run hot since this policy was announced, with students saying that the scheme is fantastic, and asking when it starts and when can they pay off their loans and get their 10 percent bonus.

The student loan scheme helps students to pursue their education and life goals by removing some of the financial barriers to tertiary study. If those Labour members do not understand that students are concerned about their ability to pay off their loans, then those members are out of touch. I cannot believe that after an election campaign in which that party constantly talked about duplicitous, hidden agendas—and the public profoundly rejected all that and voted in the National Government—we have here a little bill that does an awful lot for students to help them pay off their loans, but Labour is not backing it.

Access to high-quality tertiary education enriches people’s lives and increases their employment prospects. It strengthens our economy by helping to build New Zealand’s skills base, and that is all the more important at a time of economic recession. It is important that we keep our graduates here in New Zealand and contributing to our country, and this Government is determined to put measures into place that can help them do that and pay off their loans. We are keeping our promise to students to help them retain those interest-free loans, and this policy is a way of encouraging them to pay off their loans faster and enable them to participate and contribute to New Zealand’s society and economy.

This Government is concerned, especially at this time of economic recession, that the interest-free policy has removed a strong incentive for borrowers to make extra repayments on their student loans. But the Labour Opposition, of course, supports that—“Don’t pay it back.”, they have said; “Why would you pay it back?”. Since the introduction of interest-free student loans, the proportion of repayments that the Inland Revenue Department has received from borrowers has declined. Students are listening to the Labour Party and they are not paying off those student loans. But we are keen to see those people pay off their loans as early as possible so that they then have more freedom in their lives to make those major life decisions that the previous speaker, Maryan Street, was talking about, such as buying a house.

The other thing the Labour Party does not seem to be in the least bit concerned about is that the cost of the student loan scheme to the Government has increased markedly in recent years as a result of non-repayments. The longer borrowers take to repay their loans, the more expensive the borrowing is to the Crown. At the very time when we are concerned about the borrowing of the Crown, that party is voting against a bill for political and ideological reasons rather than supporting early repayment methods for student loans. The current cost of every dollar of future lending is 39.15c; in 2002 the cost of every dollar lent was 23c. So this repayments bonus aims to address those issues by providing an incentive for faster repayment, shortening repayment times, and reducing the costs to the Crown. Students all over the country are delighted, and have sent in messages expressing that and asking when they can start repayments.

The Minister of Revenue, in introducing the bill, laid out the design parameters for it. National members believe that we have more than kept our promise since we went on the campaign. This bonus scheme is more widely available now than when it was promised prior to the election, so we have over-delivered on that promise that we made to students.

The scheme is now available to overseas-based borrowers as well as to domestic borrowers. During the election campaign we talked about only domestic borrowers, but overseas borrowers in fact take even longer to repay their student loans and are more likely to have overdue repayments than are domestic borrowers. So this bonus will encourage faster repayments and greater compliance from people overseas who have loans. Large student loan balances are often perceived by expats as a reason to delay coming home to New Zealand, or to refuse to come home at all. So this policy will give them the opportunity to pay off that debt quicker and, hopefully, to remove that barrier.

In addition to that, when we campaigned we talked about making a one-off payment of $500 on top of the normal repayments, but now we have decided that we will spread that $500 over the year; on a weekly basis that is $10 a week. We will build that $500 extra and qualify borrowers for the basis—

Hon Trevor Mallard: You’ll be ripped off more easily.

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: That is less than a packet of cigarettes. I am not sure how much beer that will buy—I doubt that it will buy very much—but $10 a week is not a lot to save, and that brings in a whole group of other students who might have found it difficult to make a lump-sum payment of $500 but who can now spread that over the course of a tax year. This is an example of the Government listening—listening—to the people out there in the community who wrote to us expressing concerns about having to find that lump sum of $500, and we have found a way to expand the range of borrowers who can take advantage of it.

I turn to the third area where we have extended this policy. During the election campaign we talked about an eligibility time for the bonus of 10 years. We have decided that eligibility will not be limited to the 10 years, as we originally stated, so that means that the bonus will be available to those who are able to make those extra repayments later in their working lives, for one reason or another. It is estimated that the measures in this bill will encourage about 14,700 more borrowers to make those additional repayments of $500 or more on their loan.

Hon Trevor Mallard: How many? Sorry, can you repeat the figure? We couldn’t quite hear it.

Hon ANNE TOLLEY: Certainly, I am happy to repeat that. We believe that this bill will encourage about 14,700 more borrowers to make additional repayments of $500 or more on their student loans. We estimate that this measure will reduce repayment times of those who take up the bonus by 1.5 years, and we believe that it will decrease the upfront cost of new lending to 39.4c. How could anyone vote against that? The only reasons people could vote against a bill like that, which proposes that scheme for the benefit of student loan borrowers, and for the taxpayer in New Zealand, would be ideological reasons. It would be because they just could not bring themselves to vote for something that National is proposing.

In summary, I tell the House that the measures in this bill aim to encourage more people to make extra student loan repayments over and above their compulsory obligation. Shorter repayment times will mean that New Zealanders will be debt-free earlier, and the costs of the student loan scheme to the Government will be reduced.

I thank the Minister of Revenue for his nod on that particular matter.

This is a sneaky little bill. It is designed to rip off people who have been students, and I think that we do not have 14,700 graduates of tertiary institutions who are stupid enough to be ripped off in this way in New Zealand this year, next year, or in any other year.

If we are to believe the analysis of the New Zealand Union of Students’ Associations, it tells us that the average student debt is now something around $28,800. For the Flavell family that equates to a burden of about $86,500, so we are not talking small money for some families. Except for just one of my children, after 7 years of tertiary education their loan has already topped $60,000, so obviously we do not fit the average profile. As if that is not bad enough, the data tells us that the burden is on an upward roll. The average student debt has risen by 54 percent since 2004, and I understand that it is now a staggering 147 percent higher than in 1998. There is a possibility that these costs will cripple the nation if they continue. They are huge issues of economic and social importance that are critical to an investment in the leaders of tomorrow.

This Student Loan Scheme (Repayment Bonus) Amendment Bill amends the Student Loan Scheme Act to create incentives for student-loan borrowers to pay loans back. It is, in that respect, a response to the policy problem of the high debts being incurred by our student population. Over 60 percent of all students face high debt—a threshold that has remained relatively constant since 2004.

What do we mean by high debt? Some 26 percent of students owe more than $15,000 in debt before they even start looking in the job market. To some of us $15,000 may not sound much, but for students like my children it is a hell of a lot on top of things like food, transport, accommodation, living expenses, and general bills, which are all expenditure items that in their own right have increased dramatically in the last 5 years. In just one area—the price of food—we might get a glance at the impact of rising student debt. According to the food price index, the price of food rose by 22 percent in the 5 years from March 2004 to March 2009. No one in this House would deny that the cost of petrol, for example, has jumped a massive 39 percent in the same period, and that has obviously taken a huge toll.

So, with all due respect, this bill is very much a small drop in the bucket when one looks at the big picture, and we really must look at the big picture. For the average student there will be very little capacity to make additional repayments and thus receive any kind of bonus under the scheme.

The crucial information missing from this bill is, as it stands, what it would take for the average student to make additional loan repayments. What are the real costs of income tax, GST, mandatory student-loan repayments, credit card debt, KiwiSaver, rent, increased food and petrol costs, and any savings to be made for a first-home purchase or maybe planning for children? For the average student there will be very little capacity to make additional payments and thus receive any kind of bonus under this scheme.

I will make a point about the relationship of student-loan repayments to any other additional loan repayments. The issue of student debt cannot be seen as one of only student-loan repayments. The average debt of students has now forced the student population to look elsewhere for borrowings. We see a large increase in other loans, including loans from parents and personal bank loans. The proportion of students with bank overdrafts has risen by 19 percent in the last 5 years, and there has been a 32 percent increase in the amount owed on credit cards. When we consider this sort of information it is as if we can feel our pockets emptying pretty smartly, especially when we consider our own children. I come back to whether this bill is the answer.

The trouble with the bonus scheme concept is that it targets only income earners who are graduates earning at the sufficient level. The Union of Students’ Associations suggested that it really benefits only those who are already in a quite well-off position—the privileged few who will now get a 10 percent discount for early payback. We in the Māori Party are interested in another concept called “pay forward”: what is the investment we are making in the leaders of tomorrow? How will we ensure that those who are already facing disproportionately high levels of student debt—Māori, Pasifika, women—are also able to benefit? I ask what we are doing about them.

If we look at just one part of the picture—that is, Māori who studied for level 1 to 3 certificates—we get a sense of the scope of the problem. Nearly 70 percent of Māori who studied for level 1 to 3 certificates without completing and over 50 percent of Māori who studied at diploma and bachelor level without completing had made no progress in repayments 5 years later. A big factor in the lack of this repayment is basically low income, especially income around the student loan repayment threshold. The average income for Māori who studied for level 1 to 3 certificates was just above the repayment threshold. The inequalities are entrenched, with the average income for Māori with level 1 to 3 certificates being notably lower than for non-Māori with the same level of qualification.

These are complex issues; issues that require a far more comprehensive and sophisticated response than simply a 10 percent bonus on repayments and what that can achieve. Although a scheme is a good idea, it is a bare minimum, and it will certainly not be effective in addressing the real underlying barriers to participation in tertiary education that we in the Māori Party are particularly interested in.

We are certainly interested in the recommendation from Te Mana Ākonga, the national Māori student body, which provides representation for all Māori student rōpū at whare wānanga nationwide. Te Mana Ākonga has recommended that the preferred option is barrier-free education. By this it means that universal student allowances would be set at a living level, a fee abatement scheme would be in place, and every effort would be made to make it easier to invest in education.

I would draw the attention of the House to the very useful piece of research from Raymond Torres published in November 2008 that concludes that social development is a powerful generator of productivity that leads to economic growth. If we are thinking of the future, it will be education and health that will lead us out of the economic recession, and it is in this respect that we must take up the opportunities to help address the harsh repayment obligations already confronting most loan borrowers. With such tough economic times we have to agree with Sophia Blair from the New Zealand Union of Students’ Associations, who stated: “Helping to address the massive amount of student debt held in our communities is a key way the Government could choose to support families through this recession.”

We will support this bill at the first reading because even a little drop in the ocean is a help, we say, but we are also putting on record our view that we must start thinking creatively and proactively about how we address the drivers of debt, once and for all. We will be interested in the debate as it goes through the various stages of approval. Kia ora tātou.
