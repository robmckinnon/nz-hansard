Energy (Fuels, Levies, and References) Biofuel Obligation Repeal Bill

First Reading

  • Debate resumed.

Hon PETE HODGSON (Labour—Dunedin North) : It will come as no surprise to this House that the Labour Party will be opposing this legislation, vociferously. We think the decision of the Government to wind back biofuels is simply wrong. We think that it is unjust. We think the process that is being used by the Government is particularly unjust. We think that it is wrong for a Government to pass legislation that takes away the productive capacity of this or that individual enterprise without the enterprise having an ability to submit its case. We think that is wrong. We think that is unjust. We think that it is economically damaging. Quite apart from the fact that this company or that company will have to close, and that there will be a loss of jobs—and in the case of one such company the loss of 46 jobs—it is economically damaging. Quite apart from that, it is also economically damaging in the sense that it does not allow New Zealand to make slow and early steps—and let us make it clear that the progress of biofuels is in its infancy—towards a biofuels future. That has now been stopped by this legislation.

Hon Gerry Brownlee: No, no!

Hon PETE HODGSON: The Minister is deluding himself. It is environmentally damaging in the sense that our ability to replace the fossil fuels used in our transport sector will now be reduced.

Hon Gerry Brownlee: No, it is not.

Hon PETE HODGSON: It will be reduced, and that will be environmentally damaging.

Most important, it seems to me that this legislation will go through the House this week without a rationale, and it is that absence of a rationale that I want to concentrate on. The rationale, of course, was what the Minister in his first reading speech attempted to lay out. I listened to the speech very carefully and I have taken notes from it. I find that the argumentation of the Minister’s first reading speech is dishonest. I find that the argumentation is dishonest, and I am saying that it is dishonest in order to stay well within the Standing Orders. I now want to lay out, step by step, why I think the argumentation is dishonest.

The Minister began—I thought quite correctly—by pointing out that biofuels can cause enormous damage. They can displace an awful lot of arable agricultural space, for example, and they can also cause a great deal of deforestation. That is known; that is accepted. The Minister made the point correctly. However, he failed to point out two other things. The first is that the legislation this bill seeks to repeal does not allow for that type of biofuel to come into New Zealand. Secondly, by the repeal of that legislation, such biofuels will now be able to come into New Zealand—

Hon Gerry Brownlee: Rubbish!

Hon PETE HODGSON: —unless there is some regulation that the Minister has forthcoming that we do not know about. He sits there and says “Rubbish!”; he is wrong. It is a matter of fact that the biofuels legislation that was passed by this House earlier this year contains a significant number of sustainability safeguards. Furthermore, it is a matter of fact that the Ministry for the Environment and other agencies had indicated to the Government of the day—and would have indicated to this Government, too, I have no doubt—that their final regulatory regime would be ready by about July 2009. Yet we are getting rid of this legislation. We are getting rid of the sustainability parts of it that are in the primary legislation. The Government is squealing that somehow that is a good thing, but it is clearly something that will take our country backwards.

The Minister says there are not enough biofuels now to supply the sales obligation. What should be a surprise about that? The Government would lay out a sales obligation, give the industry notice, put up the obligation by a little bit each year, and then the industry would invest to meet that target. Those targets will no longer exist if this legislation is repealed—those sales obligations will no longer exist—and most, if not all, of the biofuel industry in this country will shut down. Mark my words!

The Minister said that the standards were not in place; I say that if he had waited 7 months they would have been in place. The Minister said that the standards could not be put in place, but that is contrary to the advice that officials gave to this Government, and to the select committee, on the original bill, etc.

But the thing I disliked most was the Minister saying that the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment opposed the bill and said that it was wrong.

Hon Dr Nick Smith: That’s right.

Hon PETE HODGSON: It is right.

Hon Dr Nick Smith: Absolutely right.

Hon PETE HODGSON: It is right, but what is wrong is that the bill was changed, and the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment then said that the legislation was in satisfactory form. The legislation was passed with her blessing, but the Minister has failed to acknowledge to the House that that parliamentary officer did her job. The Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment went into the select committee process with the bill as introduced and said it was not good enough, and it was not. The select committee fixed it up so that it was, and she said then that it was satisfactory, but the Minister in his first reading speech failed to advise Parliament of the views of one of Parliament’s own officers on the bill as it came out of the select committee—failed to advise Parliament. That is what I think is dishonest argument. I think the Government is bewildered on this; I think it is bewildered, illogical, and wrong.

Let us now go to the next point that the Minister raised, which is to do with money. The Minister said that the price of biofuels would cost somewhere between 2c and 8c a litre additionally. Well, that was the advice received by the select committee from the oil companies—that is what Chevron Oil said, or BP said. If the Minister is to become some sort of spokesperson for the “seven sisters”, that is fine and dandy, but why did the Minister not also advise the House what the official advice would have been on that cost differential? Why did he not do that? It seems to me that he did not do it because it did not suit his argument. Here—unfortunately for the Minister—are the official advice figures: the cost would not be from plus 2c to plus 8c per litre; the cost would be from minus 4c to plus 1.3c per litre. That was the advice that was given to the select committee—

Hon Gerry Brownlee: Just like the climate change—Kyoto credit analysis.

Hon PETE HODGSON: —and whether the Minister believes that advice, or not, it is really pertinent that he did not put those figures into the public arena when he was trying to give this lovely measured example of why he has to undertake this act of environmental vandalism. The member has made a botch of this. He has done it for substantially political reasons. It will mean loss of jobs. It will mean that our ability to progress biofuels will not be able to proceed.

There is the whole case of Mr McNicholl and his investments, but I will leave that for other speakers to talk about, because that is an example of someone who invested on the basis of certainty and law, and who found that that certainly did not carry through to the next Parliament, and that the incoming Government was going to roll back that law. That is an example, I think, of where the Government has decided to take a series of steps for political reasons rather than for reasons of good policy. All I can say is that in the entire first reading speech, which is when we would expect the rationale to come out, there is a Minister who is using dishonest argument, who is using figures that are selective, and who is in fact totally befuddled when it comes to the logic of sustainability because this legislation sweeps away the sustainability criteria that are now in the primary legislation. At the end of this week there will be none of that, and then anything will be able to flood into this country.

So here we are, in only the second week of Parliament, and the list so far is that the emissions trading scheme will be put to a so-called high-level select committee, that biofuels will be wound back, that somehow or another it is really bad to have halogen or fluorescent lamps around this country, and that the country will have other forms of environmental vandalism—all of which add up, in total, to this country moving into reverse. Later this week we will even be saying that it is OK to have gas-fired power stations all over again.

Does the member not know that climate change is now up front of, and central to, global politics? Does the member not realise that there have been elections in recent times in Australia and in the US? Does the member not realise that far from being a leader around the place on a response to climate change, this country is now becoming a follower? It has lost its way, it is a slow follower—it is not a fast follower, by any means—and it is illogical, out of date, heading in the wrong direction, and completely befuddled. That is a disgrace, in my view, to this country.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH (Minister for the Environment) : I want to make a number of important points in commending Gerry Brownlee on this Energy (Fuels, Levies, and References) Biofuel Obligation Repeal Bill, but I will not let pass the lectures from the Labour Party on climate change. The only things New Zealand led in the 9 years of a Labour Government were increased emissions and deforestation. They are the only areas in which Labour, in 9 years of Government, was able to lead on climate change policy. I say to members opposite that before they get on their feet and start lecturing us, they should make some apologies for their appalling record. I am advised by officials that the figures we will report to the United Nations next year and the year after that will be the legacy of the previous Government’s appalling record around deforestation and the increase in emissions.

The second point I make is that the Leader of the Opposition, in opening this session of Parliament, said he would hold the Government to account for its promises. What did the National Party promise on biofuels? It promised that it would repeal this stupid compulsion and replace it with a tax incentive. Why is it that now that we are trying to fulfil our promises, we get these sorts of arguments from members opposite?

The fundamental difference between members opposite and those on the Government side of the House is that Labour members believe in compulsion. They believe in the nanny State. Members on this side of the House believe in incentives, and let me go through the reasons why such incentives make very good sense. Firstly, I will deal with sustainability issues. I am yet to hear any Labour member provide some logic as to why the compulsory obligation for biofuels came into effect on 1 October even though the sustainability standard is at least 12 months away. What a loopy policy! Fancy saying to people: “You’ve got to have biofuels, but we haven’t worked out what the rules are about sustainability.” How can people make sensible decisions about investment when they do not know what the rules are? It was a classic case of putting the cart before the horse, and that is why we need to repeal the legislation.

The second thing I want to raise is that members opposite have claimed that with this legislation there is no sustainability standard for importing biofuels. The honest truth is that if someone wants to import ethanol made from palm oil from Brazil or Indonesia, there is absolutely nothing to stop him or her from doing so under the old legislation that was passed by the previous Government. I say to members opposite that they are misleading the House in saying otherwise. It is true that the compulsory requirement is not met, but it is not true to say that any control at all is imposed on the importation of sustainable biofuels.

I come to the next really important point. The median range estimate on the cost of this biofuel obligation is 4c per litre. Officials say that each cent per litre represents $60 million. In other words, the biofuel obligation amounts to a cost obligation of $240 million per year.

Moana Mackey: They did not tell us that it was 4c per litre. That is not what the officials said.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: I ask Ms Mackey how many members in this House believe that we have proportionality correct when the cost obligation is $240 million on biofuels, despite all of the questions asked internationally about their advocacy, and when we are spending $4 million a year on the greenhouse gas consortium looking at agricultural emissions. I think members of Parliament instinctively know that those proportions were all wrong.

Then we hear the ultimate in economic lunacy from those members. They claimed that the biofuels bill was actually going to save 5c a litre. Well, hang on a moment—

Nikki Kaye: Yeah, right!

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: Exactly. Would we need to have compulsion if the legislation was actually going to cost less than existing fuels? Not on your Nelly! I say to those dreamers on the other side of the House that they should connect with the economic reality. The only reason for compulsion was that the cost was greater.

That is why the approach of the new Government makes such good sense. We are giving a kick-start to biofuel technology. Let us provide a consistent tax advantage of an exemption from excise as currently applies to ethanol. It is far better, as we face this challenge of climate change, not to go in the nanny State direction where we compel people to do things, but rather to provide a financial incentive—

Hon Dr Michael Cullen: Oh, dear.

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: Well, I am not surprised that Dr Cullen likes compulsion. He spent 9 years on the Government benches telling ordinary New Zealanders how to live every part of their lives—how much water they could have in a shower, what sort of light bulbs they could use, and how much biofuel to use. I ask the House what the wisdom is in believing we can define, down to the narrow part of a percentage point, the exact, perfect amount of biofuel that should be used.

Pete Hodgson is wrong. He claimed that the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment supports a retention of a compulsory obligation on biofuels. That is wrong. The Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment said, and this is absolutely right, that if we narrowly define the criteria for what sustainable biofuels are—and we do not know what they are yet, because it has not been worked out—and we then use compulsion, we provide a wonderful cash cow for those in the biofuel industry. It is all very well for members opposite to talk about the odd job that might be created as a cash cow when there is compulsion, but what about the number of jobs that will be lost by the cost imposition of $240 million a year across the economy? Of course if I was a biofuel manufacturer I would want this law to stay as it is. It is a cash cow, but the real question for this Parliament is whether that approach is sensible. In the Government’s view, a financial incentive that is provided by way of the excise is a more sensible way to go.

I commend the Minister for dealing with this issue prior to Christmas.

Moana Mackey: Why is it not going to select committee?

Hon Dr NICK SMITH: That is a very good question, and I will tell the member why that is. It is because the previous Government’s policy has oil companies floundering around right now as they try to work out how to meet this obligation. Those companies do not know whether Brazilian ethanol will qualify. They are scrambling around, and that industry needs certainty. I say that this Parliament needs to move quickly. It needs to deal with the absolute mess that the previous Government’s biofuel policy was in, and that was a compulsory standard with no biofuel standard in place.

I hope the next speaker might possibly explain to the House why we should ignore the Secretary-General of the United Nations, who has made a plea for Governments not to introduce mandatory biofuels obligations. Have members opposite not read the House of Commons’ report in the United Kingdom that makes absolutely plain that such mandatory obligations are both economic and environmental lunacy? Have members opposite not read the report of the OECD that makes plain that these biofuels obligations are doing more harm in the world than good?

I say to members opposite that a tax incentive is the right way forward, and I will tell the House why that is the case. The truth is that we do not know how much biofuels will cost. My view is that if they are relatively inexpensive, to provide a tax incentive may see them go to 10 or 15 percent. I do not have, and this Government does not have, some magical number. But we say that a financial incentive will put pressure on the biofuels industry to make its products as cost-competitive as possible, rather than providing the blank cheque of compulsion.

My very last point is that, yes, this bill does philosophically divide the House, because members on this side take an approach of having financial incentives in respect of supporting biofuels, as opposed to the nanny-State politicians who have been thrown out of Government.

Hon Dr MICHAEL CULLEN (Labour) : If that member opposite, the Hon Nick Smith, keeps mentioning the words “nanny State” long enough, he convinces himself that he has some kind of argument in these matters. I can only assume that some of the people opposite must have had nannies when they were young whom they did not like, so somehow the word “nanny” keeps them going in some kind of big way.

Hon Dr Nick Smith: Tell us about the showers, Michael.

Hon Dr MICHAEL CULLEN: I wish the member would have a cold shower from time to time, and I am happy for him to have a 100-litre-a-second cold shower whenever he wants one. This is a man who gave a speech about economic lunacy. He talked about half of that in his speech, actually, because that was lunacy more than economic lunacy.

Let me just take him through the illogicality of his argument. He claims to want to see biofuels used; I think he said that. I think the Bluegreen manifesto said National wanted biofuels. He claims he wants to see certainty for those in the industry, and then he says he does not have a clue how much biofuels there may be if we go to a tax-incentive system. Where is the certainty and where is the guarantee that we will move to biofuels? He says that he wants to make sure that biofuels come from renewable sources. So do we all. That is why the legislation, as revised by the select committee, provides a process to ensure that sustainability. The member is still attacking the Biofuel Bill as it was introduced. He did not even read the legislation that came out of the select committee. We have certainty for the companies, which consists of basically saying “We do not care what you do. We do not care if you have any biofuels.” So what message will they take from that? They will take the message “Don’t have any biofuels.” What about those companies that have already started putting in tanks to cope with the higher proportion than the present 0.5 percent? Where has their certainty gone? Where has their return on investment gone?

Let me take the member through the economic arguments. He somehow seems to think that if we have a tax incentive, then that will solve the problem. Actually, it is not really a tax incentive. It is not taxing biofuels; it is continuing the regime where biofuels have no excise duty on them. Has that produced a rash of investment in biofuels? The regime that the National Government is suggesting is the regime before the biofuel legislation came into force, minus the requirements in the legislation for some kind of sustainability, certification, and reporting. That is what Gerry Brownlee’s bill actually states: it removes the requirement, it removes the provisions around sustainability. That is where we have been for years and we do not have any significant usage of biofuels in New Zealand. The member wants that, but he is not going to get it.

But if we have no tax on biofuels, then what does that do? Firstly, it reduces the Government’s revenue from road usage. We have hypothecation now on road-user taxes so if there is a reduction in revenue the Greens and I will not see more money for public transport, and the National Government and I will not see more money for roads. We will have less investment in roading, and less investment in public transport—

Hon Dr Nick Smith: Rubbish!

Hon Dr MICHAEL CULLEN: —unless—wait for it—there is a further transfer out of the Crown account into the land transport account, which must come from one of three sources. It must come, first, from increased taxation elsewhere, such as personal taxes, company taxes, or GST; secondly, from cutting spending on services elsewhere; or, thirdly, from borrowing, which means for future generations, firstly, higher personal taxes or GST; secondly, a reduction in services; or, thirdly, more borrowing for their grandchildren, which will lead to even bigger personal taxes, or cuts in services. There is no free lunch in fiscal policy. The member has been there long enough now to have learnt that very, very simple fact. He is asking for the Government to be able to tax elsewhere or to be able to cut social services. We have had a voluntary regime up until the passage of this Act and it has done nothing to produce biofuel usage in New Zealand. You see, what the member is arguing is basically the big old Dick Cheney position. He is kind of the Dick Cheney of the National Party these days—he wants to do what big oil companies tell us to do. He does not want to do what some poor little New Zealander with only a few million dollars to invest has been trying to do to help this country, because that person does not count. When it comes to the interests of Shell, BP, Mobil Oil, and all the rest of them, that poor little New Zealander does not count as far as National is concerned. National members get up in this House and make speech after speech, with tears pouring down their faces, as they talk about small business and all the rest of it. What is this bill about? It is about the interests of major international corporations versus the interests of New Zealand and New Zealand business. That is what this bill is about.

Goodness me, the main criticism that can be made of the current Act is not that it is the nanny State; it is basically leaving the kids at home with directions about where to find the food in the fridge. I think people could say that the problem with the current legislation is that it does not go far enough, not that it goes too far. I hope that Ms Fitzsimons does not criticise us too much for that when she gets up and speaks, because I think we are probably basically of one mind in that respect. There were jobs in this for New Zealand, there was certainty moving forward as higher levels of biofuels began to be phased in, there was a regime around sustainability—

Hon Dr Nick Smith: What about the cost?

Hon Dr MICHAEL CULLEN: The cost, as the member well knows, depends upon what the future cost of oil is. Ah, suddenly there is silence. The penny drops. Oil, at the moment, is under US$50 a barrel. Less than a year ago—6 or 7 months ago—it was US$153 a barrel. If any member in this House thinks seriously that he or she knows what the cost of oil will be in 5 years’ time, I would tell them: “Do not waste your time in here, mate. Go out and make yourself a few billion dollars on the international market.”, because if a member has that knowledge, it is knowledge that nobody else in the world has. It is very precious and it is worth vast amounts of money. Why would one spend one’s time in Parliament beating one’s gums about the nanny State if one knows what the future price of oil will be? The member does not know that. None of us know that. We know—and I occasionally chip at the Green Party about this—that it will go up and down. We know there is huge volatility about oil prices, but we also know one simple thing: the long-term trend of oil prices will be up. The trend may have been downwards recently over the last few months, but it will be up over the long term. Why? Because at the end of the day it has to be a shrinking resource in relation to demand. If we really want to see people in the Third World increasing their living standards, then whatever we do, the chances are that it will increase their demand for fossil fuels such as oil—a pity, but it is true. However much we manage to do in the next 10 years that is almost a certainty in that regard.

So there will be pressure upon a finite resource. We do not know how big that finite resource is because we do not know what has yet to be discovered, but one thing we do know, utterly, logically, completely, and obviously, is that at some point the resource is finite and, therefore, it will cost more to extract. Therefore, it will be demanded more and it will cost more. The cost argument is actually a particularly weak argument in this respect. What of the cost on the other side? The less we use biofuels the greater the liability under the Kyoto Protocol, or under any likely subsequent agreement, in terms of a charge to the Government because, in the first instance, that is a charge upon the Government, it becomes a liability on the Government accounts, and, therefore, it has to be met in some form or another. Again, there is no free lunch here. This can be met in effect by saying to the public that we are sending price signals that are designed to encourage them to use more efficient vehicles, and to drive their cars more carefully in terms of how they accelerate, etc., all of which have very significant impacts upon fuel consumption and can save people far more money than the impact of 4c a litre, or whatever it was the member was claiming in that regard. We have seen petrol prices at the pump vary by something like 70-odd cents a litre within the last year or so, and the member’s heart bleeds—bleeds—for 4c a litre when that is a tiny fraction of almost day-to-day trends. We have seen petrol prices go up or down by 5c a litre in a day as a consequence of market movements over the last year.

So the member has to get with the programme in that regard. He simply does not seem to grasp the point that this denial, this endless series of moves to deny, to put off the emissions trading scheme, to cancel the biofuels obligation, to try to set up new thermal power stations for no particular reason, to add to baseload capacity when there is a huge amount of potential renewable capacity for baseload generation—all of that suggests that in National we have a party that stupidly opposes what the previous Government did for the sake of opposing it, and is now stuck with the stupidity of its position.

JEANETTE FITZSIMONS (Co-Leader—Green) : This tragic little Energy (Fuels, Levies, and References) Biofuel Obligation Repeal Bill, which is being rushed through all its stages under urgency by a Minister of Energy and Resources who is ignorant of most aspects of his portfolio—apart from perhaps sexy coal—will do exactly the opposite of what the Minister says it will do. It will replace a potentially sustainable local industry—stable, secure, and well known—with intermittent imports of biofuels from unknown sources, which are likely to include palm oil from South-east Asia grown where the tropical forests, the last refuge of the orang-utan, used to grow, and corn ethanol with a higher carbon footprint than petroleum, subsidised by the US Government. That is what this bill will do.

Let me tell members about who is already investing, or is poised to invest, in biofuels in New Zealand. Although there are many companies doing little bits, there are three major companies involved. The company that people have heard most about is BioDiesel Oils (NZ) Ltd. Its managing director is Tom McNicholl, and it has a plant already operating in East Tāmaki that makes small quantities of bio-diesel from tallow, as well as a large plant under construction in Waharoa to make 60 million litres a year. That plant has now been mothballed because there will be no market for the fuel under this plan—and I will tell members why that is so in a minute—and that has caused the loss of 46 jobs. The point Mr McNicholl makes is that unless the oil companies are required to set up blending facilities, there will be no possibility of selling biofuels for blending, but only the possibility of selling it for use in 100 percent biofuel vehicles, which is a much more difficult market. In his letter to me, Mr McNicholl states “Exempting excise and road-user charges from biofuels act only as a subsidy to fuel consumers. They do not compel the use of biodiesel, unlike the biofuel obligation.”

Secondly, there is Ecodiesel Ltd in South Auckland, which is producing small quantities of bio-diesel from tallow. It was planning an expansion of its production to 40 million litres, but that has been delayed for at least 6 months because of the uncertainty created by this bill.

Thirdly, there is Biodiesel New Zealand Ltd, which uses rapeseed oil grown as a rotational crop with other food crops, so that the green material is ploughed back in as a green soil conditioner. The oil is extracted from the seeds, and the seed cake is used as an animal feed. That is a sustainable biofuel, and that company was planning to produce 70 million litres, but that is now under review.

That is what this bill has done. Those are three significant entrepreneurial companies in New Zealand that want to do the right thing by sustainability, and that are all undoubtedly sustainable by any definition of the term, but now their investment is seriously under threat.

Hon Gerry Brownlee: Why?

JEANETTE FITZSIMONS: Perhaps if the member listens, he will find out why.

The intention of the bill is to set up a stable local market. It would have actually been better for sustainability to say that we will not allow the import of any biofuels, and that we will have them made only in New Zealand, so that we know where they come from and that they are sustainable. The Greens could have supported that, but unfortunately people got nervous about the World Trade Organization and so forth, and did not think that was an option.

Mr Brownlee says the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment recommended against the passing of the Biofuel Bill, and that is true. She came to the Local Government and Environment Committee when the bill was in its form as introduced to the House, and she said “the bill currently before Parliament should not proceed in its current form”. That bill was not proceeded with in the form it had then, because that was before the Green Party negotiated with the Government a sustainability provision. We made it clear that without that sustainability provision we would not support the bill at all. That provision was drafted—and I had a large hand in that—it went through the select committee, and it was adopted. As far as I can make out, that sustainability provision is the most stringent sustainability provision in the world, yet it is being abolished by the Government today. It says that to be sustainable a fuel has to reduce by at least 35 percent the carbon it produces relative to petroleum, it must not be grown on land suitable for food production—it must not compete with food—and it must not be grown on land of high conservation or biodiversity value. Those are the three criteria—they are in section 34H(3) of the principal Act—and those have now gone.

Members opposite have been making a big feature of the fact that the regulations to give effect to that section are not yet drafted. They are partly drafted. The Minister and I met with the officials and said the idea that they could not do that until 2011 was rubbish. We asked whether they could do it by the middle of 2009. They said yes, so they were directed to do that by the middle of 2009. The amount of biofuel that is required under the legislation to be used in New Zealand by the middle of 2009 is so infinitesimally minute that there was no reason not to proceed without the regulations. But the fact is that because the section is in the Act, there is still a conceptual standard against which to measure biofuels that come into the country. It was always the intention that there would be a reporting obligation for those biofuels.

In her advice to the committee, the parliamentary commissioner provided a table of carbon emissions for a wide range of different biofuels around the world, showing that some of them—like the US corn-to-ethanol programme—have higher carbon emissions on a life cycle basis than petroleum, and others, particularly those made from wastes like tallow, have very low carbon emissions. Not all biofuels are equal. That is the thing the National Party has never been able to grasp.

The Minister said that the work on drafting the regulations will continue. Well, I ask what he will do with those regulations, because now there will be no primary legislation under which they can exist. We cannot have regulations that just hang in the air.

The Minister also says biofuels will cost up to 8c a litre more than petrol. That figure comes from the oil companies. I remember back to the days when we had a big campaign to get lead out of petrol, because it poisoned children’s brains. The oil companies told us repeatedly that petrol would be massively more expensive—I think they said 10c a litre more expensive at the time—if the lead was taken out. Eventually we forced them to take the lead out, and nobody even noticed any change to the price. Those things are not required for bio-diesel, which uses the same systems as diesel. Another advantage of the Act was in trying to create a local biofuel industry that we knew would be predominantly bio-diesel. It would mean that the obligation could be met from domestic production, and that would, as I said before, eliminate a number of the concerns about sustainability.

When we think about compulsion, we need to remember that the Act is very, very flexible. It allows the oil companies to implement their obligation how they best see fit, and where they determine it needs to be implemented. The oil companies told us during the select committee process that they simply could not access enough biofuel to meet the original obligation of 3.4 percent. However, when we looked into that using the oil companies’ own figures, which they had provided to the select committee, we found that with all the development that was in progress they could meet the original target of 3.4 percent. To say they could not meet the target when we had dropped the obligation in the Act down to 2.5 percent seems slightly ridiculous.

Mr Brownlee likes to talk in terms of litres. Of course, the obligation uses units of energy, not of volume. To meet the 2.5 percent obligation requires 5.5 petajoules of biofuel; for the 3.4 percent obligation it was 8 petajoules. We were told that the potential for domestic production over the period to 2012 was for up to 4 petajoules of bio-diesel from tallow, 2 petajoules of bio-diesel from rapeseed oil, and 0.3 petajoules of biofuel from ethanol. That is enough to meet the original obligation of 3.4 percent in the bill, but because the legislation was designed to provide certainty and to provide the basis for a domestic biofuel industry, we did not feel the need to push that target, and we took the officials’ advice.

We have heard a lot about the impact of the biofuel obligation on the price of fuel. Dr Smith is choosing simply to quote the oil companies, and not the official advice that we received at the select committee.

Hon Dr Nick Smith: What’s your figure?

MOANA MACKEY: Of course, one of the difficulties about saying how much this measure will cost is that we need to take into account the price of oil, the price of biofuels, the exchange rate in relation to imports, and also how the obligation will be implemented. If the oil companies chose to sell 100 percent biofuel direct to customers and none of it through the pump, there would not be any impact on the price of fuel at the pump. Every week at the select committee Dr Smith would ask the officials to go away and do some more crystal ball - gazing, and every time they came back with advice he would say it was not good enough, because they could not accurately predict the figures. As Dr Smith knows—and I hate to think of all the grey hairs he gave the very young officials from the Ministry of Economic Development we had at that time—the officials came up with a group of tables that took into consideration a number of scenarios. Those tables said that for petrol it could possibly be a negative number, which meant it would be cheaper—

Hon Dr Nick Smith: If it’s cheaper, why do you need compulsion?

MOANA MACKEY: Oh, my Lord! The tables also said that for Gull Petroleum, which currently sells biofuels more cheaply than the other oil companies sell their regular petrol, with bio-diesel the price could possibly go up by 1c to 3c per litre.
